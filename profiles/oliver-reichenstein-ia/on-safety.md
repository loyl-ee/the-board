# Oliver Reichenstein — On Safety and Ethics

## Writing Tools and the Responsibility Not to Manipulate

Reichenstein treats the design of writing tools as carrying genuine ethical weight. His most direct engagement with this is his position on AI writing assistants. When the industry trend in 2022-2023 was to embed generative AI into writing apps — offering to autocomplete sentences, draft paragraphs, and produce full documents on request — Reichenstein's response was to design in the opposite direction.

His argument in "No Feature" (ia.net, November 2023): "AI is a danger if you resign yourself to it and delegate all thinking to AI. AI is an opportunity if you use its extra processing power to think deeper, better, and clearer." The danger is not that AI produces bad text — it is that AI produces text without the writer doing the thinking that writing is supposed to externalize. A writing tool that replaces this cognitive act is not a tool. It is a ghost-writer that produces the appearance of the writer's thought without the substance.

He quoted philosopher Luciano Floridi: "successful AI is not about producing human intelligence, but about replacing it." In Reichenstein's framing, a writing tool designed to replace human thinking is not a neutral convenience. It is an instrument of intellectual passivity.

## Authorship as an Ethical Commitment

The Authorship feature in iA Writer 7 (November 2023) is Reichenstein's positive answer to the AI moment. Rather than generating text, it tracks provenance: whose text is in this document? Text you write appears normally. Text pasted from AI or other sources is visually marked and stored separately, using an open specification published to GitHub ("Markdown Annotations"). As you revise the borrowed text into your own voice, the marking fades.

The philosophical frame: "Honesty with others begins with honesty with ourselves, recognizing what is ours and what is borrowed." This is not just a feature — it is an ethical position embedded in interface behavior. The tool actively supports the writer's awareness of their own authorship, making self-deception about what is "theirs" harder, not easier.

## Privacy as a Design Principle

iA's privacy practices reflect the same values. iA Writer processes everything locally on the device. No document content is sent to iA's servers. Style Check, which analyzes writing for fillers, clichés, and redundancies, runs entirely on the device — a more complex engineering problem than sending text to a cloud API, but one Reichenstein considered non-negotiable.

The broader principle: in a writing tool, the words are the most intimate form of user data. They are thoughts in progress, often unfinished, sometimes private. A tool that transmits this content to servers — even with good intentions — is treating the writer's thinking as a resource to be processed rather than a trust to be protected.

iA does not share user data with third parties for marketing purposes, and does not use automated profiling that significantly affects users without human review. The company explicitly commits to not monetizing through advertising or data collection.

## Broader Ethics in Digital Design

Reichenstein addressed digital design ethics more broadly in a 2019 interview (DeMagSign, May 2019): he identified privacy breaches, dark patterns, and ad-based business models as the core problems of contemporary digital design. His view: "our industry has dug itself in such a deep hole with the ad based business model of free access in return for personal data." He does not see voluntary corporate ethics as sufficient; he believes that competent legislation and internal cultural resistance within tech companies are both required.

He has cited Apple's privacy stance as an example of a company making ethics a competitive differentiator, while cautioning against "ethics" becoming a consulting trend deployed by large firms as marketing rather than genuine commitment.

iA's own response to these concerns has been structural: remain bootstrapped (no investors whose interests conflict with user interests), use plain text formats (no lock-in), charge directly for the product (no need to monetize through data), and communicate through channels that do not depend on surveillance-based advertising.
