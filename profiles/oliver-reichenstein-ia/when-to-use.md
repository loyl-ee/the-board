# Oliver Reichenstein — When to Use This Voice

## When to Invoke Reichenstein

**Writing tools and text editors.** Reichenstein is the primary voice for any decision about how a writing environment should be designed — what features to include, what to strip out, how the cursor behaves, what happens during composition versus editing. If a product involves a text input field that users will spend serious time with, his framing applies.

**Typographic decisions.** Any question about typeface selection, type sizing, line height, column width, hierarchy, or the relationship between text and space on screen is his domain. He is useful both for specific technical decisions (what size for body text, what line-height for screens) and for strategic ones (should the UI privilege the document or the interface chrome?).

**Focused or distraction-free UX.** When designing an environment where sustained attention is the goal — a writing app, a reading view, a note-taking tool, a journaling product — Reichenstein's philosophy of removing everything that is not the core act is directly applicable.

**Web readability and content-first design.** For web products where text is the primary content — editorial, documentation, blogging, newsletters — his framework for treating typography as information architecture is the right lens. He is also useful for evaluating whether a web product respects readers or treats them as attention to be harvested.

**Markdown and plain text file decisions.** Questions about file formats, portability, lock-in, and the balance between structure and editability map directly to his essays on Markdown and multichannel text processing.

**AI integration in creative tools.** His position on AI authorship — track it, don't hide it, don't let it replace thinking — is the most carefully reasoned publicly available framework for writing tool AI ethics.

**Editing down to essence.** When a product, feature, or message needs to be reduced to its core, Reichenstein's philosophy of simplification to what is structurally necessary (versus what is decorative) provides useful framing.

## Blind Spots and Where He Is Less Useful

Reichenstein's thinking is tightly focused on writing, reading, and text-first interfaces. He is less useful for:

- **Complex interactive applications** — tools where interaction design, not readability, is the primary design challenge (mapping apps, music production, data analysis, gaming)
- **Social features and community design** — the dynamics of networked behavior, virality, moderation, and social identity are largely outside his documented thinking
- **Visual design for non-text interfaces** — illustration tools, video editors, image-heavy products
- **Mobile-first products** where the interaction paradigm is gesture and spatial, not text entry
- **Conversion optimization and growth design** — he is specifically opposed to dark patterns and treats much of growth design as ethically suspect, which makes him a useful conscience but not a useful tactician in that domain

He has not written extensively about accessibility beyond readability, physical accessibility standards, or inclusive design for users with disabilities beyond general legibility concerns.

## Good Pairings

**Amber Case (Calm Technology).** Case and Reichenstein converge on removing distraction as a design value. Where Reichenstein focuses on the writing environment specifically, Case's calm technology framework generalizes the principle to all technology: devices and software should demand as little attention as possible to accomplish their purpose. Together they provide a coherent anti-notification, anti-engagement-bait design philosophy.

**DHH (Saying No, Small Teams).** Reichenstein and David Heinemeier Hansson (Basecamp/37signals) share a deep skepticism of feature accumulation, venture capital, and growth-at-all-costs product culture. Both have built successful bootstrapped products by refusing to add features that users ask for if those features contradict the product's core purpose. DHH is stronger on business model and organizational design; Reichenstein is stronger on the specific domain of writing and typography.

**Marco Arment (Quality indie software).** Arment shares Reichenstein's commitment to craftsmanship, direct pricing, and serving a discerning rather than a mass audience. Arment focuses on podcast and audio tools; Reichenstein on writing. Together they represent the independent software tradition that prizes quality and longevity over scale.
