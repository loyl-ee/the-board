# Oliver Reichenstein — On the Customer Journey

## Respecting the Writer's Intelligence

Reichenstein's user philosophy rests on a specific assumption about who iA Writer's user is: a person who takes their craft seriously. The app does not explain itself or apologize for its constraints. It offers no onboarding tour that says "here's what iA Writer does." It expects the writer to arrive with intent and provides an environment suited to serious work.

This is a deliberate stance, not an oversight. Most consumer software is designed for the lowest-friction user — someone who might abandon the product if they encounter any friction at all. Reichenstein inverts this. The constraints that would cause a casual user to bounce — no formatting toolbar, no font picker, a fixed monospace typeface — are precisely the features that attract the user iA Writer is for. The product self-selects for writers who want to think about what they write, not writers who want to make their documents look impressive.

He acknowledged that this is not for everyone: "not everyone was a fan and not everyone needs to be." The product was built with the assumption that a coherent, well-designed experience for a specific audience is more valuable than a compromised experience for a mass audience.

## The App Store as Discovery Layer

iA Writer competes in the App Store alongside hundreds of writing apps, many of which offer more visible features — rich formatting, templates, tagging systems, AI autocomplete. Reichenstein's App Store strategy has relied on iA Writer's reputation and word-of-mouth among writers who care about their tools, rather than on screenshots that emphasize feature counts. The product's awards — four App of the Year wins, Apple Design Award Finalist 2025 — have helped signal quality to a discerning audience.

The free trial model (two weeks, no sign-up required on Mac and Windows) reflects respect for the user's autonomy: try it on your terms, see if it works for you, then decide. There is no dark pattern designed to get the user to commit before they've understood what they're buying.

## User Feedback and Product Evolution

Reichenstein takes user feedback seriously as a signal, but filters it carefully. The response to iA Writer 7's Authorship feature ("Whoa, Feedback!," ia.net, December 2023) illustrates this: the feature was broadly praised by writers who engaged with it thoughtfully, while generating skepticism from users who had expected a more conventional AI writing assistant. Reichenstein noted that most users who read beyond the headline understood the approach. The product was not changed to accommodate users who wanted AI to write for them — because those users are not the audience.

This is a coherent customer journey philosophy: attract users who share your values, be honest about what the product does and does not do, and iterate based on feedback from users whose needs align with the product's purpose. It is a narrower funnel than mass-market consumer apps pursue, but it produces a more loyal, more engaged user base.

## Localization and Purchasing Power

iA's pricing philosophy extends to its customer journey internationally. For iA Presenter, the company adapted pricing to purchasing power using the Big Mac Index — a monthly subscription costs approximately the local price of one Big Mac in each market. This is a genuine attempt to respect users in markets where international software prices represent a significant income fraction. The design of the purchase experience is itself an expression of user respect.

## Note on Documentation Gaps

Reichenstein does not write extensively about marketing strategy, user acquisition, conversion funnels, or customer lifecycle management in the conventional product management sense. His published thinking focuses on product philosophy and design rationale. The customer journey, for iA, is largely implicit: build something excellent for a specific audience, price it honestly, and let the work speak.
