# Oliver Reichenstein — On UX

## Removing Distraction as the Core UX Act

Most UX frameworks focus on what to add: affordances, feedback, onboarding, discoverability. Reichenstein's UX philosophy is largely organized around what to remove. For writing tools, he has argued that the single most powerful UX intervention is the elimination of everything that is not writing. No formatting toolbar means no opportunity to procrastinate by adjusting fonts. No settings screen means the writer cannot spend time tweaking preferences instead of drafting. No feature notification, no syncing status bar, no word-count badge — none of these are neutral additions. Each one is a small cost paid in attention.

His key insight: the writing environment is not just a container for writing. It is itself a designed mental state. The physical qualities of a space affect how people think and work in it — and digital environments are no different. A cluttered writing interface produces cluttered writing. A spare, structured environment supports clear, structured thought.

## The Writing Environment as Mental Architecture

Reichenstein has drawn on his experience using physical typewriters during iA Writer's design process. He noted that he wrote better on a typewriter than on a computer, and he analyzed why: the typewriter removes all options except the act of writing. The clack of keys, the resistance of the mechanism, the centering of the cursor at the vertical middle of the page — all of these physical facts shape the psychological state of the writer.

iA Writer translates these effects into digital form: the typewriter scrolling (cursor locked to the center of the screen), the single fixed typeface, Focus Mode's fading of non-active text, and the absence of a formatting toolbar all recreate the typewriter's constraint. As one user described it: "The blue cursor blinking at the centre of my screen triggers a deep desire to put words down." That psychological response is the UX outcome being designed toward.

## Focus Mode: Attention as Interface

Focus Mode — which fades all text except the current sentence or paragraph — is iA Writer's most distinctive UX feature. It operationalizes the concept of attentional focus in interface form. When only the sentence you are writing is visible at full contrast, the rest of the document becomes ambient rather than distracting. You are aware the rest of the text exists, but you are not drawn to revise it before the current thought is complete.

This is a specific UX position: most writing apps treat the document as a flat plane of equally weighted attention. Focus Mode introduces hierarchy into the writing experience — what you are writing now is privileged over what you have already written. This matches how experienced writers describe the ideal mental state for drafting: present-tense, forward-moving, non-revisionary.

## On Spell-Check, Grammar, and Automated Correction

Reichenstein's approach to spell-check and grammar assistance is more nuanced than simple rejection. iA Writer uses operating system-level spelling tools rather than building proprietary alternatives — a choice that reflects his philosophy of integration over feature accumulation. However, he has been consistently skeptical of AI-powered grammar assistants that replace the writer's judgment rather than informing it.

His Style Check feature (introduced 2020) illustrates the distinction: it highlights potential problems (fillers, clichés, redundancies) but performs no corrections. The writer sees the flag and makes the call. Compare this to Grammarly-style tools that accept changes automatically or that propose rewrites — those tools move the locus of decision-making from writer to algorithm. Reichenstein's position is that this transfer of agency is not neutral: it trains writers to defer to automated judgment rather than develop their own ear.

More pointedly, AI writing assistants that generate prose on the writer's behalf raise a deeper UX problem. In "Writing with AI" (ia.net, November 2023), he argues: "using AI in the editor replaces thinking. Using AI in dialogue increases thinking." The UX distinction is between a tool that generates output for you (which degrades your capacity) and a tool that helps you articulate and refine your own ideas through dialogue (which develops your capacity).

## The Cursor as Presence

Reichenstein has described the cursor — specifically the blinking text cursor in iA Writer, rendered in blue — as a significant UX element. It is the user's presence in the document: where you are, where you are about to act. A well-designed cursor communicates this clearly and without distraction. A poorly designed cursor — wrong size, wrong color, too little contrast — subtly undermines the sense of inhabiting the document.

This level of attention to the cursor is characteristic of Reichenstein's approach: UX considerations extend to details so small that most designers would consider them beneath notice, but which collectively determine whether the writing environment feels alive or inert.

## UX and Writing Tool Responsibility

Reichenstein treats writing tool UX as a domain with genuine ethical weight. The design of an environment that people use for hours daily — that shapes the pace, rhythm, and quality of their thinking — carries responsibilities that interface design for, say, e-commerce does not. A writing tool that interrupts frequently trains fragmented attention. A writing tool that makes it easy to produce AI-generated text without the writer noticing trains intellectual passivity. The UX decisions are not just preference — they are upstream of the quality of thought produced.
