# Oliver Reichenstein — Core Philosophy

## Typography as the Foundation of Design

Reichenstein's single most consequential intellectual contribution is his 2006 essay "Web Design is 95% Typography" (ia.net). The argument was simple and radical: since 95% of the information on the web is written language, the primary discipline of web design is typography — not visual decoration, not color theory, not layout as an end in itself. Typography, in his framing, is not about choosing fonts. It is about shaping text for optimal performance: leading, spacing, hierarchy, column width, color, white space. It is engineering for the human eye and mind.

The essay provoked a storm of protest from web designers who felt dismissed. Reichenstein followed up a month later ("Reactions to 95% Typography," ia.net, November 2006) refining his argument: typography in practice means treating text as a user interface. The successful sites of that era — Google, eBay, Craigslist — worked because their designers treated text functionally, not decoratively.

This is not purely a visual claim. It is a philosophical one: the form of writing is inseparable from the thought it carries. How text is arranged determines whether ideas land or fail. Typography is not the decoration on top of communication — it is the medium through which communication is made possible or destroyed.

## Design Is How It Works

Reichenstein aligns with Steve Jobs' maxim that "design is how it works," but applies it specifically to the written word. In his 2013 essay "Learning to See" (ia.net), he adapts Paul Klee's instruction to painters — "see with one eye, feel with the other" — to designers: the trained design eye must coexist with the ability to feel as an ordinary user does. The designer who loses access to naive perception produces work that impresses other designers and baffles everyone else.

His deeper claim is that design is fundamentally an act of thinking. In "Design as Thought" (ia.net, June 2024, co-authored with philosopher Orlando Budelacci), he states: "Designing is thinking. Thinking is giving form to indefinite matter." This is not metaphor — it is his actual theory of what designers do. Design precedes aesthetics; the beautiful surface, if it comes at all, is the result of rigorous thought, not its starting point.

## Writing as Thinking

Reichenstein treats writing not merely as communication but as cognition. Writing is how you discover what you think. He extends this to product design: iA Writer was built on the premise that the act of writing, if it is to be genuine, must involve the full cognitive effort of the writer. Any tool that makes it easy to produce text without thinking — whether by offering premade templates, by autocompleting sentences, or by generating AI prose — is not helping the writer. It is replacing the writer.

In "No Feature" (ia.net, November 2023), he quotes philosopher Luciano Floridi: "successful AI is not about producing human intelligence, but about replacing it." Reichenstein's design response is to build tools that resist this replacement.

## Focus as the Highest UX Value

For Reichenstein, removing distraction is not a feature — it is the entire point. The writing environment is a designed mental state. When iA Writer launched in 2010, its then-radical proposition was: no formatting toolbar, no font picker, no color options. The writer has only the cursor, the text, and a single typeface. Every element that is not writing is an invitation to procrastinate.

He wrote: "In iA Writer, you have no choice but to think." ("Design as Thought," ia.net, 2024.) This is intentional. The constraint is the product.

## The Web as a Reading and Writing Medium

Reichenstein has consistently argued that the web is, at its core, a reading and writing medium — not primarily a visual medium, not primarily an interactive medium. This shapes everything from his typography essays to his product decisions. If the web is first and foremost where people read and write, then the designers of web experiences are in the business of typography and information architecture, whether they admit it or not.

His 2012 work on responsive typography ("Responsive Typography: The Basics," ia.net) extended this view into the era of multiple devices: the reading experience should adapt to viewing distance and device context, not just screen width. Typography should serve the reader wherever they are.

## Saying No to Complexity

Reichenstein's philosophy has a consistent negative dimension: the active rejection of feature accumulation, visual noise, formatting excess, and AI-generated content. He has compared iA Writer to "a scalpel in a world of Swiss army knives." The scalpel metaphor is precisely chosen — a scalpel does one thing, perfectly. Adding a can opener to it would ruin it.

This connects to the Swiss design tradition he was raised near and discovered intellectually in Tokyo. The work of Müller-Brockmann, Ruder, and Tschichold proceeds from the same premise: that clarity and restraint are not aesthetic preferences but ethical commitments to the audience. Good design does not impose itself. As he put it: "Good design is invisible." ("Good Design is Invisible," ia.net, The Verge interview, July 2012.) When design calls attention to itself, it has failed its primary purpose.

## Philosophy and Design as One Discipline

Unlike most designers, Reichenstein is explicit about his philosophical debts. He studied under the European philosophical tradition and brings that seriousness to product decisions. He references Giambattista Vico's "Maker's Knowledge" — the idea that we understand what we make — as a foundation for why designers can produce genuine knowledge, not just artifacts. He draws on Anaïs Nin ("We don't see things as they are, we see them as we are") to explain why designers must continually fight their own trained perception to stay in contact with users' actual experience.

This makes him unusual in the design world: someone who can argue at the level of ideas, not just aesthetics, about why a particular interface decision is right or wrong.
