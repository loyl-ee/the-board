# Oliver Reichenstein — On Business

## iA's Unusual Position

iA operates in a design world dominated by either venture-funded startups chasing growth at all costs or large agencies serving corporate clients. iA is neither. It is a fully independent, bootstrapped company with no outside investors. According to Reichenstein, everything iA does is 100% self-funded. This has been a deliberate, defining choice since iA's founding in Tokyo in 2005 — and it shapes every business decision.

The absence of external investors means iA is not optimizing for acquisition, for a funding round, or for a growth metric imposed by people who do not build the product. It means they can spend ten years developing a physical notebook (the iA Notebook took over 100 prototypes and was in development for a decade before launch), can resist adding AI features when AI is the industry trend, and can walk away from business models they find ethically compromised.

## The Dual Model: Consulting and Product

iA has operated with two revenue streams since its early years: consulting for major clients and revenue from its software products. The clients have included Die Zeit, NHK, Wikipedia, Condé Nast, The Guardian, Asics, Red Bull, UBS, KPMG, Monotype, and Ringier. The products are iA Writer, iA Presenter, and iA Notebook.

In a 2012 interview (ia.net, The Verge), Reichenstein explained the rationale: "We plan to continue both working for clients and on products, because we feel that both benefit from the experience we gain in each field." Consulting work grounds the team in real-world information design problems at scale; product work forces them to solve those problems in a way that serves individuals, not just corporate stakeholders. The cross-pollination is intentional.

## Pricing as Philosophy

Reichenstein's pricing thinking is unusually honest. For iA Writer, the model is "pay once per platform, own it forever" — a direct rejection of subscription dependency for individual users. For iA Presenter, launched in 2022-2023, he published detailed public reasoning about pricing ("Presenter Pricing (II)," ia.net, February 2023): subscriptions should cost roughly the price of one Big Mac per month in each local market (using the Big Mac Index as a purchasing-power proxy), and outright ownership should cost roughly 2-3x the annual subscription price.

He is explicit about something many software companies leave unstated: a bootstrapped five-person studio has nothing in common with a large enterprise's ability to pay. Pricing should reflect purchasing power, not just dollars extracted. Student access to iA Presenter is free, distributed outside the App Store using school email verification.

## Staying Small as Strategy

Reichenstein has consistently resisted scaling iA into a large organization. The studio has remained small — a deliberate choice, not a limitation. Small teams can maintain coherent vision, move thoughtfully, and avoid the organizational drag that produces bloated software. This mirrors his product philosophy: just as iA Writer is powerful through restraint, iA the company is effective through focus.

He abandoned commercial social media platforms in 2023, shifting communication to Mastodon, newsletters, YouTube, and LinkedIn — partly as a philosophical statement and partly as a business decision to own the relationship with users directly, without algorithmic intermediaries.

## On the Business of Writing Software

Reichenstein has not written extensively about the business of software as a category, but his practice demonstrates a coherent position: charge fairly, own your file formats (plain text and Markdown ensure users are never locked in), do not monetize through advertising or data collection, and build for the long term. iA Writer has been continuously developed for 15 years. That durability — rare in consumer software — is itself a business argument, one that earns trust and loyalty among users who take their craft seriously.
