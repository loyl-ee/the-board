# Oliver Reichenstein — On Design

## Typography as the Atomic Unit of Design

Reichenstein's view of typography is not decorative but structural. In his foundational 2006 essay "Web Design is 95% Typography" (ia.net), he invoked Emil Ruder's 1969 observation that typographers must "divide up and organize and interpret this mass of printed matter in such a way that the reader will have a good chance of finding what is of interest to him" — and argued this describes web design precisely. Typography is not a style applied to content after the thinking is done. Typography is how structure becomes legible.

He distinguishes macro-typography from micro-typography. Macro-typography encompasses grid systems, column width, hierarchy, format — the large-scale decisions that shape how readers navigate a document or page. Micro-typography covers kerning, letter-spacing, punctuation detail — the subatomic level of readability. On the web, macro-typography is the primary leverage point; the designer works with format and grid rather than fine-grained type control. In "Good Design is Invisible" (ia.net/The Verge interview, July 2012), he stated: "Good design is invisible. Good screen design happens in the subatomic level of microtypography, the invisible grid of macrotypography, and the invisible world of interaction design."

## Swiss Design DNA

Reichenstein grew up in Basel, the city that gave rise to the International Typographic Style — the Swiss Style associated with Müller-Brockmann, Emil Ruder, Armin Hofmann, and Adrian Frutiger. He absorbed this tradition not in formal education (he studied philosophy) but through immersion — discovering the Swiss typographic masters in Tokyo bookshops, which he described as "like discovering grammar after already being able to speak." The Swiss Style's core commitments — grid structure, rational composition, objective clarity, rejection of ornament — are legible throughout iA's work and in iA Writer's typographic choices.

But Reichenstein also absorbed Japanese design sensibilities during his years in Tokyo. He noted deep similarities between Swiss and Japanese design cultures: both emphasize restraint, precision, and attention to negative space. Japanese designers' experience with the complexity of kanji develops a spatial awareness — a sensitivity to the relationship between form and surrounding space — that Reichenstein found complemented rather than contradicted his European training.

## Whitespace as a Design Element

In Reichenstein's practice, whitespace is not empty space left over after content is placed. It is the primary structural element. A column width that produces comfortable line length — roughly 65-75 characters for body text — requires significant margin space on wider screens. That margin is not waste; it is what makes the text column readable. Compressing margins to add more content is, in his framing, a typographic error — the same as reducing leading to fit more lines, which destroys rhythm.

This is directly connected to his critique of average web design: most web pages fail not because of bad color choices or ugly graphics but because text is set too wide, too small, at the wrong leading, in too many competing visual weights.

## Rejecting Decoration

Reichenstein aligns with Dieter Rams' principle that "good design is as little design as possible — less, but better, because it concentrates on the essential aspects, and the products are not burdened with non-essentials." He considers skeuomorphism (mimicking real-world textures and materials in interfaces) by definition a failure: "Skeuomorphism is by definition bad design," he said in a 2012 interview (Creative Bloq), though he distinguished between problematic literal imitation and effective use of conceptual metaphor (such as the Desktop folder metaphor).

His positive design standard is invisibility. When design is working perfectly, users do not notice it — they simply have the experience the design was intended to create. Noticed design is design that has inserted itself between the user and their goal.

## Type and Reading Speed

The practical side of Reichenstein's typography work concerns reading performance. In "Responsive Typography: The Basics" (ia.net, June 2012), he documented iA's research on type sizes across devices: body text on iPhone (held close) requires smaller sizes than body text on iPad (held further away); desktop displays require adjustment for varying monitor sizes. The standard benchmark iA arrived at — Georgia 16px for body text on desktop — became one of the most-cited references in web typography discussions.

His finding that 140% line-height works well for screen text (versus the tighter settings natural for print) reflects how screen rendering's anti-aliasing and pixel grid introduce visual noise that wider leading counteracts. These are not aesthetic preferences — they are performance measurements for the reading act.

## The Grid as Typography

Reichenstein treats grid design as a branch of typographic discipline, not a separate visual practice. The grid emerges from the text: once the body text size and column width are established, the grid follows. This is the inverse of how most designers work (establish a visual grid, then pour in content). Starting from typography means the text is always the primary design element — everything else is organized to serve it.

In his redesign work for major publications — Die Zeit, NHK, Wikipedia — this approach produced designs that felt legible and hierarchically clear without appearing heavily styled. The layouts worked because the underlying typographic structure was right, not because distinctive visual elements made them recognizable.

## Good Design as Invisible Work

The title of his 2012 Verge interview — "Good design is invisible" — encapsulates his design aesthetic. The designer's goal is not to be seen. The goal is to create an experience so well-shaped that users move through it without friction, without confusion, without noticing the hand that shaped it. This is a more demanding standard than producing impressive-looking work, because invisible excellence has no obvious surface to point to and say "this is what I did."

It requires the designer to consistently subordinate self-expression to user experience — a discipline that Reichenstein treats as both a professional requirement and an ethical one.
