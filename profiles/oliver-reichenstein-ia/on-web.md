# Oliver Reichenstein — On the Web

## The Web as a Reading Medium

Reichenstein's most foundational and most contested claim is simple: the web is, at its core, a reading and writing medium. "95% of the information on the web is written language," he wrote in his 2006 essay "Web Design is 95% Typography" (ia.net). This was not a descriptive observation only — it was a programmatic claim about what web design must therefore be. If the overwhelming majority of web content is text, then the primary discipline of web designers is typography, which is to say: the art and science of making written language readable.

This claim was radical in 2006, in the middle of the Flash animation era, when many designers treated the web as a primarily visual medium and typography was an afterthought constrained by the small set of web-safe fonts. Reichenstein argued that this was the profession's fundamental error — and that the web's best-performing sites (Google, eBay, Craigslist, YouTube, Flickr) demonstrated the power of typographically rigorous, text-as-interface design whether or not their designers explicitly thought of themselves as typographers.

## The Follow-Up: Typography as Method

In "Reactions to 95% Typography" (ia.net, November 2006), Reichenstein clarified his argument in response to the "storm of protest" from designers who felt the original essay had dismissed their visual work. His position: typography in practice is not choosing fonts or making fonts. It is designing text for optimal performance — which means treating text as a user interface. "Focus on typography, ask yourself, which text is functional, which text is passive, systematize them, order them hierarchically, simplify, create an interface."

This is a design method, not just an aesthetic preference. It produces web interfaces that are organized by the logic of their content rather than by the logic of visual decoration.

## Redesigning the Web's Reading Institutions

iA's consulting work has been a long-running laboratory for these ideas. The firm redesigned Die Zeit (one of Germany's most respected newspapers), NHK (Japan's public broadcaster), Wikipedia, and publications for Condé Nast and The Guardian, among others. These redesigns were anchored in typographic discipline: clear hierarchy, appropriate column widths, sufficient leading, reduction of visual noise that competed with text. The iA approach made heavy-content publications — the kind where readers spend significant time with long-form text — more legible and structurally clear without imposing heavy visual branding on top of the content.

## Responsive Typography

Before the term "responsive design" entered widespread use, iA was thinking about how typography must adapt to different reading contexts. "Responsive Typography: The Basics" (ia.net, June 2012) documented the research behind iA Writer's cross-device typography. The core insight: type size is not determined by aesthetic preference but by reading distance. A phone held at arm's length requires smaller type than a tablet held further away; a desktop monitor requires different consideration still.

Reichenstein established that Georgia 16px was a reasonable baseline for readable body text on desktop screens — a benchmark that influenced a generation of web typography discussions. He also documented why 140% line-height works better on screen than in print: pixel rendering introduces visual noise that wider line spacing compensates for.

The framework treats typography not as a fixed artifact but as an adaptive system — one that must serve the actual reading conditions of actual users rather than presenting a single idealized layout.

## Performance as Part of Design

Reichenstein has treated web performance — page load speed, server response time, elimination of unnecessary scripts — as a design concern, not a purely technical one. A slow page is a bad reading experience. Bloated JavaScript that delays text rendering is a typographic failure before it is a technical failure. This connects to his overall philosophy: anything that gets between the reader and the text is a design problem.

He has expressed criticism of web trends that prioritize visual richness over performance — heavy animations, large image backgrounds behind text, font-loading strategies that cause layout shifts — as failures of the design priority hierarchy: text first, everything else in service of it.

## The Web's Current State

In more recent writing and public statements (Mastodon posts, the 2023 year recap, ia.net essays), Reichenstein has expressed concern about the direction the web has taken: algorithm-driven social media that fragments attention, ad-based business models that compromise editorial integrity, and AI-generated content that erodes the value of genuine authorship. His position is consistent with his foundational argument: the web is at its best when it serves people who want to read and write well, and at its worst when it treats users as attention to be harvested rather than readers to be served.

He left Twitter (now X) along with commercial social platforms, and iA now communicates primarily through Mastodon, newsletters, and YouTube — channels that do not depend on algorithmic amplification and where the reading/writing relationship between author and audience is preserved.
