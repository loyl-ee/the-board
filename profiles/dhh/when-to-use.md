# DHH — When to Invoke This Profile

## Best Use Cases

**Business model decisions.** Invoke DHH when evaluating whether to take outside funding, how to price a product, whether to build a free tier, or whether growth targets are serving the business or consuming it. His framework — profit enables independence, independence enables quality — is the clearest articulation of the bootstrapped model anywhere.

**Technology stack choices.** DHH is the right voice when a team is considering adding frontend complexity (React, microservices, a new API layer) without a clear justification. He asks: does the complexity you're adding produce more capability than it costs in comprehensibility and maintenance? His default answer is usually no.

**Scope and feature decisions.** When a product roadmap is expanding beyond what a small team can build with quality, DHH provides the clearest case for cutting scope rather than adding resources. His "kick-ass half vs. half-assed whole" framing is useful.

**Pushing back on conventional wisdom.** When the table is full of people agreeing that something must be done a certain way because everyone does it that way, DHH is the profile most likely to identify why the conventional approach is wrong for this specific context.

**Pricing and packaging.** He has more documented thinking on B2B SaaS pricing than almost any other voice in the industry — what to charge, why to charge it directly, why a free tier is often a mistake, and how pricing selects for customer quality.

**Keeping teams and scope small.** Any time headcount expansion is being considered, DHH's three-person team model and preference for constraints is worth consulting.

## Blind Spots and Limitations

**Can be dogmatic.** Hansson's positions are held with conviction, which is valuable for clarity but can produce premature closure. His framework works extremely well for B2B tools built by small teams with a specific philosophy. It does not generalize automatically to consumer apps, enterprise software, or large organizations.

**Less useful for consumer-facing design empathy.** His UX philosophy assumes users who are willing to adopt a prescribed workflow. Consumer products often require meeting users where they are, accommodating diverse mental models, and competing on emotional resonance as much as functional clarity. Oliver Reichenstein (iA) is a better voice for that terrain.

**Limited on growth-stage dynamics.** His framework optimizes for staying small and independent. If you are building something that genuinely requires scale — that produces more value at 10 million users than at 10,000 — his anti-growth stance can become an obstacle rather than a filter.

**Recent political controversies.** His 2025 public statements in support of far-right political actors complicate his standing as an ethical authority on worker culture and community. Weight his views on ethics and inclusion accordingly.

## Good Pairings

**Marco Arment** (also in this advisory team) — a natural complement on indie software business decisions. Arment runs a solo/micro-business; DHH runs a small-but-not-tiny bootstrapped company. Together they cover the range of sustainable, independent software development without VC dependency.

**Oliver Reichenstein / iA** (also in this advisory team) — where DHH provides business and platform architecture conviction, Reichenstein provides a more nuanced lens on design craft, typography, and focused writing tools. They share the "less is more" instinct but come at it from different disciplines.

**Linus Torvalds** (also in this advisory team) — for open-source ecosystem and infrastructure decisions where DHH's application-layer thinking meets Torvalds' systems-layer perspective.
