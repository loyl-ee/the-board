# DHH — On Customer Relationships

## The Customer Is Not Always Right

Hansson rejects the maxim that the customer is always right — not because customers are unimportant, but because treating every customer preference as correct produces incoherent products. The customer knows what they want. The product team knows what the product is. These are different kinds of knowledge, and conflating them results in software that has no spine.

At 37signals, the response to a customer feature request is not automatically a roadmap ticket. It is a question: does this fit the product's vision? Requests that don't fit are declined — even from paying customers, even from important accounts. Hansson has described this as a form of respect for the customer: you are telling them clearly what the product is and what it is not, rather than pretending to accommodate them and delivering a compromised version of both visions.

## Building for Customers You Respect

The corollary to this position is that the right customer relationship begins with choosing the right customer. Both Basecamp and HEY are priced and designed to attract a specific kind of user: someone who values focused tools over comprehensive platforms, who is willing to pay for software that respects their attention, and who trusts the product team's judgment enough to adopt a prescribed workflow.

Customers who want maximum configurability, who demand enterprise SLA guarantees, or who need the product to integrate with every tool in their stack are not wrong — they are simply not the target customer. Hansson is explicit that this is a filter, not a failure.

## Pricing as a Relationship Signal

Hansson's pricing philosophy is partly a customer selection mechanism. A product priced at zero attracts everyone — including customers whose relationship with the product will be adversarial (demanding features, threatening to leave for a free alternative, expecting support on par with paid tiers). A product priced at a non-trivial amount attracts customers who have already decided the value is real.

HEY launched at $99/year. Basecamp charges per team rather than per seat, which Hansson has argued is fairer for growing teams and filters for customers who see the product as a business tool rather than a personal utility. Neither product has a free tier. This is intentional: the customer relationship starts with a transaction that establishes mutual commitment.

## Not Owing Customers Everything

Hansson has also addressed the question of what companies owe their existing customers when a product changes. His position: customers who bought a product have a right to what they bought. They do not have a right to dictate the product's future direction, and they do not have a right to block the company from serving new customers differently.

When 37signals has made controversial product decisions — including the 2021 internal policy changes at Basecamp — Hansson has been willing to accept customer defection as the cost of the company operating according to its own values. This is not indifference to customers but a refusal to be governed by them.

## Documented Gaps

Hansson's documented views focus on B2B/prosumer software customers. His framework is less developed for consumer-facing products where users have less tolerance for opinionated constraints, where price sensitivity is higher, and where the relationship is less transactional and more emotional. Caution is warranted when applying his customer relationship model to consumer contexts without adjustment.
