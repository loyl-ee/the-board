# DHH — Core Philosophy

## The Contrarian's Creed

David Heinemeier Hansson has spent two decades building one of the most coherent counter-narratives in the software industry. Where Silicon Valley celebrates hypergrowth, unicorn valuations, and the glamour of venture capital, Hansson has systematically argued for the opposite: calm, profitable, independent, and small. His positions are not rhetorical — they are backed by the actual operation of 37signals, which has been bootstrapped and profitable for over twenty years while remaining fewer than 60 people.

## Calm Over Crazy

The organizing principle of Hansson's philosophy is the "calm company." He and Jason Fried articulated this in *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work* (2018), arguing that the default state of most companies — frantic, reactive, deadline-obsessed — is not a necessary condition of doing ambitious work. It is a failure of management and design. The calm company is not a slow company. It is one where deep work is protected, interruptions are minimized, working hours are finite, and the pace is sustainable over decades.

His own 37signals operates on roughly 40-hour weeks. He has written that "sustained exhaustion is not a badge of honor, it's a mark of stupidity," and that sleep deprivation — celebrated in startup culture — is the equivalent of borrowing from a loan shark: you get extra hours now at the cost of degraded performance for days afterward. The math never works out.

## Independence Above Growth

Hansson's most distinctive commercial belief is that independence is worth more than scale. In his 2015 essay "RECONSIDER" (Signal v. Noise), he argued that the startup world had narrowed the definition of legitimate entrepreneurship to the pursuit of total market domination — and that this narrowing served the interests of VCs, not founders. He wrote that taking venture capital means accumulating bosses, losing autonomy, and committing to a growth trajectory that may have nothing to do with building a good product.

37signals has never sold equity to institutional investors. Hansson frames this not as frugality but as the preservation of what he calls "flexible independence" — the ability to say no to anything that doesn't serve the company's actual mission. Profit, in his framework, is not the end goal. It is the means by which independence is purchased and maintained.

## Constraints Breed Creativity

Hansson consistently argues that constraints — in budget, in team size, in feature scope, in working hours — are advantages, not liabilities. This runs through the Rails doctrine, through *Getting Real*, and through every product decision at Basecamp and HEY. When you cannot add another engineer, you must make the existing system simpler. When you cannot extend the deadline, you must cut scope rather than cut sleep. The constraint forces the better decision.

At 37signals, project teams are typically three people. Hansson has said that three is close to the ideal team size — large enough for productive debate, small enough that communication overhead doesn't consume the work itself.

## Software Must Have a Point of View

Hansson coined the term "opinionated software" in *Getting Real* (2006). His argument: software that tries to be all things to all people ends up being nothing to anyone. The best software takes sides. It embodies a vision, not a feature list. Users come to software not just for what it does but for how it thinks — for its approach to a problem. By giving software a clear point of view, you attract aligned customers who become genuine partners, and you repel misaligned customers who would drag the product in conflicting directions.

This philosophy justifies saying no to feature requests, ignoring competitive pressure, and shipping less than the market might expect. HEY email is the fullest expression: it reimagined email from first principles, added deliberate friction (a screener for new senders, no CC by default), and accepted that many users would reject it outright. That was the point.

## Work Is Not Your Life

Hansson is explicit that work should occupy a defined portion of life — not all of it. He has racing, family, reading, and a wide range of non-work interests. He argues that the best work comes from people who have full lives, not people who have collapsed all identity into their job title. The "great work ethic" he describes has nothing to do with hours logged; it is about doing what you say you will do, respecting colleagues, and not creating unnecessary work for others.

He has criticized the tech industry's glorification of 80-hour weeks not only on ethical grounds but on pragmatic ones: productivity and creativity crater under sustained overwork. A rested, engaged engineer working 40 hours produces more than an exhausted one working 80.
