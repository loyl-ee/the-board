# DHH — On UX

## Users Don't Always Know What They Want

Hansson's UX philosophy begins with a rejection of the idea that users are always the best judges of what they need. He draws a distinction between what users ask for (more features, more options, more flexibility) and what would actually serve them (a clear, opinionated tool that solves the problem without burdening them with decisions). Giving users everything they request is not good UX — it is the abdication of design judgment.

This does not mean ignoring users. It means filtering user requests through a coherent product vision rather than treating each request as an equal vote for a feature roadmap. In Hansson's model, the product team has a responsibility to understand the underlying problem better than any individual user, and to design for that problem rather than for the stated solution.

## The Courage to Say No

The UX implication of opinionated software is that a team must be willing to disappoint users who want the product to be different. Hansson and Fried stated this explicitly in *Getting Real*: "You're either on the bus or off the bus." A product that tries to accommodate every user's preferred way of working becomes shapeless — not useful to anyone in particular.

HEY is the fullest test of this principle. At launch in 2020, HEY received significant user criticism for features it deliberately lacked: no folders (only bundles), no CC function by default, no integration with existing email clients, no option to disable the Screener workflow. Hansson's response, consistently, was that these were not omissions but decisions. The product was designed around a specific theory of how email should work. Users who disagreed were not wrong — they just had a different theory, and they should use a product built around that theory.

This is UX as conviction rather than UX as accommodation.

## Opinionated Defaults Shape Behavior

HEY as a UX case study: the Screener (a holding area where new senders wait for approval before reaching the Imbox) is not just a spam filter. It is a UX statement about consent and attention. When you use HEY, you are never reachable by someone you haven't approved. This changes the user's relationship to their inbox — from reactive to selective. The friction is the feature.

Similarly, the separation of email into Imbox (action required), The Feed (newsletters and updates), and Paper Trail (receipts and notifications) is not a preference. It is built into the product's structure. You cannot collapse these categories back into a single inbox. The workflow is embedded in the UX architecture.

Hansson has argued that this kind of embedded workflow — where the tool teaches you how to use it by making other approaches impossible — is more valuable than maximum configurability. A tool that can be set up in any configuration puts the burden of UX design on the user. A tool with a point of view does that work on the user's behalf.

## The Limits of User Research

Hansson has not written extensively on formal UX research methods. His public positions suggest skepticism toward extensive A/B testing and user interviews as primary product drivers — not because data doesn't matter, but because data collected from users about an existing product can only optimize that product, not reimagine it. HEY did not emerge from user research on Gmail. It emerged from a theory about what email should be.

His position aligns with the view (associated with Henry Ford, though the attribution is disputed) that if you ask users what they want, they say a faster horse. The job of product design is to build the car.
