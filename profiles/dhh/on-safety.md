# DHH — On Ethics, Worker Welfare, and Platform Power

## The Harm of Hustle Culture

Hansson has been one of the most consistent critics of the hustle culture that defines much of the tech industry. His critique is not primarily ethical — it is practical. He has written that "sustained exhaustion is not a badge of honor, it's a mark of stupidity" (*It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work*), and that the creative and cognitive performance degradation from overwork makes it a net negative even by the productivity measures its advocates invoke.

His essay "Sleep Deprivation Is Not a Badge of Honor" (Signal v. Noise, 2008, updated 2018) documents the compounding costs of insufficient sleep: degraded creativity, reduced cognitive performance, and the irony that sleep-deprived people systematically underestimate how impaired they are. He has argued that sustainable, rested work produces better outcomes than exhausted heroics — and that the culture of martyrdom around overwork serves no one except those who benefit from normalizing it.

At 37signals, these beliefs are operational. The company does not expect or reward long hours. Project deadlines are adjusted by cutting scope, not by demanding overtime. This is presented not as a benefit but as a design constraint: the company is built to produce its best work in 40 hours a week.

## No-Meeting and Asynchronous Work

Hansson and Fried have written extensively about the cost of meetings and real-time interruption to knowledge workers. Their position: a meeting is a real-time event that pulls everyone out of independent work simultaneously. Most meetings could be an asynchronous message. Most messages could wait hours for a reply. The ambient expectation of instant responsiveness — sustained by tools like Slack — is a workplace design failure.

37signals uses Basecamp for internal work, and the culture is explicitly asynchronous: long-form written updates replace status meetings, proposals are posted and commented on over time rather than decided in conference rooms, and employees are not expected to be reachable instantly.

## The Apple App Store Fight

When HEY launched in June 2020, Apple rejected an update to the iOS app on the grounds that HEY did not offer in-app purchases — meaning Apple could not extract its standard 15-30% revenue cut. Apple's initial approval, then reversal, caught 37signals mid-launch. Hansson's response was immediate and public: he called the demand extortion, compared Apple's position to a telephone company demanding a cut of business revenues, and declared "There is no chance in bloody hell that we're going to pay Apple's ransom."

He testified before Congress as part of antitrust hearings on platform power. He argued that Apple's simultaneous role as App Store gatekeeper and competitor (Apple offers iCloud email) created an irreconcilable conflict of interest, and that the selective enforcement of App Store rules — Gmail and Netflix operating on different terms than HEY — demonstrated the arbitrariness of Apple's monopoly power.

His core argument on Apple: "Apple isn't just a dominant player in this market, they are the market. Without access to the iPhone, we're dead." He has framed this as a systemic issue for entrepreneurship: "This is how you stifle entrepreneurship, depress innovation, and prevent the next generation of makers from ever having a chance to compete." (Signal v. Noise, 2020)

The dispute was eventually resolved through a new App Store policy allowing reader apps to link to external sign-up pages — a change attributed in part to the HEY controversy.

## The 2021 Basecamp Controversy

In April 2021, Hansson and Fried announced a policy banning political and social discussions from Basecamp's internal Basecamp channels. The stated rationale: these discussions were becoming "ever more stressful, unnerving, and counterproductive," and the company's internal tools were not the right venue for debates about fundamental philosophical and political differences. Hansson wrote that "no comment thread on Basecamp is going to close the gap on fundamental philosophical and political differences."

The policy was triggered in part by internal debates about a historical list of customer names some employees had found funny — some of which were ethnic names — and a new DEI committee initiative. Roughly 18 of 57 employees took buyout packages and left.

This episode is a documented controversy in Hansson's public record. Critics argued the policy suppressed legitimate discussions about racial equity and employee concerns. Supporters saw it as a reasonable attempt to protect a calm work environment. The tension between Hansson's genuine advocacy for worker welfare (no-meeting culture, sustainable hours, distributed work) and his decision to limit internal political expression remains an unresolved thread in his philosophy.

## Employee Privacy and Remote Work

Hansson's advocacy for remote work (*Remote: Office Not Required*, 2013) includes a privacy dimension: employees working from home have more control over their time and environment, are less subject to surveillance and performative busyness, and can structure their days around their own productive rhythms. He has argued that the insistence on in-office work often has more to do with managerial comfort and control than with actual productivity requirements.

## A Note on Recent Controversy

In 2025, Hansson published a blog post expressing support for far-right British activist Tommy Robinson and anti-immigration protests. This prompted calls from within the Ruby community for his removal from project governance. This is documented here factually: it is a significant recent controversy that affects how his views on ethics and worker welfare should be weighted.
