# DHH — On Business

## The Most Documented Domain

Business philosophy is where Hansson has written and spoken most extensively. His positions are unusually specific, empirically grounded in 37signals' own history, and consistently contrarian relative to the dominant startup playbook.

## Bootstrapping vs. Venture Capital

Hansson's critique of venture capital is not ideological opposition to outside money — it is a structural argument about what that money costs beyond the term sheet. Taking VC money, in his analysis, means:

- Acquiring new bosses who have their own return-timeline pressures
- Committing to a growth trajectory that may conflict with building a good product
- Losing the ability to say no to opportunities that don't fit the company's actual mission
- Entering a pipeline (angels → Series A → Series B → IPO) from which exit is difficult

37signals operated for years on revenues from its consulting work before Basecamp became self-sustaining. Hansson describes the approach as: build on the side, don't bet on the product until the product can carry its own weight. He accepted a small personal investment from Jeff Bezos early on, but with no board seat and no control rights — he considers this meaningfully different from institutional VC.

His essay "RECONSIDER" (Signal v. Noise, 2015) is the clearest statement of this position. He argues the startup world has been captured by a narrative that serves investors — the idea that the only legitimate entrepreneurial ambition is to "disrupt" and "dominate." Companies that build profitable, sustainable, independent businesses rarely tell their stories because they have no systemic incentive to do so. The result is a distorted picture of what success looks like.

## The Profit-as-Independence Model

At 37signals, profit is not retained for reinvestment in growth — it is distributed annually to partners. Hansson frames this as removing risk year by year rather than accumulating it. The company's public statement on this is direct: profit functions like stem cells — convertible into any business need, without strings attached. Investor money comes with conditions. Profit does not.

This model has proven durable. 37signals remained unaffected by the 2008 financial crisis (when VC funding froze) and by the 2022 tech bear market (when venture-backed competitors faced mass layoffs and down rounds). Hansson has pointed to companies like Asana — which lost nearly a billion dollars over four years while its stock fell 87% — as evidence that growth-at-all-costs is not a strategy but a bet, and usually a bad one.

## Pricing Philosophy

Hansson believes software should be priced to reflect its value, not discounted to acquire users. He is critical of the "race to free" that characterized a generation of SaaS companies — building audiences on zero-cost tiers with the intent of converting some fraction later. In his view, this model trains users to expect free, devalues the category, and creates businesses that are fundamentally dependent on advertising or data monetization.

Both Basecamp and HEY charge directly. HEY launched at $99/year per person — a deliberate non-trivial price point. Hansson has argued that price acts as a filter: it selects for customers who see value in the product rather than customers who are just avoiding paying for something else. The customer who pays is a different customer than the one who doesn't, and the relationship is different.

The ONCE product line (launched 2023) went further, attempting to sell self-hosted software for a one-time purchase — explicitly rejecting the subscription model that dominates SaaS. The initial Campfire product recouped its investment, though Hansson acknowledged the market for self-hosted one-time-purchase apps was narrower than expected, and later products shifted models accordingly. He treated this as useful market data, not a failure of principle.

## Hiring Slowly and Keeping Teams Small

Hansson has consistently argued that headcount is a cost that compounds. Each additional employee creates coordination overhead, communication surface area, and management burden. 37signals operated for years with eight people. It grew to roughly 60, and Hansson considers that close to a ceiling — large enough to handle multiple products, small enough that individuals still have meaningful scope and context.

His preference for three-person project teams is a specific, operational expression of this philosophy. Three people are enough for productive disagreement. More than three, and meetings start consuming the work. He argues that most companies hire to manage the complexity created by previous hires, rather than hiring to do the actual work.

## Saying No to Growth for Growth's Sake

Perhaps the most radical element of Hansson's business philosophy is what he refuses to do. 37signals has not pursued acquisitions, has not launched into adjacent markets to capture more of a user's workflow, and has not expanded headcount to match revenue growth. When the company is profitable and customers are well-served, adding more is not obviously good. Hansson has said plainly that he does not want to run a company of 500 people — not because he couldn't, but because he doesn't want to.

This is not passivity. It is a specific strategic choice to optimize for a kind of work and life that scale tends to destroy.
