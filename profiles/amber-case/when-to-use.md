# Amber Case — When to Use

## Invoke Amber Case When

**Designing notifications.** Any decision about whether to send a notification, what channel to use (push, email, badge, haptic, in-app banner), when to send it, and what the default opt-in state should be. Case's progressive interruption hierarchy and notification audit framework are directly applicable. The question to ask: am I using the lowest-interruption channel that reliably communicates this information?

**Designing for users with attention constraints or ADHD.** Case's framework is structurally aligned with what attention-constrained users need: fewer interruptions, clearer urgency hierarchies, peripheral rather than central communication, and minimum viable feature sets that reduce the cognitive overhead of managing the product itself. She doesn't write specifically about ADHD, but her principles are the right ones.

**Building ambient information displays.** Status dashboards, background monitoring features, activity indicators, progress trackers — any UI element that should communicate ongoing state without demanding active attention. Case's periphery/center distinction and her taxonomy of ambient communication channels (lights, tones, haptics, visual patterns) are the reference framework.

**Reducing cognitive load in an existing product.** When a product feels overwhelming, complex, or draining to use — run the notification audit, apply the minimum viable technology test, and evaluate whether communication channels match the urgency of the information they carry.

**Deciding how much attention your product should demand.** Any strategic question about engagement mechanics, onboarding depth, permission request timing, or feature breadth. Case provides the ethical framework: your product exists at the edge of a user's life, not the center. Design for that reality.

**Onboarding UX that respects user time.** Applying the principle of introducing features gradually and requesting permissions at contextually appropriate moments rather than at first launch.

**IoT and connected product design.** Case wrote the book literally for the IoT era. Her principles are especially well-suited to physical products with digital components.

## Blind Spots

**Business growth strategy.** Case's business thinking is oriented toward sustainable, trust-based relationships and privacy-first product design. She does not provide frameworks for growth hacking, acquisition strategy, pricing, or competitive market positioning.

**Visual brand design.** Her aesthetic references are functional (tea kettles, traffic lights, escalators) rather than brand-expressive. For questions about visual identity, brand language, or emotional design, other voices are better suited.

**AI-specific interaction patterns.** Case's framework predates the current AI interface era. Her principles apply to AI products (especially around avoiding anthropomorphization and maintaining human decision authority), but she has not written specifically about conversational AI, LLM UX, or agent-based interfaces.

**Content strategy and editorial.** She does not address voice, tone, copywriting, or content-driven user experiences.

## Good Pairings

**Jony Ive** — when calm design principles need to be expressed through physical form and material. Ive's reduction-through-craft complements Case's reduction-through-function. Together: less that is also beautiful.

**Oliver Reichenstein (iA)** — when designing focused software, especially writing or reading tools. Reichenstein's typography-first, distraction-eliminating approach to software design shares Case's values and extends them into editorial and information design.

**Amelia Wattenberger** — when designing AI interfaces that need to respect user attention and avoid overwhelming cognitive load. Wattenberger's work on ambient, progressive AI interfaces is the natural application layer for Case's principles in the AI context.
