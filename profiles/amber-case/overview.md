# Amber Case

## Who She Is

Amber Case is an American cyborg anthropologist, user experience designer, author, and public speaker based in Portland, Oregon. She is best known for two interconnected bodies of work: her anthropological framing of humans as cyborgs — organisms whose mental capacities are extended by digital tools — and her practical design framework for *calm technology*, which holds that well-designed technology should demand as little human attention as possible.

Case grew up in Denver, Colorado, in a household of broadcast engineers. She studied sociology and anthropology at Lewis & Clark College, graduating in 2008. Her senior thesis examined the social consequences of the then-newly released iPhone — an early signal of where her thinking would lead. She took a cyborg anthropology course with Professor Deborah Heath that set the direction of her career.

## Career Arc

In 2008, Case co-founded CyborgCamp, an unconference on the future of humans and computers. In 2010, she co-founded Geoloqi with Aaron Parecki — a location-based software platform that allowed developers to build privacy-conscious, context-aware location services. Geoloqi was acquired by Esri in September 2012; Case became Director of R&D at Esri's Portland office, with the entire Geoloqi team retained.

In 2015, she published *Calm Technology: Principles and Patterns for Non-Intrusive Design* (O'Reilly), the definitive treatment of her design framework. The book drew on Mark Weiser and John Seely Brown's 1995 Xerox PARC research on calm computing and translated it into actionable principles for the IoT era. The book's publication launched a five-year worldwide speaking tour.

She has been a Research Fellow at MIT's Center for Civic Media, a Fellow at Harvard's Berkman Klein Center for Internet & Society, and a 2021 Mozilla Fellow. In May 2024, she launched the Calm Tech Institute, a standards and certification body that evaluates products against an 81-point framework covering attention, periphery, durability, light, sound, and materials.

## The TED Talk

Case's TEDWomen 2010 talk, "We Are All Cyborgs Now," has accumulated over 1.2 million views. In it, she argues that humans have already crossed into cyborg territory — not through implants, but through the cognitive exoskeleton of smartphones and computers. These devices function as "external brains," storing memory, mediating communication, and enabling a second self that exists and acts independently online. The talk reframes the question of human-technology integration from a science-fiction concern into a present-day anthropological fact.

## Recognition

Case has been named to *Fast Company*'s Most Influential Women in Technology (2010), *Inc.* magazine's 30 Under 30, and National Geographic's Emerging Explorers (2012). She received the Claude Shannon Innovation Award from Bell Labs. Notable consulting clients include Microsoft, Samsung, Google, Virgin, Airbnb, and reMarkable.

## Why She Replaced Tiimo on This Board

Tiimo, the Danish ADHD planning app, was a practitioner-level voice — a product, not a thinker. Case operates at the level of first principles. She offers a coherent philosophical framework for why attention-respecting design matters, backed by academic grounding in anthropology, a decade of product-building experience (Geoloqi), and a formal codification of principles (the eight Calm Technology principles, the Calm Tech Institute certification). Her lens is especially valuable when making decisions about notification design, ambient information displays, cognitive load, and how much of a user's attention any product has the right to claim.
