# Amber Case — On Business

## Geoloqi: Building a Privacy-First Location Company

Case's most direct experience building a technology business came through Geoloqi, which she co-founded with Aaron Parecki in 2010. Geoloqi was a developer-facing platform for building location-based services — geofencing, location triggers, and context-aware notifications. What distinguished Geoloqi from competing location platforms was its explicit orientation toward user privacy and minimum viable data collection.

The company was built on the principle that location data is intimate and that earning user trust required giving users meaningful control. Rather than aggregating location data as an asset (the dominant model of the era), Geoloqi's approach was to help developers build services that used location *contextually and transiently* — the right information delivered at the right moment, without accumulating a surveillance dossier on the user.

This was a business philosophy as much as a design philosophy: the product would only work if users trusted it enough to share location, and users would only trust it if the service demonstrably respected that data. Privacy was a feature, not a compliance burden.

## The Esri Acquisition

Geoloqi was acquired by Esri in September 2012. The deal was unconventional — Geoloqi had not taken venture capital funding, and the acquisition was structured to allow the seven-person team to continue operating from Portland as Esri's R&D center rather than being absorbed into a larger organization. Case became Director of R&D at Esri R&D Center Portland.

Case described the acquisition as something that "just made sense" — Esri's mapping infrastructure complemented Geoloqi's location platform in ways that venture capital could not have replicated. The acquisition preserved the team's culture and autonomy while giving their technology a pathway to much larger scale.

The Geoloqi story demonstrates Case's consistent preference for sustainable growth over growth-at-all-costs: a small team, bootstrapped, acquired by a strategic partner rather than flipped to venture capital. The business logic and the design logic are aligned — both favor the minimum necessary rather than the maximum possible.

## The Calm Tech Institute as a Business Model

The 2024 launch of the Calm Tech Institute represents a different kind of business model: a standards and certification body, explicitly analogous to Underwriters Laboratories (UL) for electrical safety or LEED for sustainable building. The Institute evaluates products against an 81-point framework and awards certification to those that meet its standards.

Case has articulated this vision directly: "Our goal with the Calm Tech Institute is to completely change what we expect from technology." The certification model creates a business incentive for calm design — companies pay for certification because consumers and enterprise buyers increasingly value it. Notable companies that have engaged with the Institute's work include Microsoft, Samsung, Google, Airbnb, and reMarkable.

## The Business Case for Calm Design

Case argues that calm design is good business for reasons beyond ethics. Products that abuse user attention create backlash, churn, and eventually regulatory scrutiny. Products that respect user attention build trust, which compounds over time into loyalty and reduced support costs. The "less is more" principle she articulated around location data applies broadly: "Do not send too many messages, but you'll get a lot more information about when to send those messages." Restraint in communication improves the signal-to-noise ratio of every message that does get sent.

Her consulting work with enterprises reflects this framing — she helps organizations audit their notification and attention footprint not as an aesthetic exercise, but as a business risk and trust-building exercise.
