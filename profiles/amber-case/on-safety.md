# Amber Case — On Safety

## Technology That Takes Too Much Attention Is Harmful

Case's ethical position is grounded in a straightforward empirical claim: attention is finite, and technology that consumes more of it than necessary imposes a real cost on human well-being. This is not metaphorical. "Notification fatigue" — the depletion of focus and willpower caused by constant interruption — is a documented cognitive phenomenon. "We are constantly overwhelmed by the number of notifications, which exhausts our willpower and decreases our focus."

The design implication is ethical: a designer who builds a product that over-notifies is not merely making a UX error. They are choosing, knowingly or not, to impose a cognitive tax on every user of that product. At scale — millions of users receiving unnecessary alerts daily — this is a public health concern, not merely a design preference.

Case has been direct about this: "The stress you experience from the technology is by design — either because designers did not fully prioritize your attention and humanity." The passive construction obscures an active choice. Designers and product managers decide notification defaults. Those defaults, aggregated across all users and all apps, shape the cognitive environment that everyone lives in.

## The Ethics of Notification Design

Case draws a sharp distinction between notifications that serve the user and notifications that serve the product's engagement metrics. This is an ethical distinction, not just a design preference.

Notifications that serve the user:
- Communicate information the user needs to make a decision or take an action
- Arrive at a moment when the user can act on them
- Use an interruption channel proportionate to the urgency of the information
- Are sent because the user would want to know, not because the sender benefits from the user knowing

Notifications that serve engagement metrics:
- Drive traffic back to the app or site regardless of user need
- Exploit variable reward psychology (unpredictable timing, unpredictable content) to maximize open rates
- Optimize for clicks and returns rather than user benefit
- Treat the user's attention as a resource to be harvested rather than respected

The Calm Tech Institute's certification standard makes this distinction explicit and measurable: one of its criteria is that products should default non-essential notifications to off, rather than requiring users to opt out of interruptions they never asked for.

## Privacy in Location Services

Case's work with Geoloqi produced specific thinking on privacy as a safety issue in location-based services. Location data is among the most sensitive personal data a product can collect — it reveals home address, work location, social relationships, religious practice, medical visits, and political activity. The capacity for harm is proportionate to the sensitivity.

Geoloqi's design philosophy addressed this through several principles:
- **Data minimization**: collect the minimum location data necessary to deliver the service
- **Transience**: do not accumulate location history as a permanent record unless the user has explicitly requested it
- **User control**: give users meaningful visibility into and control over what data is collected and how it is used
- **Purpose limitation**: use location data only for the purpose the user has consented to, not for secondary commercial purposes

These principles align with modern privacy regulation (GDPR, CCPA) but were articulated by Case as design philosophy before most of that regulation existed. Her argument was not primarily legal but ethical: users trust location-based products with sensitive information, and that trust creates an obligation.

## Designing for Cognitive Load and Mental Health

Case connects notification design to mental health through the mechanism of cognitive load. Attention is not infinitely elastic — sustained over-demand produces fatigue, anxiety, and reduced capacity for the kinds of focused thought that humans value most. Products that are designed without regard for cognitive load are not neutral; they are contributing to a systemic degradation of the cognitive environment.

The Calm Tech Institute's certification framework reflects this explicitly: its 81-point evaluation includes criteria related to attention, periphery, light, and sound — all of which have documented effects on cognitive load, stress, and sleep. The blue light criterion (devices should not emit disruptive blue light during evening hours) directly addresses the sleep disruption caused by screens, which is both a safety issue and a quality-of-life issue.

## The Underwriters Laboratories Analogy

Case's most powerful safety framing is the analogy between the Calm Tech Institute and Underwriters Laboratories (UL), which was founded in 1894 to evaluate the safety of electrical appliances. Before UL, electrical fires and shocks from consumer appliances were common — not because electricity was inherently dangerous, but because there were no standards for safe design. UL certification created a shared standard that made safety legible and marketable.

Case argues that the current moment in digital technology is analogous to the pre-UL era in electrical technology: products routinely harm users through poor design (attention abuse, cognitive overload, sleep disruption, privacy violations), but there are no shared standards for what "safe" design looks like. The Calm Tech Institute is her attempt to establish those standards — to make attention safety as legible and marketable as electrical safety.
