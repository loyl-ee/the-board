# Amber Case — Philosophy

## The Foundational Premise: Attention Is the Scarce Resource

Case's central philosophical claim is that the defining constraint of the twenty-first century is not bandwidth, processing power, or storage — it is human attention. The number of devices, notifications, and alerts competing for awareness has grown exponentially; the amount of attention any human has to give has not. This asymmetry is the problem that all of her work attempts to address. As she has stated directly: "Calm technology is the idea that the scarce resource in the 21st century is not technology, it's our attention."

This premise leads directly to an ethical stance: technology that takes more attention than it needs is not merely annoying — it is wasteful, and at scale, harmful.

## The Weiser-Brown Foundation

Case did not originate the phrase "calm technology." She discovered it as an undergraduate in 2007, while writing her senior thesis on the iPhone, in a 1995 paper by Xerox PARC researchers Mark Weiser and John Seely Brown titled *Designing Calm Technology*. Weiser and Brown were responding to a problem they saw emerging in the mid-1990s: information technology was increasing complexity rather than reducing it. They proposed that truly mature technology would move to the *periphery* of human attention — present and informative, but not demanding.

Their core articulation: calm technology is "that which informs but doesn't demand our focus or attention." A calm technology moves easily from the periphery of attention to the center when needed, and back again when the need passes.

Case took this framework — which had remained largely academic — and built it into a practical design system for the IoT era. Her 2015 book is the primary vehicle for this translation.

## We Are All Cyborgs

Parallel to calm technology, Case's cyborg anthropology framework shapes how she understands the human-technology relationship. She defines a cyborg not as a creature with implants but as any organism that has extended its capacities through external tools. By this definition, humans have been cyborgs since the first tool use: "A hammer is an extension of our fist; a knife an extension of our teeth; and cave paintings, an extension of our brain."

What is new about the smartphone era is the nature of what is being extended. Prior tools extended physical capacities. Digital devices extend the *mental* self — memory, communication, identity, and presence. "What we're looking at is not an extension of the physical self, but an extension of the mental self." The phone is a mental exoskeleton, not a physical one.

This framing matters for design. If a device is genuinely an extension of cognition, then its design failures are cognitive failures. A badly designed notification system does not merely annoy the user — it malfunctions in the mental workspace where thinking happens.

## The Periphery Is Where Calm Lives

The most important spatial concept in Case's philosophy is the distinction between the *center* and the *periphery* of attention. The center of attention is where conscious, focused work happens. The periphery is where ambient awareness operates — the background monitoring that allows humans to remain oriented without actively attending.

Good technology, in Case's view, operates primarily at the periphery. It provides information — status, progress, alerts — without pulling the user's focus to the center unless something genuinely requires it. The tea kettle is her canonical example: it signals readiness through a whistle (or a visible boil), does its work silently, and only enters consciousness at the moment it is needed.

"A lot of technology is not using our peripheral attention, but instead our primary attention, which makes it tiring and difficult to use." The failure mode of most modern technology is not that it breaks — it is that it demands center-stage attention for things that belong at the edge.

## The Hierarchy of Notification Needs

Within the calm technology framework, Case articulates an implicit hierarchy of interruption types, from least to most intrusive:

1. **Ambient presence** — the device exists and is working; no signal needed
2. **Haptic feedback** — touch-based, private, low-interruption
3. **Status lights** — visible to the user, passive, require no decoding
4. **Status tones** — brief audio signals indicating state
5. **Status shouts** — louder, more urgent audio
6. **Popups and alerts** — high-interruption, should be reserved for genuine emergencies or explicitly opted-in content

The principle governing this hierarchy: use the lowest-interruption channel that effectively communicates the necessary information. Move up the hierarchy only when genuinely warranted. Popups and voice alerts are the most cognitively expensive; they should be the last resort, not the default.

## Machines Shouldn't Act Like Humans

A consistent theme in Case's philosophy is the danger of anthropomorphizing technology. "Machines shouldn't act like humans and humans shouldn't act like machines." When technology speaks in human language, makes decisions on behalf of users, or simulates emotion, it blurs the boundary between tool and agent — and in doing so, it transfers cognitive burden to the user (who must now evaluate the machine's judgment) rather than supporting the user's own judgment.

Her preferred counter-model: technology should "inform us so that we can make our best decisions," not make decisions for us. The ideal is transparent, interpretable status communication — the light that tells you the oven is at temperature, not the assistant that tells you when to cook dinner.

## The Business Critique

Case is direct about the structural problem: technology companies are often incentivized to maximize engagement, which means maximizing attention capture. "The fundamental issue behind all of this is that we have companies now that are larger than entire countries. It's a threat to them to say, 'Hey, use our services less.'" This creates a design culture that optimizes for the wrong metric. Calm technology represents a deliberate counter-orientation — designing for minimal necessary attention rather than maximum possible engagement.
