# Amber Case — On Customer Journey

## The Fundamental Assumption Failure

Most product design, Case argues, begins from a flawed assumption: that the user's attention is available, focused on the product, and ready to receive communication. The reality is almost never this. Users encounter products mid-task, mid-thought, mid-conversation. They are cooking, driving, caring for children, working on something else. Designing as though users are fully attentive at every touchpoint is a category error that compounds at every stage of the customer journey.

The corrective assumption: the user's primary task is never your product. Your product exists at the edge of a life, not at the center of it. Every design decision should be made under this constraint.

## Attention as a Scarce Resource Across the Journey

The customer journey is, from a calm technology perspective, a sequence of attention decisions. At each touchpoint, the product either:
- Respects the user's attentional state and communicates appropriate information at appropriate cost
- Taxes the user's attention beyond what the moment warrants

The cumulative effect of these decisions determines whether a user trusts the product. Products that routinely over-tax attention — through unnecessary onboarding steps, intrusive permissions requests at first launch, excessive notifications after installation — start the relationship with a deficit. Trust, once eroded, is expensive to rebuild.

## Not Assuming Full Attention at Onboarding

Onboarding is the most dangerous zone for attention abuse. Products routinely front-load onboarding with the maximal request — full permissions, extensive tutorials, sign-up flows — at the exact moment when the user's trust is at its lowest and their tolerance for friction is at its most limited. Case's principle of respecting social norms and introducing features gradually maps directly onto onboarding: ask for only what is necessary to deliver immediate value, defer permissions requests to the moment they are contextually relevant, and allow users to understand the product's behavior before asking them to commit to it.

## Trust Is Built Through Restraint

Case's most important contribution to customer journey thinking is the claim that trust is built through what a product does *not* do. A product earns trust by:
- Not sending notifications the user didn't ask for
- Not escalating to high-interruption channels for low-urgency information
- Not requiring the user's full attention when their partial attention is sufficient
- Not abusing the permissions the user has granted

Each restraint is a deposit in the trust account. Each unnecessary interruption or permission overreach is a withdrawal. Products with positive trust balances experience lower churn, stronger word-of-mouth, and higher tolerance for the inevitable moments when things go wrong.

## The Long-Term Relationship Model

Case's framework implicitly treats the user relationship as long-term and reciprocal, not transactional. A product that maximizes short-term engagement through attention capture may win early metrics while destroying the conditions for long-term use. "Do not send too many messages, but you'll get a lot more information about when to send those messages" — restraint improves the quality of the relationship and the signal value of every communication that does get sent.

This is a customer journey argument, not just a design argument: the user's experience of the product over time is shaped by the cumulative weight of every attention decision the product has made. Journey design that accounts for this cumulative weight will produce different (and better) outcomes than journey design that optimizes each touchpoint in isolation.
