# Amber Case — On Design

## The Central Distinction: Calm Is Not Boring

Case is explicit that calm technology is not the same as minimal, sparse, or featureless technology. The goal is not simplicity for its own sake — it is *appropriateness*. A design that communicates nothing is not calm; it is absent. A design that communicates everything at once is not rich; it is noisy. Calm design communicates the right information through the right channel at the right moment, and then gets out of the way.

The difference between calm and boring is intentionality. A boring design fails to communicate. A calm design chooses what to communicate and what to suppress, and makes that choice based on what the user actually needs in the moment rather than what is easy to implement or what maximizes engagement signals.

## Designing for the Edge of Attention

The most powerful concept in Case's design practice is the *periphery* — the zone of background awareness where humans monitor the world without consciously attending to it. Peripheral awareness is how a driver monitors the road while having a conversation, how a cook knows when the stove needs attention without staring at it, how a parent in a park tracks a child without watching continuously.

Calm design places information in the periphery by default and pulls it to the center only when the situation genuinely requires it. This is a fundamentally different design orientation from most software design, which places everything at the center (modal dialogs, full-screen takeovers, popups) and expects the user to manage the resulting cognitive load.

Designing for the edge of attention requires answering several questions that most design processes never ask:
- What is the user's primary task when they encounter this design? (Almost always something other than using this product.)
- What information does the user need to remain oriented without actively attending?
- What channel — light, sound, haptic, visual pattern — communicates that information with the least interruption?
- What threshold should trigger a move from peripheral to central awareness?

## The Aesthetics of Unobtrusive Design

Case's aesthetic reference points are drawn from industrial design and everyday objects rather than digital software. The tea kettle (signals readiness through sound, silent otherwise), the traffic light (status communication in three states, universally understood, no instruction required), the office window (ambient light and weather information delivered continuously without any user action), the airplane lavatory sign (universal status communication without language).

What these objects share: they communicate through *affordances and states* rather than through text and explicit messages. They are visible when looked at and unobtrusive when not. They do not interrupt; they wait.

The design aspiration is invisibility through appropriateness. "Calm technology describes a state of technological maturity where a user's primary task is not computing, but being human." The highest achievement in calm design is a product the user does not think about — one they trust implicitly because it has never punished that trust with an unnecessary interruption.

## Information Periphery Systems

Case identifies a category of design she calls *information periphery systems* — ambient displays and background awareness tools that provide ongoing situational awareness without demanding focus. Examples include:

- Weather displays that use light or color rather than text
- Status indicators on shared devices (occupied/vacant, charging/not, online/offline)
- Progress bars and activity indicators that communicate state without demanding response
- Haptic alerts that convey urgency through touch without disrupting visual focus

The design discipline for these systems is *compression*: taking a complex or ongoing state and expressing it in a form that can be processed in peripheral vision or in a glance. A trend graph compresses weeks of data into a shape; a color-coded status light compresses a complex system state into one of three conditions.

## Communication Without Voice

One of the most specific design principles Case articulates is the preference for non-verbal communication channels. Voice and language are the most cognitively demanding communication modes — they require the user to parse language, which pulls them into the center of attention. Every alert that speaks a sentence when a tone would suffice is a small act of cognitive theft.

Case's hierarchy moves from language toward signal: text → sound → light → haptic. Each step down the hierarchy reduces the cognitive demand of receiving the communication. "Technology can communicate, but doesn't need to speak." The Roomba's status tones communicate across any language without requiring translation. The oven light communicates temperature without requiring the user to read a display. The buzzing phone communicates urgency without requiring the user to stop what they are doing.

## Graceful Failure as Design Principle

Case insists that graceful failure is a design requirement, not an edge case. Every product must have an answer to the question: what happens when this fails? The design answer should be: the product defaults to its simplest usable state. The escalator becomes stairs. The smart lock has a physical key backup. The automated home still has manual overrides. Designs that require perfect connectivity, perfect state, or perfect user behavior to function are fragile by design — and fragility is an interruption waiting to happen.

## The Invisible Ideal

The aspirational endpoint of calm design is a product that disappears into the background of a life well-lived. Users do not talk about products that work — they talk about products that broke. The measure of success in calm design is not delight (which requires noticing) but trust (which requires not noticing problems). The best calm technology is the technology the user forgets they are using.
