# Team Alto / Snowman — On Business

## Small Studio, Selective Ambition

Snowman is deliberately small relative to its output and reputation. Founded as a three-person team in 2012, the studio had grown to approximately 30 employees by 2022 — still intimate by industry standards. This size is a business decision. A smaller team means fewer projects undertaken, more care devoted to each one, and a culture that can hold its values without dilution. Snowman does not chase volume. Their output since 2015 amounts to a handful of titles, each selected carefully and developed over long timelines. Alto's Odyssey took three years after Alto's Adventure. Alto's Odyssey: The Lost City revisited ideas shelved during the 2017 development of the original sequel. The studio treats patience as a competitive advantage.

## Premium Pricing as Philosophy

Snowman's default business model on iOS is premium pricing: a single upfront payment, no ads, no in-app purchases, no monetization mechanics embedded in the gameplay. This was the model for Alto's Adventure ($2.99–$4.99 at various points on iOS) and Alto's Odyssey. Ryan Cash has said the team would "always err on the side of premium, so we don't have to think about monetization when we're designing a game." This is a design constraint that creates freedom: when there is no ad revenue to optimize for, no IAP funnel to protect, the game can be designed entirely around player experience. The Android releases of the Alto games adopted free-to-play models — a pragmatic concession to that ecosystem's piracy rates and norms — but the team treated these as platform adaptations, not philosophy shifts. The iOS experience remained their canonical version.

## Publishing as Creative Partnership

Beyond their own development work, Snowman operates as a publisher — but a particular kind. They function as creative partners for studios making work aligned with their aesthetic values. Where Cards Fall (The Game Band), Skate City (Agens), and subsequent titles were not acquisitions of convenience. They were relationships built around shared vision. Andrew Schimmel, Snowman's senior producer, described their publishing goal as creating "meaningful experiences that you may sit with for a short time but that sit with you for a long while." Snowman describes their best collaborations as beginning from "chance encounters" — organic relationships with makers whose sensibility matched their own.

## Apple Arcade as the Right Home

Snowman was among the first studios to commit to Apple Arcade at its September 2019 launch, releasing both Skate City and Where Cards Fall on the platform simultaneously. Ryan Cash described himself as excited "as developers, publishers and players ourselves." Apple Arcade's subscription model solved a genuine business tension: it allowed Snowman to release premium-quality, no-IAP experiences to a broad audience without requiring players to pay per game. Andrew Schimmel called it "the perfect home to present new experiences to such a wide variety of players." The relationship with Apple was also longstanding — Snowman had been an Apple-featured developer since Alto's Adventure, and both the Alto games had been prominently featured in App Store editorial and earned Apple Design Awards.

## Netflix as the Next Platform Relationship

In 2022 and 2023, Snowman deepened a second platform partnership — this time with Netflix Games. Lucky Luna (2022) and Laya's Horizon (2023) were developed in-house and published through Netflix's subscription gaming service. The logic mirrored the Apple Arcade rationale: a subscription model allows Snowman to design without monetization pressure. Lead producer Owais Akhtar stated plainly: "We can design games right from the start without having to worry about ads or purchases." Cash cited Netflix's global subscriber base of over 200 million as an opportunity to "introduce our games to players" who might never have encountered premium mobile gaming through the App Store. The Netflix relationship also pushed Snowman's internal ambitions — Laya's Horizon was described as their "biggest internal game project and most ambitious title."

## The Pok Pok Spin-Out

In 2021, Snowman announced Pok Pok — a separate studio focused on creative, non-addictive digital play for children ages 2 to 6, led by Melissa Cash (Ryan's sister) and co-founders who had originally joined Snowman. Pok Pok raised CAD $3.9 million in 2022. Ryan Cash separated the entities intentionally, noting that Snowman may work on more mature titles for general audiences while Pok Pok is specifically focused on young children. The spin-out reflects Snowman's broader instinct: rather than diversifying within one entity, they build focused studios around focused missions.
