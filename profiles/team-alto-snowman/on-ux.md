# Team Alto / Snowman — On UX

## One Button: The Ultimate UX Statement

The most important UX decision Snowman ever made was the single-tap control scheme in Alto's Adventure. One finger, one action. Tap to jump; hold to backflip. This is not a stripped-down version of a more complex system — it is the entire system, chosen after testing and rejecting every mechanic that required more than one touch. Harry Nesbitt observed that anything requiring multiple inputs "didn't really feel right." The UX implication is profound: there is no learning curve. There is no controller to learn, no gesture vocabulary to acquire, no tutorial to survive. A two-year-old and an adult can both pick up the same device and do the same thing. The skill emerges from mastery of timing within that single interaction, not from the complexity of the input surface.

## No Tutorial, No Onboarding, No Hand-Holding

Alto's Adventure begins. You are on a mountain. You snowboard. Nothing is explained. This is an intentional UX choice: the game trusts that the single-tap control is self-evident, that the visual feedback of the world is enough, that players are capable of discovering and learning without being taught. The absence of an onboarding tutorial is itself a form of respect — it treats users as intelligent and curious rather than as people who need to be managed through an experience. The game begins immediately, without ceremony, because the first moment of play should be the experience itself.

## Loading Into Gameplay Immediately

Related to the no-tutorial principle is the commitment to getting players into actual gameplay as fast as possible. Alto's Adventure does not ask players to set up a profile, agree to terms mid-session, or sit through an elaborate title sequence before they can play. The UX philosophy treats every moment before the player is actually experiencing the game as friction to be eliminated. This aligns with Snowman's broader belief that the product should get out of its own way and let the experience speak.

## Zen Mode: The UX Gift That Removes Itself

Zen Mode is one of the most unusual UX decisions in Snowman's portfolio. It strips out the score, the coins, the power-ups, and the game-over screen. There is no failure state. There are no metrics. The UX is reduced to pure motion through a beautiful world with a calming ambient soundtrack. What makes this remarkable is that it removes the UX of the game itself — the feedback systems, the reward structures, the progress indicators — in favour of something that is purely experiential. Players reported using Zen Mode as a genuine meditation practice. The UX team effectively built a product mode whose goal is to make the product invisible, leaving only the experience. This is UX as gift rather than as retention strategy.

## Accessibility Through Beautiful Simplicity

Snowman's games reach audiences far outside the traditional gaming demographic — not through accessibility features in the technical sense, but through the fundamental simplicity and beauty of their design. Ryan Cash noted the opportunity that platforms like Apple Arcade offer: to present games "to someone who's played a lot of puzzle games or vice versa" — players who would not self-identify as gamers but find themselves drawn in by something that doesn't feel threatening or demanding. The one-button control, the absence of tutorials, the gorgeous visuals, and the premium pricing model (which signals quality without exploitation) together create a UX that is accessible to anyone. Snowman's games have reportedly been played by players ranging from small children to elderly people encountering touchscreen gaming for the first time.

## The UX of Kinesthetic Controls in Laya's Horizon

In Laya's Horizon (2023), Snowman's creative director Jason Medeiros pushed the interaction model further with what he calls "kinesthetic controls" — controls that feel like the action they represent. Tilting the device steers the wingsuit. The sensation of the tilt, the visual horizon shifting, and the audio responding dynamically to speed and altitude all combine to create an embodied experience. Medeiros described the goal: "We were gonna make controls that feel like something for the player to do." This is UX philosophy at its most ambitious — the interaction model is not just simple or intuitive; it is physically resonant, creating a felt sense of inhabiting the game's world.

## Respect for Session Shape

Snowman designs with an awareness of how mobile games are actually played: in fragments, in pockets of time, without the luxury of sustained two-hour sessions. Each run in Alto's Adventure is self-contained and can last as long or as short as the player chooses. Jason Medeiros described Laya's Horizon in similar terms: "You can jump off the mountain, fly down to the base, and land in a couple of minutes." The UX respects that a player's time is their own. There is no penalty for stopping. There is no streak at risk. There is no notification pressuring a return. The session shape is dictated by the player, not by the product.
