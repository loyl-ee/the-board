# Team Alto / Snowman — On Customer Journey

## The Player Relationship, Not the User Funnel

Snowman does not talk about users in the language of acquisition, retention, and churn. Their public communications frame the relationship with players in terms of care, gratitude, and sustained connection. Ryan Cash has said it is "important to regularly maintain relationships with players and anyone who has shown support towards the projects." This is not a CRM philosophy — it is a craftsperson's relationship with their audience. Snowman makes things for themselves and for people who want the same things. The relationship begins there.

## Discovery Through the App Store and Apple Editorial

Alto's Adventure launched in February 2015 and was heavily featured by Apple's editorial team on the App Store. This was not accidental — Snowman had an existing relationship with Apple through their earlier productivity apps, and Alto's Adventure was a game that Apple could feature with pride as an example of what the platform was capable of. Apple Design Awards for Alto's Odyssey (2018) and Where Cards Fall (2020) deepened and formalised that relationship. For Snowman, App Store editorial and Apple platform events have functioned as their primary discovery channel. Their games are not marketed through aggressive paid acquisition or viral social mechanics — they are surfaced through curation and critical attention.

## Word of Mouth and Critical Resonance

The Alto series spread substantially through word of mouth — the kind that happens when a game makes someone feel something they want to share. The launch of Alto's Adventure brought 500 tweets before the team had even officially announced the game. Within days there was coverage in WIRED, TIME, The Verge, and TechCrunch. This organic critical resonance is not a marketing strategy that can be manufactured, but it can be earned: by making something genuinely beautiful and emotionally coherent. The most meaningful feedback Snowman received was not a review score — it was letters from players describing how Alto helped them cope with illness, grief, or anxiety. That kind of testimony represents a player journey that extends far beyond the product.

## Updates as Care, Not Live Service

Snowman's updates to the Alto series have not followed the live-service pattern of frequent content drops designed to re-engage lapsed players. Zen Mode was added to Alto's Adventure in 2016 — not as a content update, but as a genuine gift to players who had found their own use for the game. Alto's Odyssey: The Lost City revisited ideas that were shelved during the original development, offering genuinely new content to players who wanted more without simply recycling or inflating the experience. The philosophy of updates mirrors the philosophy of games: each addition should be meaningful, not merely present.

## Sequels as Emotional Continuation, Not Commercial Obligation

Snowman resisted treating Alto's Odyssey as a foregone conclusion. Ryan Cash stated explicitly: "We didn't want to treat a followup as a forgone conclusion." The three years between Alto's Adventure and Alto's Odyssey — and the further three-plus years before The Lost City — reflect a studio that will not ship until it has something genuinely new to say. This pacing is unusual in an industry driven by release calendars and sequel pressure, but it shapes the player relationship profoundly. Players who return to a Snowman game after a long wait are returning because the team waited until it was ready. That trust — built slowly and honoured by restraint — is itself a form of player relationship management.

## Community Without Manipulation

Snowman maintains community presences on Twitter, Instagram, Facebook, and Discord, and has published a podcast (Art and Craft) that explores the game development process. These channels are used for genuine communication — sharing behind-the-scenes work, responding to players, announcing releases — rather than as behavioural re-engagement tools. There are no push notification campaigns, no streak mechanics, no daily login bonuses. The community exists because players care, and Snowman tends it without trying to exploit it.
