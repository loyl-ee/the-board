# Team Alto / Snowman — Overview

## Who They Are

Snowman (formally Built By Snowman, Inc.) is a small independent game studio based in Toronto, Canada, founded in 2012 by Ryan Cash and Jordan Rosenberg. They are best known for Alto's Adventure (2015), an endless snowboarding runner that became one of the most critically acclaimed mobile games ever made, earning a Metacritic score of 92 and coverage in WIRED, TIME, The Verge, and beyond. The follow-up, Alto's Odyssey (2018), won an Apple Design Award. Their third game under the "Team Alto" banner, Alto's Odyssey: The Lost City, launched exclusively on Apple Arcade in July 2021. As of 2022, the studio had grown to approximately 30 employees and had spun out Pok Pok, a separate studio focused on creative digital play for children.

Snowman operates both as a developer and as a publisher. Beyond the Alto series — which is developed in collaboration with UK-based studio Land & Sea, the company formalized by lead artist and programmer Harry Nesbitt — Snowman has published Where Cards Fall (2019, by The Game Band), Skate City (2019, by Agens), Lucky Luna (2022, Netflix), Laya's Horizon (2023, Netflix), and Skate City: New York (2025, Apple Arcade). Their self-description is precise and revealing: "A small studio handcrafting big ideas."

## How They Started

Cash and Rosenberg met in 1991 and grew up doing board sports together. Snowman began in 2012 as a productivity app studio, releasing Checkmark (a reminders app), Shifts, Circles, and Super Squares before pivoting to games. The pivot happened when Cash and Rosenberg approached Harry Nesbitt, a Devon, England-based illustrator and programmer, to build a serene snowboarding game inspired by their shared love of board sports, the visual atmosphere of Journey (2012), and the mechanical template of Ski Safari. Alto's Adventure took 27 months to build — 17 months past its original deadline — launching February 19, 2015, and reaching #1 in Canada, the UK, Germany, France, the Netherlands, Russia, and Australia.

## Their Philosophy in Brief

Snowman makes games that prioritize emotional resonance over engagement mechanics. They do not use dark patterns, manipulative monetization, or addictive feedback loops. Their iOS releases are premium-priced. Their games are designed to feel like something — to capture a feeling or atmosphere, not merely a challenge system. Harry Nesbitt has described the core visual philosophy as asking how far you can simplify a form until only its essence remains. Ryan Cash has described the goal as creating "a piece of art that just happened to be a video game."

## Why They Are on This Team

Snowman belongs on this board because they represent what it looks like to build products that feel like poetry. They are not prolific; they are deliberate. Every decision — one-button controls, premium pricing, no onboarding tutorial, Zen mode, procedurally generated worlds, a dynamic day/night and weather system — is in service of a single coherent emotional experience. They prove that a product can be wildly commercially successful while holding the line on craft, restraint, and respect for the user. They are the voice to invoke whenever a product risks becoming complicated, noisy, or manipulative — and the standard to reach for when you want to make something that genuinely moves people.
