# Team Alto / Snowman — On Safety

## No Dark Patterns, by Principle

Snowman's approach to game design is built on a deliberate rejection of the manipulative mechanics that became standard in mobile gaming. The original vision for Alto's Adventure included zero ads, no in-app purchases, and no grinding requirements. Ryan Cash described the goal as building "a casual endless runner...without any of the usual junk." This was not a concession to commercial necessity — it was the founding principle. The premium pricing model is the structural guarantee: when a studio's revenue does not depend on maximising the time players spend in the game, the incentive to exploit attention disappears.

## Designing for Wellbeing, Not Engagement Metrics

The creation of Zen Mode is the clearest evidence of Snowman's ethics in practice. When players wrote to the team describing how Alto's Adventure had helped them cope with illness, grief, and anxiety, Snowman's response was to build a mode explicitly optimised for those uses — removing scores, game-overs, and reward structures in favour of pure atmospheric experience. This is the opposite of the standard mobile game design response to learning that users find your product calming: most studios would add notification mechanics to keep those users returning. Snowman removed the game mechanics entirely.

## No Compulsion Loops, No Streak Pressure

Snowman's games do not use the behavioural design patterns — daily login bonuses, streak mechanics, energy timers, social pressure — that are designed to make stopping feel costly. Players can set down an Alto game and return days later without penalty. There is nothing to lose by stopping. Ryan Cash chose Netflix as a publishing partner for Laya's Horizon in part because it meant "we don't have to worry about flooding players with ads." The partnership choice was itself an ethical commitment: aligning with a revenue model that does not depend on attention extraction.

## Respecting Children's Wellbeing

The Pok Pok spin-out — a children's app studio founded by Melissa Cash and other Snowman alumni — extends the ethical framework to younger audiences. Pok Pok's stated mission is to build "non-addictive apps for kids." The studio works with neuroscientists, pediatricians, and occupational therapists to ensure the product supports development rather than exploiting it. Ryan Cash separated Pok Pok from Snowman as a distinct entity, in part to ensure its focus on children was undiluted. The spin-out reflects a coherent belief: the ethical obligations to young users are specific and demanding enough to warrant a dedicated structure.

## Ethical Publishing Standards

As a publisher, Snowman applies the same ethical framework to the games they distribute. Where Cards Fall, Skate City, and their other published titles are premium or subscription-based experiences without dark patterns. Sam Rosenthal, developer of Where Cards Fall, described a philosophy of "invisible design" that guides the player without manipulating them — an approach Snowman's publishing standards reinforce. The studio has not published freemium titles with manipulative monetisation. Their publishing choices are consistent with their development ethics: quality, respect for the player, and meaningful experiences over engagement maximisation.
