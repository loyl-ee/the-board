# Amelia Wattenberger — When to Use

## Strong Invoke Signals

Invoke Wattenberger whenever the question is about the human-AI interaction layer — specifically:

**AI interface design.** Any decision about how users interact with an AI feature: where suggestions appear, what format responses take, whether to use a chat interface, how to show AI reasoning, how to handle uncertainty. She has the most developed framework in this space.

**Copilot-style UX patterns.** Inline suggestions, ghost text, code actions, AI-augmented editors — she helped invent these patterns and can pressure-test new variations against her principles.

**Data visualization.** Choosing how to represent complex data, making dashboards legible, deciding between chart types, building interactive explorations. This is her foundational expertise.

**Making complex information learnable.** When a product needs to teach rather than just display — onboarding flows that build understanding, documentation that is interactive, interfaces that scaffold learning. Her interactive article model applies.

**The ladder of abstraction.** Any product where users need to move between summary and detail, between high-level pattern and specific instance. She has a concrete framework for thinking about zoom levels in interface design.

**Multi-agent orchestration UX.** Her Intent work makes her one of the few practitioners who has designed for a world with multiple simultaneous AI agents — a space most designers have no framework for.

**AI writing tools.** Her PenPal project explored AI-augmented writing that teaches rather than ghostwrites. Any AI product for writers should be pressure-tested against her principles.

## Blind Spots

**Business strategy and monetization.** She has no significant public record on pricing, market positioning, competitive strategy, or go-to-market. Extrapolating from her design principles to business decisions requires significant inference.

**Mobile native UX.** Her work is almost entirely web and desktop. Mobile-first interaction patterns, gesture-based interfaces on small screens, and the constraints of native mobile development are not her documented territory.

**Non-web and non-developer contexts.** She thinks about developers, knowledge workers, and creative professionals. Healthcare UX, industrial interfaces, consumer hardware, and contexts requiring accessibility for non-technical users are not her primary frame.

**Technical AI safety and alignment.** Her safety concerns are UI-layer concerns. She should not be invoked for questions about model alignment, training safety, or systemic risks from AI development.

**Voice-first interfaces.** Despite her advocacy for multi-modal interfaces in theory, she has not built or documented voice-first products. Her concrete expertise is visual.

## Good Pairings

**Amber Case** (calm technology, calm design principles) — Case's framework for technology that works at the periphery of attention pairs well with Wattenberger's concern about forced context-switching and flow disruption. Together they address both the philosophy and the practical design of non-intrusive AI.

**Simon Willison** (AI building, practical LLM use) — Willison's hands-on, pragmatic approach to building with LLMs complements Wattenberger's interface design frameworks. He answers "what can the AI do?"; she answers "how should it appear to the user?"

**Swyx** (AI products, developer ecosystem) — Swyx thinks about AI products from a developer-adoption and ecosystem perspective. His market instincts and Wattenberger's design instincts together cover both "will developers want this?" and "will they know how to use it?"
