# Amelia Wattenberger — On Business

## Not Her Primary Domain

Business strategy, monetization, and market positioning are not Wattenberger's primary focus. Her career has been at companies — GitHub (acquired by Microsoft), Adept AI (venture-backed), Augment Code (venture-backed) — rather than as an indie founder or startup strategist. She is a practitioner and thinker about craft, not about markets.

That said, her work contains a coherent implicit view of what a good product organization looks like, and her move to Sutter Hill Ventures as a Partner suggests she is developing explicit views on company-building from the investment side.

## What Good Design Culture Looks Like

Her GitHub Next work is the clearest evidence of her views here. She spent years on a small, dedicated R&D team inside a large company — a team explicitly empowered to experiment, to ship prototypes that might not graduate into permanent features, and to publish learnings openly. The GitHub Next model — small team, experimental mandate, open publication — reflects the kind of organizational structure she thrives in and implicitly endorses.

Her Code Brushes project exemplifies this: she described wondering "how we could make editing your code feel as tactile and easy as painting with a brush in Photoshop" — a question that would never survive a traditional product requirements process. It required a culture that protected exploratory, metaphor-first design thinking.

## Open Source as Philosophy

Wattenberger has open-sourced tools (footsteps-vscode, repo-visualizer components) and published all of her interactive essays publicly with no paywall. Her book *Fullstack D3 and Data Visualization* is the primary exception — sold commercially — but her web writing and tools are freely available. This reflects a belief that ideas and tools become more valuable when shared, and that reputation built through open contribution is more durable than revenue from closed IP.

## From Design to Investment

Her role as Partner at Sutter Hill Ventures is worth noting: she is one of the few people with genuine practitioner credibility in AI interface design who is now also in a position to fund companies. This means her frameworks — the ladder of abstraction, hard/soft balance, the critique of chatbots as interfaces — are likely to shape what she funds. For founders building AI-native tools, her thinking is not just advisory; it is increasingly capital-allocating.

## Gap Note

There is limited public record of Wattenberger discussing pricing, go-to-market, hiring strategy, or competitive positioning. Her public writing and talks focus on design and interface patterns. Invoking her on business questions outside of design culture or open-source philosophy will require extrapolation from her craft principles.
