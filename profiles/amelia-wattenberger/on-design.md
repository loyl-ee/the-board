# Amelia Wattenberger — On Design

## Data Visualization as Storytelling

Wattenberger's foundational practice is data visualization — specifically, the web-native kind that responds, animates, and invites exploration. She has described her approach as starting without reference to existing chart types: "I like to approach data visualization without thinking of existing chart types and just thinking through what are the different metrics and how can we visualize each one of these." This is a discipline of first principles. The chart form should emerge from the data's nature and the story's need, not from a library of standard options.

Her years at The Pudding reinforced this. The Pudding publishes data-driven interactive essays — pieces where the visualization IS the argument. You cannot separate the conclusion from the form of presentation. This trained her to treat visual design choices as argumentative choices: what you show, how much you show, what you hide, what you emphasize — these are rhetorical decisions.

## The Ladder of Abstraction as Design Principle

Her most original design concept is the ladder of abstraction. She draws the term from Roy Peter Clark's writing guide, where it describes the spectrum from concrete detail (a specific dog, a specific bone) to abstract category (animals, possession). Good writing moves deliberately between these levels.

Wattenberger extends this to interface design. Google Maps is her canonical example: as you zoom, the interface shows different information — not just the same information smaller or larger, but genuinely different categories of information appropriate to that zoom level. A well-designed interface is not just a display; it is a navigation system through levels of abstraction.

For AI products, this becomes: what level of abstraction does the user need right now? Can the interface help them move up (toward broader patterns and summaries) or down (toward specific details and evidence) on demand? Her PenPal writing application explores this: hovering over a sentence could show where it falls on a concrete-to-abstract scale, or a sad-to-happy emotional scale, computed via embeddings. The visualization makes the implicit explicit — it shows the writer something about their own writing they could not see unaided.

## Making Complex Things Learnable Through Interaction

Her CSS Cascade interactive article is the clearest demonstration of her design approach to pedagogy. The CSS Cascade is notoriously confusing: a system with multiple overlapping rules about which styles "win" in a conflict. Most explanations are static text with code examples. Wattenberger built an interactive piece where you answer quiz questions embedded in the explanation, see animated visualizations of cascade resolution, and accumulate understanding through doing rather than reading.

She believes interactivity is not a cosmetic feature — it is a structural learning mechanism. "People remember things better when they're a little bit flashy or novel," she has noted, but the deeper point is that interaction forces active processing rather than passive reading. You cannot skim an interactive quiz the way you skim a paragraph.

## The Aesthetics of Restraint and Richness

Her essays and projects have a consistent aesthetic: generous whitespace, precise typography, animation that serves comprehension rather than decoration, color used systematically rather than expressively. There is a tension between her belief in richness (the web is more powerful than we use it) and her distaste for gratuitous complexity. The resolution is that every layer of complexity should earn its place by improving understanding.

Her essay "Our Interfaces Have Lost Their Senses" (wattenberger.com, 2025) mourns the flattening of interfaces — the loss of tactile, auditory, and spatial dimensions that gave computing its texture. She envisions interfaces that support multiple simultaneous modalities: typing, voice, gesture, visualization, haptics. This is not a call for sensory overload; it is a call for richness that respects human biology. "We live in a flat land, with everything behind a glass display case," she writes. Her design practice pushes against that flatness.

## Infinite Canvas and Spatial Organization

In "Evolving the Infinite Canvas" (wattenberger.com), she proposes extending infinite canvas interfaces — the Miro/Figma style of free-form spatial arrangement — with behaviors borrowed from the physical world: objects that resist overlapping, magnetic grouping, the ability to extract organizational logic (color, proximity, reading order) into structured data. The insight is that humans have sophisticated spatial reasoning that flat, vertically-scrolling interfaces ignore. Good design should leverage that cognition rather than flattening it into a list.

## The Hard/Soft Design Spectrum

"Bridging the Hard and the Soft" (wattenberger.com) is her formal treatment of the designer's challenge with AI: how do you provide both the structure users need to feel oriented (hard/deterministic) and the flexibility AI enables (soft/generative)? She proposes segmentation (hard and soft at different scales) and gradients (hard/soft ratio adjusting based on expertise, task type, context). This is mature design thinking — not "AI means everything is flexible now" but "flexibility must be earned by understanding when structure is more valuable."

## Design and the Web as Medium

Wattenberger's design philosophy is inseparable from her love of the web as a medium. "Browsers now are so powerful and most sites just don't do anything with all of that power," she has observed. Her work is an ongoing argument that the web is capable of being a genuinely expressive, educational, embodied medium — not just a document delivery system. Every interactive essay on wattenberger.com is a proof of that argument.
