# Amelia Wattenberger — Philosophy

## Core Conviction: Interfaces Should Teach, Not Just Function

Wattenberger's deepest belief is that the job of an interface is not to complete tasks but to create understanding. This comes directly from her background in psychology and neuroscience, her time as a data journalist, and her years building interactive explanatory articles at The Pudding. Her CSS Cascade interactive article — often cited as the best-designed explanatory web article of its era — is the proof of concept: you can teach a difficult technical concept entirely through interaction, requiring no prior context.

She carries this belief into every domain she enters. When she writes about AI interfaces, the question is never just "does this work?" but "does the person using it understand what happened and why?"

## The Level of Abstraction Is a Design Decision

One of Wattenberger's most original contributions is treating "level of abstraction" as a first-class design choice. She draws from Roy Peter Clark's writing-craft principle that good writers move up and down a "ladder of language" — from concrete imagery to abstract concepts — and extends it to interface design and AI systems.

Her AI Engineer Summit 2023 talk, "Climbing the Ladder of Abstraction," presents this framework directly: a well-designed interface lets users zoom in and out of their data. The Google Maps example she uses is clarifying — as you zoom, the relevant information changes. The interface does not dump everything at every level; it understands what is useful at each level of granularity. She applies this to text summarization, hotel search, code comprehension — anywhere an AI could help a user navigate between the full detail and the high-level pattern.

This framework implies a design responsibility: every AI-powered product must decide what level of abstraction to offer, and that choice shapes what the user can understand and do.

## Chatbots Are the Wrong Default

In her most widely-read essay, "Why Chatbots Are Not the Future of Interfaces" (wattenberger.com, 2023), she argues that the blank chat box is a failure of interface design masquerading as a feature. Her critique has four pillars:

First, text inputs have no affordances — they look identical to search boxes, login forms, and credit card fields. They provide no cues about how to use them well or what they cannot do. Second, prompts force users to type context that could be embedded directly in the interface — the AI's persona, the task's structure, the desired output format. Third, chatbot responses are isolated — there is no working buffer, no way to compare outputs, no persistent canvas for iterative refinement. Fourth, the implementation-evaluation loop is broken — good tools let users choose when to switch between doing and judging, but chatbots force constant back-and-forth that fragments concentration.

## The "No Man's Land" Problem

Wattenberger identifies a particularly dangerous design failure she calls "No Man's Land": the zone where a task is 50% automated and 50% human, but the human is no longer genuinely in control of the outcome. In this zone, "the human is still required to make decisions, but they're not in control of the outcome." Users feel like machine operators pressing buttons rather than authors making choices.

Her prescription is binary: either fully automate rote work (removing the human burden entirely) or build tools that genuinely augment human decision-making by giving users more information, more control, and more agency. The middle zone — where AI does the work but humans are nominally responsible — is where bad products live.

## Hard Logic and Soft Logic Must Be Balanced

In "Bridging the Hard and the Soft" (wattenberger.com), she extends her thinking to the design of AI systems generally. Deterministic (hard) interfaces provide structure, stability, and predictability; AI-powered (soft) interfaces offer flexibility and adaptability. But excess softness without structure creates confusion — the blank chat box again. She argues for two strategies: segmentation (using hard and soft logic at different scales within the same product) and gradients (adjusting the hard/soft balance based on user expertise, task type, and context). Novice users need more rigid guidance; experts need more flexibility.

## The Web Is an Expressive Medium, Not a Document Format

Running beneath all of her work is a conviction that the web is severely underutilized as a medium. "We kind of just ported newspapers into web format," she has said, "but the web is so much more powerful." She advocates building interfaces that respond, teach, visualize, and interact — not just display text. Her own essays are demonstrations: they use animation, interactivity, and embedded code examples to make the argument through form as much as content.

## Embracing Humanity, Not Just Efficiency

Wattenberger's final philosophical position is a form of humanism about technology. Against the grain of pure productivity optimization, she argues that when "you strip away too much friction, meaning and satisfaction go with it." She wants to see "more tools and fewer operated machines" and believes we should be "embracing our humanity instead of blindly improving efficiency." The best AI interfaces will not make humans feel redundant — they will make humans feel more capable, more curious, and more in control.
