# Amelia Wattenberger — On the Web

## The Web as Her Medium

Wattenberger's entire body of work is web-native. She does not write static essays; she builds interactive experiences that happen to contain prose. She does not create static charts; she builds visualizations that respond, animate, and invite exploration. The web — with its combination of computation, rendering, interactivity, and universal distribution — is the medium she chose and the medium she argues for.

Her conviction is that the web is radically underused. "Browsers now are so powerful and most sites just don't do anything with all of that power," she has observed. "We kind of just ported newspapers into web format." The opportunity is to build experiences that could not exist in print, that exploit the browser's computational capacity, that treat the reader as a participant rather than a passive recipient.

## wattenberger.com as Practice-What-You-Preach

Her personal site is itself an argument. The interactive articles there — on the CSS Cascade, on D3, on AI interfaces — are designed to teach through interaction. The CSS Cascade article embeds quizzes in the explanation. The embeddings essay ("Getting Creative with Embeddings") includes live demonstrations of the technique it describes. The AI interface essays use animation and responsive design to illustrate their points about what good interfaces feel like.

This is the consistent principle: the form of the communication should match its content. An essay about rich, multi-modal interfaces should itself be rich and multi-modal. An essay about how data visualization teaches should teach through data visualization.

## D3.js and the Web's Data Layer

Her mastery of D3.js — JavaScript's premier data visualization library — is central to her web practice. D3 requires understanding the browser's rendering pipeline deeply: SVG, the DOM, data binding, transitions, scales. It is both powerful and demanding. Her book *Fullstack D3 and Data Visualization* is the definitive practical guide to this library, covering everything from first charts through animated Sankey diagrams and React integration.

Her approach to D3 reflects her broader web philosophy: "With D3 you're really understanding different concepts like how do I convert numbers from the data world to the physical world." D3 is not a chart library; it is a framework for thinking about the translation between data space and visual space. This translation — what she calls "turning data into meaning" — is the fundamental act of data visualization on the web.

## Observable Notebooks

Wattenberger maintains a presence on Observable (the web-based computational notebook platform co-created by D3's author Mike Bostock) under @wattenberger2. Observable represents a different model of web publishing — executable, shareable, forkable notebooks where code and output appear together. Her notebooks extend her interest in interactive, computational communication.

## The Infinite Canvas and Spatial Web

Her essay "Evolving the Infinite Canvas" imagines a richer web: interfaces where content is spatially organized rather than vertically stacked, where physical-world behaviors (objects that resist overlapping, magnetic grouping) apply to digital content, and where users interact with the same data across multiple simultaneous representations. This is an argument for the web as a spatial medium — more like a room than a scroll.

## The Sensory Web

In "Our Interfaces Have Lost Their Senses" (wattenberger.com, 2025), she mourns what has been lost as interfaces became flatter: the tactile quality of physical switches, the spatial dimensions of early computing. She envisions web interfaces that support voice, gesture, spatial organization, and ambient response — not as gimmicks but as ways of returning richness to human-computer interaction. She calls for technology that "moves with us, speaks our language, and molds to our bodies."

## Web as the Right Platform for AI Interfaces

Her newest work (Intent at Augment Code) is a desktop application — a departure from her web-only history. But her core thinking about what AI interfaces should be remains web-influenced: responsive, contextual, multi-modal, and capable of showing data in relationship rather than in isolation. Whether web-based or native, the design principles she developed through web work apply.

The web remains her primary public presence, through wattenberger.com, her Observable notebooks, and her open-source GitHub projects. It is where she thinks out loud and where her influence propagates.
