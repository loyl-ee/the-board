# Jony Ive — On the Customer Journey

## Empathy as Design Tool

Jony Ive does not use the word "empathy" as a buzzword — he uses it as a technical description of a method. Understanding how people actually experience an object, rather than how they report experiencing it, is the foundation of the Apple design process as he practised it.

This distinction matters. People are often inarticulate about their own responses to designed objects. They cannot say precisely why one phone feels right and another does not; they struggle to identify the individual details that aggregate into a sense of quality. But the responses are real. "While people might not be able to articulate why they care and why they prefer one thing over the other, I really think that most people are very discerning." (*Financial Times*, cited 2015.)

The designer's job is to be more sensitive to these responses than the users themselves — to feel what they feel before they feel it, and to design toward it.

## How People Actually Use Things

Apple under Ive studied actual product use rather than relying on stated preferences or survey data. The distinction runs through multiple product decisions: the iPhone's no-stylus design came from observing that people lose, break, and forget styluses; that the mental overhead of "find the tool to use the tool" interrupts the task people actually want to do. The solution was not to make a better stylus. It was to eliminate the need for one.

The same logic drove the unification of hardware and software design under a single creative leader. Separate design organisations produce products where the physical and digital feel like two different teams' work — because they are. Users experience this discontinuity as vague dissatisfaction that they cannot attribute to a specific failure. Ive's insistence on designing both as one continuous problem was an act of empathy: honoring the fact that users experience the product as one thing.

## The Unboxing Experience as Designed Emotion

One of the most frequently cited expressions of Ive's customer journey thinking is the Apple unboxing experience. He and Jobs spent significant time designing the packaging of Apple products — an unusual allocation of creative attention in an industry that generally treats packaging as a logistics problem.

Ive is quoted as saying: "Steve and I spend a lot of time on the packaging. I love the process of unpacking something. You design a ritual of unpacking to make the product feel special. Packaging can be theater, it can create a story." (Source: multiple, including Leander Kahney, *Jony Ive: The Genius Behind Apple's Greatest Products*.)

The engineering of the MacBook or iPhone box — where the lid rises slowly, resisted by controlled air pressure — is not accidental. It creates a brief pause between anticipation and revelation. The product does not fall out of the box; it is presented. This designed moment is the first physical experience the user has with the product, and it sets the emotional register for everything that follows.

Ive has described obsessing over the way a cable was coiled inside packaging because "millions of people would experience it." The scale of that experience — multiplied across global sales — made the cable coil worth caring about. The aggregation of small, carefully designed moments across the entire customer journey is what distinguishes Apple's experience from competitors who treat the product as the only designed object.

## The Customer as Deserving Extraordinary Care

Implicit in Ive's customer thinking is a view of customers as people who deserve to have care taken on their behalf — whether or not they ever become conscious of it. This is not condescension. It is respect. The person who buys an iPhone is trusting that everyone involved in making it was paying attention. Ive's job, as he understood it, was to honour that trust.

"What we make stands as testament to who we are." The customer receiving the product receives not just an object but a communication about the values of the people who made it. A product made with genuine care communicates care. A product made carelessly communicates carelessness. Users sense both, even when they cannot say why.

## What This Means in Practice

Applying Ive's customer journey thinking means asking, at every stage of the experience — first encounter, first use, daily use, moment of confusion, moment of delight — what the person is feeling and whether the design is serving that feeling honestly. It is not sufficient to ask whether the product works. The question is whether it feels like someone thought about you when they made it.

The corollary is that customer journey failures are design failures. A confusing onboarding, an inconsistent interaction model, a package that fights you to open — these are not edge cases. They are the product.
