# Jony Ive — On Design

## The Industrial Design Process

Jony Ive's industrial design process is characterised by three qualities that distinguish it from most corporate product development: obsessive prototyping, physical engagement with materials, and an unusually long period of internal development before any external exposure.

Prototyping is not a phase in Ive's process — it is the process. He has described creating over one hundred prototypes for components as small as a single button. The iPhone's home button went through more than fifty iterations. The iPhone screen itself went through over a hundred. This is not iterating on software builds or adjusting specifications on paper — it is making physical objects, handling them, discarding them, and making different ones. "The design process at Apple is very much about designing and prototyping and making. Push to the prototype stage sooner rather than later, because concrete objects lead to greater understanding and better feedback."

The purpose of this intensity is to encounter problems that cannot be anticipated on paper or screen. Materials behave differently from how they appear in specification. Weight distribution matters in ways that drawings cannot predict. A tolerance that is theoretically acceptable can feel wrong in the hand. The only way to know is to make the thing and hold it.

## The Design Studio Culture

Ive's Apple design studio was legendarily closed — access was restricted to the core team of approximately twenty designers and senior Apple leadership. The studio itself was a large industrial workshop: makers and industrial designers worked alongside font designers, sound designers, motion graphics experts, colour designers, and soft-materials specialists. The space was built for physical making, not for presentation.

The team's longevity was a deliberate strategy. The core group of designers — many of them from the UK, US, Japan, Australia, and New Zealand — had worked together for roughly two decades by the time Ive left. The shared history meant shared intuition: the team did not need to explain their aesthetic values to each other, only to argue about whether a specific decision met them. "One of the hallmarks of the team is this sense of looking to be wrong." (BusinessWeek.) Challenging your own assumptions was a team norm, not a threat.

Ive instituted shared meals as part of the studio culture — reportedly having team members take turns cooking breakfast for each other. The ritual was not incidental. It was an expression of the same care philosophy that governed product work: attention to the people you work with, expressed through making something for them.

## The Braun Influence and Dieter Rams

Ive's most acknowledged intellectual debt is to Dieter Rams, the German industrial designer who led Braun's design from the 1950s through the 1980s and developed the ten principles of good design. Ive wrote the foreword to the 2011 Rams monograph *As Little Design as Possible*, and in 2010 sent Rams a personal iPhone with a letter acknowledging the inspiration his work had provided.

The connection is not merely philosophical — it is visible. The original iPod closely resembles Braun's 1958 T3 transistor radio. The iOS 7 world clock app mirrors Braun watch and clock designs. Apple's calculator app for years referenced Braun's 1987 ET66 calculator. These are not accidents or homages in the way that pastiche is homage. They are evidence of a shared conviction: that the right answer to a design problem tends to look a certain way, and that this way is worth reaching for even when it has been reached before.

Rams's first principle — good design is innovative — and his last — good design is as little design as possible — form the poles of Ive's approach. True innovation does not mean novelty. It means finding a better solution. And "as little design as possible" means not adding design as decoration, but removing everything that does not serve the object's purpose.

## White as Intention

The use of white across Apple's product line — from the original iMac, to the iPod's white earbuds, to the white iPhone, to Apple's retail packaging — was deliberate and thought-through rather than a house style habit. White in this context communicates absence of pretension, clarity, and a kind of honesty: this object is not trying to be something it is not.

More broadly, colour in Ive's work is always a considered decision. The translucent candy colours of the original iMac were a statement that a computer could be cheerful, approachable, and alive — a direct rebuttal of the beige-box mentality that dominated the 1990s PC market. The anodised aluminium of the later MacBook line said something different: precision, permanence, seriousness. Both choices were correct for their moment, because both were honest about what the product was and who it was for.

## Prototyping as Philosophy

The phrase "prototype to learn, not to prove" captures something central to Ive's method. Apple's design team made prototypes not to demonstrate a solution but to discover problems. An early iPhone prototype was famously described as resembling a bar of soap — technically functional, commercially impossible. The process of making it, handling it, and rejecting it was how the team understood what the iPhone needed to be.

"If you are truly innovating, you don't have a prototype you can refer to." This quote — attributed to Ive in multiple sources — captures the paradox of designing something genuinely new: you cannot look at what has worked before, because nothing has worked before in this form. The prototype is the question, not the answer.

## Making and Designing as One Activity

Ive has consistently argued that designing and making cannot be separated without compromising integrity. "We feel that more than ever there has been a disconnect between designing and making." (*Wallpaper**, 2016.) This shapes his view of design education: "While [design schools today may have] sophisticated virtual design tools, the danger in relying on them too much is that we can end up isolated from the physical world... the only way you truly understand a material is by making things with it." (Core77, 2010.)

This is why his team developed expertise in manufacturing processes — CNC machining, anodisation, glass lamination, sapphire cultivation — that would be considered unusual in most design studios. You cannot design something well if you do not understand how it comes to exist. "Designing and making are inseparable if you want the ultimate product to have integrity."

## The Role of Restraint

Ive's most consistent design virtue is restraint — knowing what to leave out. He has described the danger of designers who communicate their cleverness through their products: "One of the things that really irritates me in products is when I'm aware of designers wagging their tails — when there's a very clear desire on the part of the designer to be noticed." Good design, for Ive, is the opposite of self-display. The designer disappears; the object speaks directly to the user. "There's an odd irony here, I think our goal is that you would have a sense that it wasn't designed." (Vogue, 2014.)

This is the hardest standard in design to meet, because it offers no credit for effort. The sign of success is invisibility.
