# Jony Ive — On Product

## The Honest Use of Materials

At the core of Ive's product philosophy is a conviction that materials carry inherent truth — and that the designer's job is to understand and honour that truth rather than mask it. "The best design explicitly acknowledges that you cannot disconnect the form from the material — the material informs the form." (Core77 interview, iPhone 4, 2010.)

This is not a stylistic preference. For Ive, using a material dishonestly — applying a wood grain texture to plastic, or simulating metal finishes — is a form of lying to the user. The object pretends to be something it is not, and people sense this even if they cannot name it. Authenticity in material is part of authenticity in design, which is part of authenticity in the relationship between maker and user.

He has described Apple's material exploration process: "We experiment with and explore materials, processing them, learning about the inherent properties of the material — and the process of transforming it from raw material to finished product." (Core77, 2010.) This is not a metaphor. The design team physically worked with materials — machining, anodising, polishing — to understand what each one would and would not do. The iPhone 4's glass-and-stainless-steel construction, the MacBook Air's precision-milled aluminium body, the ceramic and sapphire elements of the Apple Watch Edition — each reflects material chosen because it was right for the object, not merely because it was available.

## Making Things That Feel Inevitable

The highest compliment Ive can imagine for a design is that it feels inevitable — as though no other answer were possible. This is the opposite of novelty. "Making the solution seem so completely inevitable and obvious — it's so hard!" He has noted that the most celebrated designs are often described afterwards as obvious, and that this is the mark of success, not the absence of creativity.

The distinction between *different* and *better* matters enormously to him. "Different and new is relatively easy. Doing something that's genuinely better is very hard." Novelty is easy to produce; it requires only deviation. Genuine improvement requires deep understanding of what already exists and sustained effort to resolve what is actually wrong with it.

## The Feel in the Hand

Ive has spoken repeatedly about tactile experience as a primary evaluation criterion — not a secondary refinement. "A big part of the experience of a physical object has to do with the materials." (Core77, 2010.) When he and Jobs were developing the original iPhone, they evaluated prototype after prototype physically — how it sat in the palm, how the edge met the finger, what the weight communicated before you looked at the screen.

This is part of why Ive was deeply skeptical of stylus-based interfaces. The stylus inserts an instrument between hand and screen — it mediates the interaction rather than making it direct. The iPhone's multi-touch breakthrough was, in part, a conviction that the most honest interface is the one that requires nothing between the user's intention and the object's response. Your finger on glass is as direct as designed interaction gets.

The tactile dimension of Apple products is not accidental. It is the result of thousands of hours of handling prototypes — assessing weight, edge profile, button resistance, surface texture. "Those three black splits are co-molded in, and then the band goes through more processes... the tolerances are extraordinary." (Core77, iPhone 4, 2010.) These are manufacturing decisions that most users will never consciously register, but that collectively determine whether an object feels considered or approximate.

## Details Most People Never Notice

Ive's account of what constitutes good product design centres on details that are beneath conscious perception. He has described being driven "crazy" by misaligned icon corners in iPhone app grids — visual details that most users would never consciously identify as the source of aesthetic unease, but that would nonetheless register subconsciously.

"People sense care, even if they can't explain it." This belief — articulated repeatedly across interviews — is the justification for the obsession. It is not self-indulgence. It is a theory of human perception: that people are, in aggregate, more discerning than they know, and that accumulated small details determine whether something feels right or merely functional.

The Apple product design process included prototyping single elements — a button, a chamfered edge — hundreds of times. The home button on the original iPhone reportedly went through over 50 prototype iterations for its angle alone. The iPhone's design process took two years before the team felt confident in the result. This is not perfectionism as neurosis. It is a methodology based on the conviction that users deserve that level of care, and that it will be felt.

## Products as Statements of Who Made Them

Ive has said that what you make testifies to who you are. "What we make stands as testament to who we are." This gives product decisions a moral weight beyond commercial logic. A company that releases a product with an obviously inferior detail — because fixing it would cost time or money — has made a statement about what it values. Users sense this, even without being able to say precisely why.

For Ive, this extends to the manufacturing process itself. He and his team developed deep expertise in fabrication — anodisation, CNC machining, glass bonding, sapphire cultivation. Understanding how something is made is not optional background knowledge for a designer; it is essential to designing something that has integrity. "Designing and making are inseparable if you want the ultimate product to have integrity." (*Wallpaper**, 2016.)

## The Apple Watch: A Case Study in Product Difficulty

The Apple Watch illustrates how complex Ive's product philosophy becomes in practice. He described its development as "a difficult and humbling program" because wearable products bring a layer of cultural expectation — what a watch should be — that purely technological products do not carry. "As soon as something is worn, we have expectations of choice. Even though Apple Watch does so many things, there are cultural, historical implications and expectations." (Tech Times, 2014.)

The round-versus-rectangular debate was resolved on functional grounds: "When a huge part of the function is lists, a circle doesn't make any sense." (*The New Yorker*, 2015.) But the cultural dimensions — the fact that a watch is worn, and that wearing is personal in a way that pocketing is not — shaped every aspect of the design from bands to crown interaction. Ive consulted traditional watchmaking experts during development, not to reproduce horological conventions but to understand them deeply enough to depart from them with integrity.
