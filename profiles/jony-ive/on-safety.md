# Jony Ive — On Safety and Responsibility

## Designing Out Confusion and Frustration

One of Jony Ive's consistent positions is that poor design imposes a burden on users that is neither inevitable nor acceptable. "If you eat something that tastes dreadful, you don't assume that the issue is with you." (Cambridge Union lecture, 2018.) The same logic applies to designed objects: when a product is confusing, frustrating, or counterintuitive, the failure belongs to the designer, not the user.

This is a form of safety thinking, even if Ive does not frame it in those terms. A product that confuses people causes real harm — wasted time, wrong actions, missed information, errors in important tasks. The design goal of radical clarity is not merely aesthetic; it is protective. Ive has described the entire simplification project at Apple as being about reducing the cognitive burden on users, getting design "out of the way" so that people can do what they actually came to do.

## Accessibility and Consideration for All Users

Ive has not spoken extensively on record about accessibility as a distinct design category, but his underlying philosophy — that products must work honestly and clearly for the people who use them — naturally encompasses it. The principle that "people sense care, even if they can't explain it" applies as much to the experience of a user with low vision or motor impairment as to any other user.

Apple's overall accessibility record — VoiceOver, dynamic type, motor accessibility features — was built during Ive's tenure as CDO and reflects the design culture he cultivated. Whether Ive personally drove these decisions or whether they emerged from the broader organisation he shaped is not fully documented in public sources.

## The Planned Obsolescence Tension

This is the most ethically contested dimension of Ive's legacy, and he has not fully resolved it publicly.

Ive's stated mentor, Dieter Rams, lists among his ten principles of good design: "Good design is long-lasting. It avoids being fashionable and therefore never appears antiquated. Unlike fashionable design, it lasts many years." Apple's product cadence — annual iPhone releases, deliberate discontinuation of older formats, hardware not repairable by users — is in direct tension with this principle.

Ive has not addressed planned obsolescence directly in documented interviews. His stated goal has always been to make something that is genuinely the best it can be, not to make something designed to be replaced. The question of whether annual hardware releases serve users or merely Apple's revenue cycle is one that his philosophy raises but does not answer.

He has been described as genuinely conflicted on the technology industry's broader impact. He has acknowledged feeling "responsibility" for the societal effects of products like the iPhone — the attention capture, the social disruption, the distraction. Whether this reflects a changed view of the products he spent his career making, or simply an honest recognition of consequences he did not intend, is unclear.

## Technology's Broader Responsibilities

In more recent public appearances — including reflections after his Apple departure — Ive has spoken about the responsibility designers now carry in a world where the products they make have significant social effects. He told interviewers around 2025 that "there's a responsibility now to design against" the unintended consequences of powerful technology products.

This has shaped his framing of the OpenAI/io project: the device he is designing with Sam Altman is explicitly positioned as one that should bring "peace and calm" rather than the fragmented attention and notification overload that smartphones have produced. The design problem, as Ive frames it now, includes the psychological and social effects of the interaction model — not just whether the object is beautiful and functional.

## What He Holds Firm On

Despite ambiguities, Ive's position on one responsibility question is clear: a designer who does not understand how something will be made cannot fully be responsible for what they release. The insistence on deep manufacturing knowledge, on prototyping rather than rendering, on understanding materials physically — all of this is about ensuring that the final product is what the design intended. Releasing something you do not fully understand is, for Ive, a form of irresponsibility.
