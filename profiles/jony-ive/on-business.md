# Jony Ive — On Business

## Design as Competitive Strategy

Jony Ive never publicly positioned himself as a business thinker, but his work at Apple represents one of the most effective demonstrations of design-led business strategy in corporate history. His core belief — that caring deeply about making something genuinely good is both the right thing to do and the most commercially effective thing to do — proved correct at a scale few would have predicted.

When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company was weeks from insolvency. Ive had been considering leaving. Jobs's visit to the design studio changed the trajectory: he recognised that the best work in the building had been suppressed by cost-focused management, and he gave Ive the authority to produce designs without commercial compromise. The resulting products — iMac, iPod, iPhone, iPad — did not merely recover Apple's finances. They made it the most valuable company on earth.

Ive's designs commanded premium prices not through marketing positioning but through material and experiential reality. Long before Apple was known for premium pricing, his work pushed costs upward in pursuit of purity. The approach required a chief executive willing to trust that consumers would pay for the difference. Steve Jobs was; that trust was foundational to the model.

## Apple's Premium Model

Apple's design-first business model is the clearest case study in Ive's commercial thinking. The argument: if you make something genuinely better — not superficially different, but genuinely better — people will recognise it, even subconsciously, and they will pay for it. Ive has consistently described Apple's goal not as making money but as making great products, with the commercial success following naturally: "Apple's goal isn't to make money. Our goal is to design and develop and bring to market good products."

This is not naivety about commerce. It is a specific theory of competitive advantage: companies that use design as a differentiator build the kind of loyalty that price competition cannot buy and cannot easily erode. When a product feels right in a way you cannot quite explain, you do not shop on price.

## The Design Team as Business Asset

One of Ive's most explicit business contributions was building and sustaining the small, world-class design team that produced Apple's work. He described himself as most proud of "the lasting work done to create a design team, process and culture at Apple that is without peer." Other major technology companies maintained design teams in the hundreds or thousands; Ive's team consisted of approximately 20 designers who had worked together for years.

The business logic: consistency of aesthetic sensibility and trust between collaborators produces better work than scale. The same group of people, with shared taste and shared history, can move faster and with more coherence than a larger team working from specifications.

## LoveFrom's Business Model

LoveFrom, founded in 2019 with Marc Newson, represents a different model: a small creative collective that works with a handful of major clients on deeply considered projects rather than maintaining a broad commercial practice. Clients have included Ferrari, Airbnb, King Charles III, and OpenAI. Apple was the initial primary client under a consulting arrangement, which ended in 2022.

The LoveFrom model is effectively a studio model borrowed from architecture: a named creative principal, a small elite team, and long-term client relationships built on trust rather than transaction. Whether this scales in the way Apple's model scaled is an open question. The acquisition of Ive's AI hardware venture io by OpenAI in 2025 for approximately $6.5 billion suggests that the market assigns extraordinary value to his creative credibility.

## Ive's Limitations as a Business Voice

Ive's business thinking is most powerful when design and product are the primary lever of value creation. He is less useful as a guide to pricing strategy, distribution, organisational structure beyond the design function, or competitive dynamics where design is not the differentiator. Invoke him on questions of product investment and trade-offs, not on market entry or financial modelling.
