# Paul Hudson — Philosophy

## Core Conviction: Learning Should Be Free and Practical

Paul Hudson's entire output rests on a single belief that he has never wavered from: Swift education should be freely accessible to anyone who wants it, regardless of where they live, what they can afford, or what educational background they have. The free content on hackingwithswift.com is not a loss leader or a teaser — it is the point. The paid tier (Hacking with Swift+) exists for those who want more depth, not as a gate that blocks the fundamentals.

This conviction is practical rather than ideological. Hudson understands that the most visited educational resource wins by being the most useful, and the most useful resource is the one that doesn't make you prove your budget before letting you in.

## Learn by Doing, Not by Reading

Hudson has articulated what amounts to his teaching philosophy in a maxim he uses consistently: "Programming is an art. Don't spend all your time sharpening your pencil when you should be drawing." (hackingwithswift.com/about)

He follows this with an advocacy for the classic engineering progression — "make it work, make it right, make it fast" — which means a learner should not wait until they fully understand something before trying to build with it. The act of building reveals the gaps; reading does not.

This philosophy shows up in the structural design of all his courses. The 100 Days of SwiftUI, for instance, introduces a concept and then immediately has the learner build an app that applies it. There are no extended theory sections that defer practical work. You code on day one.

## Teaching as a Multiplier

Hudson approaches teaching not as secondary to his technical work, but as the primary activity that multiplies value across the entire Swift community. He is, in his own words, "a full-time Swift author and speaker." The writing and teaching are not side projects that supplement app development — they are the main event.

His belief, demonstrated through consistent behavior, is that one teacher who makes a subject accessible multiplies the productivity of thousands of developers who can now ship things they could not ship before. The return on that investment is not personal financial gain (most of his work is free) but ecosystem growth that ultimately benefits everyone who works with Swift.

## Free Updates as a Trust Contract

One of Hudson's most explicit philosophical statements concerns book updates. After releasing the first edition of Pro Swift in 2016, he has continued to update it for free through every major Swift version. In September 2024 he posted: "I know some other folks charge for new editions, but that's something I refuse to do — when you trust me with your learning, I want to go above and beyond what you expect." (x.com/twostraws, September 17, 2024)

This is not just a pricing policy. It is a statement about the relationship between educator and learner. He frames that relationship as one based on trust — and breaking that trust by charging repeatedly for the same promise is something he explicitly refuses to do.

## Community as a Force Multiplier

The 100 Days challenges are not just content delivery — they are deliberately engineered to use community accountability as a teaching tool. The rules require learners to post daily progress publicly and actively discourage "lone wolf" learning. Hudson's design premise is that learning in public, with others doing the same thing at the same time, creates momentum that solo study cannot.

He has directly stated his willingness to answer questions posted publicly — encouraging learners to tweet at @twostraws when stuck — which means the community includes direct access to the author at scale. This is an unusual commitment that positions him as a participant in the community rather than just its architect.

## Practical Over Theoretical, Always

Hudson does not teach computer science in the abstract. He teaches how to build apps that people use. His tutorials almost always culminate in a working project that a learner can show to someone, submit to the App Store, or build upon. The metric of success is not "understand the concept" but "shipped the thing."

This preference for practical outcomes over theoretical depth informs every product decision he makes — which topics to cover, how to sequence material, when to stop explaining and start coding.

## Accessibility as a Non-Negotiable

From the beginning of his teaching career, Hudson has embedded accessibility into every tutorial on iOS development. He does not treat it as an optional extra or an advanced topic. In his words from the Accessibility wrap-up chapter in Hacking with iOS: "Accessibility isn't something that's 'nice to have' — it should be regarded as a fundamental part of your application design, and considered from the very beginning onwards." (hackingwithswift.com/books/ios-swiftui/accessibility-wrap-up)

This same philosophy — accessibility baked in from the start rather than retrofitted — applies to his own tools. When he built the Ignite static site generator, he specifically ensured accessibility was built into the HTML output by default.
