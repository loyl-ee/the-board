# Paul Hudson — On Engineering

## Swift-First Engineering

Hudson's engineering practice is deeply Swift-native. His teaching at Hacking with Swift and the 100 Days of Swift/SwiftUI series is built on the premise that learning Swift means learning to think in Swift — value types over reference types, optionals as a design feature not a burden, protocols as the primary unit of abstraction. This is not a stylistic preference; it is an engineering discipline that leverages the language's type system to catch bugs at compile time rather than runtime.

The engineering standard he teaches: if something can be expressed as a type constraint, it should be. An optional that is passed through five layers of code without being unwrapped is not a design problem — it is a type system telling you that your design has a question that has not been answered.

## Teaching-Driven Development

Hudson's approach to documentation and learning reflects a principle that generalises to engineering: if you cannot explain a piece of code simply, you do not fully understand it. His tutorials are written to be comprehensible to beginners, which requires that the underlying concepts be understood at a level that strips away unnecessary complexity.

The engineering application: write documentation as if explaining to someone who does not know your codebase. The act of writing forces you to identify the parts of your design that are genuinely complex (and therefore explain them carefully) versus the parts that only seem complex because they are poorly organised.

## Accessibility as a First-Class Engineering Requirement

Hudson has consistently placed accessibility at the centre of his Swift teaching — not as an optional module at the end of a course, but as a requirement woven throughout. SwiftUI's accessibility modifiers, Dynamic Type support, VoiceOver labels, and reduce motion support are taught as baseline engineering requirements.

His argument: accessibility is not a feature for a small number of users — it is a quality standard that benefits all users. Dynamic Type support (letting users choose their preferred text size) makes text more readable for everyone. Sufficient colour contrast is clearer for everyone, not just users with visual impairments.

## The 100 Days Model as Engineering Practice

The 100 Days of Swift and 100 Days of SwiftUI — Hudson's flagship free courses — are structured around consistent daily engineering practice: a defined amount of work, completed every day, for 100 consecutive days. The engineering discipline behind this structure mirrors good professional practice: consistent, incremental progress on complex systems produces better results than intermittent bursts.

The engineering application: treat complex technical learning (a new language, a new framework, a new architectural pattern) as a 100-day project rather than a weekend sprint. Daily practice builds the kind of muscle memory and intuition that weekend workshops cannot.

## Open Source Education as Engineering Ethics

Hudson has made Hacking with Swift free — the tutorials, the sample code, the 100 Days courses. His engineering position is that access to quality technical education should not be limited by ability to pay. The paid content (books, videos) funds the free content; the free content reaches the developers who could not otherwise afford professional education.

The engineering ecosystem benefit: a developer community with access to quality free education produces better code, files better bug reports, and builds better products. Investing in developer education is an investment in the quality of the software you depend on.
