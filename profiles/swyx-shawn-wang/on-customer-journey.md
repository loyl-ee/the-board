# Swyx — On Customer Journey and Community

## Developer Relations as a Discipline

Swyx spent the core of his pre-AI career in developer relations — at Netlify, AWS, Temporal, and Airbyte — and he has written systematically about what DevRel is, how to measure it, and how to scale it. His essay "Measuring Developer Relations" ([swyx.io/measuring-devrel](https://www.swyx.io/measuring-devrel)) is a reference document in the field.

His North Star metric is simple: monthly active developers. Not impressions, not conference attendance, not Twitter followers. Active developers. If that number is growing and accelerating, the community is healthy. If it is flat or declining, something is wrong regardless of the surface metrics.

He also holds a structural view: DevRel's value over affiliate marketing or advertising is in "depth, breadth, consistency, access, insight, community, email list building" — not raw reach. This is why developers distrust marketing and why DevRel exists as a separate function. The moment DevRel starts optimizing for impressions over insight, it loses the thing that makes it worth having.

## Community Building Around an Emerging Discipline

Swyx's AI Engineer community is his most ambitious application of these principles. When he published "The Rise of the AI Engineer" in June 2023, there was no community of AI Engineers — there was no agreed name for the role, no conference, no shared vocabulary. His thesis was that a community needed to be intentionally constructed around the emerging discipline before the discipline fully formed, to give it the vocabulary and institutions it would need to cohere.

The AI Engineer Summit (October 2023) was that institution-building in practice: a curated gathering designed not for networking but for identity formation — giving a dispersed group of practitioners a shared experience and a shared language. The 10:1 applicant-to-ticket ratio suggests the demand was real.

## Conference Organizing as Community Infrastructure

In his essay on organizing the AI Engineer World's Fair 2024 ([swyx.io/aiewf-2024](https://www.swyx.io/aiewf-2024)), swyx is unusually candid about the operational reality of conference building:

- Speaker acquisition is an external dependency problem: every speaker who commits but doesn't appear creates downstream failures
- Curation matters more than scale: a 6% acceptance rate from 500+ proposals creates signal that the talk at a swyx-organized conference is meaningful
- Diversity requires intentionality: 50% female keynote speakers in 2024 required deliberate outreach, not just an open call
- "People don't show up to things — even when they have RSVP'ed yes, even when they have paid for it" — plan for attrition

His model for the ideal conference is the World's Fairs of a century ago: showcasing "the best that the world has to offer." Not a product expo, not a sponsor showcase — a genuine exhibition of what the field can do.

## Enabling, Not Owning, Community

A key principle from his DevRel writing: scale requires shifting from an ownership model (DevRel does everything community-related) to an enablement model (DevRel enables everyone else to do DevRel). This applies directly to how he has built the AI Engineer ecosystem — Latent Space provides the media infrastructure, the conference provides the physical gathering, and the Discord and Paper Club provide the ongoing community, but the content and conversation happen at the community level, not just the editorial level.

## The Developer Customer Journey

For developer-facing products, swyx's mental model of the customer journey differs from traditional B2C or B2B frameworks. Developers discover through:
- Peer recommendation and community reputation (not advertising)
- Seeing something work in public (not case studies)
- Being able to try it themselves in minutes (not sales calls)

This is why learning in public, building in public, and conference community-building are strategically equivalent approaches for developer products. They all optimize for the same thing: creating visible proof that something works, in front of people who can evaluate that proof and tell their peers.
