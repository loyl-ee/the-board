# Swyx — On AI Safety

## A Pragmatic Builder's Stance

Swyx holds a consistent, explicitly pragmatic position on AI safety: he takes it seriously as a concern without centering his work around it. He is not a doomer and not an accelerationist — he is an engineer who has chosen to focus on what practitioners can build responsibly today, rather than speculating about long-term existential scenarios.

The clearest statement of his position: Marc Andreessen pointed out that there exists a profession of "AI safety expert," "AI ethicist," and "AI risk researcher" who are "paid to be doomers" — but there is no corresponding institutional role for builders and pragmatists who want to take AI seriously without catastrophizing. Swyx explicitly occupies that space.

He views many AI discussions about AGI timelines and "P(Doom)" estimates as "nice hallway conversations for freshman year but not very useful for work." This is not dismissal — it is a deliberate editorial choice about what is actionable for the AI Engineers he serves.

## What Latent Space Explicitly Does Not Cover

The Latent Space about page ([latent.space/about](https://www.latent.space/about)) explicitly de-emphasizes "law, regulation, politics, safety, X-risk, defense/biotech applications, and BigTech power plays." This is a product and editorial decision as much as a philosophical one: the audience is engineers building products, and safety as an abstract research topic is not actionable for them. Safety as an engineering practice — how you evaluate models, how you detect failure modes, how you design for appropriate trust — is a different matter.

## Safety as an Engineering Practice

Where swyx's thinking on safety is most valuable is in its operational framing: responsible AI deployment is an engineering discipline, not a policy debate. His concerns are:

- **Evaluation standards**: His work with Cognition involves building evaluation infrastructure for coding agents — ground-truth testing that determines whether an AI system actually works. Rigorous evaluation is his answer to "how do you deploy AI safely."
- **Failure mode design**: His emphasis on building AI products in verifiable domains (code that runs, outputs that can be checked) reflects an implicit safety principle — build where you can detect failure, not where you must trust output blindly.
- **Trust calibration**: His writing on AI UX consistently addresses the risk of miscalibrated trust — users who over-trust AI outputs in high-stakes situations. Good product design manages this through interface signals, not through warnings that users ignore.

## Anthropic and the Interpretability Bet

Swyx has expressed genuine appreciation for Anthropic's approach to safety. He believes that "having a deep focus on interpretability and alignment may pay off in the long run, getting the smartest people and providing time and space away from hype to introspect on how models work and how to turn behaviors on and off in models" — which he views as "very important for the overall ecosystem." This is not an endorsement of a specific alignment research agenda but an acknowledgment that serious technical safety work has value that doesn't show up immediately.

## The Shoggoth Framing

"AI Engineers will tame and ride Shoggoth" — his most memorable line from "The Rise of the AI Engineer." The Shoggoth is an internet metaphor for the unpredictable, alien nature of large language models. Swyx's framing is deliberately optimistic: AI Engineers are not victims of this unpredictability — they are the practitioners who learn to work with it productively. "Taming" does not mean eliminating the unpredictability; it means building with it responsibly.

## Honest Gaps

Swyx does not write in depth about AI governance frameworks, regulatory compliance, AI bias and fairness methodologies, red-teaming practices, or constitutional AI approaches. For those topics, the team should consult Dario Amodei's profile or seek dedicated AI safety researchers. Swyx's contribution on safety is the frame: pragmatic engineering responsibility, not theoretical safety research.
