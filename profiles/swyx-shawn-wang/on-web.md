# Swyx — On the Web

## swyx.io as a Digital Garden

Swyx's personal site [swyx.io](https://www.swyx.io) is a canonical example of the "digital garden" approach to personal publishing — a mode of web presence that he helped popularize alongside Joel Hooks and Maggie Appleton. Unlike a traditional blog (a reverse-chronological feed of finished thoughts), a digital garden is an evolving public notebook: incomplete ideas, ongoing notes, essays at various stages of completion, all linked and growing over time.

His [Digital Garden Terms of Service](https://www.swyx.io/digital-garden-tos) formalizes the social contract: "I have a right to be wrong or incomplete in my Digital Garden, either due to paucity of time or knowledge." This is a principled rejection of the idea that you must achieve expertise before publishing. The garden is the record of the learning process, not just the results.

The practical result: over 626 essays, notes, talks, tutorials, podcasts, and snippets accumulated on swyx.io across his career. This is a comprehensive public record of a developer's intellectual development from front-end engineer through developer advocate to AI engineering community builder.

## Learning in Public on the Open Web

The web, for swyx, is not a distribution channel — it is a learning environment. His advocacy for "learning in public" is specifically advocacy for using the open web as the venue: blogs rather than private notes, public Q&A rather than private slack channels, Twitter threads rather than DMs.

He distinguishes himself from the "learn in private" default: "most people 'learn in private', and lurk." The open web creates the surface area for serendipitous connection — a VP reads a comment on a blog post and hires the author, a mentor sees a tweet and offers a correction that changes a learner's trajectory. These connections are structurally impossible for private learners.

His position is not maximalist: "I'm not advocating for living life a hundred percent public. But it's possible to go from zero to 5% and see a lot of benefits." This makes it a practical prescription rather than an identity claim.

## Twitter/X as Real-Time Thinking

Swyx uses Twitter/X ([@swyx](https://x.com/swyx)) as a platform for real-time thinking — announcing projects (smol-developer's viral launch was a tweet), sharing links, testing ideas before they become essays, and engaging with the AI engineering community. He built significant professional reputation through Twitter activity in the 2018-2023 period, demonstrating that the platform can function as a legitimate professional development surface when used with intellectual honesty rather than performance.

His Twitter practice reflects the "learn in public" ethos: he shares half-formed ideas, corrects himself publicly, and treats replies as feedback rather than attacks. The platform's real-time nature makes it appropriate for fast-moving fields like AI engineering, where the monthly cadence of a blog post is too slow.

## Latent Space as a Media Product

Latent Space ([latent.space](https://www.latent.space)) is swyx's most sophisticated web product: a newsletter, podcast, and YouTube channel that functions as a technical media company serving the AI engineering community. With 200,000+ subscribers and an estimated 1.5 million all-time unique podcast listeners (as of 2026), it is one of the most significant technical media properties in the AI space.

He describes the 2026 strategy for Latent Space in "Scaling without Slop" ([latent.space/p/2026](https://www.latent.space/p/2026)) — expanding into a podcast network, adding an AI for Science show, growing a physical studio presence in San Francisco, and doubling emphasis on writing and thinking. The challenge he identifies: "The most important problem in media now is scaling without slop, period." AI makes it easy to generate volume; the hard thing is maintaining quality.

Latent Space also explicitly defines what it does *not* cover: law, regulation, politics, safety, X-risk, defense/biotech applications, and "BigTech power plays." This editorial discipline — deliberately constraining scope — is itself a web philosophy: better to be authoritative in a defined area than diffuse across everything.

## The Open Source Web

Swyx has contributed extensively to open-source developer knowledge infrastructure: the React TypeScript Cheatsheet (still a major resource), the smol-ai GitHub repositories, and the [swyxio/ai-notes](https://github.com/swyxio/ai-notes) repository (his running notes on AI developments, serving as the public datastore for Latent Space research and writing). This last project embodies his digital garden philosophy applied to technical research: working notes, made public, that become more useful as they accumulate.

He is a GitHub Star, a recognition of developers who make exceptional contributions to open-source communities — an institutional acknowledgment of his contributions to public knowledge on the web.
