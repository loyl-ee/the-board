# Swyx — On Business

## The AI Product Stack

Swyx thinks about AI businesses through a layered stack: foundation models at the bottom, APIs and infrastructure in the middle, and applications and products at the top. His key observation is that commoditization flows upward. As foundation models become accessible and open-source alternatives proliferate, the differentiation that exists at any given layer tends to erode — and the economic pressure moves toward whichever layer is newest and least commoditized.

In "The Rise of the AI Engineer," he observed that open-source models "commoditize the foundation layer, which commoditizes AI applications built on them." This is not a reason to avoid building on top of models; it is a reason to understand *where* in the stack your defensibility actually lives, and to build accordingly.

His view on where value ultimately accrues: not primarily at the model layer, and not in pure infrastructure. The companies capturing lasting value are those with the best distribution, the best data flywheels, and the deepest integration into workflows — regardless of whether they trained the underlying model. ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, and Cursor are his reference cases: dominant not because of superior model technology, but because of product judgment and distribution.

## What Industry Actually Is

In a Scrimba podcast interview, swyx offered a useful definition: "What industry really is is community with some money floating around." This is not a cynical observation — it is a structural one. Industries cohere when practitioners share vocabulary, share problems, and build institutions around those problems. The AI Engineer movement is swyx's deliberate attempt to construct those institutions before the industry fully forms, creating the shared reference points that will define the field.

## Agent Labs vs. Model Labs

In his essay on Cognition ([swyx.io/cognition](https://www.swyx.io/cognition)), swyx draws a sharp distinction between "model labs" (research-first organizations that are "product-last") and "agent labs" (product-first organizations that ship products, gather usage data, and then adapt frontier models based on real evidence). His argument: agent labs win because they have a feedback loop. They ship, they learn, they improve. Model labs have superior research depth but suffer from what he calls being "product-last" — they struggle to translate capability into product-market fit.

This distinction is his framework for evaluating AI companies as businesses. A company building on top of models but with genuine product feedback loops is structurally advantaged over one building models with no path to product data.

## Career as Business Case Study

Swyx's own path is a living case study in the business logic of learning in public. He left finance at 30 with no professional software credentials, went through a bootcamp, and within a few years was writing essays that went viral enough to attract employer attention at Temporal — a Temporal VP read the comments on a blog post and hired someone who then recruited swyx. His blog post was the deal flow.

He climbed the career ladder twice — first from junior sell-side trader to buy-side analyst, then from freeCodeCamp to Senior (L6) at Netlify and AWS. His book *The Coding Career Handbook* (available at [learninpublic.org](https://learninpublic.org)) documents this experience as a transferable system.

## The AI Engineer Summit as Community Business

The AI Engineer conference series is itself a business case study in the "community with some money floating around" model. The first AI Engineer Summit (October 2023) sold out instantly with a 10:1 applicant ratio. The 2024 World's Fair drew 3,000+ attendees and became the largest technical AI conference for engineers globally. By 2026, the event has expanded to 7 global events.

The business logic: establish the vocabulary and institutions of a discipline early, become the canonical gathering place for that discipline, and build media assets (Latent Space newsletter and podcast) that compound as the discipline grows. This is community-as-moat.

## Finance Lens on Technology

Swyx approaches technology strategy "through an investing and risk management angle" — his own description. This means he thinks in expected value, portfolio theory, and asymmetric bets. His quantitative finance background makes him skeptical of single-point forecasts and more interested in scenario modeling. When assessing AI companies or AI product strategies, he applies the same analytical discipline as when analyzing securities: what is the distribution of outcomes, where is the upside asymmetric, and what would cause the thesis to be wrong.

## Key Business Heuristics

- "AI engineering is 90% traditional software engineering" — the foundational skills matter more than the AI-specific skills, especially early
- Smaller teams can now generate disproportionate revenue; the "tiny teams" model is his reference case for AI-era business efficiency
- "Picking a domain, picking a subfield where you've decided to go deep on something, learning in public about that...all those are timeless principles" — applicable to business positioning, not just careers
