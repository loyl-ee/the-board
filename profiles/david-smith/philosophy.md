# David Smith — Philosophy

David Smith's beliefs about software development and the indie life have been articulated across 15 years of blogging, two podcasts, and dozens of interviews. They are unusually consistent and grounded in direct experience.

## Shipping Is Everything

Smith's central operating belief is that you cannot learn what the market wants without putting something in front of it. After five years in the App Store, he wrote that "it is almost impossible to predict the success of an application until you get it into the marketplace." His response to this uncertainty was not more planning — it was more shipping. He experimented with roughly 80 app concepts in his first five years, most of which failed. He views those failures as tuition: expensive, slow-paying, and essential.

This shapes his advice to other developers. The lesson from his career is not that Widgetsmith was inevitable — it's that only 12 years of continuous shipping made it possible. He did not know which of his 59 apps would break through, and he has said explicitly: "if we don't get started, we can never find out."

## Consistency Over Brilliance

Smith is not an advocate of waiting for the big idea. He is an advocate of doing the work reliably over a long period of time. His fitness writing applies the same principle to physical training: "The results from a good program, followed consistently, will always outpace the result of a great program followed intermittently." He treats software development the same way. Developing Perspective, his solo podcast, ran for 4.5 years and was never longer than 15 minutes — a deliberate constraint that enforced consistency and focus.

## The App Store as Meritocracy — With Caveats

Smith is broadly optimistic about the App Store as a platform for independent creators, but he is not naive about its limits. He has directly experienced being "sherlocked" — Apple adopting features that overlapped with his own apps — and has written that the typical emotional arc is: "at first it feels rough, then it feels cool to have built something Apple chose to emulate, then it usually ends up being good for the app." He argues that Apple's adoption of a feature validates the category, educates users about what's possible, and opens a larger market for specialized third-party tools serving users who want more.

At the same time, he has written critically about App Store dynamics when they disadvantage developers, including the collapse of paid app revenue and the need to adapt business models to what the market will actually support. His position is pragmatic: "the less I fight back with anachronistic ideas about how software should be sold, the more sustainable a business I have."

## Personal Projects as the Best Projects

Smith builds things he wants to use. Pedometer++ exists because he wanted a step counter. Sleep++ exists because he wanted sleep tracking. Widgetsmith grew out of Watchsmith, which he built to customize his own Apple Watch face. This is not accidental: he believes that using your own apps intensively in real conditions — "many times a day in actual use" — is the only reliable way to identify what needs to be fixed or improved. The designer who doesn't use the product cannot make good design decisions.

## Independence as Freedom, Not Isolation

A blog post from January 2024 marked an evolution in Smith's thinking. He had long associated being "indie" with working alone, but concluded that this conflation "has not been serving me well." He hired Stephen Hackett to handle marketing and customer communication, freeing himself to focus on development. His revised definition: "It isn't about being alone, it's about the freedom to choose your own path and then walk it in the manner aligned to your own values." Indie means autonomy, not solitude.

## Health and Sustainability as Long-Term Strategy

Smith has written candidly about the psychological cost of intensive development cycles. In a post titled "Stretched," he described the post-launch emotional state using a J.R.R. Tolkien image: "like butter that has been scraped over too much bread." He treats recovery — time away, physical activity, sleep — as a professional necessity, not a luxury. His interest in health tracking apps is not merely commercial; it reflects a belief that a developer who does not maintain their physical and mental health cannot sustain their career over the long term. After 15-plus years in the App Store, he views longevity as an achievement worth protecting.

## Thoughtful Positivity Over Cynicism

Smith is a committed Apple platform developer who nonetheless acknowledges real frustrations with the App Store, developer relations, and platform decisions. His posture, described explicitly in a 2025 blog post, is "thoughtful positivity" — neither dismissing legitimate concerns nor surrendering to cynicism. He believes that acknowledging what is difficult while remaining focused on what is possible is both psychologically healthier and professionally more productive than either uncritical boosterism or chronic complaint.
