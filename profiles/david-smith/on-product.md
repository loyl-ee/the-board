# David Smith — On Product

## Solving Problems You Personally Have

Smith's product philosophy begins with personal need. Nearly every significant app in his portfolio started because he wanted it to exist: Audiobooks because he wanted to listen to public domain recordings on his iPhone; Pedometer++ because he wanted a step counter; Sleep++ because he wanted Apple Watch sleep tracking; Watchsmith because he wanted custom Watch complications; Widgetsmith because he wanted home screen widgets with the same aesthetic control as Watchsmith.

This is not merely an origin story preference. Smith believes that personal use during development is the only reliable source of product insight. He writes that if "you aren't using an app under development many times a day in actual use you will really struggle to get it right." The designer who does not live inside the product cannot feel the friction, the missing features, or the moments of delight that determine whether users return.

## The Day One Approach to New Platforms

Smith has been a "day one" developer for three Apple platforms: iPhone (2008), iPad (2010), and Apple Watch (2015). He views early platform adoption as a deliberate strategic and personal choice. On new platforms, the field is small, the competition is limited, the editorial attention from Apple is disproportionate, and — crucially — the fundamental platform intuitions that take years to develop can only be built by being there early.

He writes: "There is something different about starting to work on a platform from its infancy, where things are very uncertain and the future prospects are not clear." He views this uncertainty not as a deterrent but as the point: it is where growth happens, and growth is what keeps his work engaging over a 15-plus year career.

This philosophy directly enabled Widgetsmith. Smith had been working with Apple Watch complications in ClockKit and then SwiftUI for Watchsmith. When Apple announced WidgetKit at WWDC 2020, he recognized the API immediately because he had already built the relevant muscle. He built Widgetsmith in six weeks and submitted it within a 12-hour window of iOS 14's release, an execution that would have been impossible without years of prior platform investment.

## Widget Design as a Distinct Product Category

Widgetsmith expanded Smith's product thinking in a specific direction. He had originally conceived it as a "niche tool for power users" who wanted granular control over widget content and style. The TikTok moment revealed a much larger audience: mainstream users who wanted home screen personalization as an expression of identity and aesthetic taste, not technical capability.

This forced him to think carefully about what kind of product Widgetsmith actually was. Version 2.0 (November 2020) pivoted the product's presentation from configuration-first to theme-first: rather than asking users to build from scratch, the app offered curated starting points that could be modified. The aesthetic and functional options remained, but the entry point changed to serve the user who didn't know where to start.

He has continued to treat Widgetsmith as an exploration space. Widgetsmith 5 (September 2023) introduced interactive widgets in multiple formats — photo galleries, carousels, gamified lockets, hybrid information-plus-image widgets — with explicit uncertainty about which directions would prove popular: "I have no idea if the directions I've come up with are going to be popular." He ships experiments and learns from user response.

## Health Tracking as a Product Category

Smith's health apps share a coherent product philosophy: track automatically where possible, present clearly, resist judgment. Sleep++ evolved from a manual-start app to one that "simply requires you to wear your Apple Watch overnight" and determines sleep duration and quality from motion and health data without user input. This reduction of friction — the shift from "you must remember to start" to "you just wear it" — reflects a consistent product value.

His health apps also share a UX pattern: simple summary first, detail on demand. Sleep++ shows a single readiness score derived from heart rate variability and resting heart rate, but decomposes it into component values for users who want to understand the underlying data. Pedometer++ shows a step count against a goal, celebrates completion with confetti, but surfaces weekly charts and detailed walking data for engaged users.

He is careful about the emotional weight of health data. Unlike Pedometer++'s confetti celebration of hitting step goals, Sleep++ uses subtle visual indicators for sleep achievement — Smith explicitly noted he "don't want to be quite too dramatic with how I illustrate your goal" given that sleep difficulties are often beyond a user's control.

## Portfolio Discipline vs. Single-App Focus

Smith's approach is unusual: he maintains a portfolio of active apps rather than concentrating entirely on one flagship. This creates genuine product tension — each app demands attention, and attention is finite for a solo developer. His solution has evolved over time: develop systems (FAQ pages, video tutorials, scaled support) that reduce the ongoing maintenance burden on less active apps, and reserve deeper creative investment for apps that are in active development cycles.

He also practices active portfolio curation. He has removed apps from the App Store when they no longer provide a reasonable user experience on modern devices, arguing that abandoned apps harm the App Store ecosystem and users deserve to know that whatever they download will be maintained. This discipline prevents the portfolio from becoming a liability.
