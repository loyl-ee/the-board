# David Smith — On Web

## david-smith.org as Primary Web Presence

Smith's website at david-smith.org has been his primary web publishing platform since 2011. It functions as a longform blog covering iOS development, App Store economics, product design, personal reflection, and platform analysis. Posts range from brief observations to multi-thousand-word technical and strategic essays. The archive now spans 15 years and represents an unusually complete public record of one indie developer's career.

He does not write for traffic or SEO. Posts are published when he has something to say, at irregular intervals. The content value is high and the audience is self-selecting: other developers, Apple community observers, journalists covering the App Store, and occasional product thinkers who follow his work. This is the opposite of content marketing — it is genuine sharing of his thinking, which happens to be valuable to the right readers.

A distinct section of the site is the Design Diary (hosted at david-smith.org/dnd/), which documents his design and development process with in-progress screenshots and detailed process notes. He began this as a personal discipline tool — articulating design decisions forces clarity — and has shared it publicly because "there is fundamental value in the capture itself."

He also maintained a public iOS version statistics page (david-smith.org/iosversionstats/) that tracked real-world iOS adoption rates using aggregate data from his apps, making it useful to the entire developer community. The page has since been retired because the data collection method stopped working and he judged rebuilding it not worth the effort.

## Twitter and Mastodon

Smith was an active Twitter user (@_DavidSmith) throughout the 2010s and early 2020s, using the platform for real-time reactions, brief technical observations, feature announcements, and direct engagement with users and other developers. His viral Widgetsmith tweet — "Widgetsmith just went viral on TikTok...which is a phrase I don't even really understand but is having a rather robust result on my life right now" — became one of the most shared moments in indie iOS developer history and was widely cited in media coverage of the app's rise.

He has since moved significant social media activity to Mastodon (@_Davidsmith@mastodon.social), consistent with a broader independent-web preference, while maintaining a Twitter/X presence. His Mastodon posts show the same character as his Twitter output: direct, thoughtful, occasionally self-deprecating, and engaged with the developer community.

## Podcasting as Extended Writing

For Smith, podcasting has served a similar function to blogging — a medium for thinking out loud about indie development — rather than a marketing vehicle. Developing Perspective (2011–2015) was explicitly designed to be brief (never longer than 15 minutes) and focused on practical lessons from his own experience. Under the Radar (2015–2025, with Marco Arment on Relay FM, 331 episodes over 10 years) maintained a 30-minute maximum and a format focused on specific topics relevant to indie developers rather than news commentary.

He has described his motivation for podcasting as creating "a venue for sharing my experiences and hard learned lessons." The audience was other developers, not the general public, and the measure of success was whether the content was useful to that specific audience.

## No Traditional Marketing

Smith runs no advertising for his apps, has no PR operation, and has made no significant marketing investments. His customer acquisition channels are: organic App Store discovery, Apple editorial featuring (earned by being a day-one adopter of new APIs), word of mouth, and user-generated content (particularly Widgetsmith home screen shares). His blog and social media presence contribute to his reputation within the developer community, which generates occasional press coverage, but this is a side effect rather than a strategy.

This is not naive: Smith has thought carefully about the economics and concluded that the App Store's own distribution mechanisms, combined with building products whose users naturally share them, are more cost-effective for an indie developer than paid acquisition. The Widgetsmith experience — 131 million downloads with zero marketing spend — is the most extreme validation of this approach, though he would be the first to acknowledge that it also involved extraordinary luck in the form of a TikTok moment he did not engineer.

## Web vs. Native

Smith's own products are native iOS apps; he has no public web app portfolio. His web presence is entirely informational (blog, app pages, privacy policies). This reflects both his technical background and his conviction that the iPhone and Apple Watch are the right platforms for the problems he is solving. There is no indication he views web as a meaningful channel for his app businesses.
