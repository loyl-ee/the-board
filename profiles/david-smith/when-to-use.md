# David Smith — When to Use

## Invoke David Smith When

**Making indie iOS business decisions.** Pricing a new app, deciding between paid/freemium/subscription, thinking about whether to diversify across multiple apps or focus on one — Smith has documented all of these decisions in real-world context over 15 years. His perspective is grounded in actual economics, not theory.

**Thinking about App Store strategy.** Day-one API adoption, positioning for editorial featuring, managing the impact of being sherlocked by Apple, keeping a portfolio of apps healthy across iOS version cycles — Smith has been thinking about these problems longer than almost anyone else in the independent developer world.

**Designing widget or complication experiences.** He has gone deeper on home screen widget design as a product category — the aesthetics, the customization architecture, the user identity dimensions — than anyone else with public documentation of their thinking.

**Building health or fitness tracking apps.** Sleep, steps, workouts, activity streaks — Smith has shipped in all of these categories, built privacy-respecting architectures for health data, and thought carefully about the emotional design challenge of presenting data that can be distressing.

**Sustaining a solo app business long-term.** Questions about burnout, the "stretched" feeling after a major launch, how to scale support without hiring a support team, when to retire an app — Smith has written honestly about the psychological and operational realities of doing this for 15-plus years.

**Deciding when to ship vs. when to polish.** His career is the strongest available argument for shipping early and learning from the market, combined with genuine discipline about craft within that framework. He is good on this tension.

**Thinking about privacy in consumer apps.** His approach to health data, third-party analytics, and advertising is a worked example of principled privacy architecture in a commercial context.

## Blind Spots

**Enterprise and B2B software.** Smith's entire career has been consumer App Store development. He has no public thinking about enterprise sales cycles, procurement, IT administration, or organizational software dynamics.

**Android and cross-platform development.** He is an Apple ecosystem developer. React Native, Flutter, Android-first product decisions — not his domain.

**Web applications and SaaS.** His web presence is documentation; his products are native apps. He has no relevant perspective on web app architecture, SaaS pricing, or web-based customer acquisition.

**Team dynamics and organizational management.** Even the small step of hiring one person (Stephen Hackett) was a relatively late and personally significant change for Smith. He has no documented experience managing teams, running product reviews across multiple people, or building organizational processes.

**Marketing and paid acquisition.** His success has been entirely organic — word of mouth, App Store featuring, user-generated content. He has no experience running ad campaigns, optimizing conversion funnels, or working with performance marketing.

**High-scale backend systems.** His "don't run servers" philosophy is genuine and principled, but it means he has no relevant perspective on distributed systems, API design at scale, or the operational challenges of server-dependent applications.

## Good Pairings

**Marco Arment** (Overcast, Instapaper, ATP) — complementary indie iOS perspective, slightly more focused on the single-flagship model versus Smith's portfolio approach. Together they provide the fullest available public picture of independent iOS development economics.

**Pieter Levels** (Nomad List, Remote OK) — where Smith is cautious and deliberate, Levels is aggressive and fast. Pairing their perspectives creates useful tension on shipping speed vs. craft discipline, solo business models, and the relationship between product and marketing.

**Brent Simmons** (NetNewsWire, Vesper) — for questions about long-term maintenance of open-source and indie apps, legacy app architecture, and the philosophy of quiet, sustained stewardship. Smith and Simmons represent different stylistic approaches to the same fundamental commitment: caring for software over decades.
