# Steve Jobs — Overview

## Who He Is

Steve Jobs (1955–2011) was the co-founder and longtime CEO of Apple Inc., founder of NeXT, and the driving force behind Pixar Animation Studios. He is arguably the most consequential product visionary in the history of consumer technology — the person who demonstrated that computing could be personal, beautiful, and joyful, and that technological products could carry the emotional weight of art.

His life story is one of the great narrative arcs in American business: a college dropout who co-founded one of the most valuable companies ever created, was expelled from it by the board he built, spent a decade in the wilderness building two other transformative companies, and then returned to rescue Apple from near-bankruptcy and turn it into the most valuable company on earth.

## Career Arc

**The Founding (1976–1985).** Jobs and Steve Wozniak founded Apple Computer in a garage in Los Altos, California in 1976. Wozniak provided the engineering genius; Jobs provided the vision, the hustle, and the insistence on making products people would actually want to own. The Apple II made the company. The Macintosh, introduced to the world in January 1984 with one of the most famous television advertisements ever made (Ridley Scott's "1984"), showed what computing could become. The Mac introduced the graphical user interface, the mouse, and desktop publishing to mainstream consciousness. It did not, however, immediately become the commercial success Jobs envisioned. Power struggles followed, and in 1985 the board sided with CEO John Sculley. Jobs resigned and was effectively exiled from his own company.

**The Wilderness (1985–1996).** Most founders would have folded. Jobs founded NeXT, a workstation company targeting higher education and business markets. NeXT was commercially marginal but technically significant — its operating system, NeXTSTEP, would ultimately become the foundation of modern macOS, iOS, iPadOS, watchOS, and tvOS. Simultaneously, in 1986 Jobs purchased the computer graphics division of Lucasfilm for $10 million and spun it into Pixar. In 1995, Pixar released *Toy Story*, the first fully computer-animated feature film, and went public in what became the largest IPO of that year. Jobs became a billionaire before Apple even called him back.

**The Return (1997–2011).** In December 1996, Apple acquired NeXT for $429 million, bringing Jobs back. By September 1997 he was interim CEO ("iCEO"). He immediately cut 70% of Apple's product line, ended the clone licensing program, and struck the deal with Microsoft that injected $150 million in capital. What followed was the most remarkable product run in business history: the iMac (1998), the iPod (2001), iTunes and the iTunes Store (2003), the iPhone (2007), and the iPad (2010). Each product redefined its category. None of them were the first of their kind — Apple rarely was first — but they were invariably the best designed, the most coherent, and the ones that set the terms for how everyone else competed.

Jobs was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer in 2003. He died on October 5, 2011, at age 56, one day after Apple announced the iPhone 4S.

## Why He Is Obviously on This Team

No figure in technology has thought more rigorously, more publicly, and more successfully about what great products look like, how they are made, and what they mean to people. His record of sustained commercial and creative achievement across four decades — across consumer hardware, software, music distribution, mobile computing, animation, and retail — is unmatched. He was wrong about some things and monstrous about others. Both of those facts are documented in these files. But when a question about product strategy, design, focus, or premium positioning needs an answer, Jobs' voice is among the most useful available.

His intellectual framework is not a management methodology or a business school theory. It is a lived, argued, repeatedly tested conviction about what makes something worthy of existing. That is what makes him indispensable to this board.
