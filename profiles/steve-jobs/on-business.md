# Steve Jobs — On Business

## Vertical Integration as Strategy

Jobs was an unabashed advocate for vertical integration at a time when the conventional wisdom in Silicon Valley and business schools ran in the opposite direction. The 1990s orthodoxy held that companies should focus on their "core competency" and outsource everything else — a model that produced the Wintel ecosystem and made Microsoft and Intel enormously profitable.

Jobs rejected this. He believed that making hardware, software, and services as an integrated system — rather than optimizing each layer independently — was the only way to achieve the quality and coherence he demanded. "We control the product design from end to end," he said repeatedly. This conviction shaped every major Apple product: the original Mac required software written specifically for its hardware; the iPod worked only with iTunes; the iPhone ran only iOS; the App Store distributed only iOS apps. Customers occasionally chafed at the lack of openness. Jobs did not care. He was trading short-term user freedom for a category of experience that open, fragmented systems could not produce.

The commercial vindication of this strategy became clear after Jobs' return to Apple. Between 1997 and 2011, Apple's vertical integration allowed it to build products that competitors could not replicate even when they had access to the same underlying hardware components. Competitors had the same chips and cameras and glass that Apple had. What they could not copy was the integrated system design — the fact that Jobs controlled the silicon architecture, the operating system, the developer tools, the hardware form factor, and the retail experience simultaneously.

## The Premium Market as the Only Market Worth Having

Jobs had no interest in market share for its own sake. He understood early — and maintained throughout his career — that the premium segment of any market is the one where margins, brand equity, and customer loyalty compound. He was willing to be 5% of the market if that 5% generated 50% of the profit.

This was not mere posturing. When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company had been pursuing market share through licensing its operating system to clone makers and trying to compete on price. It had nearly destroyed the company. Jobs killed the clones immediately. He kept Apple's prices high and accepted a smaller market in exchange for better margins, more loyal customers, and the ability to continue investing in design and engineering. The iPod was priced higher than any competing MP3 player. The iPhone was priced higher than any competing smartphone. The MacBook Air was priced at a substantial premium over comparable PC laptops. None of these decisions hurt Apple; all of them worked because the products were good enough to command the premium and, in many cases, to define what "premium" meant.

Jobs articulated the underlying logic to Fortune in 1998: "Innovation has nothing to do with how many R&D dollars you have. When Apple came up with the Mac, IBM was spending at least 100 times more on R&D. It's not about money. It's about the people you have, how you're led, and how much you get it."

## Profit as Result, Not Goal

Jobs was emphatic — in interviews, in internal communications, and in his conduct — that making money was not the purpose of Apple. It was the result of doing the right work. He told Isaacson: "My passion has been to build an enduring company where people were motivated to make great products. Everything else was secondary." He contrasted Apple's orientation with what he saw as Microsoft's: Microsoft had won the OS war by selling to OEMs who competed on price, which made Microsoft wealthy but, in Jobs' view, produced mediocre software because there was no one accountable for the full experience.

Jobs was not indifferent to commercial success — he tracked Apple's financials closely and celebrated profitable quarters. But he treated profit instrumentally: it was the fuel that allowed Apple to keep building products it believed in. When he discussed competitors who cut corners on design or materials to hit a price point, he treated it as a category error — they were optimizing for the wrong thing.

## The 1997 Product Line Reduction

The most vivid demonstration of Jobs' business philosophy was his first major act on returning to Apple. The company had approximately 350 products. Jobs reduced it to roughly 10. He built a simple two-by-two grid: consumer desktop, consumer portable, professional desktop, professional portable. Everything outside that grid was cut.

The efficiency gains from this reduction were substantial — Apple could focus manufacturing, supply chain, engineering, and marketing on a small number of products and execute them at a level that was impossible across 350 SKUs. But the strategic insight was more fundamental: focus creates coherence, and coherence creates brand identity. A company that makes 350 products is not known for any of them. A company that makes 10 great products is known for all of them.

Jobs described the orientation in a BusinessWeek interview in 2004: "We're always thinking about new markets we could enter, but it's only by saying no that you can concentrate on the things that are really important."

## Pricing Power from Design

Jobs understood that design is not a cost center — it is a source of pricing power. Because Apple products were recognized as being designed at a fundamentally different level than competitors, Apple could charge more for them, and customers would pay. The iMac's distinctive form factor in 1998 generated consumer waiting lists. The iPod's click wheel and white earbuds made it instantly identifiable and desirable. The iPhone's full glass face and single home button made everything before it look dated. Each design decision translated into market advantage that could not be easily replicated, because it was not merely aesthetic — it was the product of a deeply integrated design-and-engineering process that competitors did not have.

Jobs told Isaacson: "Picasso had a saying: 'Good artists copy; great artists steal.' We have always been shameless about stealing great ideas." He applied this to business as well as design: the premium retail experience (borrowed from Four Seasons hotels and Ritz-Carlton, per Ron Johnson's account), the Genius Bar concept, the theater-in-the-Apple-Store format — all were adapted from non-technology contexts because Jobs was looking for great ideas wherever they existed.
