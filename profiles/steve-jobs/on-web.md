# Steve Jobs — On the Web

## Apple's Web Presence and the apple.com Standard

Jobs treated apple.com as an extension of Apple's product philosophy: clean, focused, and designed to the same standard as the hardware it represented. Under Jobs, Apple's website was consistently among the most distinctive in consumer technology — sparse where competitors were cluttered, image-led where competitors were text-heavy, designed to evoke the product rather than explain it in spec sheets.

He extended this to Apple's web services. iTools (launched 2000) provided free web-based services — email, storage, a homepage builder — to Mac users. It was renamed .Mac in 2002 and became a paid service. The ambition was right; the execution was frequently troubled.

## MobileMe: The Cautionary Case

MobileMe (launched July 9, 2008, as the replacement for .Mac) was one of the most documented failures of Jobs' tenure — notable not just because it failed, but because of how Jobs responded internally.

MobileMe launched simultaneously with the iPhone 3G, iPhone 2.0 software, and the App Store — a single day that overwhelmed Apple's servers and operations. MobileMe immediately had widespread outages: email delivery failed for extended periods, syncing between devices was unreliable, and customers who had paid $99/year for the service received little or none of it.

Jobs gathered the MobileMe team in the Town Hall auditorium on Apple's campus. His opening question, according to multiple accounts published in Fortune's "Inside Apple" (2011): "Can anyone tell me what MobileMe is supposed to do?" When employees answered, he replied: "So why the f**k doesn't it do that?" He fired the manager of the MobileMe group on the spot.

Jobs acknowledged the failure publicly in a measured email: "It was a mistake to launch MobileMe at the same time as iPhone 3G, iPhone 2.0 software and the App Store... MobileMe was simply not up to Apple's standards — it clearly needed more time and testing." The full email, published by Gizmodo in 2008, captures the gap between Apple's product ambitions and its web services capabilities.

MobileMe was eventually shut down and replaced by iCloud in 2011. Former Apple engineering project manager Erin Caton later noted that line engineers had raised concerns before launch — concerns that were overruled. This is a case where Jobs' internal culture of top-down authority contributed to a failure that could have been caught earlier.

## Flash vs. HTML5: "Thoughts on Flash"

On April 29, 2010, Jobs published "Thoughts on Flash" — an open letter explaining why Apple would not allow Adobe Flash on the iPhone, iPod touch, or iPad. It is one of the most consequential documents in the history of the modern web.

Jobs made six arguments against Flash:

1. **Openness:** He argued Flash was a proprietary system controlled by Adobe, while HTML5, CSS, and JavaScript were open standards.
2. **Full web:** He disputed Adobe's claim that Flash was necessary for "the full web," noting that many major video sites had already adopted HTML5.
3. **Reliability and security:** He cited evidence that Flash was "the number one reason Macs crash" and had significant security vulnerabilities.
4. **Battery life:** Flash software video decoding consumed significantly more power than hardware-accelerated decoding; no Flash player existed for mobile with hardware acceleration.
5. **Touch:** Flash was designed for mice and cursors, not fingers — it relied on hover states that had no touch equivalent.
6. **Developer platform control:** His most strategic argument: allowing a third-party runtime layer between iOS and developers would allow Adobe (not Apple) to define what iPhone apps could and couldn't do.

Adobe disputed nearly all of these arguments. Critics noted that Jobs' criticism of Flash for being "closed" applied with equal force to the App Store itself. Jobs was not wrong that Flash was technically problematic on mobile; he was also not exclusively motivated by technical concerns.

The letter effectively accelerated HTML5 adoption across the industry. Adobe announced the end of Flash for mobile browsers in November 2011. Flash itself died in December 2020.

## The App Store as a Web Counter-Strategy

Jobs initially told developers at WWDC 2007 that the way to build applications for the iPhone was through web apps — mobile-optimized websites accessed through Safari. This was not his preference; it was a holding position while the App Store was developed (it launched in July 2008).

Once the App Store launched, it became the primary channel for software distribution on iOS — explicitly bypassing the open web as a distribution mechanism. This was a deliberate strategic choice: native apps, distributed through Apple's curated store, provided better performance, richer access to device hardware, and tighter quality control than web apps could offer in 2008. It also gave Apple a revenue stream (30% of app sales) and a control mechanism that the open web did not.

Jobs believed the App Store demonstrated that Flash was unnecessary: by 2010 he could point to 200,000 apps on the platform, including graphically rich games, as evidence that developers did not need Flash to build compelling mobile experiences.
