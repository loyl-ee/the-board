# Steve Jobs — On Design

## Design Is Not How It Looks — It Is How It Works

Jobs' most often-cited statement about design came from a 2003 *New York Times Magazine* profile: "Most people make the mistake of thinking design is what it looks like. People think it's this veneer — that the designers are handed this box and told, 'Make it look good!' That's not what we think design is. It's not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works."

This redefinition was consequential. In most of the technology industry, design was treated as the last step: engineers built the product, and then someone made it look presentable. At Apple under Jobs, design was integrated into the product's conception. The form and the function were worked out together, by teams that sat adjacent to each other and argued about the same problems from different perspectives.

Jobs believed that design decisions were engineering decisions and engineering decisions were design decisions. The single button on the original iPhone was not a graphic design choice — it was an interaction philosophy made physical. The click wheel on the iPod was not a visual element — it was an input mechanism designed around the human thumb's natural range of motion. The aluminum body of the MacBook Air was not styling — it was a manufacturing process (unibody CNC machining) that produced a stronger, thinner, and lighter result than conventional assembly.

## The Back of the Drawers

The foundational story in understanding Jobs' design philosophy is about his father, Paul Jobs, a machinist and carpenter. Paul Jobs taught his son that the back of a fence — the part facing the wall, which no one would ever see — had to be finished as carefully as the front. Because the craftsman would know. And knowing changes how you do everything else.

Jobs carried this lesson into every Apple product. He insisted that the circuit boards inside the original Macintosh be laid out beautifully, even though no customer would ever open the machine. He insisted that the inside of the original Mac's case be signed by every member of the design team — an act that made no commercial sense and was everything to do with what kind of people Apple wanted to be.

He described the principle most directly in a 1985 *Playboy* interview: "When you're a carpenter making a beautiful chest of drawers, you're not going to use a piece of plywood on the back, even though it faces the wall and nobody will ever see it. You'll know it's there, so you're going to use a beautiful piece of wood on the back. For you to sleep well at night, the aesthetic, the quality, has to be carried all the way through."

The implication is that quality standards are not about visibility — they are about identity. Once you make something bad where no one can see it, you have become the kind of organization that makes bad things.

## The Jony Ive Partnership

Jobs' collaboration with Jony Ive, who joined Apple in 1992 and became VP of Industrial Design under Jobs, was the most consequential design partnership in technology history. They met almost daily for fifteen years, lunching together at the design studio, walking around tables covered in foam models, arguing about proportions, materials, and meanings.

Ive described the relationship: "Steve had this incredible ability to be laser-focused on the most important problems. When we were designing the Mac, he would talk about the relationship between the weight and the material. Not just as abstract properties but as something that communicated care." (Entrepreneur, various interviews)

Jobs gave Ive more authority than any designer had previously held at a technology company. The design studio at Apple was (and remains) a separate building with restricted access — not because designers are precious, but because Ive and Jobs believed that design thinking had to be protected from the institutional pressure to compromise for manufacturing convenience or cost reduction. Jobs supported Ive when operations teams pushed back on design decisions that made production more difficult or expensive.

Ive's own account of what Jobs taught him: "Steve used to say that you have to deeply understand the essence of a product in order to be able to get rid of the parts that are not essential." This is the subtractive theory of design — that the designer's job is not to add but to remove, until only the essential remains.

## Packaging as Product Experience

Jobs and Ive treated the box a product came in as part of the product. This was not conventional at Apple's competitors, where packaging was a logistics and cost problem. For Apple, packaging was the beginning of the customer relationship — the first physical interaction a customer had with the device, before they had even seen the device itself.

Jony Ive stated explicitly: "I love the process of unpacking something. You design a ritual of unpacking to make the product feel special. Packaging can be theater, and it can create a story." He and Jobs reportedly spent significant time and budget on packaging for every major Apple product.

The iPhone's packaging was studied in detail before launch: the box was sized to require two hands to open, the layers of the unboxing were sequenced to build anticipation, the fit of the components was engineered so that nothing rattled or slid. The unboxing experience was not an afterthought — it was a designed sequence with a beginning, a middle, and a climax.

## Simplicity as Hard Work

Jobs often challenged the assumption that simplicity is simple. He argued that achieving genuine simplicity requires more work, not less: "Simple can be harder than complex. You have to work hard to get your thinking clean to make it simple. But it's worth it in the end, because once you get there, you can move mountains." (BusinessWeek, May 1998)

This distinction — between surface simplicity and deep simplicity — is fundamental to understanding Apple's design under Jobs. It was not minimalism as aesthetic preference. It was minimalism as the result of ruthless prioritization about what the product needed to do and the elimination of everything else. The challenge is that this kind of simplicity is expensive: it requires solving hard problems completely rather than partially, and partially-solved problems have a way of requiring visible complexity to manage.
