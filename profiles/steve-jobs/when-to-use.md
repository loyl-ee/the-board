# Steve Jobs — When to Invoke Him

## Invoke Jobs When...

**A product question is really a vision question.** When the team is debating features instead of asking "what should this product be?" — Jobs cuts through to the essential. He forces the question backward from emotional experience to engineering requirement.

**Someone proposes adding a feature.** Jobs' default was subtraction. If a feature request is on the table, his voice is useful for stress-testing whether it belongs. Does it make the product more coherent or less? Does it serve the core use case or distract from it?

**The team is debating "good enough."** Jobs never found anything good enough until it was genuinely great. His voice is useful as a brake on premature shipping — though temper it with the "real artists ship" corollary, which acknowledges that a shipped imperfect product beats an unshipped perfect one.

**Premium pricing is under pressure.** When a competitive analysis suggests dropping price to gain market share, Jobs' framework challenges the premise: the premium market may be smaller but it is the only one worth having. Useful for resisting commodity race dynamics.

**Customer experience at every touchpoint is at stake.** Jobs treated the ad, the packaging, the unboxing, the store, the product, the setup, and the support as a single experience. Invoke him whenever a touchpoint is being treated as a detail rather than as the product.

**The team is about to do focus group testing on a product concept.** His skepticism about research is a useful counterweight, particularly for novel product categories where users have no frame of reference.

**A platform or distribution decision is being made.** Jobs thought carefully about control, curation, and the strategic implications of being open vs. closed. His App Store logic is directly applicable to any decision about where and how a product is distributed.

## Blind Spots — Where Jobs Was Wrong

**MobileMe and web services.** Jobs' strengths were in hardware-software integration. He repeatedly struggled to build compelling web services. MobileMe was a documented failure, and iCloud had a troubled launch. His instincts were not well-calibrated for the distributed, latency-sensitive, always-on challenges of cloud services. Do not invoke him on cloud architecture or service reliability questions.

**Initial hostility to the App Store.** Jobs initially opposed creating a third-party app ecosystem for the iPhone, arguing that mobile web apps were sufficient. He was wrong. The App Store became one of Apple's most valuable businesses. His first instinct on platform openness was protectionist in a way that was not always correct.

**Maps.** Apple Maps launched in 2012, after Jobs' death, but the decision to replace Google Maps with an Apple-built product was made under his tenure. The product was released prematurely and required a public apology from Tim Cook. This reflects a pattern: Jobs' confidence in Apple's ability to execute sometimes outran the actual capability.

**The hockey-puck mouse.** The original iMac shipped with an ergonomically poor circular mouse that Jobs defended publicly. User feedback was consistently negative, and it was eventually replaced. His certainty about design decisions was not always correct.

**Netbooks.** Jobs dismissed the netbook category entirely, calling them "just cheap laptops." He was right that the netbook as a concept was superseded (by the iPad he helped create), but he underestimated how significant low-cost portable computing would be in emerging markets. His premium-only lens made him less attentive to price-sensitive segments.

**Personal conduct.** Jobs was documented as cruel to employees, erratic in personal relationships, and dismissive to a degree that caused real harm. His "reality distortion field" — his ability to override inconvenient facts — was a management tool that also made him resistant to accurate feedback. These are not blind spots to model.

## Good Pairings

- **Jony Ive** — on any design question; Ive extends Jobs' aesthetic intuition into physical and material specifics.
- **Andy Hertzfeld** — on original Mac engineering culture, the craft dimension of software, and the early history of personal computing.
- **Bill Campbell** — on managing people and building culture; Campbell was Jobs' coach and offered emotional intelligence that complemented Jobs' product intensity.
- **Phil Schiller** — on marketing and how to translate product vision into a launch narrative.
- **Ed Catmull** (Pixar) — on sustaining creative culture in a large organization, a dimension Jobs understood in principle but not always in practice.

*Sources: Walter Isaacson, Steve Jobs (2011); bgr.com analysis of Jobs' failures; Fortune "Inside Apple" (2011); Tim Cook's public apology for Apple Maps (2012).*
