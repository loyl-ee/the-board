# Steve Jobs — On the Customer Journey

## "Customers Don't Know What They Want Until You Show Them"

Jobs' most controversial customer-facing position was his skepticism about conventional market research. He stated in an interview with Inc. magazine in 1989: "It's really hard to design products by focus groups. A lot of times, people don't know what they want until you show it to them." He returned to this idea throughout his career.

The criticism of this view is that it became a justification for ignoring users — that Jobs used it as permission to be autocratic about product decisions. The more charitable and accurate reading is that Jobs was making a specific epistemological point: users can accurately report their experience of existing products but cannot reliably specify what a fundamentally different product would need to be. No amount of market research in 1999 would have told Apple to build the iPod, because users had no frame of reference for a device that combined a 5GB hard drive, a clickwheel interface, and deep integration with a desktop music management application. The insight came from observing behavior (people were ripping CDs, managing files manually, using underpowered MP3 players) and asking what would make that behavior not just better but completely different.

This view has a legitimate lineage. It reflects the distinction between stated preferences (what people say they want) and revealed preferences (what they actually do), and the additional category of latent preferences (what they would want if they knew it existed). Jobs operated primarily in the third category.

## The Apple Store as a Customer Experience

When Jobs decided Apple needed its own retail stores in 2000, the conventional wisdom was that manufacturer-owned retail was a dead end — Gateway had just failed spectacularly at it. Jobs hired Ron Johnson, then vice president of merchandising at Target, and together they spent months studying hospitality businesses rather than retail businesses. Johnson's insight — drawn from stays at Ritz-Carlton hotels — was that great service is about creating emotional connection, not efficient transactions.

The Apple Store was designed around the question "How do we enrich people's lives?" rather than "How do we sell products?" The layout was open, with products available for hands-on exploration without sales pressure. The Genius Bar — a counter staffed by people whose job was to solve problems rather than sell products — was modeled on hotel concierge desks. When Johnson first proposed the Genius Bar, Jobs called it "so idiotic it'll never work." A day later, Jobs was filing for the "Genius Bar" trademark.

The Apple Store became the highest-revenue-per-square-foot retail operation in the world, exceeding even luxury jewelers like Tiffany & Co. The reason was not clever merchandising — it was that the store delivered the same emotional standard as the products. Walking into an Apple Store was itself an experience, not merely a transaction venue.

## The First Impression as a Product Decision

Jobs believed that the first moment of contact between a customer and a product was a product decision, not a marketing or packaging decision. He applied this to every touchpoint: the advertisement that introduced the product (the 1984 Mac ad, the "Here's to the Crazy Ones" campaign, the silhouetted iPod dancers), the unboxing ritual, the first time the device powered on, the setup experience.

He was particularly focused on the unboxing. Jony Ive described the philosophy: "I love the process of unpacking something. You design a ritual of unpacking to make the product feel special. Packaging can be theater, and it can create a story." Apple's packaging engineers spent months on box design — the resistance when opening an iPhone box was engineered to slow the user down by a fraction of a second, enough to create anticipation, a sense that what was inside was worth the ceremony of the opening.

Jobs' point was that customer experience begins before the customer touches the device. If the packaging communicates care, quality, and attention, the customer arrives at the product already disposed toward it favorably. If the packaging is an afterthought, the customer arrives at the product with lowered expectations that affect how they perceive everything that follows.

## Observation Over Surveys

Jobs famously did his own informal customer research by visiting Apple Stores and observing how customers behaved — watching without being recognized, listening to conversations. He trusted this kind of direct behavioral observation over surveys or focus groups because it bypassed the gap between what people say and what they do.

He was known to lurk outside the Palo Alto Apple Store, watching through the window to see how customers interacted with products, who stopped, what they picked up, how they responded to staff. This was not eccentric behavior — it was a systematic methodology. Jobs believed that the designer's job was to watch people more carefully than they watched themselves, and to make products that resolved their frustrations before they had fully articulated them.

The corollary was that Jobs held himself to an unusual standard of empathy: not empathy as a sentiment but as a discipline. He asked, repeatedly, "What would I want if I were the customer?" — and took the answer seriously enough to make expensive product decisions based on it.
