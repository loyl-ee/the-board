# Steve Jobs — On Safety, Ethics, and Responsibility

## The App Store as a Walled Garden: The Security Argument

Jobs launched the App Store in July 2008 with a deliberate design decision: all applications distributed through it would be reviewed and approved by Apple before being made available to users. This "walled garden" model was the opposite of the open web, and it attracted immediate criticism from developers and civil liberties advocates who argued it gave Apple too much control over what software could exist on devices users owned.

Jobs' public defense of the App Store review process was primarily framed in terms of user protection. He argued, in interviews and in "Thoughts on Flash," that a curated distribution system created a smartphone ecosystem in which users faced very low risk of malicious applications. Before the App Store, mobile software was distributed through carriers (with their own opaque controls) or not at all. The App Store replaced that with a system that, whatever its faults, made distributing malware to iPhone users extremely difficult.

The security argument was substantiated by evidence: the iOS ecosystem, despite its enormous user base, has had a dramatically lower rate of malware incidents than Android, which has a more open distribution model. Whether this outcome is entirely attributable to App Store review, to iOS's sandboxing architecture, or to a combination of both is debated — but the directional result has been consistent.

Jobs was also candid about the paternalism involved. In a 2010 interview at the D8 conference, he said: "We have rejected a lot of apps and have been criticized for that. But people seem to want to buy iPhones. The more you look at it, the more you realize this is the right path." He acknowledged the tension between openness and curation and chose curation on behalf of the users he saw as his primary responsibility.

## Privacy Stances

Jobs expressed strong views on privacy in public settings, particularly at the D8 conference in 2010. When Walt Mossberg asked him about privacy, Jobs said: "Privacy means people know what they're signing up for, in plain English, and repeatedly. I'm an optimist; I believe people are smart, and some people want to share more data than other people do. Ask them. Ask them every time. Make them tell you to stop asking them if they get tired of your asking them. Let them know precisely what you're going to do with their data."

This was a statement of principle — that informed consent, clearly expressed and repeatedly confirmed, was the correct model for data use. Jobs was particularly critical of advertising-driven models (a pointed contrast with Google's business) that monetized user data without clear disclosure. Under his tenure, Apple's products did not carry advertising, and Apple's business model did not depend on selling information about users.

**Gap note:** Jobs died in 2011, before the full emergence of algorithmic surveillance capitalism, Facebook's data practices scandal (2018), and the contemporary privacy-as-product-feature debate. His views on these developments can only be inferred from the principles he articulated, not from direct statements.

## Adobe Flash as a Safety Argument

In "Thoughts on Flash" (April 2010), Jobs wove safety arguments into his technical and strategic case. He noted that "Flash is the number one reason Macs crash" (citing data from the time) and that Flash had "security holes." He argued that allowing Flash on iOS would mean Apple "can not be responsible for the security of the device" — because a third-party runtime could expose users to vulnerabilities Apple could not audit or patch.

Critics noted that this argument had a convenient alignment with Apple's commercial interest in preventing Flash from routing users away from the App Store. Both things can be true: the Flash security concerns were real (Adobe itself acknowledged chronic security vulnerabilities in Flash, and the product was eventually discontinued partly for this reason), and Apple's business interest in blocking it was also real.

## The Responsibility of Scale

Jobs did not often speak about the ethical responsibilities that came with making products used by hundreds of millions of people. But the architecture of his decisions implies a view: he believed that the maker of a tool bears some responsibility for how that tool is used, and that designing a tool to be used only in certain ways — through App Store review, through the choice not to support Flash, through the deliberate constraints of iOS — was a legitimate exercise of that responsibility.

This is most clearly visible in his rejection of the idea that Apple should be neutral between uses of its platform. The App Store actively rejected categories of content he considered harmful or offensive. He justified this in a September 2010 email to a user: "We do believe we have a moral responsibility to keep porn off the iPhone." This was a paternalistic position, and it was genuinely Jobs' position — not a PR stance.

The counterargument — that a platform serving hundreds of millions of users has a responsibility to be neutral, and that Apple's curation power is itself a form of unaccountable authority — was not a view Jobs found compelling. He trusted Apple's judgment more than he trusted the market's.
