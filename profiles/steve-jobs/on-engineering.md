# Steve Jobs — On Engineering

## Real Artists Ship

Jobs' most direct engineering principle is the most demanding: a product that is not shipped does not exist. "Real artists ship" — the phrase he used repeatedly inside Apple — was not permission to ship sloppy work. It was a rejection of perfectionism as an excuse for indefinite delay. The engineering discipline is knowing when something is good enough to put in front of users and having the courage to make that call.

The corollary Jobs enforced was equally demanding: what ships must be excellent. He famously told the original Mac team that artists sign their work. The Mac's case was designed with the team's signatures moulded inside, invisible to users. The standard of quality applied to things no user would ever see.

## Engineering as Expression of Vision, Not Feature Specification

Jobs treated engineering as the expression of a product vision, not the execution of a feature list. The iPod's engineering was not "make an MP3 player" — it was "put 1,000 songs in your pocket." The constraint was the vision: every engineering decision was evaluated against whether it served that statement. Features that did not serve it were cut regardless of whether they were technically achievable.

The engineering implication: before beginning implementation, the team must agree on the one sentence that describes what the product does. Engineering decisions that cannot be justified by reference to that sentence are features the product does not need.

## No to A/B Testing as Engineering Philosophy

Jobs was explicitly opposed to using A/B testing to make product decisions. His position: if you have to A/B test, you do not have a vision. A company with a clear product philosophy knows what it is trying to achieve and can evaluate decisions against that philosophy. A company that A/B tests every button position has outsourced its product judgment to statistics.

The engineering implication: A/B testing is a signal that the product team lacks conviction. Use it sparingly and never as a substitute for design judgment.

## Focus on the Critical Path

Jobs' engineering management style was to identify the critical path — the features and capabilities that determined whether the product would succeed — and ensure they received disproportionate engineering attention. Non-critical-path features were cut, delayed, or given minimal resources. The original iPhone shipped without copy/paste and without the App Store because those were not on the critical path to the core experience.

The engineering discipline: not all features are equal. Identify the ones that define the product and protect the engineering time required to make them exceptional.

## Insanely Great as an Engineering Standard

"Insanely great" was Jobs' standard for engineering quality. The phrase sounds hyperbolic; in practice it meant: have you gone one more iteration beyond the obvious solution? Have you asked whether there is a better way? Have you tested the experience from the user's perspective rather than the engineer's?

His famous example: when the Mac's trash can was first implemented, the team thought it was finished. Jobs used it for a day and came back with a list of ways it still felt wrong. The final version took several more weeks and felt qualitatively different. That difference — between "works" and "insanely great" — is the engineering investment he demanded.
