# Steve Jobs — On Product

## Product Philosophy: Start with the Emotion

Jobs did not build products by assembling features. He started with an emotional destination — what should a person feel when they use this? — and worked backward to engineering. The canonical statement of this came from the iPod's development: Jobs wanted a music player that put "1,000 songs in your pocket." Not 1,000 songs in a device with a 5GB hard drive and a click interface. The feeling first. The engineering to support it second.

This approach produced products that felt inevitable rather than assembled. When Jobs introduced the iPhone in January 2007, he described it as "an iPod, a phone, and an Internet communicator" — three products in one. The crowd laughed because they assumed he was joking. He was not. He had synthesized the emotional logic of what people actually wanted their phones to be, rather than extrapolating incrementally from what phones already were.

## The Annual Roadmap as a Portfolio Decision

Jobs treated Apple's product portfolio as a deliberate narrative rather than an accumulation of decisions. He held annual off-site meetings with his top 100 people where the central question was: what are the ten most important things Apple should do? He would then make the group cross off the bottom seven. The remaining three became the focus of the coming year.

This discipline meant that Apple's product decisions were not driven by opportunity (we could make this) or customer request (people are asking for this) but by portfolio coherence (does this belong in the story we are telling?). The Mac, the iPod, the iPhone, and the iPad each represented a deliberate expansion of that story — personal computing, music in your pocket, the internet in your pocket, a post-PC device for consumption — rather than a random set of bets.

## A/B Is for Losers

Jobs held a famous contempt for A/B testing, focus groups, and market research as inputs to product decisions. He did not believe that showing users two versions of a product and measuring which one they preferred produced meaningful guidance, because users could only evaluate things that already existed within their frame of reference. Testing a better horse does not tell you whether people want a car.

This was not anti-data arrogance — it was a coherent position about what kind of product decisions data can inform. Jobs was willing to use data on pricing, retail traffic, component quality, and operational efficiency. What he was not willing to do was let data substitute for taste and conviction in product design. He stated this directly in a BusinessWeek interview in May 1998: "But in the end, for something this complicated, it's really hard to design products by focus groups. A lot of times, people don't know what they want until you show it to them."

## Killing Products Before They're Ready (and After)

Jobs cancelled products that were nearly finished. He killed the Newton (a product he had not been responsible for building) immediately upon returning to Apple in 1997. He cancelled the first version of the Final Cut Pro before shipping it and had it completely rebuilt. He scrapped the original user interface design for Mac OS X after seeing a demo he considered inadequate.

The willingness to kill near-finished products is one of the most operationally difficult expressions of product discipline. Most organizations cannot do it because the sunk cost psychological pressure is overwhelming and the political capital required to cancel something five hundred people have spent two years building is enormous. Jobs had enough authority and enough conviction to do it routinely. The result was that Apple shipped fewer products but almost never shipped something embarrassingly bad.

## The "One More Thing" Strategy

Jobs developed a presentation practice of concluding major product announcements with "And one more thing..." — which signaled that the most important announcement had been saved for last. The technique exploited recency bias: the final item in any list is the one most likely to be remembered. The "one more thing" was usually the product Jobs was most excited about, and reserving it for last ensured it received undivided attention after the audience had already been warmed up.

The "one more thing" was also a statement about Apple's pace of innovation: there was always something more. The company was never done. This contrasted with competitors who held single-product launch events with a single focal point. Apple events had a cadence and a narrative, with the headline product arriving after context had been established.

## The Mac / iPod / iPhone / iPad as a Portfolio Narrative

Looking at Jobs' product decisions over the last fifteen years of his life, a coherent sequence emerges:

- **Mac (1984):** Personal computing becomes human-centered. Graphical interface, mouse, desktop publishing.
- **iMac (1998):** Computing becomes beautiful. Color, form, consumer accessibility.
- **iPod (2001):** A thousand songs in your pocket. Computing enters daily life as an intimate companion.
- **iTunes Store (2003):** Distribution transformed. Legal digital music at 99 cents a song.
- **iPhone (2007):** The internet in your pocket. Touch computing at scale, no keyboard, one device.
- **App Store (2008):** A new software economy. Millions of developers, billions of downloads.
- **iPad (2010):** The post-PC era. Computing without the computer's baggage.

Each product in this sequence was not merely a new product — it was an argument about what computing was becoming and where Apple stood in relation to that trajectory. Jobs called the iPad the "most important thing" he had ever done. The trajectory shows why: it represented the culmination of his conviction that computing should be intimate, invisible, and insanely great.

## "Real Artists Ship"

At a Macintosh team retreat in January 1983 in Carmel, California, Jobs told his team: "Real artists ship." The documented source is Andy Hertzfeld's account on folklore.org and Frank Rose's 1989 book *West of Eden*, which corroborates the story.

The phrase expressed a belief Jobs held about the difference between people who think about making things and people who actually make them: the latter have to accept imperfection as the price of completion. The Mac was not everything Jobs wanted it to be when it shipped in January 1984. It shipped anyway. The product that exists in the world is the only one that matters.
