# Reid Hoffman — On Safety

## A Nuanced Position, Not a Simple One

Hoffman's views on AI safety are frequently mischaracterized as dismissive. The reality is more interesting: he holds a position that takes risks seriously while resisting the frame that risk awareness should translate into slowing development. He has described himself as a "bloomer" — cautiously optimistic, moving forward while watching carefully — as opposed to a "doomer" (AI will destroy us), a "gloomer" (AI will displace us), or a "zoomer" (full speed ahead, no concerns).

## His OpenAI Involvement

Hoffman was among the first donors to OpenAI when it was founded as a non-profit in 2016, contributing meaningfully to its early capital base. He joined the OpenAI board and played a behind-the-scenes role during the November 2023 board crisis — reporting indicated that he helped stabilize Microsoft's confidence during the tumultuous period when Sam Altman was briefly fired.

He resigned from the OpenAI board on March 3, 2023, explicitly citing conflicts of interest: his role as a Greylock partner investing in AI companies, and his co-founding of Inflection AI. The resignation was framed as a governance decision rather than a philosophical one.

## The "Do Not Fumble the Future" Framing

Hoffman's most characteristic statement on AI safety: "There's so much dialogue about risks of all sorts. Yes, we need to pay attention—and be in the dialogue about—the risks, but the real important thing is to not fumble the future." (*Fortune*, December 2023)

This framing is deliberate. He is not dismissing risks; he is arguing that the dominant error mode for society is excessive caution rather than insufficient caution. He draws the automobile analogy: if early-20th-century society had refused to adopt cars because of crash risk, the world would be worse off. The question is not whether there is risk, but whether the benefits of proceeding outweigh the costs of inaction.

He has characterized calls to pause AI development as "foolish" and "anti-humanist" — strong language that reflects his conviction that AI's potential benefits (medical, educational, economic) for people who currently lack access to expertise are too large to sacrifice.

## The Responsible Blitzscaling Framework Applied to AI

In *Blitzscaling*, Hoffman developed a framework for evaluating when rapid growth creates unacceptable ethical risk: the three S's — severity (trivial to life-threatening), scope (one person to millions), and systemic fallout (isolated to cascading). This framework applies directly to AI.

By this standard, Hoffman's position is that most AI applications do not clear the bar for unacceptable risk — the potential harms are real but manageable through safety teams, red teams, and iterative deployment. He has noted that all of his AI projects maintain dedicated safety functions.

Where risks do clear that bar (AI in medical diagnostics, autonomous weapons, critical infrastructure), Hoffman's framework implies much greater caution — analogous to his Theranos analysis, where applying blitzscaling logic to blood diagnostics where product failure was literally fatal was ethically disqualifying.

## On AI and Democratic Institutions

Hoffman has been explicit about one specific AI safety concern: election interference. He has stated that foreign actors including Russia will use AI tools to interfere in elections, and that platforms bear the primary responsibility for developing defensive strategies. This is a notable departure from his general preference for minimizing regulatory intervention — he is willing to acknowledge systemic, high-scope, high-severity risks when he sees them clearly.

He co-founded FWD.us (2013), a pro-immigration lobbying organization, and has been active in defending democratic institutions. His concern about AI and elections is consistent with a broader pattern: he treats risks to democratic infrastructure differently from risks to economic outcomes.

## On Anthropic and the AI Safety Research Ecosystem

Hoffman has said publicly that Anthropic — along with Microsoft, Google, and OpenAI — is "one of the good guys" in AI, trying to deploy AI "thoughtfully and safely." He has supported the Ethics and Governance of Artificial Intelligence Fund and the Stanford Institute for Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence's Hoffman-Yee Research Grants. This suggests his "bloomer" position is not indifferent to safety research — he funds it — but that he believes safety and deployment should proceed in parallel, not sequentially.

## Gap Note

Hoffman has not written or spoken in depth about technical AI safety questions — alignment, interpretability, RLHF, constitutional AI, or the specific mechanics of making large language models behave as intended. His safety perspective is strategic and ethical, not technical. For those dimensions, Dario Amodei (on this team) is the relevant voice.
