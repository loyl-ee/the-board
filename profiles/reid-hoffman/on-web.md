# Reid Hoffman — On the Web

## The Web as Professional Infrastructure

Hoffman built LinkedIn as a web product at a specific historical moment — 2003, during the early maturation of web-as-platform, post-dot-com-bust. His view of the web was never about novelty or aesthetics: the web was a connective tissue for professional relationships that had previously been constrained by geography, time zones, and the limits of physical networks.

LinkedIn's design as a web product reflected this philosophy. Profiles were public and Google-indexable from the beginning — a deliberate decision that made LinkedIn part of the web's information layer, not a walled garden. A user's LinkedIn profile would surface in Google searches for their name, creating inbound traffic that grew the network without paid acquisition. The web's open architecture was a distribution mechanism, not just a deployment target.

## Professional Identity on the Open Web

Hoffman's original vision for LinkedIn was that professional identity should be portable, persistent, and open. Before LinkedIn, professional reputation lived in siloed contexts — the company intranet, the recruiter's Rolodex, the conference attendee list. LinkedIn moved professional identity onto the open web, making it searchable, referenceable, and network-aware.

This was an architectural choice about the relationship between professional networks and the broader web. LinkedIn profiles participate in the web's link graph. They have PageRank. They show up in searches. This was not accidental — it was a strategic decision to use the web's existing infrastructure (search engines, link authority, email) as a distribution and growth layer.

## AI as the New Web Layer

Hoffman's most important recent position on the web is that AI represents the next connective layer — not just a feature set, but a new infrastructure for how information is discovered, organized, and delivered. He was among the first donors to OpenAI in 2016 and joined its board, resigning in March 2023 due to conflicts of interest with his AI investments at Greylock and his co-founding of Inflection AI.

His investment posture at Greylock reflects this view: as of May 2023, Greylock had committed capital to at least 37 AI-focused companies. He describes AI as "the cognitive industrial revolution" — a transformation as significant as the internet itself.

Hoffman has written on LinkedIn: "AI, like most transformative technologies, grows gradually, then arrives all at once." His 2023 book *Impromptu* (written collaboratively with GPT-4) and his 2025 book *Superagency* both argue that AI extends rather than replaces human agency on the web — that the relevant question is not human vs. AI but human + AI vs. human alone.

## From OpenAI to Inflection to Manas

Hoffman's web-layer investments trace an arc:
- **OpenAI (2016):** Early donor and board member; AI safety and general capability research
- **Inflection AI (2022):** Co-founded with Mustafa Suleyman; focused on personal AI; Microsoft licensed its technology in 2024 for approximately $650 million
- **Manas AI (2025):** Co-founded with cancer researcher Siddhartha Mukherjee; AI-enabled drug discovery with $25 million initial funding

Each of these represents Hoffman's view that AI will be embedded in professional and scientific workflows the same way the web eventually was: invisibly, pervasively, and with network effects of its own.

## Gap Note

Hoffman does not speak substantively about web technologies, frontend architecture, progressive web apps, web standards, or browser APIs. His "on the web" perspective is strategic and infrastructural, not technical. For detailed thinking about the web as a platform for software and experience, this team's better sources are Brent Simmons, Simon Willison, and DHH.
