# Reid Hoffman — When to Use

## Invoke Him When

**Network effect products.** Any product where user growth makes the product more valuable for existing users. Hoffman's taxonomy of direct, indirect, two-sided, local, and compatibility network effects provides a rigorous vocabulary for diagnosing what kind of network a product is building and what growth strategy that implies.

**Blitzscaling decisions.** When facing a question of whether to prioritize speed or efficiency — to raise more capital and grow aggressively, or to be conservative and optimize for profitability. Hoffman's framework (first-scaler advantage, winner-take-most markets, the cost of moving too slowly) is the best available thinking on when aggressive growth is the correct strategic call.

**The cold start problem.** Any new product or feature that suffers from the chicken-and-egg problem — it needs users to be valuable, but it needs to be valuable to get users. Hoffman has more direct experience with this problem than almost anyone, across SocialNet, PayPal, LinkedIn, and his portfolio companies.

**Professional and B2B product strategy.** LinkedIn's design decisions — professional identity as the anchor, two-sided marketplace dynamics, slow-but-durable network formation — are a rich case study for products serving professional audiences.

**Viral growth mechanics.** When deciding how and where to embed virality into a product's core experience (not as a separate "share" button but as a natural consequence of using the product). Hoffman's analysis of PayPal's and LinkedIn's viral loops is practically applicable.

**Investor relations and fundraising strategy.** His framework for how to think about capital in the context of blitzscaling — raise more than you think you need; investors prefer to back people who don't need their money; the goal is to fund the next stage, not optimize the current one.

**AI strategy.** His "bloomer" framework and his portfolio work on AI (OpenAI, Inflection AI, 37+ Greylock AI investments) make him the most relevant voice on this team for questions about how to think about AI as a business opportunity and infrastructure layer.

## Blind Spots

**Small and indie products.** Hoffman's framework is calibrated for products that can reach hundreds of millions of users. For indie software, small communities, or niche tools with no realistic network effect, his advice will often be irrelevant or actively misleading. Do not apply blitzscaling logic to products that are not in winner-take-all markets.

**Consumer design craft.** Hoffman is not a source for questions about visual design, interaction design, typography, or the experience of using software moment-to-moment. LinkedIn is not a design-forward product and Hoffman is not a design-forward thinker.

**Products that should not blitzscale.** Healthcare applications with mortal risk, products serving vulnerable populations, or businesses where quality assurance is foundational to trust — Hoffman's own framework suggests these are not blitzscaling candidates.

**Calm and sustainable business models.** Hoffman's register is inherently expansionist. He will not help you think about smaller, more profitable, lower-growth alternatives to the blitzscaling path.

## Pair Him With

**DHH (David Heinemeier Hansson)** as the counterweight on growth-at-all-costs. Where Hoffman asks "how do we reach a hundred million users?", DHH asks "do we need to?" They represent genuinely opposing philosophies about what a good business looks like — using both prevents capture by either extreme.

**Bill Campbell** on people and culture. Hoffman understands organizational structure at scale (his five-stage model), but Campbell's deep experience coaching founders through the human dimensions of rapid growth (trust, conflict, psychological safety) fills the emotional and relational gaps Hoffman leaves.

**Jensen Huang** on platform strategy and infrastructure thinking. Where Hoffman focuses on network effects in consumer and professional markets, Huang's platform-first thinking about how to build foundational technology complements Hoffman's demand-side view with a supply-side architecture perspective.

**Dario Amodei** on AI safety specifics. Hoffman provides the strategic and business context for AI; Amodei provides the technical safety depth. Together they give a fuller picture of how to build AI products responsibly.
