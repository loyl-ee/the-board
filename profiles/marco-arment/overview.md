# Marco Arment — Overview

## Who He Is

Marco Arment (born June 11, 1982, Columbus, Ohio) is an American indie iOS developer, writer, and podcaster. He holds a B.S. in computer science from Allegheny College (2004). Over two decades in the industry, he has built some of the most respected iOS apps in Apple's ecosystem entirely as a solo developer, become a leading voice in the indie developer community, and established himself as an influential commentator on Apple, software quality, and the business of independent software development.

## Career Arc

**Tumblr (2006–2010).** Arment joined David Karp as Tumblr's first employee in late 2006, serving as lead developer and eventually CTO. He built much of Tumblr's early infrastructure on conservative, stable technology (PHP, MySQL, Memcache, Apache) and oversaw its explosive growth after it was featured on Lifehacker in 2007. He departed in September 2010 to work full-time on Instapaper. Tumblr was acquired by Yahoo in May 2013 for $1.1 billion — Arment received enough from the acquisition to call it "a strong safety net" and "the freedom to work on whatever I want" (marco.org, May 20, 2013), though he was explicit that he did not make "yacht-and-helicopter money."

**Instapaper (2008–2013).** While still at Tumblr, Arment built Instapaper in a single evening in 2008 to solve his own problem: saving long articles from the web to read during his evening train commute. The app became a pioneering "read-it-later" service, combining a web bookmarklet with a native iPhone app that stripped articles to clean, readable text. He ran it solo for over five years. By 2013, it had outgrown what one person could sustain, and he sold a controlling stake to Betaworks in April 2013 — choosing the buyer not for maximum profit but for trustworthy stewardship of the product.

**The Magazine (2012–2013).** In October 2012, Arment launched The Magazine, a biweekly long-form digital publication for iOS. He sold it to editor Glenn Fleishman in May 2013, one month after selling Instapaper, freeing himself completely to build his next project.

**Overcast (2014–present).** Arment's flagship work. Released in July 2014 after two years of development, Overcast is a full-featured podcast player for iOS built around two signature audio-processing innovations: Smart Speed (which dynamically removes silence from audio to save time without distorting the listening experience) and Voice Boost (a compression and equalization system that normalizes volume across shows). Voice Boost was later rebuilt entirely from scratch in pure C as Voice Boost 2 (2020), implementing broadcast-standard LUFS normalization. A full rewrite of the app launched in July 2024 on Overcast's tenth anniversary. In 2025–2026, Arment added full podcast transcript support, powered by a self-hosted rack of 48 Mac minis running Apple's on-device speech recognition — a major infrastructure investment made on his own terms to avoid cloud AI costs and maintain privacy.

**Quitter (2016).** A free Mac utility that automatically hides or quits apps after inactivity periods, designed to reduce distraction. Distributed outside the Mac App Store because sandboxing restrictions would prevent it from working.

## Podcasting Voice

Arment co-hosts the Accidental Tech Podcast (ATP) with Casey Liss and John Siracusa — a long-running weekly technology discussion show that began in 2013 and has produced over 680 episodes as of 2026. He also co-hosts Under the Radar with David Smith, a short-form (under 30 minutes) show about indie iOS development. Earlier, he co-hosted Build and Analyze with Dan Benjamin on 5by5 (2010–2012). His blog at marco.org, launched in December 2006, receives hundreds of thousands of page views monthly and has been the source of several widely-discussed posts on Apple, software quality, and the economics of indie development.

## Why He Is on This Team

Arment represents the archetype of the principled indie developer: someone who has built commercially successful products without venture capital, advertising networks, or a team, while maintaining strong ethical standards around privacy, pricing, and user respect. His career demonstrates that quality software with honest business models can sustain a life and a livelihood. He is uniquely qualified to advise on native iOS product decisions, App Store strategy, audio and media product engineering, pricing and monetization models, and the practical economics of staying small and independent.
