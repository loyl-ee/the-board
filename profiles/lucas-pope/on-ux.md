# Lucas Pope — On UX

## UX in Service of Experience, Not Comfort

Lucas Pope's UX philosophy departs radically from the conventional goal of reducing friction and making interactions feel effortless. In his work, friction, stress, and discomfort are frequently the designed experience — and UX decisions exist to produce those states in the player, not to eliminate them. The question he asks is not "how do we make this easier?" but "what experience are we trying to create, and what UX conditions produce it?"

This is not an argument against usability — Pope's games are learnable and internally consistent. It is an argument that usability is subordinate to purpose. A UX that makes a bureaucratic simulation feel effortless would undermine the game's entire argument.

## Papers, Please: Stress as UX Choice

The inspector's desk in *Papers, Please* is, from a conventional UX perspective, a poorly designed workspace. Documents pile up. Rules proliferate. Discrepancies are easy to miss. The clock exerts constant pressure. Players make errors. These are not bugs — they are the designed conditions.

Pope understood that the experience of playing an immigration inspector requires the player to feel the cognitive load and time pressure that an actual inspector would feel. If the interface were optimized to make document verification fast and error-free, the game's moral argument would collapse. The stress is the point: it is what makes the player understand, at a visceral level, how ordinary people make bad decisions under institutional pressure.

He has noted that his goal was to "put the player in exactly the situation you want to share," and the UX of *Papers, Please* is a direct implementation of that goal. The interface creates the conditions of complicity.

At the same time, the UX is consistent. Rules are introduced incrementally. New document types appear one at a time. The game never punishes the player for not knowing something they haven't been told. The friction is calibrated — it is the right amount of difficulty to produce the intended experience without becoming random or unfair.

## Return of the Obra Dinn: The Book as UX System

*Return of the Obra Dinn*'s primary UX innovation is the logbook — a physical object within the game's fiction that functions as both the progress tracker and the primary interaction surface for recording deductions. Players write causes of death and assign identities in the book, and confirmed identifications are marked with a stamp.

The logbook solves a significant UX challenge: how do you manage 60 pieces of information across a non-linear investigation without creating cognitive overload? Pope's solution was to make the information management diegetic — the book exists in the game's world, carried by the player character, and recording in it is narratively motivated. This prevents the interface from feeling like a menu system imposed on top of the game.

The confirmation mechanic — correct fates confirmed in groups of three — is a considered UX decision that shapes how players engage with the puzzle. It prevents systematic single-identity guessing and rewards genuine deductive reasoning. It also provides progress feedback without revealing which specific deductions were correct, preserving the investigative tension.

Pope has said that "the book ended up being the perfect tool for setting down both the sequence of events when they're experienced out of order, and a logical rationale for inputting everyone's fate." The UX serves both the narrative (a ship's insurance investigation) and the gameplay (deductive logic) simultaneously.

## Designing for a Specific Emotional Journey

Pope's UX decisions are downstream of a clear answer to the question: what should the player feel, moment to moment, as they move through this experience? For *Papers, Please*, the answer includes stress, moral uncertainty, and the uncomfortable familiarity of routine compliance. For *Obra Dinn*, it includes the slow accumulation of understanding, the satisfaction of correct deductions, and the uncanny weight of being surrounded by the dead.

Both games produce what Pope describes as an environment of calibrated overwhelm that gives way to engagement: overloading the player initially, then providing space for the experience to become habitual, then trusting the player's own curiosity and compulsion to sustain the journey. The UX design supports that arc — it is never accidental.
