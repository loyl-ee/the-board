# Lucas Pope — On Safety

## Ethics by Design: Moral Complexity as the Point

Lucas Pope is one of the few game designers who has made ethical complexity — not ethical simplicity — the central design principle of his major works. *Papers, Please* does not present a morally clean experience. It asks the player to perform acts of bureaucratic cruelty, deny entry to desperate people, and benefit financially from their compliance with an authoritarian system. It does not offer an easy escape route, and it does not reward the player for choosing to resist the rules.

This is not carelessness — it is the design. Pope has said he "made a concerted effort to keep things vague and non-judgemental throughout the game" and that he wanted to "include elements that could apply to broad situations where it's not always a simple good/evil dynamic." The game refuses to tell the player what to think. It puts them in the conditions that produce moral compromise and leaves them to reckon with their own choices.

The ethical argument of *Papers, Please* is enacted through its mechanics: the player is not reading about ordinary people becoming instruments of oppression — they are experiencing the economic conditions that make such complicity feel rational. This is a deliberate and considered choice about how games can communicate something that a lecture or documentary cannot.

## Responsibility Toward the Player

Pope's design approach implies a responsibility toward the player that goes beyond not causing harm in the trivial sense. He is creating experiences that carry genuine emotional and moral weight — experiences that, at their best, change how players perceive situations analogous to those in the game. He has described hoping players "come away with a little more balance in how they perceive the intentions of others."

This is an acceptance of responsibility: if you design a game that is intended to create empathy or change perspective, you are accountable for whether it achieves that goal and whether the conditions it creates are proportionate to the experience. Pope has avoided gratuitous darkness — *Papers, Please* does not dwell in violence or trauma for shock value, even as it depicts a system that perpetrates both. The uncomfortable material serves the argument.

## On the Game's Ongoing Relevance

Pope has spoken publicly about the unsettling experience of watching *Papers, Please* become increasingly relevant to real-world border control politics in the decade since its release. In a 2025 interview with PC Gamer, he said: "It blows my mind that it's only becoming more and more relevant over time. Honestly, it's a tragedy." He added: "You want your work to be relevant, but at the same time, wow, I really wish it was not that fucking relevant."

This reflects a designer who understands the relationship between fictional systems and real ones, and who feels the weight of having created something that documents a kind of moral architecture that continues to exist and expand in the world.

## On AI and Intellectual Property (2026)

As of April 2026, Pope has stated he no longer discusses unreleased projects in public, citing concerns that his ideas would be "slurped up by AI" or copied before they reach development. This represents a safety concern of a different order — the integrity of creative work in an environment where ideas can be scraped, synthesized, and replicated before the original work is completed. This is an honest gap in the profile; Pope has not published an extended statement of his views on AI and games, only this protective behavioral change.

## Honest Gap Note

Pope has not published extensive statements on content rating systems, age-appropriate design, or the specific mechanics of violence in games beyond the systemic, bureaucratic violence of *Papers, Please*. His games avoid graphic violence as traditionally defined — there are deaths in *Obra Dinn*, but they are presented through stylized 1-bit rendering that frames them as historical documents rather than visceral events. His ethical design thinking is primarily about moral complexity and systemic cruelty, not about content moderation in the conventional sense.
