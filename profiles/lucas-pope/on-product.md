# Lucas Pope — On Product

## Each Game as a Complete Artistic Statement

Lucas Pope does not release features, updates to an existing product, or iterations on a proven formula. Each game he makes is a complete, self-contained artistic statement — defined by a single core mechanic, a single emotional or moral purpose, and a visual language specifically suited to that purpose. He has been explicit about his disinterest in sequels: "It is definitely intentional. It's just that a sequel doesn't interest me as much." A sequel would mean returning to solved problems. Pope is interested in new problems.

This means his product philosophy is organized around the question of what, specifically, this thing is trying to communicate — and whether every element of the design serves that communication. Anything that does not serve it is a candidate for removal.

## Papers, Please: All Mechanics Serving One Argument

*Papers, Please* is the clearest example of Pope's product philosophy at work. The game's central argument is that ordinary people become instruments of authoritarian oppression not through malice but through economic compliance — through the fact that their family's survival depends on their doing their job. Every design decision in the game exists to make the player feel this, not understand it abstractly.

The document-checking mechanic creates cognitive load and time pressure. Players must cross-reference passports, entry permits, work visas, and an ever-growing rulebook while the queue of applicants grows longer. Income depends on throughput. Thoroughness costs time. Cutting corners causes errors. Errors cost money. And money is what feeds the player's family at the end of each in-game day. The player is not told they are complicit — they experience the conditions that produce complicity.

Pope deliberately kept the fiction sparse and vague — the family's faces are barely visible, their names mostly unspecified — so that players could project their own emotional reality onto the situation. He has said: "My goal was to leave the game family largely unspecified so the player could more easily project their own family onto them." The product succeeds when the player's own imagination closes the gap.

The game also includes moral exits — moments where the player can choose to help someone despite the rules, at financial cost. These are not presented as correct choices. The game offers 20 different endings, and most of them are forms of failure. The product communicates moral complexity not by arguing for a position but by refusing to resolve the tension it creates.

## Return of the Obra Dinn: The Deductive Logic System

*Return of the Obra Dinn* presented a different product challenge: how do you design a mystery game where the mystery is genuinely solvable through in-game evidence, for 60 characters, without it becoming either trivially easy or impossibly hard?

Pope's solution was a deductive logic matrix. Players record, for each of 60 crew members, their fate (how they died) and the identity of the person or creature responsible. The book — a central object in the game's fiction — serves as the progress-tracking interface. Correct identifications are confirmed in groups of three, which Pope has explained prevents players from guessing one identity at a time and nudges them toward genuine deductive reasoning.

He has described the core design insight as realizing "the real fun part would be figuring out who people are" — the shift from witnessing deaths to identifying the victims required creating a system of environmental clues: names on cargo, accents in speech, rank insignia on uniforms, relationships between characters. The product's complexity is in the information architecture, not in the difficulty of the controls.

Pope's goal was calibrated overwhelm: "My goal for Obra Dinn was to overwhelm the player initially, then give them time to get comfortable with the core loop before relying on their internal compulsions to motivate them." He trusted that once players established a foothold in the mystery, their natural desire to complete it would sustain them without artificial incentive systems.

## Choosing Mechanics for Expressive Power

In Pope's product thinking, the question is never "is this mechanic fun in isolation?" but rather "does this mechanic produce the experience I am trying to create?" He has described the body scanner in *Papers, Please* — which strips travelers of privacy and dignity to detect concealed threats — as a mechanic that required narrative justification: "You can't just implement a scanner that strips people naked for no reason." The mechanic is disturbing on purpose; the discomfort is the point.

This is what distinguishes his product philosophy from conventional game design, where mechanics are evaluated primarily on engagement metrics or player satisfaction. For Pope, a mechanic that makes the player uncomfortable, stressed, or morally uncertain may be exactly the right mechanic — if that discomfort is the intended experience.

## Mars After Midnight: Scope as Intentional Choice

*Mars After Midnight* (2024), released exclusively for the Playdate handheld, represents a deliberate reduction in scope. Pope designed it for his children and for a specific platform with a tiny screen and a physical crank. He has said: "I feel okay not going super deep with this one." The product statement here is about matching ambition to purpose — not every product needs to carry the weight of a decade's work. A smaller platform with a specific audience warranted a lighter, warmer product that still reflected his signature mechanic of judging who gets to enter a space.
