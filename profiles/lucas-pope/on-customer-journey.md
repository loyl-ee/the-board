# Lucas Pope — On Customer Journey

## The Player as Active Moral Agent

Lucas Pope designs for players who are willing to be put in uncomfortable positions. In *Papers, Please*, the player is not a hero, a villain, or a spectator — they are a participant in a system they did not design, making small choices that accumulate into a moral record. The player's journey through the game is a journey of increasing complicity, punctuated by moments where they can choose to resist at personal cost.

Pope has described his respect for player imagination: "I have a lot of faith in the player's imaginations." He does not over-explain the emotional stakes of his games. He creates the conditions and trusts that players will fill in the meaning. The family at home — whom the player never fully sees — becomes real through the player's own projection, not through cinematic cutscenes.

This design approach assumes an engaged, thoughtful player. Pope is not designing for players who want to be entertained passively or rewarded frequently. He is designing for players who are willing to sit with ambiguity, who do not need to be told what the game thinks they should feel, and who are capable of self-reflection about the choices they make in a fictional context.

## The Player as Detective

In *Return of the Obra Dinn*, the player's journey is investigative. They move through frozen moments of death, accumulating evidence, forming hypotheses, testing them against new information. The game trusts that players will derive satisfaction from correct deductions without requiring the game to celebrate those deductions effusively.

Pope has explained that the real engagement in the game is not watching deaths — it is figuring out who is dying. "I realized the real fun part would be figuring out who people are." The player's journey is one of progressive clarity emerging from initial confusion — from 60 unknown names on a roster to a completed account of every fate.

This requires a player who is willing to take notes, revisit scenes, and tolerate uncertainty across multiple sessions. Pope designed explicitly for this kind of player: methodical, curious, and willing to engage with a problem that does not have a single correct path to resolution.

## Designing for a Specific Type of Engaged Player

Pope has not described his target audience in demographic terms — he describes them in terms of disposition. He makes games for players who want to experience something specific, not players who want to maximize entertainment per hour. He has acknowledged that this limits his audience, and has accepted that limitation as the price of making something with genuine artistic intent.

His games find their audiences through word of mouth, critical attention, and the fact that nothing else provides the same experience. Players who want what *Papers, Please* offers have no substitute. The customer journey, for those players, is one of deep engagement followed by the lasting impression of having done something — not just played something.

Pope has said that he hopes players of *Papers, Please* "come away with a little more balance in how they perceive the intentions of others" — a measure of success that has nothing to do with time-in-game, session frequency, or purchase conversion. The customer journey he is designing for ends with a changed perspective.
