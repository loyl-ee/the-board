# Lucas Pope — Philosophy

## Games as Vehicles for Ideas No Other Medium Can Carry

Lucas Pope's foundational belief is that games, because they are interactive, have a unique capacity to communicate ideas that other media cannot. In a 2013 interview with Reason magazine, he articulated this directly: "Don't tell or show something when you can make the player do it themselves instead. For me, this is the power that interactive games have over other media. You can put the player in exactly the situation you want to share and their connection with the message will be more powerful."

This is not a claim about games being superior to film or literature in general — it is a claim about a specific capability. When a player stamps a document, they perform the act of complicity. When a player fills out a logbook of causes of death, they build understanding through doing. The insight Pope extracts from the interactive form is that *participation creates a different quality of understanding than observation*. He has said: "Because games are interactive, I think the potential to affect others will be much stronger with games than with other media."

## Mechanics as Moral Argument

In Pope's design philosophy, a game's mechanic is not a delivery system for its message — it *is* the message. In *Papers, Please*, the stamp-and-inspect mechanic does not illustrate a point about bureaucratic complicity; it enacts it. The player is not reading about an immigration inspector who faces moral compromise — the player *is* that inspector, making the same calculations, facing the same impossible tradeoffs. The act of playing is the argument.

This distinguishes Pope's approach from most game design, where mechanics and theme are separate concerns that are assembled together. For Pope, they are inseparable by design. He develops games from the bottom up: "I developed the game from the bottom up. Only once I had the core mechanics of document checking working well did I start to think about a narrative." The mechanic comes first because the mechanic is the idea — the narrative exists to give the mechanic context and weight.

He has also stated that mechanics must be narratively justified to be meaningful: "You can't just implement a scanner that strips people naked for no reason." Every mechanical element requires a human logic — a reason why someone in this world would be doing this thing.

## Constraint as a Creative Tool

Pope consistently chooses constraints that other developers, especially well-resourced studios, would treat as problems to be eliminated. The 1-bit aesthetic of *Return of the Obra Dinn* was not forced on him — he chose it. *Papers, Please* uses muted, drab colors and low-resolution sprites not because he lacked the ability to do otherwise, but because those choices serve the game's emotional argument. He has explained that "the low-resolution graphics... allowed me to create visuals and animations quickly" and that muted colors helped "express a bleak mood and reduce extraneous visual clutter."

More broadly, Pope has described constraint as a source of interest: "If there's no problem, then I'm not that interested. But if there's a problem, if there's some restriction or some limitation, then I'm interested suddenly." His central design questions tend to take the form of a constraint: "Can you play a game by just playing the role of an immigration officer and checking documents?" "Can you play a modern 3D game with 1-bit graphics?" The constraint does not limit creativity — it is the creative premise.

## The One-Developer Model as a Creative Choice

Pope is a solo developer, and he frames this as a deliberate creative stance rather than a limitation or a cost-cutting measure. He has said: "It sounds bad that I have to do everything myself but it also means there's no skull barrier between the ideas." Without intermediaries between conception and execution, the idea can travel directly into the work without translation loss.

He has also explained his reluctance to hire: "I'm okay wasting my own time and energy with experimentation but would feel quite bad about inflicting that on someone else." This is not false modesty — it reflects a genuine view that experimental development, which involves long periods of uncertainty, is not a fair working environment to create for employees. The solo model is the price of creative freedom, and Pope considers it worth paying.

His wife Keiko Ishizaka serves as producer and creative collaborator, providing a crucial external perspective — but the development itself remains his alone.

## Not Making Games to Make Money

Pope is explicit that commercial goals do not drive his design decisions. *Papers, Please* was, by his own account, "intended as a small experimental project that I'd finish off before working on something more marketable." The commercial success it achieved was genuinely unexpected. The financial stability it provided allowed him to spend 4.5 years developing *Return of the Obra Dinn* without commercial pressure.

He has described the right kind of game idea as one that "keeps me awake at night." His filter is not "will this sell" but "does this idea compel me enough to spend years on it." This is not an anti-commercial position — he is aware that games need to reach people and that financial sustainability enables continued work. But the ordering is clear: the idea first, and the economics following from the idea's quality, not the other way around.

Pope has also articulated a practical theory of commercial sustainability for this approach: "if I can make the right kind of game then it's not something that needs to sell in the first 3 months only." A game with genuine artistic purpose accumulates an audience over time, through word of mouth and critical recognition, in ways that genre-product games do not.
