# Lucas Pope — On Business

## The Economics of Making Two Games in Fifteen Years

Lucas Pope's business model is, by conventional startup or studio metrics, baffling: one developer, two major releases over a decade, with development cycles of roughly 9–14 months for *Papers, Please* and 4.5 years for *Return of the Obra Dinn*. What makes this model work is that both games achieved genuine critical ubiquity, which translates into long-tail commercial sustainability rather than launch-window spikes.

*Papers, Please* sold approximately 1.8 million copies by August 2016, three years after release, and had reached 5 million copies by its 10th anniversary in August 2023. At a price point of $9.99, sustained sales over a decade produced substantial cumulative revenue. By the time Pope was deep in development of *Return of the Obra Dinn*, he was still earning meaningful income from *Papers, Please* — enough that he did not feel financial pressure during Obra Dinn's development.

*Return of the Obra Dinn* is estimated to have generated approximately $16.5 million in gross revenue through Steam, with estimated net revenue to the developer of around $4.9 million (source: Steam revenue estimators; figures are approximate). Together, these two games have established Pope's financial independence for the foreseeable future, allowing him to pursue whatever he wants next without commercial obligation.

## No Marketing Strategy, Deliberately

Pope's approach to marketing is notable for its absence of strategy. For *Return of the Obra Dinn*, he has described his approach: "I don't do the marketing or anything. I set up a booth and try to watch people play." He views event participation not as promotion but as research — observing where players get confused or disengaged.

His theory of commercial viability is genre-based rather than campaign-based: "make it different enough from other games that if you want this kind of game you pretty much just gotta buy this one game." The goal is to occupy an underserved design space so completely that the game becomes the canonical reference for that experience. Both *Papers, Please* and *Obra Dinn* achieved this — there is no meaningful competition for the specific experience either game provides.

He also explicitly rejected analytics-driven development after an earlier mobile title (*Helsing's Fire*): "I recorded a ton of analytics for everybody that played this game... and I never looked at them." His preference is to respond to player-reported problems directly, not to optimize for behavioral data.

## His Company: 3909 LLC

Pope operates through his one-person company, 3909 LLC. He has no employees beyond his wife Keiko's role as producer and creative partner. The company has no growth ambitions — it exists to enable Pope's independent development. He distributes primarily through Steam on PC and macOS, with console ports (PS4, Xbox One, Nintendo Switch) of *Return of the Obra Dinn* handled through additional publishing partnerships.

## Sustainability Assessment

Pope's model is sustainable under conditions he has consciously cultivated: radical originality (producing games for which there is no substitute), long-tail pricing (no discounting desperation), solo development (minimal overhead), and the avoidance of expensive platform dependencies. It is not a model that scales or that can be replicated without the critical recognition that makes it work. His own assessment is honest about this: "The expectations have been pretty high though, and that's definitely made me more methodical than I probably should be."

The model's weakness — extremely low output volume — is also its strength: each release is an event, covered extensively, carrying the full weight of his reputation.
