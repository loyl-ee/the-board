# Lucas Pope — On Design

## Visual Design as Argument

Lucas Pope treats visual design not as aesthetic decoration but as a component of the work's argument. Every visual choice in his games carries meaning — and that meaning is subordinate to the game's overall communicative purpose. If the game is about the grinding bleakness of bureaucratic work, the visuals should be bleak. If the game is about mystery and the horror of death preserved in frozen amber, the visuals should be stark and high-contrast.

This is not a principle about style — it is a principle about coherence. Visual design that is beautiful in a way that contradicts the game's emotional purpose would undermine the work.

## Papers, Please: Drab by Design

The visual language of *Papers, Please* is deliberately oppressive. Muted greys and browns, low-resolution sprites, minimal animation, a desk that fills the entire screen. The aesthetic communicates the setting's character before a single document is stamped. Pope has explained that he chose "muted colors and stripped details to express a bleak mood and reduce visual clutter." The spareness is not a resource constraint — it is a design decision about what the player should feel.

The inspector's booth is the entire frame. There is no world beyond the desk except a tiny window showing a grey queue of waiting travelers. The player never sees the city, the state, the larger world. This spatial constriction mirrors the character's situation: trapped in a booth, responsible for enforcing rules they did not make, seeing only the fragment of humanity that passes before them. The design enacts the theme.

The color palette also differentiates mechanically important information from background noise. Documents, stamps, and rule violations are legible because everything else is visually suppressed. Form follows function, but the function is emotional, not ergonomic.

## Return of the Obra Dinn: 1-Bit as Expressive Choice

The 1-bit monochrome aesthetic of *Return of the Obra Dinn* is Pope's most discussed design decision, and the reasoning is layered. The surface motivation is nostalgia: "I wanted to make a modern 3D game that looked like the old 1-bit games I played on my family's Macintosh Plus while growing up." But Pope was explicit that nostalgia was not sufficient justification — he actively worked to prevent the aesthetic from reading as mere retro pastiche.

He explained his rule for the visual language: "I never wanted the old-computer shit to override the old ship-shit, basically." The point was not to evoke old computers — it was to use the visual language of extreme high contrast and dithering to serve the game's atmosphere of death, mystery, and the uncanny.

The 1-bit style also has a practical expressive advantage Pope identified explicitly: "I chose the aesthetic because it stood out, but I also chose it because it's a good style if you want to obscure things, if you want to leave stuff out. The lighting is very sharp, and it leaves a lot of detail out." A mystery game that withholds information benefits from a visual language that is, by nature, reductive. The art style and the game's informational architecture work in the same direction.

He described the 1-bit rendering as the one element of the game he is "unambiguously happy with."

## Technical Mastery in Service of Expression

Pope did not choose the 1-bit style because it was easy — it was technically among the hardest things he attempted. Dithering patterns that look clean in a small window become visually disorienting when stretched to a full 27-inch screen. He spent months solving the legibility problem: "In a small window, the visuals worked well. But stretched to a full 27″ screen two feet from my face, it was exactly what I feared: a solid case for never making a full-length low-resolution 1-bit game."

His solution involved redesigning the dithering technique so patterns were anchored to the scene rather than the camera, preventing the shimmer that causes eye strain during movement. This is the kind of technical work that is invisible to players — it only exists to make the aesthetic choice sustainable over a full playthrough.

He also drew a firm line on nostalgia: he considered adding a CRT monitor filter that would make the game render as if on an old screen, but decided against it as "a nostalgia too far." The goal was to use the visual language of a past era to serve a present experience, not to simulate the past itself.

## Constraint Chosen, Not Imposed

The through-line across Pope's visual design choices is that the constraints are self-chosen and expressive rather than externally imposed. He is not working around limitations — he is selecting limitations as creative premises. The question is always whether the visual language chosen is the *right* language for the experience being created.
