# Brent Simmons — On Product

## The RSS Reader as a Product Statement

NetNewsWire is not just a utility. It is a product position. Its tagline — "More news, less junk. Faster." — encodes a set of convictions about what information consumption should look like. The app displays only articles from sources the user has explicitly chosen, in the order they were published, with no algorithmic ranking, no engagement optimization, and no attempt to keep users in the app longer than their reading takes.

Simmons has articulated this as a counterstatement to social media platforms: "NetNewsWire shows you the articles from the sites you want and only those sites." (inessential.com, "What I Do") Social media platforms profit from engagement, which means they have incentives to surface outrage and novelty. NetNewsWire has no such incentive. Its product design is, in a sense, its ethics made visible.

## Design Decisions in NetNewsWire

The three-pane layout of NetNewsWire — sidebar, article list, detail view — was a pragmatic choice Simmons made in 2002 by asking himself how he would design a Usenet newsreader. RSS was unknown to most people at the time. By grounding the interface in a familiar email-like metaphor, he reduced the learning curve to nearly nothing. This was not an ideological decision about information architecture; it was a practical one about onboarding users into a new format.

One design decision he has since reflected on with some ambivalence: that familiar email-like layout implied an inbox, and an inbox implies tasks to be completed. RSS is not a to-do list. Simmons was among the first to articulate the antidote — article age limits, which automatically expire old articles rather than letting them pile up into a source of guilt. "Your RSS reader should not be a taskmaster or a source of guilt." (inessential.com, "NetNewsWire Article Age Limits," October 13, 2018) The age limits design, introduced in NetNewsWire 5, is product thinking as user psychology.

## Open Source as Product Decision

Making NetNewsWire free and open source was a product decision as much as an ethical one. Simmons believes that transparency about development decisions — and the impossibility of revenue pressure — creates trust with users that cannot be purchased. The app's privacy policy states plainly: "We don't want any private information. We really, really don't." No tracking, no analytics, no ads, no cookies or JavaScript on the associated websites.

The open source community around NetNewsWire also functions as a product team. Contributors improve the app, catch bugs, add sync service support, and carry the project through periods when Simmons is otherwise occupied with full-time employment. This is sustainable product stewardship, not just development methodology.

## Vesper: Product Lessons from a Failure

Vesper was a beautifully designed notes app that failed because the product was built on the wrong platform first. Simmons' own diagnosis: building for iOS when the economics of that market in 2013 couldn't sustain premium paid apps was the core error. A Mac app first would have generated more revenue in the initial step and given the team a stronger financial foundation.

The product was also vulnerable to platform competition in a way that a more differentiated product might not have been. Apple's upgrade of Notes in iOS 8 effectively commoditized the product category. Vesper's differentiation — design quality, the Q Branch team's reputation — was not sufficient to overcome a free first-party alternative that had become genuinely good.

## JSON Feed: Building for the Open Web

In 2017, Simmons co-created JSON Feed with Manton Reece as a JSON-based alternative to RSS and Atom. The motivation was explicitly product-level: "developers (particularly Mac and iOS developers) are so loath to work with XML that they won't even consider building software that needs an XML parser." (inessential.com, "JSON Feed," May 17, 2017) If feeds were going to survive and attract new developers, the format needed to be friendlier. JSON Feed is a product designed to lower the barrier to entry for the next generation of feed reader developers.

## What NetNewsWire Is Not

Simmons has been equally careful about what NetNewsWire should not become. In a 2020 post, he wrote that "each new option takes NetNewsWire one step further away from the simple RSS reader that people love," requiring careful consideration about what features to add. Product discipline, for Simmons, means knowing what to refuse as much as what to build.
