# Brent Simmons — On Business

## The Hardest Product to Monetize

RSS readers are arguably the most difficult category of consumer software to build a business around. Users expect them to be free. The underlying data comes from third-party sites. The content itself is the value, not the app. Simmons built his career around exactly this product — and his history with it contains every lesson a developer needs about the economics of indie software.

NetNewsWire began as shareware ($39.95 for Pro, a free Lite version). Simmons ran it as a small husband-and-wife business at Ranchero Software and, by early accounts, made it work. He has noted that even 3% of the Mac market represents millions of potential customers — enough to sustain a small operation if the product doesn't require intensive support. The key was designing for low support overhead: make the software clear enough that users rarely need help.

## Selling to NewsGator

In October 2005, Simmons sold NetNewsWire to NewsGator. This was not a failure; it was a rational decision. NetNewsWire users needed RSS syncing across devices, NewsGator had the server infrastructure, and Simmons recognized he couldn't build that infrastructure alone. He continued as lead developer, employed by NewsGator, which is a different kind of life than indie but not a wrong one.

What followed was instructive. NewsGator eventually pivoted to enterprise software. NetNewsWire became free in 2008 as part of a promotional strategy. The consumer syncing platform was shut down. By 2011, Simmons helped arrange the sale of NetNewsWire to Black Pixel, where it received version 4.0 and iOS and Apple TV ports before going largely dormant. Simmons reflected on his NewsGator years plainly: "Everything I created while I was there is now somewhere else. Nothing of me remains there." (inessential.com, "What Happened at NewsGator," 2014) That is the risk of building inside corporate structures.

## Vesper and the iOS Business Model Problem

Q Branch — Simmons, John Gruber, and Dave Wiskus — launched Vesper in June 2013 as a $5 iPhone notes app. It was well-designed and well-received. It was not financially viable.

The core problem, as Simmons diagnosed it in his 2016 post-mortem: "if we had started with a Mac app rather than an iOS app, Vesper would have been much more successful." (inessential.com, "More Notes on Vesper," August 21, 2016) The iOS pricing environment — where users expect to pay $5 at most, and Apple upgraded Notes dramatically with iOS 8 — made it nearly impossible to sustain development on a premium-priced paid app.

He was characteristically honest about the failure. The codebase was "an iOS 6 app," lacking modern architecture. They never solved the revenue problem. And the fundamental asymmetry of the App Store — great distribution, terrible economics for premium paid apps — meant that even a beloved app from a high-profile team couldn't make the numbers work.

## Independence vs. Corporations

Simmons has worked inside large companies (NewsGator, The Omni Group, Audible) and outside them. His 2013 "Gone Indie" post captures what the inside/outside distinction costs: "It was time for me to return to my natural habitat as an indie developer." He compared himself to a dog returning to nature. The allure of independence is not just creative — it is about alignment between what you make and what you care about.

His 2024 essay "Corporations Are Not To Be Loved" (inessential.com) draws a harder line: "Corporations aren't people, and they can't love you back. You wouldn't love GE or Exxon or Comcast — and you shouldn't love Apple." He has made his peace with working inside organizations when it supports his real work, but he is clear-eyed about what corporations are and what they owe developers.

## The Free-and-Open-Source Sustainability Model

NetNewsWire since 2019 takes no money. Simmons addressed this directly in 2023: the app runs on approximately $10/month in hosting costs, leveraging free services like GitHub and Micro.blog. He acknowledged this only works because he has done well enough in the for-profit world to fund it himself. He was equally clear that this model is not a prescription for indie developers who need revenue — they should charge for their software and deserve support.

His key insight about the no-money model is the integrity argument: "There is 100% trust that we never think about revenue when making decisions." (inessential.com, 2023) For an open source project with volunteer contributors, that trust is infrastructure.

## Retirement as a Career Position

In May 2025, Simmons retired from Audible after nearly 41 years of professional employment. He was explicit about what this means for his work: he is not retiring from making apps. He plans to continue NetNewsWire and develop additional free, open source applications. "I have no plans to create apps for money." (inessential.com, "Retirement and NetNewsWire," May 24, 2025) This is a coherent position for someone at the end of a successful corporate career — but it is worth noting it is not available to developers earlier in that arc.
