# Brent Simmons — On Customer Journey

## Transparency as a Default Mode

Simmons does not treat development as something that happens behind a curtain and then gets revealed. He blogs about what he is working on, what decisions he is weighing, what he got wrong, and what comes next. This has been true since NetNewsWire's earliest days — public betas in 2002, detailed feature discussions in 2003, post-mortems on Vesper in 2016, progress posts during the open source NetNewsWire era. For his users, the development process is part of the product experience.

In the 2003 Daring Fireball interview, he described public beta testing as "way more fun than the closed approach" and credited it with attracting smart testers who provide valuable feedback and creating forward momentum and community connection. He has maintained this posture for more than 20 years.

## The Open Source Community as Customer Relationship

NetNewsWire's open source model fundamentally changes the customer journey. Users who want to can look at every line of code, open GitHub issues, submit pull requests, join the Slack community, and participate in decisions about what the app becomes. This is not a support forum — it is an invitation to co-ownership.

Simmons structured this intentionally. The NetNewsWire Slack group functions as both a user community and a contributor pipeline. The GitHub repository is where feature discussions, bug reports, and roadmap decisions live in public. A user who files a bug report can watch it get fixed, see the pull request, and track the release. This is a customer journey with radical transparency at every stage.

## Pricing Philosophy

Simmons' history with app pricing reveals a consistent pattern: he tends toward accessibility over extraction. NetNewsWire began as shareware with a free Lite version. It later became fully free (under NewsGator's ownership). In its current form it is entirely free and open source, and he has explicitly said he takes no revenue for it and plans no change to that model.

His 2016 App Store essay articulated why he thinks premium paid iOS apps are difficult: the market is trained to expect very low prices, and the economics of even a $5 app on iOS cannot sustain a small team over time. His recommendation for indie developers in that era: "Write the apps you want to write in your free time and out of love for the platform and for those specific apps." This is not cynicism — it is a realistic assessment of where sustainable revenue actually lives (Mac direct sales, contracting, day jobs).

## Blogging About the App, Not Just the App

For Simmons, the blog is part of the relationship with users. He has written hundreds of posts about NetNewsWire's development: individual feature decisions, bug investigations, user feedback responses, retrospectives on major versions, and honest assessments of what went wrong. Users who follow inessential.com get a window into how decisions are made that is unusual in commercial software and genuinely rare even in open source.

This ongoing narrative creates a particular kind of user loyalty. When users know why a feature works the way it does, when they have read the reasoning behind a design decision, they become advocates for those decisions rather than critics of them. Simmons has effectively turned his blog readership into an informed constituency for the product.

## Honest Communication About Failure

Simmons' handling of Vesper's shutdown is a model for how to communicate bad news. He and the Q Branch team made Vesper free before shutting down the sync server, provided data export tools, and explained clearly what had gone wrong and what users could expect. He published "More Notes on Vesper" (inessential.com, August 21, 2016) acknowledging the strategic mistake, the limitations of the codebase, and his own future direction. There was no spin.

This honesty is not just ethical — it is reputational infrastructure. Users who watched him handle Vesper's failure honestly trust him more with NetNewsWire. The customer journey includes endings, and how a developer handles endings shapes every future first impression.
