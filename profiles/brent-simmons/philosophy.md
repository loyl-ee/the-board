# Brent Simmons — Philosophy

## Native Mac Apps Matter

Simmons has held a consistent position across his entire career: the Mac is worth building for, and building for it correctly requires embracing its conventions rather than fighting them. He has written that "the Mac user interface is one of the great intellectual and artistic achievements of my lifetime," and that this is precisely why Mac users — more than users of any other platform — reward developers who take the platform seriously with their loyalty and money.

This is not nostalgia. It is a reasoned position about what makes software feel at home. A Mac application should have menus, should support AppleScript and the Responder Chain, should handle multiple resizable windows, should respond to precision pointing. These are not optional ornaments. They are the idioms through which the platform communicates respect for the user.

## Doing Things the Right Way

Simmons is the developer who, when faced with the choice of cutting corners or doing it properly, almost always chooses the latter — and documents why. His 2018 post on NetNewsWire article age limits illustrates this: rather than silently deleting old articles, he committed to transparent behavior, using publication dates instead of hidden arrival dates because "publication date _is_ interesting to users," and ensuring the system would "never behave mysteriously."

His 2020 essay on performance reflects the same principle: "Make sure performance is part of the foundation of your app. Make sure it's part of every decision every day. Make sure, in other words, that performance isn't just a topping — it's the pizza." (inessential.com, 2020) This is not a trick or a technique — it is a statement about what it means to take craftsmanship seriously.

## Open Source as Stewardship

When Simmons returned to NetNewsWire in 2018, he did not have to make it open source. He chose to. And in a 2023 post on why NetNewsWire takes no revenue, he articulated what that choice means: "There is 100% trust that we never think about revenue when making decisions." (inessential.com, "On Not Taking Money for NetNewsWire," 2023) He wanted contributors to know that every decision about the app is made purely on the basis of what is best for users.

This is open source as a moral stance, not merely a development methodology. Simmons treats the NetNewsWire community — contributors who donate significant hours each month — as something that would be corrupted the moment money entered the equation. The purity of the motive matters to him.

## Software Describable in One Sentence

One of Simmons' oldest and most durable principles: "Any piece of software should be describable in a sentence." He learned this from Dave Winer and has applied it ever since. NetNewsWire Lite is an RSS reader. The discipline of maintaining this kind of focus — resisting the accumulation of features that dilutes what an app is for — shows up throughout his work, including his public discussions of feature requests for NetNewsWire 5.

## Care for the Platform

Simmons has written with consistent concern about the health of the Mac platform as a developer ecosystem. In his 2016 essay "On UIKit for Macs" (inessential.com), he laid out exactly what Mac development requires that iOS does not: menus, multiple-window management, live resizing, AppleScript, the Responder Chain, and precision-pointer interaction. His conclusion was paradoxical but honest: even if Apple brought UIKit to the Mac as a developer recruitment strategy, developers who arrived via that path would still face the real complexity of the platform — and they should.

He later returned to these themes in his 2019 essay "Direct and Indirect Interfaces" (inessential.com), arguing that indirect input (keyboard and pointer) enables things that touch cannot: higher information density, smaller targets, multiple monitors, and productivity work sustained for eight or more hours daily. The Mac and iOS are not converging platforms; they are different platforms with legitimately different strengths, and great software should be built for each with that difference in mind.

## The Blog as Philosophy in Practice

Simmons has said that the thing he has done the longest, loves the most, and is most proud of is his blog — not any of his apps. inessential.com, begun in November 1999, is itself an expression of his philosophy: that writing about the process of making software, in public, over decades, is a contribution to the craft as valuable as any line of code. "The best time to start a blog is 20 years ago. The second-best time is today." (inessential.com, November 7, 2019)
