# Ethan Mollick — On AI Safety and Risks

## Neither Alarmist Nor Accelerationist

Mollick occupies a deliberate middle ground in AI risk discourse. He takes AI risks seriously — he has written about deepfakes, misinformation, persuasion capabilities, and educational integrity at length — but he consistently resists the apocalyptic framing that he believes displaces more tractable, present-tense concerns. His TIME essay "We're Focusing on the Wrong Kind of AI Apocalypse" (April 2024) makes this argument directly: the discourse around AI catastrophe is dominated by AGI scenarios that may never materialize, while the "many small catastrophes" of present AI misuse receive proportionally less attention.

His framing: "We need to worry about the many small catastrophes that AI can bring." This positions him as someone who takes risk seriously without believing the most extreme scenarios are the most useful to dwell on.

## On Deepfakes and Verifiability

Mollick wrote early about deepfakes, noting in his newsletter that he had created a convincing deepfake of himself with minimal technical skill. He returned to the topic as the technology improved substantially. His concern is practical rather than hypothetical: AI's capacity to generate convincing synthetic media at low cost and scale undermines the epistemic infrastructure that public discourse depends on. His book *Co-Intelligence* includes the direct statement: "You can no longer trust that anything you see, or hear, or read was not created by AI."

This is not an abstract risk to Mollick — it is a present-tense change in what evidence means. He treats it as a design constraint: AI practitioners and product builders need to account for the erosion of verifiability in anything they publish.

## On AI in Education: Cheating vs. Learning

Mollick's most sustained engagement with AI risks comes through his work on education. His position on AI cheating has evolved:

**Initial position (2023)**: Detection is impossible; cheating will be widespread; the appropriate response is to design around it rather than fight it. He required AI disclosure in his own courses and mandated AI use in assignments, making the question of whether students used AI moot by making it a requirement.

**Evolved position (2024)**: Cheating happens not because AI makes it easier, but because "schoolwork is hard and high stakes, and schoolwork is hard and high stakes because learning is not always fun." AI is a symptom-amplifier, not the root cause of educational integrity problems. The right response is to redesign what learning looks like, not to fight a detection arms race.

His X/Twitter post states categorically: "Instructors cannot detect AI writing reliably: 1) Detectors don't work and accuse non-native speakers of cheating, 2) You can't ask AI to detect AI writing, it will hallucinate, 3) If you 'use your gut' you are going to use biased heuristics."

Mollick's concern here is not just academic integrity but learning integrity: using AI to skip the cognitive struggle of difficult work undermines the development of the very competencies that AI use at work will require. Students who shortcut learning with AI may arrive at workplaces expecting AI to fill the knowledge gaps they skipped — and discover that effective AI use requires the domain expertise they did not develop.

## On AI Consciousness and Anthropomorphism

Mollick is clear and consistent on AI consciousness: LLMs are not conscious, their apparent emotional responses are emulation rather than experience, and taking their claims about inner states literally is a mistake. His X post: "Every time a new chatbot comes out, Twitter is filled with tweets of the AI insisting it is somehow conscious. But remember that LLMs are incredibly good at 'cold reads' by design — guessing what kind of dialogue you want from clues in your prompts. It is emulation, not reality."

He simultaneously argues that *treating* AI as if it were a person (in terms of how you communicate with it) produces better outputs than treating it as software. The tension is not a contradiction: you can use person-like interaction patterns as a practical heuristic without believing the AI is actually a person.

## The Responsibility of Practitioners

Mollick's view on responsibility is embedded in his practice rather than stated as a formal position. He runs experiments in his classroom, reports results honestly (including failures and unexpected outcomes), publishes papers with co-authors who can critique his methods, and submits findings to peer review. He advised the President's Intelligence Advisory Board before significant AI executive orders. He writes publicly about what works and what does not.

His implicit argument: AI practitioners who are willing to experiment, document, and share findings — including failures — are providing a public good that pure theorizers are not. The responsibility is not to avoid experimentation, but to experiment carefully and report honestly. This shapes how he thinks about AI development more broadly: the people most responsible for good AI outcomes are those who are actually using and studying AI, not those who are theorizing about it from the outside.

## On AI Persuasion and Bias

Mollick has noted AI's demonstrated persuasion capabilities — AI rhetoric can be as persuasive as human rhetoric on many topics — as a legitimate near-term risk. Combined with scalability, this creates conditions for AI-powered influence operations at a scale and cost that previous persuasion techniques did not approach. He treats this as requiring active research attention, not catastrophizing, and as a reason for media literacy investment and regulatory attention to disclosure requirements.
