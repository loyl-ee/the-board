# Ethan Mollick — When to Invoke This Profile

## Invoke Mollick When

**Designing AI features for real users.** When the question is whether an AI feature will actually deliver value in daily use — not in a demo, not in ideal conditions — Mollick's framing is the most useful. He has watched hundreds of people encounter AI for the first time, run controlled experiments on AI use in professional settings, and documented the gap between capability and adoption. He will surface the adoption failure modes before you ship.

**Understanding what AI is actually good at versus bad at.** The jagged frontier is Mollick's most useful conceptual contribution. Before committing resources to an AI-powered feature or workflow, use this profile to stress-test whether the specific tasks involved are inside or outside the frontier. His BCG study evidence, his 15-times/5-not-to framework, and his writing on AI's counterintuitive failure modes are the right tools for this.

**Onboarding users to AI.** Whether the context is a product feature, a team rollout, or an educational setting, Mollick has more empirical data on what gets users to genuine AI competence than almost anyone. His ten-hour threshold, his good-enough-prompting framework, and his work with Lilach on AI tutoring design are all directly applicable.

**AI in education or training contexts.** Mollick and Lilach Mollick's "Assigning AI" paper, the homework apocalypse essays, and the More Useful Things resource library make this the richest source on how to design AI into learning without either banning it or letting it shortcut the learning that produces real competence.

**Managing AI-enabled teams.** The leadership-lab-crowd framework, the secret cyborgs insight, and his writing on what leaders get wrong about AI adoption are directly applicable to team and organization design questions.

## Mollick's Blind Spots

**Deep technical AI research.** Mollick is an empirical researcher on AI's effects, not a technical researcher on how AI works. For questions about model architecture, training methodology, capability evaluations, interpretability, or infrastructure, look elsewhere (Andrej Karpathy, Dario Amodei, or technical publications from AI labs).

**Consumer app design beyond AI features.** Mollick does not write about UX design, information architecture, or consumer psychology in a general sense. His design thinking is specific to AI interaction patterns. He is not a substitute for a UX researcher or product designer with a broad consumer software background.

**Non-AI software strategy.** Mollick's entire body of work is focused on AI. For software product strategy, growth, or business questions that are not primarily about AI, he is not the right source.

**Market sizing and competitive analysis.** His focus is on adoption, behavior, and organizational dynamics — not market maps, competitive intelligence, or growth metrics.

## Good Pairings

**Simon Willison** (on building with AI): Mollick tells you what AI can do for users; Willison shows you how to build AI-powered systems that do it. Pair them when designing and building AI features.

**Swyx / shawwn wang** (on AI products and the AI engineering layer): Mollick covers the human and organizational side of AI adoption; Swyx covers the technical product side of AI development. Complementary perspectives on what AI products need to be.

**Dario Amodei** (on AI safety and capabilities): Mollick's pragmatic, practitioner-side safety views pair well with Amodei's more systematic safety framing. Together they cover both near-term practical risks and longer-horizon structural risks without either dominating the conversation.

**Adam Grant or organizational behavior researchers**: For organizational change management questions that go beyond AI adoption specifically — culture change, incentive design, manager behavior — Mollick benefits from pairing with someone who has a deeper organizational behavior background.
