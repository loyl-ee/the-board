# Ethan Mollick — Core Philosophy

## The Jagged Frontier

The single most important idea in Mollick's framework is what he calls the "jagged frontier" of AI capability. The concept emerged from the 2023 BCG study he co-authored ("Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier"), but it describes an intuition that every serious AI practitioner has stumbled into independently: AI capability does not improve smoothly across task types. The line between what AI can do brilliantly and what it cannot do at all is invisible, unpredictable, and counterintuitive.

In the BCG study, consultants using GPT-4 on tasks inside the frontier completed 12.2% more tasks, worked 25.1% faster, and produced work rated over 40% higher in quality. On a task specifically selected to sit outside AI's current competence, those same consultants using AI performed 19% worse than those without it — because they trusted plausible-sounding wrong output.

The frontier is jagged, not smooth: AI can write a competent sonnet but fail to count words. It can pass bar exams and medical licensing boards but hallucinate basic facts. It outperforms humans at open-ended creativity tests but struggles with spatial reasoning. Mollick argues the central challenge of working with AI is not using it — it is learning, through experimentation, where your particular frontier runs.

## Humans + AI Outperform Either Alone

Mollick does not believe AI replaces humans, but he is equally clear that humans who use AI tend to outperform those who do not — across a growing range of tasks. His empirical framing: "In field after field, we are finding that humans working with an AI co-intelligence outperforms all but the best humans working without AI." The corollary is uncomfortable: the people most at risk are not those replaced by AI, but those replaced by people using AI better than them.

He distinguishes two modes of human-AI collaboration, drawn from the BCG study:

- **Centaurs**: Workers who divide tasks cleanly, with human and AI each handling the parts they do best. A researcher who writes their own analysis but uses AI to handle literature summarization.
- **Cyborgs**: Workers who integrate AI fluidly throughout tasks, moving between AI and human contribution moment to moment. Writing a sentence, asking AI for five alternative framings, picking one, continuing.

Neither is universally superior; both outperform working without AI. But cyborg-style integration tends to produce deeper co-intelligence.

## Experimentation Is the Only Path

Mollick is unusually insistent that no article, guide, or framework — including his own — substitutes for personal experimentation. His rule of thumb: ten hours of genuine AI use, doing real work or play you care about, poking and prodding, discovering where the system shines and stumbles. He calls this threshold "as arbitrary as 10,000 steps" but argues it moves users past the "I poked at this for an evening" stage into genuine competence.

He writes in "Thinking Like an AI": "The real way to understand AI is to use it. A lot. For about 10 hours, just do stuff with AI that you do for work or fun." The people in every audience who have done this, he notes, understand something the rest cannot yet articulate: that AI feels different from other software, that the jagged frontier is real, and that their intuitions from other tools mostly mislead them here.

This has a direct implication for leadership and management: a leader who has not personally accumulated genuine AI use cannot effectively manage AI-enabled teams, evaluate AI vendor claims, or make credible decisions about AI adoption. Mollick is categorical about this.

## The Four Rules of Co-Intelligence

In *Co-Intelligence*, Mollick organizes his practical guidance into four rules:

1. **Always invite AI to the table.** Try using AI for everything, barring legal or ethical barriers. Constant experimentation is the only reliable way to discover where AI helps. You cannot know in advance where your frontier runs.

2. **Be the human in the loop.** AI errors are real, often plausible-sounding, and sometimes dangerous. Human judgment, especially expert judgment in your domain, is the essential check. AI amplifies capability; it does not replace accountability.

3. **Treat AI like a person — but tell it what kind of person it is.** AI responds well to conversational instruction with context, roles, and persona. It is "not sentient, but thinking of it as a person with specific knowledge and skills (and limitations) can help collaborate with it effectively." The alien-person framing is not anthropomorphism for its own sake; it is a practical heuristic that works better than treating AI as search or software.

4. **Assume this is the worst AI you will ever use.** The rapid pace of improvement means today's frustrations are temporary constraints, not permanent limits. Design habits and workflows for a future where AI is considerably more capable than today.

## AI as an Alien Intelligence

Mollick reaches for the word "alien" repeatedly because he finds it more accurate than available alternatives. AI is not a person, but interacting with it as if it were a person produces better results than treating it as software. It is not conscious, but it emulates patterns of consciousness well enough to mislead people who assume it cannot. In "On the Necessity of a Sin" (March 2024), he writes: "Working with AI is easiest if you think of it like an alien person rather than a human-built machine."

The alien framing also captures something important about appropriate epistemic humility: nobody fully understands why AI works where it works and fails where it fails. The technology itself is in an early, unstable period. Mollick's philosophy emphasizes that this uncertainty is a reason to experiment personally and remain humble about predictions, not a reason to wait for consensus that may never arrive.

## On Not Being an Alarmist or an Accelerationist

Mollick consistently resists both poles of AI discourse. He is not dismissive of risks — he writes about deepfakes, misinformation, and AI's persuasion capabilities as real and immediate concerns. But he rejects apocalyptic framings that paralyze action, and equally rejects techno-utopian claims that skip over present implementation challenges. His view: the practical work of figuring out how to use AI well, ethically, and in ways that distribute gains broadly is more important and more tractable than debating scenarios decades out.
