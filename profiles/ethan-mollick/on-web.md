# Ethan Mollick — On the Web and One Useful Thing

## One Useful Thing as a Media Product

"One Useful Thing" (oneusefulthing.org) is a Substack newsletter Mollick has built into one of the most widely read independent AI publications in the world. As of early 2026, it has over 427,000 subscribers and ranks in the top 20 of Substack's Business category. Mollick writes every post personally. He accepts no sponsorships. He solicits AI feedback on drafts after completing his initial writing — an application of his own methodology.

The newsletter is structurally a research translation product: Mollick takes academic research (his own and others'), field experiments, classroom observations, and early empirical findings and renders them into writing accessible to a general professional audience. This is a different function from either journalism (which covers what happened) or consulting (which recommends what to do). Mollick occupies a position between the two: he reports the state of empirical knowledge and draws practical inferences for people making decisions now.

## The Publishing Cadence and Strategy

Mollick publishes irregularly — when something significant happens in AI that warrants analysis, or when he has accumulated enough evidence from experiments to report findings. This cadence is itself a strategic choice: he does not publish to fill an editorial calendar, but to say something when he has something to say. The result is a newsletter with high average signal-to-noise ratio, which in turn drives the subscriber retention that makes the model sustainable.

His posts fall into recognizable types:
- **Concept essays**: Introducing or refining frameworks (jagged frontier, centaur/cyborg, leadership-lab-crowd)
- **Research reports**: Summarizing empirical findings from his own studies or notable external research
- **Practical guides**: How to prompt, which AI to use, when to use AI and when not to
- **Reflective essays**: Responding to major AI developments (new model releases, capability jumps) with practical interpretation

The conceptual essays tend to be the most cited and discussed. The practical guides tend to be the most shared. Together they serve both an audience of researchers and policy people who want to track the evidence base, and an audience of practitioners who want to know what to do.

## Substack as Distribution Infrastructure

Mollick chose Substack specifically for its direct-to-reader model. His primary concern is that research findings reach practitioners without being filtered through intermediaries who might oversimplify, sensationalize, or distort them. Substack's email-first model means that posts reach subscribers directly; the open web version allows secondary discovery through search and sharing.

He also maintains active presences on X/Twitter (@emollick), LinkedIn, and Bluesky, using these primarily to share links to posts, surface short empirical observations, and engage with feedback. His X presence in particular has been an important amplifier: many of his most-cited tweet threads summarize findings from papers or his own observations in ways that spread widely before the full Substack post appears.

## "More Useful Things" as a Companion Resource

Mollick and Lilach Mollick also maintain moreusefulthings.com, which functions as a prompt library and resource repository for educators and practitioners. This is the more structured, reference-oriented companion to the essay-driven newsletter — a place where the practical outputs of research (specific prompts, guidance documents, research papers) are catalogued and made accessible without requiring readers to excavate them from essays.

## Research as a Distribution Strategy

Mollick's most influential pieces on the web are often not his essays but his empirical papers, which have been picked up by major outlets — the New York Times, the Financial Times, Forbes, the Wall Street Journal — and through that coverage have reached audiences far beyond his direct subscriber base. The BCG study, in particular, generated significant media coverage and introduced the "jagged frontier" vocabulary to business audiences that would never have encountered it through academic channels.

This creates an interesting media loop: the papers generate press coverage and bring readers to the newsletter; the newsletter builds the subscriber base that amplifies future research; the essays build the author's credibility that opens access to research partners. Mollick's Substack strategy is, in this sense, also a research strategy.
