# Ethan Mollick — On AI Adoption and the Customer Journey

## Why Most People Are Underusing AI

Mollick is specific about the problem: most people who have encountered AI have not yet used it enough to discover what it can actually do. They have tried it for an evening, found it underwhelming or unreliable on the task they chose, and reached a conclusion about AI capability that is systematically biased by their sample.

This is not ignorance — it is a structural feature of how the jagged frontier works. If a user's first few AI attempts happen to land outside the frontier (tasks AI is surprisingly bad at), they correctly conclude that AI did not help with those tasks, and incorrectly generalize that AI is not useful. Conversely, users who stumble into tasks inside the frontier early develop a very different, also accurate, initial impression.

The result is that a population of AI users who each have genuine experience can have completely opposite assessments of AI's value — and both be right about their specific experiences. Mollick sees this as the primary adoption problem: the jagged frontier is invisible until you map it personally, and most people stop exploring before they have mapped enough of it.

## The Ten-Hour Threshold as Adoption Infrastructure

Mollick's solution is not a better explainer. It is accumulated time. The ten-hour threshold is his operationalization of the minimum personal AI experience needed to develop useful intuitions about where one's own frontier runs. Below that threshold, assessments of AI value are too sample-dependent to generalize. Above it, users have typically had enough diverse experiences to start building reliable mental models.

For product teams, this has a direct implication: the most important question about AI onboarding is not "how do we explain AI to users?" but "how do we get users to their first ten hours of genuine, task-relevant AI use as quickly and successfully as possible?"

## Surface Use vs. Genuine Use

Mollick draws a consistent distinction between surface AI use (asking AI to write a quick email, using it like a search engine, generating a draft you barely modify) and genuine AI use (running multi-turn iterative dialogues, using AI on complex tasks you care about, experimenting with it in uncomfortable territory where you are not sure it will work).

Surface use does not build the mental models that make AI genuinely valuable. It produces a flattering but ultimately misleading sense of AI capability — users feel they are "using AI" without developing the competence that produces real productivity gains. Genuine use requires investment, discomfort, and willingness to discover AI's failures as well as its successes.

## The Homework Apocalypse as a Customer Journey Problem

Mollick's "Homework Apocalypse" framing (July 2023) can be read as a customer journey problem applied to education. Students encounter AI as a tool for bypassing work. Educators encounter AI as a threat to assessment validity. Neither group, in this initial encounter, discovers AI's genuine learning value.

The homework apocalypse is not just a cheating problem — it is an onboarding failure at civilizational scale. Students are reaching AI through the wrong entry point (shortcut) and educators are reaching it through the wrong entry point (threat), and both are forming permanent opinions based on the wrong initial experience.

Mollick's proposed reframe: bring students and educators into AI through legitimate, designed educational use (AI as tutor, coach, mentor, simulator) before the shortcut use becomes habitual. His classroom policy — requiring AI use with disclosure — was designed to redirect the student's first AI experience from "AI does my homework" to "AI helps me learn."

## On Organizational AI Adoption Stages

From his consulting and research work, Mollick identifies recognizable stages of organizational AI adoption:

- **Phase Zero**: Ethics committees form. Adoption stalls. The organization is governed by AI risk frameworks calibrated to hypothetical harms rather than actual capabilities.
- **Phase One**: Co-pilot deployment. "Talk to your documents" solutions built. Works moderately well; not transformative.
- **Phase Two**: Direct tool access. Workers experiment individually. Secret cyborgs proliferate.
- **Phase Three**: Organizational design becomes central. Leadership engages with AI strategy. The Lab and Crowd frameworks become possible.

Most organizations, as of 2024-2025, were still in Phase One or Two. The gap between Phase Two (individual productivity gains) and Phase Three (organizational transformation) is where most of the unrealized value sits — and the gap is primarily a leadership, incentive, and workflow redesign problem, not a technology problem.
