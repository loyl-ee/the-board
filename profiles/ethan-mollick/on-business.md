# Ethan Mollick — On Business and AI

## Productivity Gains Are Real, Unequally Distributed

Mollick's view of AI in business begins with the empirical record, not speculation. The BCG study he co-authored established that AI assistance produced a 12.2% increase in tasks completed, a 25.1% speed improvement, and a 40% quality increase — on tasks inside AI's competence. Subsequent studies in legal work, coding, writing, and healthcare show similar directional results. These are not marginal productivity improvements.

But the distribution matters. In the BCG study, the lowest performers gained the most from AI assistance — AI compressed the gap between the bottom and the top. Workers who were already expert gained less in percentage terms from AI, but gained the most in *type* of work: AI freed them from rote tasks and let them operate at the level of their genuine expertise. Meanwhile, workers who trusted AI on tasks outside the frontier — where AI performed poorly — did worse than those who used no AI at all.

The business implication: AI is not a rising tide that lifts all boats equally. Understanding which boats it lifts, and which it capsizes, is the core management challenge.

## Managers Must Use AI Personally

Mollick is blunt about this. In interviews, at conferences, and in his writing, he returns to the same point: a manager who has not personally accumulated genuine AI use cannot lead AI adoption, cannot evaluate AI vendors, and cannot make credible decisions about AI deployment. "Use these models a lot. Use frontier models to figure out what they do," he advises. "The only way to understand how these systems can help is through consistent, hands-on experience."

His evidence: MBA students in his Vibefounding course, equipped with management skills and personal AI familiarity, built startup prototypes in four days that surpassed what semesters of traditional coursework produced. The competence was management competence — the ability to scope problems, define deliverables, evaluate quality — applied to AI delegation. Without domain expertise and personal AI familiarity, that competence cannot transfer.

## The Leadership-Lab-Crowd Framework

In "Making AI Work: Leadership, Lab, and Crowd" (May 2025), Mollick proposes a three-part organizational structure for capturing AI gains:

- **Leadership**: Senior leaders must articulate a vivid, specific vision of what the organization looks like with AI — not vague productivity mandates, but concrete images of transformed work. They must model AI use themselves. They must clarify whether efficiency gains translate to headcount reduction or business expansion, because workers who believe the former will hide their AI use.

- **The Lab**: A small, dedicated team that runs two-week experiments, discovers what the Crowd has already figured out, builds proof-of-concept systems, creates internal benchmarks, and generates demonstrations that help the rest of the organization envision transformation.

- **The Crowd**: The entire workforce. Over 40% of American workers already report using AI at work; many more use it and do not report it. Mollick's term for this latter group is "secret cyborgs" — workers who have automated significant parts of their jobs, do not disclose it, and will not share what they have learned unless given safety and incentives to do so.

The framework addresses the core paradox Mollick identifies: individual productivity gains from AI are well-documented, but organizational performance improvements remain elusive. The gap is structural. Workers hide AI use; leaders do not use it themselves; labs do not exist; crowds experiment without feedback loops. Organizations that close this gap will have genuine AI advantage.

## The Danger of Early, Wrong Implementations

Mollick is direct about a pattern he observed early and warned against repeatedly: organizations that deployed narrow AI solutions in 2022-2023 — "talk to your documents" chatbots, basic co-pilot tools with minimal process change — now own technical debt and user skepticism. "If you built a 'talk-to-our-documents' chatbot when I was warning you not to do that a year and a half ago, you now have a mediocre chatbot," he told Insight Partners.

His broader argument: AI capabilities are improving faster than organizational decision cycles. Organizations that froze strategy around GPT-3.5-era capabilities are now governed by ethics committees calibrated to 2023 risks while models have advanced several generations. The right response is not to move recklessly, but to build for the near-future capabilities rather than optimizing solely for current constraints.

## On Competitive Moats and the Bitter Lesson

Mollick draws on the "bitter lesson" from AI research — the empirical finding that general models trained at scale consistently outperform hand-crafted, domain-specific solutions — to assess business risk. His view: companies whose competitive advantage derives from proprietary data or process expertise face genuine disruption as general AI models improve. Companies whose value comes from relationships, judgment, and the interaction process itself (not just the output) retain defensibility.

"If the output is what matters in your business, you're in trouble of being bitter lessoned. If the process matters — the conversations, the writing of the report more than the report itself — then there's hope." This is not a comfortable message for many knowledge-work businesses, but Mollick treats it as essential diagnostic clarity.

## Four Strategic Questions for Organizations

From "Strategies for an Accelerating Future" (February 2024), Mollick suggests organizations evaluate AI through four questions:

1. What once-valuable tasks can AI now automate, and what does that mean for our workforce?
2. What previously impossible work becomes feasible, and what new products or services follow?
3. What services can now expand to broader markets or be democratized?
4. What premium, personalized services can now be offered at scale?

The questions push against the default tendency to use AI purely to reduce cost, and toward using it to expand capability and reach.
