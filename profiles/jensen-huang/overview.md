# Jensen Huang — Overview

## Who He Is

Jensen Huang (born Jen-Hsun Huang, February 17, 1963, Taipei, Taiwan) is the co-founder, President, and CEO of NVIDIA Corporation — a position he has held continuously since the company's founding in 1993. Under his leadership, NVIDIA transformed from a graphics chip startup into the world's most valuable company, with a market capitalization exceeding $3 trillion by 2024 and ambitions that stretch across accelerated computing, artificial intelligence, robotics, and autonomous systems.

## From Taiwan to Denny's

Huang's path to Silicon Valley is one of the more remarkable origin stories in technology. Born to a chemical engineer father and schoolteacher mother, he spent his early childhood in Taiwan and later Thailand as his father's work moved the family. At age nine, he and his older brother were sent ahead to the United States to live with an uncle in Tacoma, Washington. The uncle, misreading a boarding school brochure, enrolled them at the Oneida Baptist Institute in rural Kentucky — a reform school for troubled youth, not the prestigious academy he imagined. Huang spent two years there cleaning bathrooms daily, facing bullying, and learning English in isolation before his parents arrived in the U.S. and relocated the family to Portland, Oregon.

He went on to study electrical engineering at Oregon State University (BSEE, 1984) and Stanford University (MSEE, 1992), working at LSI Logic and Advanced Micro Devices before co-founding NVIDIA. The founding meeting happened over coffee at a Denny's restaurant in San Jose in April 1993. Huang was 30 years old.

## The NVIDIA Arc

Huang and his co-founders Chris Malachowsky and Curtis Priem started with $40,000 and a simple thesis: build a chip optimized for graphics that general-purpose CPUs could not match. The early years nearly killed the company — a botched contract with Sega in 1996 forced Huang to lay off more than 60% of staff and pivot entirely in under a year. The resulting chip, the RIVA 128, shipped in August 1997 and sold one million units in four months. NVIDIA survived.

The decision to launch CUDA in 2006 — a software platform allowing developers to use GPUs for general computation — was widely ignored at launch. Huang invested heavily anyway, funding university programs, writing textbooks, and seeding research labs with hardware. Thirteen years later, every major AI model in the world ran on CUDA. The H100 and A100 GPU lines, and the DGX systems built around them, became the infrastructure of the AI revolution.

## Why He Is on This Team

Jensen Huang represents a specific and rare capability: the ability to identify a zero-billion-dollar market a decade before it exists, invest in the infrastructure for that market at enormous cost, build the developer ecosystem that makes it real, and survive the years of losses and skepticism in between. He did this once with GPU computing, and again with AI infrastructure.

He belongs on this advisory team for decisions involving platform strategy, long-term technology bets, hardware-software integration, ecosystem moats, and surviving industry transitions. His perspective is especially valuable when the question is whether to build the picks-and-shovels layer or the application layer — and why owning the former, done right, creates more durable value than the latter.
