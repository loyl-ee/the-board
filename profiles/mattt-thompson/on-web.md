# On Web — Mattt Thompson

## NSHipster as a Publishing Platform

NSHipster is Thompson's primary web presence and its design reflects a deliberate view about what a developer publication should be. The site is a static journal — articles, authors, no comments section, no social engagement mechanics beyond the content itself. It is not optimized for engagement metrics but for readability and searchability. The Creative Commons BY-NC license makes its content freely shareable for non-commercial purposes, treating the web as a medium for knowledge distribution rather than a gated content product.

The site operates as an archive as well as a publication. Articles from 2012 remain accessible and relevant because the foundational Cocoa APIs they document have not been deprecated. This archival permanence is itself a design choice: good documentation is not timely content, it is reference material that compounds in value over time.

## Flight School as a Web-Delivered Product

The Flight School book series was sold directly via Gumroad — a web-native delivery mechanism for a traditional book product. This approach bypassed the App Store and the iBooks ecosystem entirely, positioning the books as PDF and digital products sold on the open web. The model is instructive: Thompson used web infrastructure to maintain control over pricing, distribution, and the reader relationship, while the content itself was focused on Apple platform development. The web was the delivery mechanism; Apple's ecosystem was the subject matter.

## His View on Web vs. Native

Thompson has not written extensively about web vs. native as a philosophical debate, but his career and output offer indirect evidence of his position. He spent a decade building and celebrating native iOS and macOS development, treating the Cocoa and later Swift ecosystems as the primary creative medium. His TechCrunch interview from 2012, when he was at Heroku, focused on mobile apps as cloud applications — the insight being that what makes an iPhone app valuable is its connection to server-side infrastructure, not the device alone. This framing treated web and native as complementary rather than competitive.

His more recent work at Hugging Face, focused on bringing AI models to Apple devices using Swift and Core ML, continues this native-first orientation. The question is not web vs. native but: for this user, in this context, what interface serves them best? For Thompson, Apple's native platforms consistently represent the best-quality interface for end users on Apple hardware.

## Approach to the Open Web

NSHipster's use of Creative Commons licensing and its published GitHub repository of article sources (github.com/NSHipster/articles) reflects Thompson's commitment to the open web as an infrastructure for knowledge. He has not been a vocal advocate for web standards or browser-based development, but his publication practices position him on the side of openness: content that can be linked, shared, remixed, and archived is more valuable than content locked inside a proprietary platform. This is a values position about the web as a commons, even if it is rarely stated explicitly.
