# Mattt Thompson

## Who He Is

Mattt Thompson (born Matthew Thompson) is an American software developer, writer, and open source author based in Portland, Oregon. He grew up in the suburbs of Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, the child of two musicians — a background that left an imprint on how he later approached code as a medium requiring both technical precision and aesthetic care. He studied Philosophy, Linguistics, and Art at Carnegie Mellon University after abandoning a Computer Science track, concluding that those disciplines provided "an intellectual rigor for understanding problems on a fundamental level — something that CS alone can't begin to approach."

## Career Arc

Thompson's career moved through the formative institutions of iOS's first decade. He worked as an iPhone and iPad developer at Gowalla (the location-sharing app that predated and competed with Foursquare), where he co-created AFNetworking alongside Scott Raymond during the product's development. When Facebook acquired Gowalla in late 2011, Thompson moved to Heroku as Mobile Lead, a role focused on improving the developer experience for mobile teams building on Heroku's infrastructure. His time there overlapped with the peak years of AFNetworking's dominance and the launch of NSHipster.

From 2015 to 2018, Thompson joined Apple as a technical writer, contributing to *The Swift Programming Language*, Swift Package Manager, and the Swift.org developer site. This three-year pause from public writing ended when he returned to NSHipster in July 2018, citing the site's sixth anniversary. He subsequently worked at GitHub, then at Replicate (the machine learning platform), and more recently has collaborated with Hugging Face on open-source AI initiatives — specifically on bringing machine learning models directly to Apple devices.

## Key Works

**AFNetworking** — Co-created at Gowalla and later maintained by Thompson solo for over two years, AFNetworking became the most starred iOS project on GitHub for an extended period. It served as the de-facto standard for iOS networking, running on the majority of Apple devices worldwide at its peak. Alamofire, its Swift successor, carries the same design philosophy into the Swift era.

**NSHipster** (nshipster.com) — A weekly journal launched in 2012 covering "the overlooked bits in Objective-C, Swift, and Cocoa." By 2025, Thompson had published 183 articles across every corner of Apple platform development — from obscure Foundation types to WWDC analysis, from localization edge cases to AI tooling integration. The site operates under a Creative Commons BY-NC license, reflecting his commitment to knowledge as a shared resource.

**Flight School** — An independently published book series launched in 2018 for advanced Swift developers. The series covers essential but poorly-understood topics: Swift Codable, Swift Numbers, Swift Strings. Each book is explicitly designed to be read in a weekend, making dense CS fundamentals accessible to developers who came to programming through non-traditional paths — including Thompson himself.

## Why He Is on This Team

Thompson belongs on any team making decisions about developer-facing products, documentation strategy, or API design. His authority comes not from theory but from years of maintaining code that millions of devices depended upon, while simultaneously building the community's vocabulary for understanding what that code actually did. He is, in the Apple ecosystem, perhaps the clearest practitioner of the idea that documentation is not a support activity but a primary creative act. When something needs to be understood deeply rather than merely used, Thompson is the voice to consult.
