# On Business — Mattt Thompson

## Open Source as Reputation Capital

Thompson's approach to open source is explicitly relational and reputational rather than purely ideological. In his Heroku interview (2013), he articulated a direct economic logic: "Code costs nothing to share, creates good will, and contributes to the gift economy. What's not to like?" He understood early that freely releasing well-documented libraries was not charity — it was a reputation-building strategy that generated career opportunities and professional trust that purely commercial work could not.

AFNetworking exemplifies this. By co-creating and maintaining the most widely used iOS networking library, Thompson became one of the most recognized names in a community of hundreds of thousands of developers. This recognition enabled his move to Heroku as Mobile Lead, his Apple appointment as a technical writer, and his positioning as a sought-after conference speaker. The business logic: give away something used daily by working developers, and they will know your name when jobs and contracts arise.

## The Gift Economy Model

Thompson has framed open source using the language of gift economies — social systems where generosity creates reciprocal obligation and status rather than direct financial return. His NSHipster article "Stewardship" (January 2014) develops this most fully. He argued that the creation of an open source project is merely the start: true stewardship requires ongoing maintenance, documentation, communication with users, and ultimately a graceful sunset. Each question a user asks is "a data point for what could be clarified or improved" in documentation. Responsible maintainers treat user questions as product feedback, not burdens.

This model has practical business implications. Thompson's "Stewardship" piece argues that semantic versioning is "a contract that library authors make to consumers" — a framing that positions open source maintenance as a professional commitment with accountability, not a hobbyist activity with no obligations.

## The Apple Ecosystem as a Business Platform

Thompson's career was built inside the Apple developer ecosystem, and he has been a keen observer of it as an economic structure. He recognized early — in the Heroku era — that "it's an Internet connection that makes a phone smart," and that mobile apps were really cloud applications wearing native clothing. This framing was commercially useful: it oriented his work (and Heroku's mobile strategy) toward the server-side infrastructure that iPhone apps depended on, not just the device-side code.

His thinking about the Apple platform is not evangelistic. His essay "The Death of Cocoa" (December 2014) argued candidly that Cocoa was becoming a liability as Swift matured — that "standard libraries should implement standards" and that a new Foundation-like layer built on Swift's type system would serve developers better. This willingness to criticize the platform's technical foundations, even as his career was built on them, reflects a business-pragmatist sensibility: attachment to a platform's current form is less valuable than understanding where it is going.

## Gaps to Note

Thompson's business thinking is strongest within the open source and developer tools space. His public writing contains little analysis of consumer app economics, venture-backed product strategy, or growth marketing. His model — give away high-quality work, build reputation, convert reputation to career opportunity — is powerful for individuals and developer-tools companies but does not map directly onto consumer product businesses.
