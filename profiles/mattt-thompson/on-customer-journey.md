# On Customer Journey — Mattt Thompson

## NSHipster's Weekly Cadence as a Community Contract

The most visible expression of Thompson's community philosophy is NSHipster's publication cadence. Launched in July 2012, the site maintained weekly publication for three years — a commitment that functions as a contract with readers. The consistency of delivery creates trust: readers know that returning to the site each week will yield something new and worth reading. This is not incidental; it is a structural choice that shapes the reader relationship over months and years, not individual article visits.

When Thompson stepped back from NSHipster to join Apple in 2015, contributor Nate Cook maintained the site. Thompson's return announcement in July 2018 — on the site's sixth anniversary — treated the continuity as a community obligation: the publication existed for readers, and its continuation in his absence demonstrated that the community's investment in it was the right thing to sustain.

## Teaching Rather Than Gatekeeping

NSHipster's premise — that obscure API knowledge is worth celebrating rather than guarding — is explicitly anti-gatekeeping. Thompson adopted the "hipster" framing not to signal elitism but to reclaim it: to take the dismissive label that mainstream developers might apply to someone excited about `CFBag` and wear it as a badge of curiosity. The site's tone is inviting and thorough, not exclusive. Every article assumes the reader is capable of understanding the full depth of the subject if given the right context.

His Flight School books extend this philosophy. By explicitly targeting developers from non-CS backgrounds and keeping each book short enough to finish in a weekend, Thompson positioned the series as lowering barriers, not maintaining them.

## Accepting Contributions

Thompson's approach to community contributions reflects his "Stewardship" philosophy: the open source maintainer's job includes welcoming contributors rather than protecting the project from outside involvement. AFNetworking's community — which he described as perhaps the project's most important feature — was sustained by accepting and integrating contributions from developers who used the library in production. The library's longevity (over a decade before formal deprecation) was partly attributable to this.

His practical framework for contribution: treat user questions as documentation feedback, treat pull requests as community investment, and treat the library's eventual sunset as a responsible act of stewardship rather than abandonment.

## Responding to Readers

The NSHipster Reader Submissions series (published annually on New Year's Day, starting in 2013) is a distinctive community touchpoint: Thompson invited readers to submit obscure API knowledge, then compiled the best submissions into a published article. This inverted the usual author-audience relationship, treating readers as co-producers of the publication. It also provided a direct feedback loop about what the community found interesting, surprising, or underserved in existing documentation.

## Community as Multiplier

Thompson has consistently framed his open source and writing work as being motivated by its effect on others rather than personal recognition. "What really keeps me active in the community is the opportunity to make things that make the lives of others better, no matter how niche the audience or marginal the improvement." This framing treats the community not as a distribution channel for his work but as the reason the work exists — a subtle but important distinction in how he relates to his audience.
