# Andy Hertzfeld — On Safety and Ethical Computing

## Responsibility at Scale

Hertzfeld has reflected, at multiple points in his career, on the weight of creating software that reaches millions of people. His framing is neither regulatory nor legal — he approaches it as a craftsman's moral consideration. From his writing on folklore.org and interviews, a consistent thread emerges: the people who build software are responsible for what it does to the people who use it. This is not simply about avoiding harm. It is about actively building things that leave users better off.

His most quoted statement on this theme concerns the historical significance of what the Mac team created: "There are occasionally short windows in time when incredibly important things get invented that shape the lives of humans for hundreds of years. These events are impossible to anticipate, and the inventors, the participants, are often working not for reasons of money, but for the personal satisfaction of making something great." (*Revolution in the Valley*, 2004). The implication is that the people making such things carry a corresponding responsibility — not just to execute well technically, but to understand the human consequences of what they are building.

## Open Source as an Ethical Position

Hertzfeld has described his turn toward open source software in 1998 as driven by a recognition that proprietary monopolies in software were "choking off innovation." He volunteered with the Open Source Applications Foundation in 2002–2003 and helped promote open source through his work with Mitch Kapor. His engagement with Linux through Eazel was also, in part, an ethical commitment: he believed users should have access to high-quality computing tools regardless of their ability to pay for commercial software.

This positions him alongside a tradition in computing — associated with figures like Richard Stallman and Linus Torvalds — that treats software freedom as a user right. Hertzfeld has not articulated this in explicitly political terms, but his choices across his career are consistent with the view that software should not be weaponized against the people using it.

## What Is Not Well-Documented

Hertzfeld has not produced documented public writing specifically about data privacy, surveillance, or the ethical dimensions of social media despite having worked at Google on Google+ during the period when these concerns were becoming central public debates (2005–2013). His public statements on privacy or the ethics of large-scale user data collection are not readily sourced. This gap should be noted honestly: one cannot reliably attribute specific positions to him on these questions without primary source documentation.

What can be said is that his career-long emphasis on software that serves users — rather than using users as inputs to a business model — reflects a set of values that would be relevant to these questions, even if he has not applied them directly in public discourse.

**Sources:** *Revolution in the Valley* (O'Reilly, 2004); NerdTV interview (2005); Wikipedia; Register interview on Eazel (2000)
