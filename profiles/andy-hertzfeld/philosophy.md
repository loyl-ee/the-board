# Andy Hertzfeld — Philosophy

## Software as Art and Engineering Combined

Andy Hertzfeld has consistently articulated a view of software that refuses to separate the technical from the aesthetic. His most direct statement on this: "It's the only job I can think of where I get to be both an engineer and an artist. There's an incredible, rigorous, technical element to it, which I like because you have to do very precise thinking. On the other hand, it has a wildly creative side where the boundaries of imagination are the only real limitation." (NerdTV interview with Robert X. Cringely, 2005)

This is not a metaphor for him. Programming, in his view, is the act of making something from nothing — and like all acts of making, it expresses the character and values of whoever is doing it. He has long believed that great software is recognizable not just for what it does but for what it feels like: a quality that comes from the person or people at its center being genuinely, almost obsessively, invested in the result.

## Passion as Prerequisite

Hertzfeld holds that passion is not a personality trait but a functional requirement for great work. His formulation is direct: "You show me a great program and I'll show you a passionate individual somewhere behind it. To make a great program there's got to be at least one person at the center who is breathing life into it. In a ferocious way."

This belief shaped how he evaluated both code and people throughout his career. At Apple, it meant the Macintosh team worked hours that would look unreasonable from the outside — but that Hertzfeld has consistently described as intrinsically motivated, driven by excitement rather than obligation. The team, in his telling, were their own ideal customers. They built the Mac because they wanted it to exist.

## Incremental Truth-Finding Over Grand Plans

One of Hertzfeld's most practically useful philosophical positions is his skepticism toward big design documents and comprehensive specifications. He describes his approach as resembling "a sculptor or craftsman where if you tried to spec out the final thing, it will end up not right." (Notion blog interview)

The alternative is iterative discovery: you build something, run it, feel it, and the next direction reveals itself. He has said: "You're not really doing something very revolutionary if you're not running into some dead ends." The Mac's most transformative feature — mouse acceleration, where physical speed maps to proportionally larger pixel jumps — was discovered through exactly this process. The team had worked for a year with sluggish cursor movement before the right interaction emerged through experimentation, not specification.

This was what would later be called agile development, though Hertzfeld never used that terminology. The underlying principle is epistemological: you cannot know in advance what something should feel like. You have to make it and find out.

## Technology and the Humanities Are Not Separate

Hertzfeld came of age in an era when computing was increasingly splitting between technically oriented engineers and humanistically oriented users. He rejected this divide. The Macintosh project, as he has described it, was explicitly about closing that gap — making a computer that ordinary people could use not by simplifying what they could do, but by redesigning the entire interaction model.

He expressed this through his insistence that the people working on the user interface needed empathy as their core characteristic: "People who work on the user interface side need to have empathy as a key characteristic. But if you are writing device drivers you don't really need to understand humans so well." This is a genuine distinction, not a dismissal — but it reflects his belief that the parts of software that touch people require a fundamentally different sensibility than the parts that do not.

## The Best Software Feels Inevitable

Hertzfeld has described the experience of encountering genuinely great design as discovering something that seems like it could not have been otherwise — as if the solution were already latent in the problem and simply needed to be revealed. This is what he noticed in the Apple II when he first examined it deeply: not just that it worked, but that its design choices reflected a creative intelligence that had pushed past the obvious answers to find the real ones.

He aspired to build the Mac to the same standard. The goal was not to solve the immediate engineering problem but to find the answer that would make users feel that this was simply the way things should be. That quality — the sense that excellent design expresses something true about the problem rather than merely solving it — is central to his understanding of what software can be at its best.
