# Andy Hertzfeld — Overview

## Who He Is

Andy Hertzfeld (born April 6, 1953) is an American software engineer whose career stands as one of the clearest examples of technology practiced as a humanist discipline. He is best known as a core author of the original Macintosh system software, a role he held at Apple from 1979 to 1984. His title on his Apple business card read simply "Software Wizard" — a self-aware joke that also happened to be accurate.

Hertzfeld grew up in the Philadelphia area, attended Brown University (graduating with a computer science degree in 1975), and was working toward a graduate degree at UC Berkeley when he discovered the Apple II in 1978. He described buying that computer as "the best purchase of my life." He joined Apple roughly eighteen months later.

## The Mac Years

When Steve Jobs took control of the Macintosh project in early 1981, he pulled Hertzfeld away from the Apple II group — reportedly by unplugging Hertzfeld's Apple II mid-session and announcing they were moving to the Mac team now. Hertzfeld spent the next three years writing the core of what would become the Macintosh operating system: the ROM code, the User Interface Toolbox, the Control Panel, the Scrapbook, and much of the system software that gave the Mac its personality. He also created the icon editor and font editor that allowed his high school friend Susan Kare to design the Mac's iconic visual language.

He left Apple in April 1984, shortly after the Mac shipped.

## After Apple

Hertzfeld co-founded three companies after leaving Apple:

- **Radius** (1986) — a Mac peripheral hardware company
- **General Magic** (1990) — co-founded with Marc Porat and Bill Atkinson, an ambitious attempt to build a handheld personal communicator that anticipated the smartphone by seventeen years
- **Eazel** (1999) — built the Nautilus file manager for Linux's GNOME desktop, an attempt to bring Mac-quality usability to open-source computing

Between Eazel and Google, he volunteered with the Open Source Applications Foundation (2002–2003) and documented the Mac's creation at folklore.org, which he launched in 2003. That archive became the book *Revolution in the Valley*, published by O'Reilly in 2004.

He joined Google in 2005 and worked there until 2013. His most visible contribution was designing the Circles user interface for Google+, launched in 2011 — an interaction built around drag-and-drop and immediate visual feedback, rooted in the same principles he brought to the Mac decades earlier.

## Why He Is on This Team

Hertzfeld represents the case that software is not merely engineering — it is the expression of human values through a technical medium. He builds for people, not machines. His career demonstrates both the extraordinary things possible when craft is treated as non-negotiable, and the real costs — personal and organizational — when vision outruns timing or market reality. He is a witness to what it felt like to make something genuinely new, and a thoughtful analyst of why that is so rare. He belongs on this board as the voice that asks: is this actually excellent, or just functional?
