# Andy Hertzfeld — On User Experience

## The Mac as the First Computer That Treated Users as Humans

Hertzfeld has described the Macintosh as "the first personal computer that was both easy to use and affordable" — a compound claim that matters. Ease of use was not new in 1984; the Xerox Star and the Apple Lisa had both demonstrated graphical interfaces before the Mac. But both required either institutional budgets or significant technical sophistication to own. The Mac's proposition was different: a computer designed for ordinary people at a price ordinary people could consider.

The difference was not just price. It was an underlying philosophy: that the person using the computer was not a supplicant adjusting their behavior to accommodate the machine, but a human being whose needs and intuitions the machine should accommodate. This inversion — from user serving computer to computer serving user — was the Mac's essential UX contribution.

## Direct Manipulation

The core interaction model Hertzfeld helped build was what computer scientists would come to call direct manipulation: users interact with visible representations of objects, actions are immediately reversible, and the results of operations are immediately visible. Files are not paths in a command-line hierarchy; they are icons you can see, move, and trash. The machine's state is always on screen.

Hertzfeld understood the psychological significance of this approach. He carried it forward explicitly in his Google+ Circles work, where the design was built around drag-and-drop with visible, satisfying feedback. The goal, as he described it, was to make an inherently tedious task — categorizing contacts — feel engaging and even pleasurable through the quality of the interaction itself. He noted that "drag and drop can be a very satisfying gesture if you get feedback" — a design insight rooted in the Mac's original UX principles.

## Making the Computer Feel Alive

One of Hertzfeld's recurring aspirations has been software that feels responsive in a way users experience as almost animate — where the machine seems to understand intent and react with appropriate personality. The Mac's Finder, the animated icons, the desktop sounds — these were not gratuitous embellishments. They were signals that the computer was aware of the user, that actions had consequences, that the system had state and memory.

He articulated a long-term vision of this principle: "some kind of wearable computer with a telepathic UI would be ideal" — indicating that he understands the trend lines moving toward computing that increasingly disappears into the environment while remaining responsive to human needs. At Eazel, he explored extending this to the Linux desktop, imagining presence indicators that would show when friends were online — social awareness woven into the file system itself.

## Empathy as a Core Competency

Hertzfeld's most direct statement on UX design is about who should be doing it: "People who work on the user interface side need to have empathy as a key characteristic. But if you are writing device drivers you don't really need to understand humans so well." This is a genuine functional distinction. The engineer who optimizes interrupt handlers does not need to model a human mind. The engineer who designs what appears on screen when a user's action fails does.

Empathy, in Hertzfeld's framework, means being able to inhabit the experience of someone who does not know what you know — someone encountering the software without your understanding of how it works. This is harder than it sounds, especially for people who are technically expert. The Mac team's advantage was that many of them were, to some extent, also representative users: they were building something they wanted to use, which gave them a baseline of genuine empathy even before they thought of it as a design method.
