# Andy Hertzfeld — On the Web

## Eazel: Web-Connected Desktop Computing

Hertzfeld's most substantial engagement with the web as a platform came through Eazel (1999–2001), which he co-founded to bring Mac-quality design to the Linux desktop. The Nautilus file manager was not simply a local file browser — it incorporated Mozilla's web rendering engine, allowing users to browse the web, company intranets, and WebDAV shares from within the same interface as their local files. This was an early vision of what would later be called cloud computing: blurring the boundary between local and networked storage.

Hertzfeld also explored more speculative integrations: he described wanting to bring presence and instant messaging directly into the desktop, so that users could see friends' availability represented alongside their files. These ideas anticipated the social layer that would later define consumer web applications, though Eazel closed in 2001 before they could be realized.

## Google and the Web as Medium

Hertzfeld joined Google in 2005 and spent eight years there working primarily in JavaScript — a fact he mentioned in a 2006 interview, representing a significant shift from his native C and assembly-language background. His most public Google contribution, the Google+ Circles interface (2011), was a web application built with the kind of direct manipulation and visual feedback he had pioneered in the Mac era. He described knowing from Mac experience "that drag and drop can be a very satisfying gesture if you get feedback," and designed Circles accordingly.

Hertzfeld's documented views on the web as a medium beyond these projects are limited. His broader observation from around 2005 is relevant: he predicted that "the personal computer itself will become less important as the user's computing world expands into dozens of different devices, all united by the network," transforming computers into "more of a communications device than anything else." This framing suggests he understood the web not as a destination but as infrastructure — the connective tissue of an expanding computing environment.

His creation of folklore.org (2003), a purpose-built web platform for collaborative historical storytelling, also demonstrates practical engagement with the web's distinctive characteristic — the link — as a medium for non-linear narrative, something he articulated explicitly in the site's design rationale.
