# Andy Hertzfeld — On the Customer Journey

## Designing for the Person, Not the Use Case

Hertzfeld does not use the language of "customer journey" or "user personas" — these are frameworks that postdate his formative work. But his practice reflects something more concrete than most who do: he has consistently described building software that he himself wanted to use, for people he understood as whole human beings rather than as abstractions to be optimized.

The most direct articulation of this is from his folklore.org essay *The Apple Spirit*: "Again, we were our own ideal customers, designing something that we wanted for ourselves more than anything else." The Mac team was not conducting ethnographic research or running focus groups. They were building the computer they would have chosen if such a thing already existed. The empathy was intrinsic, not performed.

## The "Bicycle for the Mind" Ethos

The phrase "bicycle for the mind" — Steve Jobs' metaphor for what a personal computer could be — shaped the Mac team's sense of purpose. Hertzfeld documented Jobs' use of this metaphor and internalized its implication: that a computer, like a bicycle, should amplify human capability without replacing human judgment. The machine is a tool that makes the person more effective at what they are already trying to do. It does not decide where to go. It makes getting there easier.

This framing put Hertzfeld's UX work in a particular moral context: software that was difficult to use, or that confused or frustrated people, was not just a design failure — it was a failure to fulfill the machine's purpose. The goal was not feature completeness; it was whether the person using the machine left the interaction more capable than when they started.

## From First Contact to Long-Term Relationship

Hertzfeld's career at Apple demonstrated an unusual sensitivity to how people first encountered computers. The Mac's out-of-box experience — the startup chime, the "Hello" script, the Happy Mac icon — was designed with explicit attention to the emotional experience of a first-time user encountering something genuinely new. Every element of the first encounter was considered.

His later work at Eazel extended this to an underserved audience: Linux users who found the existing file manager — and the desktop itself — alienating and difficult. Nautilus was explicitly designed to provide multiple interface levels, accommodating novice and expert users within the same application. The idea that a single product could honor the journey from beginner to expert, rather than forcing users to migrate to different tools as they grew, reflects a longitudinal view of how software relationships develop over time.
