# Andy Hertzfeld — On Business

## Apple: Artistic Values Over Commercial Values

Hertzfeld has described Apple in the early 1980s as a company with "artistic values more than commercial values." This was not an accident — it was the consequence of Steve Jobs' relentless insistence that what they were doing mattered beyond its commercial outcome. Jobs' job, as Hertzfeld understood it, "was to drum into us how important what we were doing was to the world, and that if we succeeded, Apple would be in a position to change the world."

This environment made extraordinary things possible. It also made the company difficult to sustain. Hertzfeld left Apple in April 1984, just months after the Mac shipped, after a management dispute. He has been candid that the post-Mac Apple lost something essential: the idealistic version of the team that he valued had "apparently vanished" as the company absorbed the Mac and Lisa divisions and became a more conventional organization.

## General Magic: Vision That Outran Its Moment

General Magic, which Hertzfeld co-founded with Marc Porat and Bill Atkinson in 1990, is his most instructive business experience. The company imagined a handheld personal communicator — networked, personal, and beautiful — that anticipated the smartphone by seventeen years. It was, in a meaningful sense, right about what the world would want. It was also a commercial failure.

Hertzfeld has been explicit about why. The team "kept adding, becoming more and more ambitious," and he identified this as the central lesson: "the temptation always to make something more inclusive, to make it more powerful, to make it better, to do more" is overwhelming for a founding team that believes deeply in its vision. The solution he has articulated is discipline: "digest it in bite-sized pieces," implement vision "in increments" rather than "all at the same time."

General Magic also illustrated the risks of a company's relationship with its corporate parent. Apple had initially funded General Magic, but "about a year later decided they would rather BE General Magic" — meaning Apple moved to compete directly rather than support the spinout. This experience shaped Hertzfeld's view of how large companies relate to innovation that threatens their existing position.

## Eazel: The Open Source Interlude

Eazel (1999–2001) was a bet that the emerging Linux desktop could be made as accessible as the Mac. The company built Nautilus, a file manager for GNOME that incorporated presence, rich media previews, and a layered user interface designed for novice and expert users alike. The Nautilus code survived Eazel's closure and was incorporated into GNOME 1.4, where it remains the default file manager (now called Files). The business failed; the software endured.

## The Pattern

Across his career, Hertzfeld's business experiences point toward a consistent tension: the conditions that produce genuinely original software — deep conviction, obsessive craft, idealistic team culture — are structurally at odds with the operational discipline required to build a sustainable business. He is not naive about this; he has lived it repeatedly. His advice for founders is to work on a problem you personally want solved, ship incrementally, and resist the pull toward comprehensiveness. Whether he himself has always followed this advice is a separate question.
