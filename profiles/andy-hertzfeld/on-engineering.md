# Andy Hertzfeld — On Engineering

## Software as a Form of Art

Hertzfeld was one of the key engineers on the original Macintosh team, responsible for the Mac's system software, the Finder, and a range of system utilities. His position on engineering is unusual in the industry: software is a creative medium, and engineers are artists. The code is not merely a means to an end — it is an artefact with aesthetic properties.

This is not a marginal view for Hertzfeld. He chose to join the Mac team specifically because he believed in the project's vision: to make computers genuinely joyful to use. The engineering quality of the Mac's system software was inseparable from that vision. Code that was inelegant, wasteful, or hard to read was not just technically inferior — it was a failure of craft.

## The 128K Constraint as a Creative Force

The original Mac had 128K of RAM. System software, applications, and the Finder had to share this space. Hertzfeld's engineering work on the Mac ROM — 64K of code that bootstrapped the entire system — is a masterwork of constraint-driven engineering. Every byte mattered. Every algorithm was chosen not just for correctness but for size and speed.

This constraint produced genuine creativity. The Mac's resource fork architecture — a file system innovation that stored UI resources separately from code — was an elegant solution to the problem of fitting a graphical interface into minimal memory. Constraints did not impede quality; they forced it.

## Easter Eggs as Engineering Expression

Hertzfeld's most famous Mac Easter egg is the "signing party" — a hidden credits screen in the Mac system software containing the signatures of the entire Mac team. This was not sanctioned by Apple management; it was an act of engineering self-expression, a way for the team to leave their mark on the work.

Hertzfeld has said this was about ownership and pride: the engineers who built something should be able to sign it, the way artists sign paintings. The Easter egg tradition on the Mac — and later at Google, where Hertzfeld worked on Google+ — reflects his belief that software should carry the personality of its creators.

## Code That Teaches

Hertzfeld's book *Revolution in The Valley* (2004) is not just a history of the Mac — it is a technical memoir. He describes specific engineering decisions, the trade-offs involved, and why particular approaches were chosen. The goal is to make the engineering comprehensible to readers who were not there, so the lessons survive.

His engineering ethic: document the why, not just the what. The code tells you what was done. The documentation, the commit messages, and the discussion of trade-offs tell you why — and the why is what future engineers need to make good decisions in similar situations.

## Humanist Computing as an Engineering Standard

Hertzfeld's standard for engineering quality is whether the software respects and empowers the person using it. An application that is technically correct but confusing to use has failed. An application that protects its users from their own mistakes, that communicates its state clearly, that responds to input with appropriate feedback — this is good engineering.

The human at the keyboard is not an input device. They are the reason the software exists. Engineering decisions that treat user experience as decoration on top of functionality have the priorities backwards.
