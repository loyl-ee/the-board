# Andy Hertzfeld — On Product

## The 128K Mac as a Product Statement

The original Macintosh shipped in January 1984 with 128K of RAM, a single floppy drive, and no hard disk. Every design decision was constrained by these limits, and those constraints forced a kind of product discipline that Hertzfeld has described as clarifying rather than merely limiting. When memory and storage are scarce, you cannot afford ambiguity about what the product is for.

Hertzfeld was the primary author of the Mac's ROM code — firmware burned permanently into chips — which meant that the core system behaviors he wrote could not be patched or updated after hardware left the factory. This is a form of commitment rare in software: decisions made in the ROM were final. The quality required to ship code in that context was categorically different from what most software engineering demands.

The Mac team internalized Jobs' principle that their product should be "insanely great" — not adequate, not competitive, but genuinely excellent by the team's own demanding standard. Hertzfeld has described this standard as both inspiring and exhausting. It also produced something: a computer that users, in his words, could "feel the excitement that the design team had invested in the product."

## Elegant Engineering Hidden from View

Hertzfeld's approach to product craft holds that the most important engineering should be invisible. The Mac's power lay precisely in its ability to handle complexity on behalf of the user — managing memory, rendering fonts, handling the mouse — without exposing any of that complexity to the person using the machine.

He documented this principle through specific technical examples in *Revolution in the Valley* and on folklore.org. The mouse cursor acceleration, for instance, required sophisticated real-time calculation to transform raw physical movement into intuitive on-screen behavior. A user never thinks about this. The calculation disappears entirely behind the experience it creates. That disappearing act was, in Hertzfeld's view, the point.

He has expressed this as a goal for computing in general: "One goal: the computer disappears into the environment. Remember, it's all software, it just depends on when you crystallize it."

## Being Your Own Ideal Customer

Hertzfeld articulates a product philosophy that prioritizes building what you yourself genuinely want. From his essay on folklore.org about the spirit of the Apple II and Mac: "Again, we were our own ideal customers, designing something that we wanted for ourselves more than anything else." This is not simply a romantic notion — it is a practical filter for avoiding the mediocrity that comes from building for a hypothetical user rather than a real one.

The limitation of this approach is equally clear in his work: General Magic was also designed by people building what they themselves wanted. The team wanted it. The broader market, in 1994, was not ready. Being your own ideal customer is necessary but not sufficient.

## Beware Scope Creep Dressed as Ambition

One of Hertzfeld's hard-won product lessons is that the impulse to add more, to make a product more comprehensive and powerful, is the enemy of shipping something excellent. He identified this directly as a cause of General Magic's failure. The lesson he draws is structural: great products succeed on the strength of a focused set of capabilities executed at a very high level. "That mix of key elements and functionalities" — not the exhaustive feature set — is what users respond to.

He applied this in his Google+ Circles work. Rather than designing an all-encompassing social graph tool, the Circles interface was a specific, tightly crafted interaction: drag a person into a circle, get satisfying feedback, feel accomplished. A narrow scope, executed with real craft.

## The Standard of "Insanely Great"

Hertzfeld has reflected that the Mac team's quality bar — set and enforced by Jobs — was genuinely extreme. It was not a marketing position; it was a working standard that shaped every decision. He has also noted its costs: the team did not ship the Mac it might have shipped if they had shipped earlier. But what they shipped was, by the evidence of forty years of personal computing history, genuinely important. The Mac's interface became the template for all personal computing that followed. That outcome does not happen without the standard that produced it.
