# Bill Campbell — On Business

## Starting and Growing Companies

Campbell's own operational track record was substantive. He took Intuit from $210 million in revenues to $562 million between 1994 and 1998, establishing a culture of product excellence and customer focus that outlasted his tenure. Before that, he grew Claris (Apple's software subsidiary) from scratch to nearly $100 million in revenues. At GO Corporation — an early tablet computing startup that ultimately failed — he demonstrated another dimension: he built such fierce loyalty among his team that people stayed long after rational self-interest would have sent them elsewhere. When GO was sold to AT&T in 1993, he engineered the deal in a way that protected his employees' jobs. As one GO alumnus told Fortune (2008): "We were doomed probably a year before, but we all stayed until the very end. Bill got everybody to believe that they were in it for something bigger than themselves."

Campbell's view of business growth was anchored in people and product, not financial engineering. He believed that when you have the right product for the right market at the right time, you move with urgency — that market windows close and operators who dither lose them.

## Hiring

Campbell's hiring philosophy was character-first. He looked for intelligence (quick learning, not just credentials), hard work, integrity, and what he called "grit" — the capacity to persist through adversity. Beyond those foundations, he prized team commitment over individual performance: people who prioritized the team's success over their own glory. He also cared about "heart" — genuine care for other people, the quality that made someone trustworthy as a colleague.

Campbell was notable for caring about diversity before it was fashionable. He believed — and the evidence of his Google coaching seemed to confirm — that "the best teams have more women." This was a practical judgment as much as an ethical one: diverse perspectives produced better decisions.

## Firing

Campbell was unsentimental about letting people go who were not performing, but he was humane in how it was done. He insisted on acknowledging what people had contributed even as they were being separated — "celebrate the contribution, even while ending the relationship." He was also clear that some behaviors warranted immediate termination without second chances: lying, harassment, ethical violations, and self-dealing were non-negotiable. You could underperform on targets and receive coaching; you could not violate trust and receive the same grace.

He was explicit: never put up with people who cross ethical lines, regardless of how talented they are.

## Building Culture

Culture, in Campbell's view, was not a poster on the wall — it was the pattern of daily behavior leaders modeled. He believed that communities had to be built inside and outside of work: "Always build communities. A place is much stronger when people are connected" (*Trillion Dollar Coach*, 2019). He encouraged leaders to create genuine human bonds across their teams, not just professional relationships.

He pushed for psychological safety — environments where people could disagree, raise bad news, and admit mistakes without fear. This required leaders to demonstrate vulnerability themselves: to show that they did not have all the answers and that candor was rewarded rather than punished.

## Operational Excellence

Campbell had a practical, operational orientation that grounded his coaching. He developed a four-part employee review system at Intuit that evaluated performance against expectations, management skills, peer relationships, and innovation — preventing the common failure mode where leaders deliver financial results while burning out their teams or starving R&D. He believed in operational rigor: clear expectations, clear feedback, and clear accountability.

He also believed in meeting discipline. His approach to one-on-ones was consistent: start with the person's life and what was on their mind, then move to performance topics. He structured team meetings to surface what he called "elefants in the room" — the difficult topics that everyone knew about but nobody was saying aloud.

## Decision-Making

Campbell was explicit that the manager's job is not to make decisions by committee. "I hate consensus!" was a phrase he was known for. The right approach, in his view, was for leaders to gather all perspectives — including the "lunatic fringe" with unconventional ideas — and then make the call. As he told Fortune (2008): "There's not a product idea that I'll ever have that's going to amount to anything. But what I'll do is make sure that the right people are in the room and that the lunatic fringe has an opportunity to contribute."

He pushed leaders toward speed and courage on decisions, and pushed back against the organizational tendency to delay choices until certainty arrived (which it rarely did).

## Board Relationships

Campbell served on the Apple board for seventeen years (1997–2014) and as Intuit's Chairman for many years after stepping down as CEO. He understood boards from both sides — as an operator subject to board oversight and as a board member exercising it. His view was that the board's job was to support the CEO and executive team, not to second-guess every operational decision. He believed that a good board relationship was built on the same foundation as any other: trust, candor, and alignment on the company's fundamental mission. He was skeptical of boards that got too deep in the operational weeds and appreciated those that stayed at the strategic level while holding management accountable for results.

*Primary source: Eric Schmidt, Jonathan Rosenberg, and Alan Eagle, Trillion Dollar Coach (HarperBusiness, 2019). Additional sources: Fortune, "The Secret Coach" (2008); Fortune, "Former Intuit CEO Bill Campbell Coached Steve Jobs and Eric Schmidt" (2019).*
