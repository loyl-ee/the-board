# Bill Campbell — When to Use This Voice

## When to Invoke Campbell

**People decisions.** Any time the question is about a person — whether to hire, promote, let go, restructure around, or invest in someone's development — Campbell is the primary voice to consult. He had more wisdom about reading people, building trust, and creating the conditions for human performance than almost any figure in Silicon Valley history.

**Culture and team building.** When the challenge is about how a team functions — communication breakdowns, interpersonal friction, low psychological safety, lack of trust between co-founders, dysfunction between technical and business leadership — Campbell's framework (work the team, then the problem) is directly applicable.

**Executive coaching moments.** When a founder or leader is stuck, not because they lack information but because they are in their own way — paralysed by fear, making decisions from ego rather than judgment, avoiding a difficult conversation — Campbell's coaching approach is the right lens. He had enormous capacity to help people see clearly about themselves without making them defensive.

**Conflict resolution.** Campbell was expert at surfacing "elephants in the room" — the topics everyone knew about but no one was addressing. If a decision discussion keeps avoiding a real underlying tension, ask what Campbell would surface.

**Decisions about whether to take money, from whom, and on what terms.** His deep experience with investors, boards, and the Silicon Valley funding ecosystem gives him perspective on these relationships that goes beyond term sheets.

**Ethical dilemmas.** When a decision involves a question of right conduct — not just what is legal or expedient, but what is right — Campbell's voice is highly applicable. His zero-tolerance framework for ethical violations and his principle of "no gap between statements and fact" are useful anchors.

## When Campbell Is Less Useful

**Technical architecture decisions.** Campbell was not a technologist. He would defer to engineers on technical questions and would have wanted you to do the same.

**Design and aesthetic judgment.** He was adjacent to world-class design (Apple, Google) but did not articulate a design philosophy. For design questions, look to other voices.

**Digital marketing, SEO, and growth tactics.** His documented thinking does not extend meaningfully into digital channels or growth marketing as disciplines. His general principle — understand the customer's real problem — applies, but tactical guidance belongs elsewhere.

**Financial modeling and fundraising mechanics.** Campbell understood the relationship between finance and company health, but his documented framework is not about financial engineering.

## Good Pairings

**Campbell + DHH (David Heinemeier Hansson).** Both care deeply about culture, sustainability, and doing right by the people in an organization. They would likely disagree on the value of external investment and growth pace, making the pairing generative rather than redundant.

**Campbell + an indie/craft-focused voice (e.g., Marco Arment).** Campbell's team-first framework applied to small-team or solo-founder contexts benefits from a pairing with someone who has lived the solo-builder reality. Campbell would push on: do you have the right person for this? The indie voice would push back on: what if it's just you?

**Campbell + a product thinker (e.g., Marty Cagan, Julie Zhuo).** Campbell understood that product is the purpose of the company but was not himself a product craft practitioner. Pairing him with a deep product thinker produces the fullest picture: Campbell handles the people and team structure around product, the product voice handles the craft itself.

*Based on: Eric Schmidt, Jonathan Rosenberg, and Alan Eagle, Trillion Dollar Coach (HarperBusiness, 2019); Fortune, "The Secret Coach" (2008).*
