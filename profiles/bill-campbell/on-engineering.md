# Bill Campbell — On Engineering

## Engineering Team Culture as the Primary Engineering Concern

Campbell's engineering contributions were not technical — he was not an engineer by training. His contribution was understanding that the culture of an engineering team is the primary determinant of what that team produces. A team with high trust, clear communication, and psychological safety outperforms a team of individually more talented engineers who do not trust each other.

The engineering principle: the quality of the code is a function of the quality of the team's relationships. Technical debt accumulates faster in teams that do not communicate well, because engineers make defensive decisions rather than collaborative ones. Addressing technical debt requires the trust to have honest conversations about what is wrong.

## Disagreement as an Engineering Discipline

Campbell was famous for insisting that disagreements be surfaced explicitly and resolved in rooms, not in corridors. His protocol: if you disagree with an engineering decision, say so directly in the forum where the decision is being made. Hallway consensus — the side conversation that produces a different decision than the meeting — is a failure of engineering governance.

For engineering teams: technical disagreements should be resolved by argument and evidence, not by seniority or political consensus. The engineer with the best argument should win, regardless of their level. Campbell's role was often to ensure that the best argument was heard.

## The Manager as Engineering Resource

Campbell's approach to engineering management was to treat the manager as a resource for the engineering team — someone who removes obstacles, secures resources, and protects engineers' time — rather than as a controller of the team's output. He described his role at Intuit and at Google as "the coach": someone who helps each person on the team perform at their best, not someone who dictates what they build.

The engineering implication: a good engineering manager knows when to intervene (when the team is blocked, when there is a conflict that is degrading productivity, when a decision needs external input) and when to stay out of the way (when the team has the information and autonomy to make good decisions).

## Trust as Technical Infrastructure

Campbell argued that trust between engineers — and between engineers and management — is infrastructure. It is not soft or optional; it is the substrate on which technical decisions are made. Engineers who trust each other share knowledge freely, flag problems early, and make decisions quickly. Engineers who distrust each other hoard information, avoid raising concerns, and spend time on political manoeuvring rather than engineering.

The engineering manifestation of trust: code review culture (honest feedback without defensiveness), incident response (clear accountability without blame), and architectural decisions (disagreement without sabotage).

## Protecting Engineering Time

Campbell was consistent about protecting engineers from unnecessary meetings, premature stakeholder requirements, and scope creep. His view: engineers do their best work in extended periods of uninterrupted focus. Every meeting is a context switch. Every ambiguous requirement is a source of rework. The manager's job is to absorb as much of this overhead as possible before it reaches the engineering team.

The engineering discipline this enables: engineers who are protected from interruptions can design better, write better code, and catch more bugs before they ship.
