# Zach Gage — When to Use

## When to Invoke Zach Gage

Consult this profile when the decision involves:

**Game design and game-like systems**
Any product that uses rules, constraints, feedback loops, scoring, or progression mechanics — not just games. If you are asking "how do we make this feel like a game without it feeling gamified in a cheap way," Gage is the right voice.

**Making a familiar thing feel new**
When the product is a known category (calendar, to-do list, puzzle, quiz, inbox) and the question is how to redesign it to reveal hidden depth or remove accumulated friction. Gage's method — understand the essential fun, then rebuild around it — applies broadly.

**Designing systems with emergent depth**
When the goal is a simple ruleset that generates complex, discoverable behavior. Gage's thinking about constraints, the three reads, and emergence is directly applicable to any product where you want low barriers and high depth.

**Mobile UX for games or game-like products**
Touch interaction design, session length constraints, onboarding without tutorials, the feel of interaction before the rules of interaction.

**Ethical monetization of games or entertainment products**
Subscription models, freemium without dark patterns, the boundary between engagement and exploitation, how to limit play in ways that protect users.

**Accessibility as a design value, not a compliance checkbox**
When the question is how to make something genuinely usable by people who do not think of themselves as your target audience.

**Subscription platform products with community**
Daily routines, shared experiences, social features in solo-activity contexts, building a subscriber community rather than maximizing user count.

**Personal branding for creative or technical founders**
The "put your name on your work" argument, reducing adversarial player/user dynamics through humanization.

## Blind Spots

Gage's perspective is less useful for:

- **Enterprise software** — his expertise is consumer and game contexts; workflow software, B2B SaaS, and enterprise UX involve constraints he has not worked with publicly
- **AI tools and LLM product design** — no documented engagement with AI-native product design
- **Large team management** — he has been explicit about running a small operation (primarily solo or with one collaborator); his advice scales poorly to teams larger than five
- **Heavy narrative games** — his work focuses on systems and emergence; story-driven games, RPGs, and narrative design are largely outside his documented practice
- **Hardware or physical product design** — his physical game work is card games; industrial design and hardware UX are outside his domain
- **Marketing and user acquisition** — he is unusually disinterested in growth metrics and his advice here ("numbers make me depressed") should be weighted accordingly for growth-stage products

## Good Pairings

- **Lucas Pope** (Papers Please, Return of the Obra Dinn) — where Gage thinks about systems and accessibility, Pope thinks about games as communication and authorial intent. Together they cover the spectrum from "how do we make this legible" to "what are we saying with this"
- **Team Alto / Snowman** — Snowman's work on game feel, aesthetic consistency, and premium mobile positioning complements Gage's systems thinking. Gage asks what the game is; Snowman asks how the game feels to hold
- **Amber Case** — Gage and Case share a commitment to respecting user/player attention and time. Case's calm technology framework and Gage's anti-dark-pattern ethics are complementary in product decisions about engagement vs. compulsion
- **Marco Arment** — on premium mobile business model, App Store dynamics, and indie sustainability; both have built premium-first iOS careers and thought carefully about independence vs. scale
