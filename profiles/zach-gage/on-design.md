# Zach Gage — On Design

## Systems Design as Primary Practice

Zach Gage's design practice is fundamentally about systems — the rules, constraints, and feedback loops that generate player behavior. He does not design moments; he designs systems that produce moments. This is a meaningful distinction: designed moments can be engineered and guaranteed, but systems-generated moments are discovered by the player, which makes them feel earned rather than constructed.

His core question for any design is whether the system produces interesting behavior: does it reward observation, learning, and strategic adaptation? Can the player hold the entire system's structure in their mind and use that understanding to make better decisions? If yes, the system has what he is looking for. If not, it is either too complex (more constraints than the player can model) or too simple (insufficient decisions to produce strategic depth).

## Constraints Create Better Games

Gage is direct about the design value of constraints. For Pile-Up Poker, he found that the game "becomes more interesting the more constricted it is" — limits on what a player can do force them to engage more deeply with what they can do. His daily puzzle caps (limiting Pile-Up Poker to five hands per day) are not just ethical design — they are constraints that make the available plays feel meaningful.

His physical game work reinforces this. Physical games naturally enforce simplicity because every rule must be explainable without software. Gage has noted that he is increasingly drawn to physical games because "familiar components (cards, dice) reduce learning barriers" and because physical games require designers to build simpler, more elegant systems — there is no software to carry the complexity.

## The Three Reads: Hierarchy in Visual Design

Gage's visual design is organized around what he calls the "three reads" — a concept adapted from graphic design and applied to game UI. At a first glance, a player should understand what to look at and what the game's essential state is. At a second read, they should understand the less-obvious but important information. The third read covers detailed information that rewards attention but is not needed to play.

He extends this to physical scale. His "band poster" rule of thumb: a game's interface should be readable at three different distances, just as a good poster works at arm's length, across the street, and from a thumbnail. This informed his GDC 2018 talk, "Building Games That Can Be Understood at a Glance," in which he coined the term "subway legibility" — the ability for someone to understand a game by watching another person play it over their shoulder on the subway.

Subway legibility is both a UX and a marketing principle: games that can be understood at a glance spread through observation. But it is also a design clarity principle. If the game's state and key decisions cannot be read at a glance, the design has accumulated too much visual debt.

## Typography and Aesthetic Clarity

Gage's visual aesthetic is distinctive in the indie mobile space: sans-serif or carefully chosen serif typography, bright and saturated colors used with restraint, minimal ornamentation, and high-contrast readability. This comes directly from his art education. For SpellTower, he wanted the game to feel "elegant" but also "pop like a video game" — selecting an old-school, elegant font and bright colors to balance sophistication with immediacy.

He describes the typographic and color work as a distinct design discipline from the interaction design: "There's a whole bit in the design that's just about making sure everybody's eyes are looking at the right part, and honestly, that part is a lot more complicated than picking the colors and stuff like that. That's all of the mechanical part of the design interaction."

Typography in his games is functional: it directs attention, establishes hierarchy, and makes state legible. Aesthetic choices are not decorative — they are informational.

## The Role of Emergence

Gage's best design moments are ones he did not plan. He describes his design process as "leaving enough room to explore game mechanics that emerge from gameplay, and when surprising moments are discovered during design, doubling down on them and exposing them to the player."

This means he builds systems loose enough that they surprise him, then identifies which surprises are generative and builds toward them. The randomness in Really Bad Chess is an example: the surprise of playing chess with mismatched pieces generated interesting strategic behavior, so he leaned into it and engineered the randomness to produce that behavior reliably. The product of emergence is then carefully preserved and amplified, not smoothed away.

## Physical vs. Digital Design Considerations

Gage has noted a meaningful difference between designing for physical and digital contexts. Digital games can modify rules in ways "almost impossible with physical boards" — Really Bad Chess could not exist as a physical game without enormous overhead. But physical games carry a literacy advantage: players arrive with cultural familiarity for cards, dice, and boards that eliminates the need to establish basic rules.

For mobile, he designs around the context of phone use: short sessions, one hand, in motion, without guaranteed attention. This constrains what kinds of decisions can be good decisions. A mobile game's design must produce interesting choices within a window of 30 seconds to 10 minutes.
