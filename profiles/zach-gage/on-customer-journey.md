# Zach Gage — On the Customer Journey

## Direct Relationship with Players

Zach Gage maintains an unusually direct relationship with players for an independent game designer. He is active on Twitter/X (@helvetica), where he responds to player feedback, discusses design thinking, and occasionally shares work in progress. This is not a marketing posture — it is consistent with his stated belief that putting his own name and identity on his work changes the player-designer relationship.

His GDC 2019 talk with Bennett Foddy ("Put Your Name on Your Game") documented the practical effects of this directness: personal branding reduced adversarial player behavior ("the adversarial relationship melts away when you are talking to a person"), tripled his mailing list, and increased press attention. Humanizing the creator changes how players engage with criticism, bug reports, and even anger.

## Player Satisfaction Over Retention

Gage's relationship with conventional product metrics is deliberately adversarial. He has said: "Numbers just make me depressed... it's a weird, secret that you don't need to do analytics to run a successful business." On retention specifically: "Every time I talk to anybody in the industry about stuff, they're always like, what's your retention? I always feel like retention... why are we even measuring that?"

This is not indifference to whether players return — it is skepticism about optimizing for return as the primary goal. Gage's position is that if the game is genuinely good and genuinely respects players' time, retention follows as a natural consequence. Chasing retention metrics creates perverse incentives: designs that keep players playing longer regardless of whether they are enjoying themselves.

He has said he never wants players to be playing one of his games and wonder "What am I doing? Why am I doing this right now?" That feeling — playing out of compulsion rather than genuine engagement — is the failure state his designs work to avoid.

## Puzzmo's Community Philosophy

Puzzmo (2023) operationalizes Gage's community thinking at platform scale. The slow-growth strategy at launch — limiting new users to 500 per day, requiring them to demonstrate "puzzling prowess" — was explicitly about community quality: building an early user base of people who would contribute constructively to a social puzzle space.

The platform adds community features — leaderboards, friend groups, shared history — drawn from multiplayer games but applied to solo puzzle experiences. The design question was how to give puzzle players the social context that games like Minecraft or Call of Duty provide their communities. Puzzmo's community is structured around shared daily experience (everyone plays the same puzzles) rather than competitive ranking or social feed mechanics.

## Player Progression vs. Player Exploitation

Gage draws a sharp line between games that help players grow (progressively exposing more depth of a system) and games that exploit player psychology (variable reward schedules, loss aversion, social pressure). His stated goal for player progression: "to get players to think critically, and then reward that critical thinking." The reward is the thinking itself — the satisfaction of the system revealing its depth — not an external incentive layer.

His Pile-Up Poker design illustrates this in practice: deliberately limiting the game to five daily hands even though players wanted more. The constraint protects players from the game becoming a compulsion. "Five hands is even more than I am sort of into. I would like it to be three." This is a designer making a worse short-term product metric decision for the player's long-term benefit.
