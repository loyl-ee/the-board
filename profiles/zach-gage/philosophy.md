# Zach Gage — Philosophy

## Core Beliefs

Zach Gage's design philosophy is best understood not as a style but as a method. He approaches each game as an investigative problem: what is the essential experience that already exists in this game, and what is preventing people from having it? His work is less about invention than excavation.

## Games as Systems That Create Meaning

Gage came to game design through conceptual art and interactive installation. His early work, including *Lose/Lose* (2009) — a space shooter that permanently deleted real files from the player's computer — was not designed to be entertaining. It was designed to provoke a question: why do players follow game rules even when the consequences are real?

This foundation shapes everything. He describes his throughline as "a deep love and exploration of systems and the generative behaviors that they create in players." Games, for Gage, are not entertainment products that also happen to have systems — they are systems, and the meaning emerges from how players move through them.

He distinguishes games from other media on exactly this point: "Video games, because they traffic in experiences, are really giving you the whole lens." A game does not describe an experience; it is one. Unlike a book or painting, a player can return to a game and have a different experience depending on what they now know — the game reflects back not just itself but the player's current understanding.

## Reimagining Familiar Games to Reveal Hidden Depth

Gage's characteristic move is to identify a game that has accumulated unnecessary friction over its history and remove that friction while preserving (or exposing) the underlying depth. He did not play Sudoku before designing *Good Sudoku*. He built it because he "couldn't find a Sudoku game that would let me play it the way I wanted to play it." The friction he identified — the counting, the bookkeeping, the busywork — was not part of Sudoku's depth. It was accumulated legacy design. Removing it revealed what Sudoku actually is: a search puzzle of beautiful technique structures.

He has described this as "removing cake problems" — a reference to a psychology study where people carrying cake couldn't memorize as many numbers. The designer's job is not only to solve hard problems for players but to take things off their plate so they can think about what actually matters.

## Understanding Before Changing

A consistent principle across his career is that you must understand what makes a game feel good before changing it. Gage has said: "Making a game in a genre you hate is actually one of the most interesting experiences ever." He has made word games (despite initially disliking the genre), pool games, and chess variants — not because he was a fan, but because he saw something in the underlying structure worth uncovering. The outsider position forces genuine curiosity.

He tests designs by asking whether he can surprise himself: "I want them to be a surprise for me. I want to have that experience that everybody else is having so that I know that the game is good." If the designer already knows exactly what will happen, the game has failed to generate emergence.

## Accessibility and Depth as Non-Competing Goals

Gage is unusual in the games industry for insisting that accessibility and depth are not in tension. His games are typically easy to begin and difficult to master — but the difficulty is earned through the player's growing understanding of the system, not through hidden information or punishing mechanics.

He describes his core goal as getting players to think critically: "the most important thing to me is making people think critically, and the most important component of making people think critically is building accessible work." Accessible here means that the player can form a model of the system, make guesses about how to improve, and test those guesses. A game whose structure cannot be held in the mind cannot be learned from.

This is also why he rejects traditional tutorials. His preferred approach embeds learning into gameplay structure, making "a lot of work to set players up to be comfortable experimenting through other more subtle ways."

## Interestingness as Cognitive Density

Gage has offered a definition of interestingness that informs his entire practice: "'Interestingness' is how many connections an idea has in your brain." An interesting game is one that connects to things you already know — familiar genres, everyday experiences, cultural literacy — while adding something that reorganizes those connections. This is why reimagining classic games is not a safe creative strategy for Gage; it is the specific approach most likely to generate genuine interestingness, because the player arrives with an existing network of associations that the new design can complicate and enrich.

## Puzzles as Respect

Gage's puzzle games share a common ethos: the puzzle is a gift, not a test. A well-designed puzzle assumes the player is capable of solving it and gives them everything they need — no more, no less. The difficulty comes from the elegance of the system, not from withholding information or exploiting cognitive bias. This is, for Gage, an ethical position as much as a design one.
