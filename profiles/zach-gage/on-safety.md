# Zach Gage — On Safety

## Ethics in Game Design

Zach Gage is one of the most publicly explicit indie game designers about the ethics of game design. His positions are not theoretical — they manifest in concrete product decisions that sometimes run against his commercial interests.

## No Dark Patterns

Gage describes his design practice as actively opposed to "dark patterns" — UX techniques that manipulate player behavior against their own interests. These include variable reward schedules (the slot machine model), artificial scarcity, loss aversion mechanics, social pressure mechanics, and engagement loops designed to maximize time-in-game regardless of player wellbeing.

His Pile-Up Poker design is a case study in this. The game involves cards, probability, and risk — structural features that overlap with gambling mechanics. Gage's public account of designing it describes deliberately resisting those overlaps: "We are not building a poker game that can ruin your life. We're not building a poker game that can suck up all your time."

The practical implementation of this stance: limiting Pile-Up Poker to five daily hands, a constraint designed to prevent the game from becoming compulsive. Gage acknowledged that "five hands is even more than I am sort of into. I would like it to be three." He is willing to make the game less engaging in the short term to protect players from the game becoming a compulsion.

## Designing for Healthy Engagement

Gage's description of healthy player engagement is specific: games should be experiences where "players are creative and clever and improve their strategy, and where anybody at any skill level can play and win." The opposite of healthy engagement is play that continues not because the player is improving or enjoying themselves but because the game's systems have engineered compulsion.

He has said he never wants a player to finish a session thinking "What am I doing? Why am I doing this right now?" That feeling — playing without genuine motivation — is the failure state. His design goal is that every session should feel chosen rather than compelled.

## Respecting Player Time and Intelligence

Gage's ethics extend to a respect for player time and intelligence that shapes his entire approach to difficulty, onboarding, and progression. His games do not waste player time with busywork. Good Sudoku eliminates counting overhead precisely because counting is not what Sudoku is interesting for — making the player do it anyway is a kind of disrespect for their attention.

His skepticism of tutorial systems is similarly ethical: long tutorials assume the player cannot figure things out, which is both an insult to the player and a design failure. A well-designed game communicates its own rules through play. The designer's job is to build the system so clearly that instruction becomes unnecessary.

## No Exploitative Monetization

Gage has never built free-to-play games with loot boxes, gacha mechanics, pay-to-win, energy timers, or dark-pattern monetization. His freemium implementations (Pocket-Run Pool, Knotwords) use IAP to unlock features or remove ads — not to create artificial friction that only money can remove, and not to gate progress behind a paywall.

His criticism of the mobile games industry is implicit in his choices rather than explicit in his public statements. He has occasionally commented on the App Store ecosystem's problems — noting that the proliferation of clones and the difficulty of charging fair prices for premium games creates structural pressure toward dark-pattern monetization — but his primary response has been to build his own models rather than to campaign against others.

## Responsibility of the Designer

Gage's conceptual art background (particularly *Lose/Lose*, his 2009 game that deleted real files) gave him early experience with the ethical weight of game mechanics. If players will follow game rules even when the consequences are real, the designer has real responsibility for what those rules are. This is not abstract for Gage — it is a founding insight of his design philosophy.

His stated position: "put things in front of people that encourage them to engage in a way that I feel is super healthy, that are appealing but also challenging." Health and appeal are not competing goals. The most appealing game experiences are often the healthiest ones — not the most manipulative.
