# Zach Gage — On UX

## UX as the First Design Problem

For Zach Gage, UX is not a layer applied over design — it is where design begins. The question of how a player first encounters and understands a game is inseparable from the question of what the game is. A game whose fun cannot be accessed without a tutorial has a design problem, not a UX problem.

His background in interactive installation art trained him to think about the moment of first contact. In gallery settings, visitors are reluctant to interact with unfamiliar objects — they need to feel confident performing the interaction before they will do it. Gage learned from this context "how to make something that someone will walk up to and use." That lesson transferred directly to mobile games, where players who are lost in the first thirty seconds leave and do not return.

## Onboarding Without Tutorials

Gage's approach to onboarding is to eliminate the need for explicit instruction. He is skeptical of tutorials on mobile: "You get one sentence and people aren't going to read it anyway." This is not cynicism about players — it is a realistic constraint that shapes design. If a game needs explaining before it can be played, the design has failed to communicate through its own structure.

His preferred approach is to embed learning in gameplay structure itself, making "a lot of work to set players up to be comfortable experimenting through other more subtle ways." In Good Sudoku, "most textual explanations are tucked away in little icons next to their respective control. If you interact with the icon, you trigger the tutorial. Otherwise you can just play the game." The tutorial is opt-in and contextual; the default path is just playing.

This respects the player's intelligence. It assumes that a player encountering a well-designed system will figure it out — and that the moment of figuring it out is itself pleasurable.

## Subway Legibility as UX Principle

The concept of "subway legibility" — coined in his GDC 2018 talk "Building Games That Can Be Understood at a Glance" — is at its core a UX principle. If a stranger on a subway can watch someone play your game for thirty seconds and understand what is happening and why it looks interesting, the UX is working. If they cannot, something about the information hierarchy has broken down.

Gage applies the "three reads" framework to achieve this:
1. **First read (glance):** What is the essential state of the game? What should the player's eye go to first?
2. **Second read (focus):** What is the less-obvious but important information for the current decision?
3. **Third read (attention):** What detailed information rewards sustained engagement?

The first read must work passively — without the player having to search for information. The second and third reads reward engagement but do not gate it.

## The Feel Before the Rules

Gage has said that when he makes interactive art and games, "the interaction is the art" — not a delivery mechanism for some other content. This means the physical, sensory experience of playing the game (tapping, swiping, the sound and haptic response of each action) is a first-class design concern, not an afterthought.

For mobile games specifically, he designs around what he calls the phone's natural context: "I love making games for the context that the phone exists in... you're in line, you pull it out, you do something for 30 seconds, you put it back." The UX must work within that context — it cannot assume sustained attention or a comfortable environment.

Good Sudoku's combination of "a focused and clear look with excellent sound design, music, and haptic feedback on the iPhone" was explicitly intended to "create a welcoming environment for concentrating on each puzzle." The haptics and sound are not decoration; they confirm to the player that their input registered and that the system is responsive. Responsiveness is trust.

## Touch Controls as First-Class UX

Gage's early iOS work included experiments with gesture-based controls that did not use virtual d-pads or button overlays. His BitPilot controls emerged from attempting to create tactile interactions that felt natural specifically on a touch screen — treating the screen as a new surface rather than a reduced version of a physical controller.

He found that experimental touch inputs could be too far from player expectations — when asked to do something genuinely unfamiliar with a touch screen, players would request familiar controls instead. This taught him to position touch UX at the edge of the comfortable and familiar, not beyond it. Touch interactions should feel natural, not require learning a new vocabulary.

## Immediate Playability, Lasting Depth

The UX goal across all of Gage's games is the same: a new player should be able to engage meaningfully within thirty seconds, and an experienced player should still be discovering new dimensions of the system months later. These are not in tension if the onboarding is structured correctly. The game begins in a simple mode that is genuinely fun, not a dumbed-down gate to the "real" game. The depth reveals itself through continued play, not through unlocking.
