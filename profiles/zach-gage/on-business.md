# Zach Gage — On Business

## Operating in a Difficult Market

Zach Gage has built a sustainable independent career in what is widely considered one of the hardest market positions in software: premium and near-premium mobile games. He declined multiple venture capital offers after SpellTower's commercial success in 2011, choosing independence over scale. That decision has defined everything that followed.

He is candid about the difficulty. The App Store ecosystem shifted dramatically from its early years, when rough prototypes could find an audience, to a landscape demanding polished, featured products. He observed that the market stabilized into something like a middle tier: "you could count on making $50,000 to $100,000" on a well-made indie game, as opposed to the earlier binary of "make $5,000 or half a million." He operates in that middle tier by design.

## Premium and Freemium: An Evolving Position

Gage did not start with a fixed ideology about monetization models. His early games — SpellTower, Sage Solitaire — were premium, pay-upfront. He shifted toward free-to-play with Pocket-Run Pool (2018) partly as a defensive move against cloning: premium games with simple mechanics are vulnerable to clones that offer lower prices with aggressive ads. Making his game free undercut that attack vector.

But the shift also had a genuine accessibility rationale. His target audience — "people who are not hardcore gamers but actually play a lot of games, don't think of themselves like that" — often lacks credit cards attached to their phones or hesitates to spend on unknown developers. Free-to-play removes that barrier.

Knotwords (2022) used a hybrid model: free core gameplay, with an $11.99 one-time purchase or $4.99/year subscription unlocking additional puzzles and features. This became a template he refined.

## Puzzmo: A Subscription Platform Business

Puzzmo (2023) represents Gage's most ambitious business experiment. Co-created with engineer Orta Therox and subsequently acquired by Hearst Newspapers, Puzzmo is a web-based daily puzzle platform positioned as a direct competitor to the New York Times Games section. The acquisition by Hearst — a traditional newspaper company — was a deliberate structural choice: avoiding venture capital pressure for exponential growth in favor of sustainable, long-term operation within an existing media institution.

The platform's business model layers free and paid access. Core daily puzzles are free; premium features (leaderboards, archives, experimental games, social groups) require an annual subscription (~$40/year) or lifetime purchase. Gage has described the strategy as: "We want to build a really great product, get an audience, compel that audience to subscribe and support us, and then build out our features to make sure everybody is happy."

The slow-growth strategy at launch — capping new users at 500 per day, requiring them to prove "puzzling prowess" — prioritized community quality over user acquisition. This is the opposite of typical consumer software growth thinking.

## Collaboration as Business Model

Gage's partnerships with Jack Schlesinger (Good Sudoku, Knotwords, SpellTower's 2020 reboot, Card of Darkness) represent a structural approach to small-studio sustainability. Schlesinger contributes puzzle generation algorithms, sound design, and game logic; Gage contributes design direction and player experience architecture. Splitting expertise across two people who trust each other reduces both risk and overhead relative to a traditional studio.

Earlier, Gage contributed iOS development to Vlambeer's Ridiculous Fishing, participating in a similar collaborative economy. He has also assisted friends — including Bennett Foddy — with iOS ports, maintaining relationships across the indie community that generate both work and goodwill.

## Putting His Name on His Work

A recurring business argument Gage makes publicly (most explicitly in his 2019 GDC talk with Bennett Foddy, "Put Your Name on Your Game") is that indie developers should brand under their own names rather than company names. His practical observation: over half of all game companies release only one title. Building brand equity in a company name wastes that equity when the company ends. Personal branding survives.

His own data supported this. Adding his name to game credits and marketing increased press coverage, journalist outreach, and — after adding a heart button linking to his identity — tripled his mailing list. He also found that the personal connection reduced adversarial player behavior: "the adversarial relationship melts away when you are talking to a person."

## Operating Philosophy

Gage's stated operating principle for arts-adjacent businesses: "The way to operate in the arts is to take small risks by doing highly risky small things that make you happy and give you experience/resumé items even when they fail catastrophically. Don't build your livelihood or taste or happiness around expecting commercial success." (@helvetica, October 2, 2018)

He has also noted, unusually for a software product person: "Numbers just make me depressed...it's a weird, secret that you don't need to do analytics to run a successful business." His skepticism of retention metrics — "Every time I talk to anybody in the industry about stuff, they're always like, what's your retention? I always feel like retention... why are we even measuring that?" — reflects a deliberate choice to optimize for quality of player experience rather than engagement KPIs.
