# Linus Torvalds — On Business

## The Non-Profit Model as Structural Solution

Torvalds deliberately chose not to work for any Linux distribution company. When he joined OSDL (Open Source Development Labs) in 2003, and later the Linux Foundation after OSDL merged with the Free Standards Group in 2007, he described his reasoning explicitly in a 2021 Tag1 Consulting interview: "I very consciously didn't want to work for a Linux company" — the goal was to appear impartial and avoid being perceived as a corporate competitor to the very companies whose engineers contribute to the kernel.

This was a strategic governance decision. The Linux Foundation model — a nonprofit that employs Torvalds as a Fellow while being funded by member companies — solves a specific problem: how does a project's principal steward remain credible and neutral when every major technology company has a financial stake in the project? By having no equity stake in any of them, and by being employed by a neutral foundation, Torvalds can reject patches from Google and accept patches from Microsoft in the same week without either decision being interpreted as corporate favoritism. He describes himself at the Linux Foundation as "literally just an employee, although a high-profile one with the title of 'fellow'."

## Corporate Involvement in Open Source

Torvalds has never been hostile to companies building businesses on Linux. He acknowledged in his TED talk (2016) that he does not resent companies making billions from the kernel: "Without doing the whole open source and really letting go thing, Linux would never have been what it is." He views corporate involvement as validation of the technical quality of the project, not as exploitation of volunteers.

His view on sustainability is unambiguous: "For complex technical issues you really need open source simply because the problem space ends up being too complex to manage inside one single company" (Tag1 Consulting, 2021). The scale of the Linux kernel — millions of lines of code, covering everything from embedded devices to supercomputers — requires a contribution base that no single company can maintain. Corporate funding and corporate contributors are therefore features, not threats.

## Red Hat, IBM, and Canonical

Red Hat has historically been one of the Linux kernel's largest corporate contributors. Torvalds' relationship with Red Hat has been broadly positive — they presented him with stock options when Red Hat went public in 1999, though he holds no operational stake in the company. When IBM acquired Red Hat in 2019 for $34 billion, Torvalds received no royalties and made no public statement opposing the acquisition; his ownership of the Linux trademark is managed through the Linux Mark Institute, not through corporate equity.

On Canonical, Torvalds has been critical at times of Ubuntu's relationship with the broader open source ecosystem — particularly around Canonical's use of proprietary components and its occasionally independent approach to Linux integration decisions. However, his interactions with Canonical have been primarily technical and have not risen to the level of public conflict comparable to his statements about Nvidia (Nvidia received a famously direct treatment at a 2012 Aalto University Q&A: "Nvidia has been the single worst company we've ever dealt with. So, Nvidia, fuck you!").

## Google and Android

Torvalds has been broadly positive about Android as a Linux success story. He has noted that Android's success demonstrated Linux's viability on consumer hardware at massive scale. In discussions about the Linux desktop, he has pointed to Android and Chromebooks as the model for how Linux becomes accessible to general users — through hardware integration that abstracts away the traditional friction.

His technical relationship with Google's kernel engineers has occasionally been combative on the LKML — Google contributors have received blunt feedback on patches — but this reflects his approach to all contributors, not a specific animus toward Google.

## The GPL as Business Architecture

Torvalds views the GPLv2 as the business model for open source infrastructure. In a 1997 interview (*Pragmatist of Free Software*), he stated: "Making Linux GPL'd was definitely the best thing I ever did." The GPLv2 creates a mandatory reciprocity regime: companies that use the kernel must contribute their modifications back. This is not charity; it is a structural guarantee that ensures corporate contributors cannot fork the project into a proprietary form and drain the commons.

He has distinguished his position from Richard Stallman's, however. Torvalds is not an ideological free software advocate. He uses proprietary tools when they are better for the job. His commitment to the GPL is pragmatic — it creates the right incentive structure — not moral. On proprietary software generally: "Me, I just don't care about proprietary software. It's not 'evil' or 'immoral,' it just doesn't matter" (March 2007). The open source ecosystem wins on technical merit; proprietary software becomes irrelevant, not condemned.
