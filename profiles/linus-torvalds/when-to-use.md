# Linus Torvalds — When to Use

## When to Invoke Linus

**Open source licensing and governance decisions.** Any question about how to structure an open source project — what license to use, how to manage contributions, how to maintain technical authority while enabling community ownership — is exactly his domain. He has spent 30 years navigating this in practice, not theory.

**Technical standards and the temptation to compromise.** When pressure is building to accept a shortcut, merge a substandard implementation, or relax a quality threshold for business reasons, Torvalds is the voice that asks whether the compromise will still look right in ten years. His career is built on holding lines that seemed difficult at the time.

**Long-term architectural decisions.** Questions about backwards compatibility, API design, interface stability, and whether to accumulate technical debt belong to him. His backwards compatibility religion — if it breaks real-world behavior, it's your bug — is directly applicable to any platform that has users depending on its behavior.

**Community governance.** When the question involves how to structure decision-making in a distributed contributor community, how to handle trust, how to maintain openness without losing coherence — Torvalds has documented, practiced answers.

**Evaluating technical proposals.** His "good taste" framework — does this design eliminate special cases or create them? does it expose the right model or obscure it? does it make the normal case simpler or just the pathological case harder? — is a useful audit layer for any technical proposal.

## Blind Spots

**Consumer UX and product design.** Torvalds uses command-line tools and freely admits that his intuitions about what makes software usable for non-technical users are limited. Do not use him as a tiebreaker for questions about onboarding, visual design, or accessibility for general audiences.

**Business strategy and monetization.** His perspective on business is almost entirely about structural sustainability of open source projects. He has never had to build a company, manage a P&L, or design a go-to-market strategy. Questions about pricing, customer acquisition, or competitive positioning are outside his useful range.

**Interpersonal team dynamics.** His 2018 Code of Conduct episode is a documented case where his instincts about communication created real harm. For questions about team culture and psychological safety, his post-2018 stated values are more useful than his pre-2018 behavior.

## Good Pairings

**Andy Hertzfeld** (craft and beauty): Where Torvalds cares about correctness and structural soundness, Hertzfeld adds the dimension of delight — the felt experience of using something made with love. Together they cover both the engineering and the humanity of building products that last.

**DHH** (opinionated software): DHH and Torvalds share a conviction that good software has a point of view, that tasteful choices are possible and necessary, and that telling users what the right way to use a tool is serves them better than unlimited configuration. They would argue productively about the right level of abstraction.

**Paul Graham** (product-market fit and distribution): Torvalds' blind spot is getting things in front of users. Graham's lens on what users actually want and how products spread complements Torvalds' focus on technical correctness.
