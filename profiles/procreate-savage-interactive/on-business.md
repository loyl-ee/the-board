# Procreate & Savage Interactive — On Business

## The Bootstrap Origin

Savage Interactive's business story begins in 2010 when James Cuda, then running a successful Tasmanian web and branding agency (Savage Media) with his wife Alanna, watched Steve Jobs unveil the first iPad. Cuda registered the name "Savage Interactive" as a business entity the same day. He subsequently shut down the agency — a working, profitable business — to pursue a drawing application for a device that had not yet shipped.

He partnered with Lloyd Bottomley, a self-taught developer and fellow Tasmanian, and the two worked through nights and weekends while managing freelance commitments to keep income flowing. The development process involved over 100 design reviews and three complete rebuilds of the application. By the time Procreate was ready to launch on March 8, 2011, the team had approximately $15,000 in savings to carry them through. There was no safety net, no investors, no runway. Cuda has described moments when they were "down to their last few dollars."

## Growing Without Capital

The company grew entirely through product revenue. Every improvement, every hire, every server cost was funded by app sales. Procreate entered the top ten best-selling iPad apps by 2016. In 2018 it became the overall best-selling paid app on iPad — a position it has held since. The App Store's global distribution model was essential: Cuda has noted that digital distribution "levelled the playing field" against larger corporations and eliminated the traditional overhead costs that would have required outside capital.

Savage's reported EBITDA reached approximately $32 million annually based on Australian financial disclosures (per Stockhead reporting), on a product priced at $12.99 as a one-time purchase. The only investors in the business are James and Alanna Cuda. Alanna serves as CFO. Cuda has been explicit on this point: "We've never taken VC funding or succumbed to acquisition offers. We're here developing Procreate thanks to each and every sale from our community."

## The One-Time Purchase as Business Philosophy

In an era where Adobe charges over $275/year for Creative Cloud and even independent alternatives have moved to subscriptions, Savage Interactive has maintained a one-time purchase model for over fourteen years. Procreate costs $12.99 — once. Procreate Pocket costs $9.99 — once. Procreate Dreams costs $19.99 — once. All major updates across that period have been delivered free to existing customers.

The decision is not merely competitive. It reflects a view that the purchase relationship between a creative tool and its user should be uncomplicated. No subscription means no friction in the creative flow, no anxiety about whether the app will keep working, no sense that Savage is extracting ongoing rent. The model builds trust. It also means the business must earn re-engagement by shipping excellent work rather than by locking users into a recurring contract.

One-time pricing also has a meaningful effect on who can use the tool. A $12.99 one-time cost is accessible to students, hobbyists, and artists in lower-income markets in ways that a $20-per-month subscription is not. "Art is for everyone" — the company's stated guiding principle — is a business statement as much as a values statement.

## Procreate Dreams as a New Chapter

Procreate Dreams, launched in November 2023, represents the company's first genuinely new product category since Procreate Pocket in 2014. Priced at $19.99 as a one-time purchase (explicitly marketed with the tagline "No Subscriptions"), Dreams reflects the same financial philosophy applied to a more ambitious product. The development took over five years. The December 2025 Dreams 2 update was delivered free to all existing customers.

The Dreams launch also signals that Savage is capable of expanding its scope without compromising its model. Procreate Dreams won the 2024 Apple Design Award for Innovation less than a year after launch — validation that the one-time purchase and multi-year development patience can produce work that competes at the highest level.

## Staying in Tasmania

Savage Interactive has never relocated to Silicon Valley, Sydney, or any other technology hub. The studio operates from a converted warehouse in North Hobart. Cuda has spoken directly about why: "I really believe that half the magic about what we do comes from not being in the valley." Tasmania's distance from the industry's mainstream creates a kind of creative insulation. The team is not attending the right parties, absorbing the prevailing consensus, or benchmarking against what everyone else is shipping. They are, in Cuda's framing, making the product feel beautiful — which is a different frame than making the product competitive.

Remaining in Hobart also means the company has been a genuine local employer and a demonstration that world-class technology products can come from anywhere.

## Team Scale

Savage Interactive operates with approximately 60 employees as of the Apple Developer interview (2022–2023 reporting). Leadership includes James Cuda as CEO and co-founder, Alanna Cuda as CFO, Lloyd Bottomley as Chief Research Officer (formerly CTO), and Claire d'Este as Chief Product Officer. For comparison, Adobe employs approximately 30,000 people. Savage has built the world's most-used iPad drawing application with a team roughly the size of a startup's Series A headcount.
