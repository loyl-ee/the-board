# Procreate & Savage Interactive — On Design

## Canvas-First: The Governing Aesthetic

Procreate's visual design philosophy can be stated in a single principle: the canvas comes first, and everything else earns its place. The application gives approximately 95% of screen real estate to the drawing surface. The toolbar exists on the periphery, minimal and dark, designed to recede. Menus appear when invoked and disappear when dismissed. There are no floating panels, no persistent inspector drawers, no modal dialogues that interrupt work. The interface behaves as if it knows it is a guest on the canvas, not the host.

This stands in deliberate contrast to the interface paradigm inherited from desktop creative software, where decades of feature accretion have produced toolbars, menu bars, side panels, and status areas that collectively claim significant portions of the screen. For Procreate, the iPad's touch-first nature provided both the design imperative and the enabling mechanism: because gestures can replace buttons, buttons can be removed.

James Cuda has described the team's core design conviction: "Design is really about solving problems. It's about interfacing with machines in a very human way." The minimal chrome of Procreate is the product of that conviction applied ruthlessly to interface design.

## Iconography as Universal Language

Procreate's UI is icon-driven by deliberate choice. Cuda has said: "Iconography is a universal language." The decision matters at scale: Procreate is used in 13+ languages, by users across cultures and education levels. An interface that communicates through well-designed icons rather than language-specific text labels is inherently more inclusive. The application's localization covers 16 languages, but the icon-first approach means that the core interaction model is language-agnostic.

This is a design decision that has compounding returns. Icons that are well-designed communicate tool purpose visually and create spatial memory — users learn where things are, not just what they are called. An interface learned through spatial memory rather than textual navigation becomes faster and more invisible in use.

## The Brush as Design Medium

In Procreate, the brush engine is not merely a technical feature — it is the primary design medium through which the application expresses its aesthetic. The 190 brushes shipped with Procreate are themselves designed objects. Each one has been tuned to respond to Apple Pencil input in ways that produce a distinctive visual quality: particular inks that bleed realistically, specific pencil textures that match paper grain, watercolor washes that behave with physical plausibility.

The Brush Studio's full-screen design is itself a considered choice. Rather than a compact settings panel, the studio presents the complete parameter space of a brush — shape, grain, dynamics, Apple Pencil behavior — in a legible, organized layout with a live preview pad. The design communicates that brush creation is a first-class activity, not a configuration task.

## Apple Pencil Integration as Design Decision

Savage Interactive treats Apple Pencil not as an accessory but as the primary design axis of the entire product. Every brush in Procreate is calibrated against Pencil pressure sensitivity, tilt recognition, and velocity. The Pencil Pro's squeeze gesture, barrel roll, and haptic feedback capabilities were integrated as design opportunities — the hover preview (showing brush size, shape, and tilt before the mark is made) treats the Pencil as an extension of the artist's intent before contact.

The haptic feedback integration in Procreate is a particular example of design at the hardware/software boundary: when making selection masks, transforming layers, or using ColorDrop, tactile feedback anchors the digital action in a physical sensation, reinforcing the sense that the tool is responding to the artist rather than merely registering input.

## The Procreate Dreams Stage

Procreate Dreams introduces the "Stage" as its design vocabulary — the animation workspace is a canvas that performs rather than a timeline that is edited. The interface borrows from Procreate's canvas-first aesthetic: the Stage is dominant, the timeline is present but controlled through touch gestures, and the "Performing" recording mode collapses the complexity of keyframe animation into the direct act of moving things on screen. The design hides the system's sophistication behind a surface designed to feel immediate.
