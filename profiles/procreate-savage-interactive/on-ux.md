# Procreate & Savage Interactive — On UX

## The Governing Principle: The Tool Disappears

Procreate's UX philosophy can be distilled into a single goal: make the tool disappear so the artist can work. Every interaction — gesture, menu, setting — is evaluated against whether it interrupts creative flow or supports it. This is why gestures replace buttons. It is why settings panels appear and vanish rather than persistently occupying space. It is why undo is two fingers on the canvas, not Command-Z via a menu bar that does not exist.

Savage Interactive describes their design aspiration on the gesture system: "Gestures keep your focus on the canvas and out of menus, speed up your workflow." This is a UX value statement, not a feature description. The point is not that gestures are clever — it is that menus interrupt, and interruption is the enemy of creative state.

## Two-Finger Tap to Undo: An Industry Standard Created by Procreate

The two-finger tap to undo is Procreate's most widely replicated UX innovation. To undo the previous action, the user taps the canvas with two fingers. To undo multiple actions in sequence, they hold two fingers down and Procreate steps rapidly back through history — up to 250 actions. Three-finger tap redoes. It is immediate, physical, and requires no shift of attention from the canvas to a menu or button.

This gesture, introduced by Procreate, has since been adopted across other drawing applications and creative tools on touch platforms. It represents the specific kind of UX insight that only comes from designing for a new interaction paradigm from first principles rather than porting desktop conventions. Procreate did not arrive on iPad asking "how do we put Undo in a menu?" — it asked "what does Undo feel like when your hands are already on the canvas?"

## A Grammar of Gestures

Procreate's full gesture system operates as a consistent language that artists learn progressively. Basic gestures — two-finger tap (undo), pinch to zoom, two-finger rotate — are instinctively learnable because they borrow from the same vocabulary users already know from using touch devices. Intermediate gestures — three-finger tap (redo), three-finger swipe (cut/copy/paste menu), four-finger tap (hide/show UI) — require repetition to become automatic. Advanced gestures — QuickShape (hold at end of a drawn shape), QuickLine (draw a straight line and hold), Eyedropper (tap and hold with finger while holding Pencil) — reward exploration and function as what Procreate calls "artistic superpowers."

The design recognizes that this learning curve is not a deficiency but a feature: "Gestures enable artists with limited movement to create more easily" and "become second nature" with practice. The progression from discovered gesture to internalized muscle memory mirrors the apprenticeship model of learning a physical craft. You do not learn a brush all at once.

## Feeling Like Physical Media

The UX goal underlying Procreate's brush and Pencil integration is verisimilitude — the sensation of physical media. Apple Pencil pressure maps to stroke width in ways calibrated to match the resistance and response of real drawing instruments. Tilt behavior mirrors the behavior of a pencil held at an angle. Velocity affects texture and edge quality in ways that parallel how ink flows differently when applied quickly versus slowly.

Procreate's gesture philosophy extends this physical media analogy: two-finger tap undo is described as a gesture that "sets digital art apart from analogue" — not by removing the undo (which has no analogue in physical media) but by making it feel physical rather than computational. Tapping the canvas is closer to slapping an eraser than to clicking a menu.

## No Subscription Friction in the Creative Flow

Procreate's UX extends to the business model. A subscription-based application introduces friction: monthly billing reminders, potential payment failures that lock the user out, the psychological taxation of an ongoing financial relationship with a tool. Procreate's one-time purchase means that after the initial transaction, the tool belongs to the user without condition. There is no "sign in to continue," no upsell nag, no feature gating behind a higher tier. The application opens, and the canvas is available. This is a UX decision as much as a business decision.

## Accessibility as Core UX

Procreate's accessibility integration is not a compliance checkbox — it is treated as product design. The application includes motion filtering (a stroke smoothing system inspired by audio signal processing that aids users with limited motor control), VoiceOver support, Dynamic Type support, AssistiveTouch compatibility, and color description notifications. The icon-based interface design is itself an accessibility decision: iconography reduces the linguistic burden on users with reading disabilities or who work in languages other than their interface language.

Claire d'Este has articulated the underlying intent: "We've always tried to keep the barrier to entry low and have a friendly first experience. You can jump in and start drawing and it doesn't feel at all intimidating."
