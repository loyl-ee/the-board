# Procreate & Savage Interactive — On Customer Journey

## From Purchase to Practice: An Apprenticeship Model

Procreate's customer journey does not begin with onboarding screens, feature tours, or nudge campaigns. It begins with a blank canvas. The application is designed so that a new user can pick up an Apple Pencil and start making marks immediately, without reading documentation or completing a tutorial. The interface is minimal enough that the initial experience is exploration rather than instruction.

The deeper features reveal themselves gradually. Two-finger undo is discovered by accident or by intuition. The Brush Studio opens one day when a user taps and holds a brush longer than usual. QuickShape appears when someone holds at the end of a drawn line. This discovery pattern is intentional — it mirrors how one learns a physical craft, where proficiency develops through doing rather than through reading a manual. Savage calls this progression explicit in their gesture documentation: basic gestures are instinctive, intermediate ones require repetition, advanced ones reward exploration.

The journey from casual dabbler to committed professional user follows a natural arc defined by the tool's depth. The canvas never becomes limiting — it expands with the user's ability.

## Official Education: YouTube and Tutorials

Savage Interactive operates an official YouTube channel that functions as the primary education platform for the product. The channel structure mirrors the user journey: a Beginner Series for new users, Learn to Procreate instructional content for developing skills, a Live Draw Series showcasing professional artists working in real time, and Procreate Asks Artists — interviews with working professionals that serve both as inspiration and implicit endorsement. The education strategy is organic and craft-oriented rather than sales-driven.

The company's website hosts an Insight section with feature announcements and detailed guides. The Procreate Handbook (accessible at help.procreate.com) provides comprehensive reference documentation. Between official content and a vast ecosystem of third-party tutorials created by the user community, the total education surface around Procreate is enormous — and most of it was built by passionate users rather than paid by Savage.

## The Community Infrastructure

Procreate's community hub — Folio at folio.procreate.com — serves as the central gathering point. It hosts the official Ideas and Suggestions forum where users submit feature requests, an artwork gallery, and general discussion boards. The company actively engages with the Ideas and Suggestions forum: each submitted thread is reviewed, discussed internally, and tagged with a status (planned, in development, released, or acknowledged but not currently planned). This transparency about what is and is not being built is uncommon in the industry and contributes substantially to community trust.

Procreate maintains an Instagram presence with over 3 million followers, a YouTube channel, and Twitter/X. The social media strategy is artist-forward rather than product-forward — the accounts showcase community artwork, feature releases, and artist stories rather than promotional messaging.

## Feature Requests: Listening Without Capitulating

Savage Interactive's approach to feature requests is notable for what it refuses as much as what it accepts. The team reads every thread submitted to the Ideas and Suggestions forum. They acknowledge community demand. And they do not simply build what gets the most votes. The design process is internally driven: the team evaluates what fits the current release's scope, what matches the product's philosophical direction, and what is technically achievable in the time available.

Cuda has articulated the underlying design question: "How do we keep that simplicity and those low barriers — but still give everyone the power they need?" Features that would add power at the cost of simplicity do not get shipped, regardless of how many users request them. Quick Shape — one of Procreate's most celebrated features — emerged from users requesting rulers and protractors (traditional physical tools). The team shipped a gesture instead: something more powerful than what was asked for, and more consistent with the product's design language.

## Loyalty Through Relationship

Procreate's user loyalty is structurally unusual because it is not maintained through switching costs, lock-in, or subscription dependency. Users who purchased Procreate in 2011 still own it and have received over a decade of free updates. The loyalty is earned, not engineered. When James Cuda said "This is my soul and I'm not prepared to sell it," he was describing the same relationship that users feel toward the product.

The August 2024 stance against generative AI — publicized broadly and warmly received by the professional artist community — deepened this loyalty. The announcement read to Procreate's core users as confirmation that the company was on their side, not on the side of the technology trend that threatened their livelihoods.
