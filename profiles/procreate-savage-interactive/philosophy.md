# Procreate & Savage Interactive — Philosophy

## The Central Conviction: Amplify, Never Replace

Savage Interactive operates from a foundational belief that software should amplify human capability, not substitute for it. Every product decision — from interface design to pricing to the refusal of generative AI — traces back to this single conviction. James Cuda, CEO and co-founder, has stated it plainly: "It's a human condition to want to create. We're trying to amplify that." The word "amplify" is not accidental. It implies the human is the source, the tool is the multiplier, and the act of creation belongs entirely to the person holding the Apple Pencil.

This philosophy stands in direct contrast to the dominant trend in software, where AI-assisted generation is positioned as democratization. Savage's counter-argument is that the act of making — the struggle, the decision-making, the personal mark — is not a problem to be optimized away. It is the point.

## Independence as a Creative Prerequisite

Savage Interactive has never taken venture capital and has declined acquisition offers. This is not posturing. It is a considered position about what independence makes possible. Chief Product Officer Claire d'Este has explained it directly: "As someone who works at Procreate, for me probably the best thing is we're independent. James is not making decisions about what we're going to do next based on tracking people's data and seeing when they click here or click there. We're doing it much more from the heart."

James Cuda has reinforced this: "We've never taken VC funding or succumbed to acquisition offers. We're here developing Procreate thanks to each and every sale from our community." The commercial relationship with the user — a straightforward one-time purchase — is understood as the source of creative freedom. Without investors demanding growth metrics or acquirers demanding integration with a larger platform, the team can ship what they believe is right, iterate through hundreds of design reviews, and hold a feature back for three years (as they did with Quick Shape) until it feels correct.

## The iPad as the Defining Canvas

Savage's philosophy is also inseparable from a belief in the iPad as the best creative surface ever built. Cuda has described the iPad Pro launch as "a game changer for the creative industry," crediting its powerful chip, large screen, all-day battery, and Apple Pencil accuracy as the hardware preconditions that made Procreate's full vision possible. This isn't brand loyalty — it's a design thesis. A tablet with precise stylus input, a high-refresh display, and a gesture-ready touch surface creates a genuinely new kind of creative tool, one that can close the gap between the mind's intention and the mark on the canvas in ways that neither a mouse nor a physical brush alone can achieve.

## Saying No to Generative AI: A Principled Stand

In August 2024, Procreate published a statement that spread rapidly across the creative community: "Generative AI is ripping the humanity out of things. Built on a foundation of theft, the technology is steering us toward a barren future." James Cuda accompanied the statement with characteristic directness, saying on social media: "I really f— hate generative AI" and "We're never going there. Creativity is made, not generated."

The reasoning was specific, not vague. Cuda's concern was threefold. First, diffusion-based generative AI systems are trained on artist-created work, typically without consent or compensation — which he characterized as theft. Second, the technology functions in ways that are antithetical to the creative journey Procreate is built to support; generation is not drawing, and conflating the two harms both. Third, and perhaps most urgently, adopting generative AI would harm the professional artists who form Procreate's most committed user base — the people whose livelihoods depend on the value of original, human-made work.

The company also noted: "Although machine learning is a compelling technology with merit, the current path that generative AI is on is wrong for our platform." This line is important — it is not a blanket rejection of machine learning, which Procreate already uses for features like stroke smoothing. It is a specific rejection of generative image synthesis, grounded in ethics, not technophobia.

## Process as Philosophy

Internally, the team's creative process mirrors the philosophy they build for. Cuda has described Savage's development culture: "Our process is not too dissimilar to making music. We get together and design and develop software until it feels beautiful." The team prototypes first, produces pixel-perfect mockups before abstract planning documents, and iterates rather than specifying. "The customer doesn't see a wireframe; they see a product, and it's much more meaningful when they can interact with that product," Cuda has said. Constraints — like maintaining a minimal interface — are productive. "Making something simple is really complex," Cuda observes. "The beauty of the product is its accessibility."

This is a studio that has internalized the belief that how you make something shapes what you make. The independence, the craft orientation, and the ethical commitments are not separate from the product — they are the product's origin.
