# Procreate & Savage Interactive — On Safety

## The Generative AI Refusal: A Landmark Ethical Stance

In August 2024, Savage Interactive published a statement on procreate.com that became one of the most widely discussed company ethical positions in the creative technology industry. The statement declared: "Generative AI is ripping the humanity out of things. Built on a foundation of theft, the technology is steering us toward a barren future."

CEO James Cuda accompanied the public statement with direct language on social media: "I really f— hate generative AI. I don't like what's happening in the industry and I don't like what it's doing to artists. We're not going to be introducing any generative AI into our products." The accompanying post was captioned: "We're never going there. Creativity is made, not generated."

The announcement was unhedged, specific, and consequential for a company of Savage's size. It received widespread praise from professional artists and illustrators — the exact community whose livelihoods were most directly threatened by the technology in question.

## The Ethical Reasoning

Cuda's objections to generative AI were not aesthetic or competitive — they were ethical, and they were specific.

**Training data provenance.** Diffusion-based generative AI models are trained on vast datasets of human-created artwork, typically assembled without artist consent and without compensation. Cuda characterized this as theft. The models produce outputs that replicate the style, technique, and often the specific visual language of living artists whose work trained the system — and do so commercially, for profit, without remuneration to the artists. For a company whose entire product exists to empower artists, implementing a system built on unauthorized use of artists' work would be a fundamental contradiction.

**Incompatibility with the creative journey.** Cuda explained that the way diffusion AI functions "doesn't really match the creative journey." Procreate is built around the act of making — the choices, the iteration, the physical engagement with the digital canvas. Generative image synthesis bypasses this process entirely. Incorporating it into Procreate would not extend the tool; it would replace the core activity the tool is designed to support.

**Impact on artists.** The professional artist community — concept artists, illustrators, comic artists, animation professionals — represents Procreate's most committed user base. These are people for whom the commercial value of their original work is their livelihood. Generative AI that can approximate professional-quality output at scale directly depresses the market for their labor. Cuda stated plainly: "I don't like what it's doing to artists."

The company was also careful to distinguish generative AI from machine learning broadly. The statement acknowledged that "machine learning is a compelling technology with merit" — Procreate already uses machine learning in features like stroke smoothing — and positioned the refusal specifically against "generative AI" (i.e., text-to-image and image-generation systems built on diffusion models trained on scraped artwork).

## Privacy in the Application

Savage Interactive's privacy stance extends to the application itself. The Procreate app collects no user data. The privacy policy states plainly: "Savage does not collect or share any data when using the Applications." All artwork, animations, brushes, and color palettes are saved directly to the user's device. No cloud sync. No account required. The applications "do not connect to any Savage web services at any time."

This is an architectural decision, not merely a policy. Building the application around local storage removes the infrastructure that would enable data collection. Two specific camera-dependent features (FacePaint and Palette Capture) request camera access but explicitly do not collect or store TrueDepth or camera data — the policy documentation calls this out specifically, suggesting the team anticipated questions about features that involve sensitive hardware.

For a drawing application — one that captures intimate personal creative work, potentially including portraiture, diary-style illustration, and professional work-in-progress — the no-data-collection stance is the only appropriate posture. Savage has made this commitment not as a minimum compliance position but as a core product value.

## The Ethics of Creative Tools

Savage Interactive's combined position — refusing generative AI, refusing data collection, maintaining a one-time purchase model that requires no ongoing user relationship beyond the product itself — represents a coherent and unusual ethical stance for a software company. The positions reinforce each other: the company does not monetize user data (local storage only), does not monetize user time (no subscription dependency), and does not implement technology that harms the users it serves (no generative AI).

James Cuda has summarized this as a commitment to supporting human creativity: "We don't know exactly where this story's gonna go or how it ends but we believe that we're on the right path supporting human creativity." That framing — uncertain about outcomes, certain about direction — is characteristic of ethical positions held under pressure.
