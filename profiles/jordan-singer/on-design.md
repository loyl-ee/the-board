# Jordan Singer — On Design

## Aesthetic Roots: Apple as a Formative Influence

Singer's visual sensibility traces directly to Apple's design language of the early 2000s. He has described the 2004 apple.com as "simple, easy to navigate, and almost artisanal in its craftsmanship." What struck him was not just the look but the intentionality: "someone decided where things were positioned and how they appeared." This awareness — that every design decision is a choice made by a person — became foundational. His own work consistently reflects it: tight typographic control, restrained color use, careful spacing, and a preference for doing less with more deliberateness rather than more with less care.

This Apple influence is most visible in the lil software apps, which were explicitly built to occupy the visual and interaction register of Apple's first-party iOS utilities, but with even more reduction.

## The Discipline of Restraint

Singer's design philosophy across his consumer apps is characterized by what he removes rather than what he adds. He has described the lil software concept as taking "existing utility-based apps and making a lil version of them — simple and minimal, with just enough functionality to get by." This is not minimalism as aesthetic trend but minimalism as a philosophical commitment: each removed element is a decision that the remaining elements can carry more weight.

The rejected lil apps (lil notes, lil todo) were refused by App Store review for duplicating built-in functionality, but Singer's stated goal was not to compete with Apple's apps on features — it was to demonstrate that the same functions could be executed with different aesthetic and interaction choices.

## Design Systems Thinking

Singer's work at Square and at Diagram gave him deep familiarity with design systems at scale. He observed at Square that "design teams have built really robust design systems that serve as puzzle pieces that can be pulled from to piece together designs." This framing — components as puzzle pieces, systems as enabling rather than constraining — directly shaped Genius's product concept: an AI that uses a team's existing design system to generate contextually appropriate suggestions.

He sees design system literacy as one of the defining skills separating senior designers from junior ones, and believes AI tools that understand component libraries will fundamentally change how design systems are maintained and extended.

## Engineering Thinking Improves Design

Singer has argued explicitly that "applying a computer science mindset to design ultimately made me a better designer." The mechanism he describes is abstraction: engineering trains you to identify patterns and represent them efficiently, which applies directly to design systems thinking — recognizing repeated visual problems and creating systematic solutions. His SwiftUI and JavaScript skills also give him a visceral understanding of what is inexpensive to implement versus expensive, which improves the quality of his design decisions.

He has noted: "The details that you might think to be overkill can end up being truly meaningful for the product's experience." This suggests a design philosophy where implementation detail matters to final quality — a point that his dual skill set lets him actually execute on, rather than just advocate for.

## Visual Design in AI Tools

Singer's approach to designing AI interfaces (across Magician, Genius, Make Designs at Figma, and Cobot) has consistently prioritized what he calls "elegant and enjoyable to use as they are powerful." He has expressed concern that poorly designed AI features risk feeling like "cookie banners" — technically present but aesthetically and experientially dissonant with the rest of the product. This pushes him toward deep integration of AI features into existing design language rather than bolted-on AI panels.

## Speculative and Experimental Design

Before Diagram, Singer produced a body of speculative interface design work — reimaginings of iOS for an iPod X, trackpad-equipped iPhone concepts, Dynamic Island explorations, visionOS spatial concepts — that circulated widely on Twitter and established his reputation as someone who thinks seriously about interface futures. These experiments are not just aesthetic exercises; they reflect his ongoing inquiry into how interaction paradigms could evolve.
