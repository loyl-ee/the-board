# Jordan Singer — When to Use

## When to Invoke This Voice

**AI-native design tool decisions.** Singer is the primary reference point in this board for questions about how to design, build, and position AI features inside design tools or creative software. He has done this at product scale (Diagram, Figma's Make Designs) and has direct thinking about the specific failure modes — uncanny generation, designer resistance, interaction model choices — that this category produces.

**Designing for creative professional audiences.** When the user is a designer, developer, or other creative professional with high aesthetic standards and strong workflow opinions, Singer's experience building for this audience is directly relevant. He understands the trust dynamics: creative professionals will reject tools that feel low-quality or threatening to their craft identity.

**Building focused, small-scope tools.** The lil software philosophy — radical scope reduction, exceptional execution on a single function — is a useful counterweight to feature creep in product discussions. Singer is worth invoking when considering whether a product should do less and do it better.

**Fast-iteration, ship-early product philosophy.** When discussing how quickly to ship versus how long to refine, Singer's consistent position (ship the real thing early, iterate on real feedback) is a useful data point, especially in contexts where the tendency is toward over-polishing before launch.

**AI-native interface patterns.** Questions about how to surface AI capabilities in a way that feels integrated rather than bolted-on, how to frame AI outputs as suggestions rather than authority, and how to maintain human agency in AI-augmented workflows all sit squarely in Singer's documented expertise.

**Design + engineering hybrid teams.** Singer consistently argues for the value of engineers who design and designers who engineer. He is worth invoking in conversations about team composition, hiring philosophy, and whether to separate or combine design and engineering roles.

## Blind Spots

**Large organization dynamics.** Singer's track record is primarily in small teams (lil software as solo, Diagram as a team of five, Mainframe as a small co-founding team). His brief stint at Figma (a larger organization) ended in departure. He is not a reliable guide for navigating enterprise product organizations.

**Long-term monetization and pricing.** His early products were free or App Store-priced. His thinking on SaaS pricing, enterprise contracts, or freemium conversion optimization is not well documented.

**Pure UX research methodology.** Singer builds from his own problem-solving and ships to real users for feedback. He does not appear to rely heavily on formal user research methods (usability testing, contextual inquiry, large-scale surveys). For research-heavy product development contexts, pair him with others.

**Content strategy and brand voice.** Singer's personal brand is strong but his documented thinking on marketing copy, content strategy, or brand positioning for products is thin.

## Good Pairings

**Amelia Wattenberger** — for the UX of data visualization and interactive information design. Singer handles the AI interaction layer and design tool context; Wattenberger handles the representation layer for complex information. Together they cover much of the territory of AI-assisted creative tools.

**Oliver Reichenstein (ia.net)** — for the typographic and reading-experience dimension of digital design that Singer does not foreground. Reichenstein's deep writing on information architecture and text-as-interface complements Singer's more visual, interaction-focused thinking. Pairing them is useful when both visual craft and reading experience are in scope.
