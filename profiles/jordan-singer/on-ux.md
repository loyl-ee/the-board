# Jordan Singer — On UX

## Designing AI Interfaces for Creative Professionals

Singer's UX work is concentrated on a specific and difficult problem: how do you design AI interfaces for people who are themselves sophisticated visual thinkers? Designers are a demanding audience — they notice every misaligned pixel, every animation timing choice, every violation of the visual language they work in all day. Building AI tools for this audience requires both technical AI capability and design craft at a level most AI tool builders do not attempt.

His approach at Diagram was to meet designers in their existing workflow rather than asking them to adopt a new interface paradigm. Magician, Genius, and Automator all lived inside Figma as plugins — familiar context, familiar interaction patterns, AI capabilities surfaced at the moment of relevance within the existing design process.

## Interaction Patterns: Autocomplete as a UX Model

The most interesting UX design decision in Genius was its interaction model: rather than a chat interface or a form-based prompt, it worked as design autocomplete. Singer described it as a tool that "ideates and iterates alongside you as you design" — responding to the context on the canvas rather than to an explicit command. This is a fundamentally different UX model from chat-based AI tools and reflects Singer's deep familiarity with how designers actually work. Designers typically think visually and iteratively; they respond to options on a canvas, not to abstract descriptions of what to build.

This autocomplete model was also the conceptual underpinning of Make Designs at Figma: AI that generates a "first draft" from the components already in a team's design system, grounding the output in existing visual language rather than generating from scratch.

## Reducing Friction at the Point of Creation

Singer has identified the "blank canvas" as the central UX problem for design tool AI. The blank canvas is where design work either starts or stalls. His tools are consistently aimed at reducing the friction of beginning: Magician generates a usable icon from a text description; Genius populates a blank frame with contextually appropriate components; Make Designs generates a first-draft screen from a prompt. The UX goal is not to generate finished work but to give the designer something to react to, which is categorically easier than generating something from nothing.

## Familiarity as a UX Principle

Singer has articulated "familiarity" as one of the three essential properties of AI agent interfaces (alongside accessibility and collaboration), directly in the context of Mainframe's Cobot. His rationale: AI agents should work within patterns users already understand from consumer software, rather than requiring users to learn a new mental model. This is a conservative UX position — it trades the possibility of novel interaction paradigms for the practical benefit of immediate usability.

The same principle appeared in Automator's UI, which Singer explicitly designed to mirror Figma's own design language so that designers working in Figma would not experience cognitive friction when switching to the automation tool.

## Making AI Legible and Non-Threatening

Singer has consistently been attentive to the emotional register of AI interfaces for designers, who initially responded with anxiety to his "Designer" plugin in 2020. His UX response to that anxiety was not to hide the AI but to make it transparent and controllable: Genius showed its suggestions rather than auto-applying them; Automator required users to build their own automations rather than having the system act autonomously; Make Designs was framed as producing a "jumping off point" rather than a finished design. This pattern — AI as suggestion, human as decision-maker — is a coherent UX philosophy that addresses both the practical quality problem (AI outputs are imperfect) and the trust problem (users need to feel in control).

## Honest Gaps

Singer has not published extensive writing on UX methodology per se. His UX thinking is primarily documented through product decisions and interview comments rather than dedicated essays or frameworks. His work is more clearly legible as UX practice than as UX theory.
