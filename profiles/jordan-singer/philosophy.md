# Jordan Singer — Philosophy

## Core Belief: Build Your Ideas, Then Ship Them

Singer's central operating principle, visible across his entire body of work, is that ideas have no value unless made real and put in front of people. He has stated explicitly that he prefers "to have people using the real thing and giving early feedback" over iterating in isolation. He frames software as permanently fluid — "there is no final state, so there is no such thing as perfect" — which gives him permission to ship early and often. This stance has produced a remarkable volume of output: consumer iOS apps, Figma plugins, open-source SwiftUI samples, newsletters, and two funded startups.

He characterizes himself as "a scrappy builder" who treats momentum as a resource: "there is nothing more important than building momentum through shipping and iterating on feedback." This is not a naive disregard for quality — his lil software apps are renowned precisely for their attention to detail — but rather a philosophical rejection of perfectionism as a reason not to ship.

## Learning by Reverse Engineering

Singer's approach to both design and code began with imitation. He taught himself web development by reading source code from sites he admired, most significantly Apple's website circa 2004. He describes that site as "simple, easy to navigate, and almost artisanal in its craftsmanship — someone decided where things were positioned and how they appeared." This observation — that design is intentional — stuck with him. He applied the same reverse-engineering method to design itself, learning by studying and replicating work he respected before developing his own voice.

The same pattern appears in how he built Diagram: he studied how GitHub Copilot augmented developer workflows and asked what the equivalent would look like for designers.

## Design and Code as a Single Practice

Singer does not treat design and engineering as separate disciplines with a handoff between them. His engineering background made him effective at building "real, working prototypes" during his time at Square, and he has consistently argued that coding is a superpower for designers: "Applying a computer science mindset to design ultimately made me a better designer." The details that emerge from understanding implementation constraints — what is technically possible, what is expensive, what is nearly free — shape the quality of product decisions in ways that pure design-side thinking cannot replicate.

This integrated view manifests in his work: his Figma plugins are production tools, not demos; his iOS apps are distributed on the App Store; his SwiftUI samples are clean enough to be widely starred and referenced.

## AI as Amplifier, Not Replacement

Singer's position on AI in design has been consistent since 2018, when he first experimented with natural language input for a Sketch plugin called Assistant. His view: AI should amplify designer capabilities the way a good tool amplifies a craftsperson's hand. He invokes Steve Jobs's "bicycle for the mind" framing, writing that "we humans are tool builders, and we can fashion tools that amplify these inherent abilities that we have to spectacular magnitudes."

When his 2020 GPT-3 Figma plugin ("Designer") generated alarm from designers worried about job displacement, he responded that "that's not the intent whatsoever. It's about saving time" and articulated "a more iterative approach to how AI impacts a designer's workflow." He consistently frames AI design tools as enabling designers to spend less time on the mechanical and more on the strategic.

At the same time, he is honest about the democratization implications: AI tools allow non-designers to create things they could not before, and he sees this as "a really exciting opportunity" rather than a threat to the profession.

## Small, Focused Tools as a Design Philosophy

The lil software project — a series of single-purpose utility apps for iOS — reflects a design philosophy in itself: remove everything except the core function, then execute that function with exceptional craft. Each lil app (lil weather, lil calculator, lil draw) does one thing and does it with intentional visual care. Singer has described the design constraint as generative: "the entire idea was to make apps that took existing utility-based apps and made a lil version of them — simple and minimal, with just enough functionality to get by."

This philosophy scales up. Diagram's early plugins were also highly focused: Magician did icon and copy generation; Automator did visual workflow scripting. Singer generally builds specific, polished tools rather than sprawling platforms, and his product track record suggests this constraint produces more adoptable products.

## On Accessibility and Democratization

Singer believes strongly that design should be accessible to everyone. His ultimate stated goal has been that "design will be so accessible that anyone will be able to visualize their ideas with low effort." This is not a rhetorical position — it shaped every product decision at Diagram, and it shapes Mainframe's positioning around making AI agents "accessible, collaborative, and familiar."
