# Jordan Singer — On Safety

## AI That Assists Rather Than Replaces

Singer's most consistent safety-adjacent position is his insistence that AI design tools should augment designer work rather than automate designers out of the process. This position was tested publicly in July 2020, when his GPT-3 Figma plugin demonstration ("Designer") generated anxious responses from designers who feared displacement. He responded directly: "that's not the intent whatsoever. It's about saving time." He framed the appropriate relationship as AI saving time on mechanical work while leaving creative decision-making with the designer.

This is not a position he has qualified significantly since. Across Magician, Genius, Make Designs at Figma, and Cobot, the pattern is consistent: AI generates options or drafts, humans decide, refine, and ship.

## The Make Designs Incident as an Object Lesson

The most concrete AI safety issue associated with Singer's work was the Make Designs controversy at Figma in July 2024. Make Designs, launched at Config 2024 with Singer presenting the vision, generated designs that closely resembled Apple's existing apps — including a weather app that looked nearly identical to Apple's Weather application. Figma pulled the feature within days of launch.

Singer's stated philosophy for the feature — that AI-generated designs should serve as "a jumping off point" while "only designers can craft a meaningful experience from a first draft" — was sound. The failure was in the implementation and QA of the underlying design system assets, not in the design philosophy. Figma's retrospective acknowledged that new components were added shortly before Config 2024 without adequate vetting. Singer and his Diagram colleague Marco Cornacchia departed Figma approximately two months after this incident.

The episode illustrates a real risk in AI design tools: when the training data or reference assets are not carefully vetted, AI generation can produce outputs that reproduce existing work in ways that raise intellectual property and plagiarism concerns. This is a systemic risk in the category, not a risk unique to Singer's work.

## Transparent and Controllable AI

Singer's UX choices reflect implicit safety thinking: his AI tools consistently surface suggestions for human review rather than auto-applying changes. Genius showed autocomplete suggestions that designers could accept or reject; Automator required explicit construction of workflow rules; Make Designs required user invocation rather than autonomous generation. This "human in the loop" architecture is the practical expression of his "assist, don't replace" philosophy.

## Honest Gaps

Singer has not published dedicated writing on AI safety, responsible AI design, model ethics, or related topics. His safety-relevant positions are inferred from product decisions and responses to specific incidents rather than from a systematic framework. He is a practitioner working on specific products, not an AI safety researcher or policy advocate. Any analysis of his views on responsible AI should note this limitation.
