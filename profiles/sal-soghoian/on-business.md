# Sal Soghoian — On Business

## The Business Case for Scriptability

Soghoian's most durable contribution to the business conversation about software is a concrete argument for why scriptable applications are commercially superior to non-scriptable ones. The argument is not about developer goodwill or technical elegance — it is about customer retention and market positioning.

His most direct statement on the subject comes from The Omni Show (December 2020): "Once you become part of somebody's workflow, they're not going to leave you. You have their loyalty until you break your scriptability." This is a claim about switching costs. A scriptable application that has been woven into a user's automated workflow is expensive to replace — not because of lock-in in the pejorative sense, but because the user has invested in building something valuable around it. That investment creates loyalty that ordinary feature competition cannot easily erode.

## Professional Users as a Premium Market

Soghoian's career was shaped by his experience with the publishing and media industries in the 1990s. These were professional users with high-volume, high-stakes workflows — producing hundreds of menus for Showtime Networks monthly, processing large print runs through complex multi-application pipelines, managing film and media assets at scale. AppleScript was not, for these users, a nice-to-have. It was the difference between a viable production process and an impossible one.

This experience gave Soghoian a clear-eyed view of where automation value concentrates: in professional markets where repetition is high, stakes are high, and time is a genuine cost. He demonstrated this for Apple management by collecting real-world use cases from enterprise AppleScript users — partly as advocacy, partly as evidence that the automation customer base was commercially significant enough to protect.

The lesson he draws from this: professional users are often the most loyal, highest-paying, and least price-sensitive segment of a software market. An application that earns the trust of a production studio, a hospital records department, or a research team through excellent automation support has effectively captured a customer for years, not months.

## Automation as a Marketing Multiplier

Soghoian has noted that automation capabilities serve a secondary function as marketing. When a developer's application supports a robust scripting interface, power users in online communities build and share workflows, tutorials, and automation libraries around it. Each shared workflow is effectively a demonstration of the application's capabilities, created and distributed by customers rather than the developer.

"It's great marketing," he has said. "You're interacting with your audience in a really productive way." (The Omni Show, December 2020.) This dynamic is visible in communities around scriptable applications: the presence of an active automation community around a product signals to prospective users that the product is serious, deep, and worth investing time in.

## The Cost of Non-Scriptability

The inverse of Soghoian's business argument is equally important: applications that remove or ignore scripting capabilities pay a quiet but real price. Professional users who built workflows around an application and find those workflows broken by a sandboxing change, an API removal, or a feature deprecation do not simply complain — they leave, and they tell their professional networks about the breakage.

Soghoian watched this dynamic play out repeatedly during his Apple tenure, particularly as App Sandbox requirements created friction for AppleScript-dependent applications in the Mac App Store. The business pressure toward sandboxing was real, but so was the cost to professional users who depended on inter-application communication.

## No Software Does Everything

A practical pillar of Soghoian's business philosophy is the acknowledgment that no software product can anticipate every user's needs. As he has stated: "No software can provide everything that someone wants to do, because every customer treats their data differently." (The Omni Show, December 2020.) This is not a criticism of developers — it is a structural observation about the diversity of professional workflows. The appropriate response, in his view, is not to try to build every conceivable feature, but to give users the tools to build the features they need themselves. Scriptability is how a product becomes infinitely extensible without requiring the developer to ship infinite features.
