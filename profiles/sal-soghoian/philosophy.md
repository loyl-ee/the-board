# Sal Soghoian — Philosophy

## The Central Credo

Every aspect of Soghoian's thinking flows from a single belief he has articulated consistently for three decades: "the power of the computer should reside in the hands of the one using it." This is not a slogan. It is the organizing principle that led him to Apple in 1997, sustained him through nineteen years of fighting internal battles for automation budgets, and drove him to continue advocating after his position was eliminated in 2016. The credo functions as both a design heuristic and an ethical commitment — a test that any software decision can be run against.

## Automation as Emancipation

Soghoian understands automation not as a productivity hack but as a form of emancipation. The computer, in its default state, does what its developers decided it should do. Scripting reverses that relationship: the computer does what the user decides it should do. This is a meaningful political distinction in his view. The professional who cannot automate their workflow is dependent on the goodwill and priorities of software vendors. The professional who can script is sovereign over their own process.

He has seen this play out in concrete terms across his career. In one frequently told example, he describes workers who learned to automate their processes at their jobs, then left to become independent consultants who were hired back at three times their previous salary — their automation knowledge had become a genuine market differentiator. For Soghoian, this is automation working as it should: creating individual capability that compounds.

## The Computer Should Do What You Say

Soghoian's view is that computing has a natural direction of authority, and for too long that authority has pointed the wrong way — from software developer to user, rather than from user outward. His life's work has been reversing that arrow. Automator was designed precisely to let non-programmers issue instructions to their computers in plain visual terms. AppleScript was stewarded to be readable English prose rather than terse code. JavaScript for Automation was introduced to bring automation capabilities to developers already fluent in a widely-used language. Each of these choices reflects a philosophy that the barrier to commanding a computer should be as low as possible without sacrificing actual power.

## Scriptability as a Democratic Right

Soghoian treats the scriptability of applications as something approaching a democratic right for power users. The argument is this: every application is effectively a platform. If only the application's developers can extend or combine it with other applications, then the application is a closed domain — users are guests who operate within prescribed boundaries. If the application exposes a scripting interface, users become participants who can define their own boundaries. Soghoian's advocacy for AppleScript dictionaries in iWork, iPhoto, Aperture, Photos, and other Apple applications was driven by exactly this logic: each app made scriptable is another tool available to the user's creative authority.

The corollary is also important: apps that break their scriptability break the loyalty of their most invested users. "Once you become part of somebody's workflow," Soghoian has said, "they're not going to leave you. You have their loyalty until you break your scriptability." (The Omni Show, December 2020.) The inverse reads as a warning to developers who regard scripting support as optional.

## Every App Is a Building Block

A foundational element of Soghoian's philosophy is that no application should be an island. Good software exposes itself to the broader ecosystem: it accepts inputs from other apps, produces outputs that other apps can consume, and responds to external instruction. In his framing, "automation allows your app to be part of a larger pool of resources." The application that refuses to participate in this ecosystem is not just limiting its users — it is making a claim that the developer's vision of how the app should be used is the only legitimate vision, a claim Soghoian finds both arrogant and commercially shortsighted.

This building-block thinking is what made the Mac's automation ecosystem exceptional at its peak: AppleScript could orchestrate Photoshop, Illustrator, InDesign, FileMaker, QuickTime Player, and the Finder in a single workflow. The limiting factor was never technical. It was developers choosing not to implement scripting dictionaries. Soghoian spent his career trying to close that gap.

## Privacy and Local Control

In his post-Apple work, Soghoian has added a privacy dimension to his automation philosophy. His strong preference is for device-resident automation — tools that run locally on the user's hardware and do not ship data to external services. His reasoning is characteristically direct: once data leaves your "home," it loses legal protections. His guiding rule on this subject is what he calls avoiding the food chain — the idea that whenever you send data to someone else to manage or process, you become part of their ecosystem in ways that reduce your autonomy.

This privacy stance aligns naturally with his core credo. Local automation is, in effect, the most user-empowered form of automation: the computation happens where the user is, under the user's authority, without intermediaries.

## Technology Serving People, Not the Other Way Around

The deepest layer of Soghoian's philosophy is a conviction about the proper relationship between technology and human beings. He is not a technology optimist in the conventional sense — he does not believe that more technology automatically means more human capability. He is, rather, a technology instrumentalist: tools are valuable insofar as they extend what people can do, and worthless or harmful insofar as they create new dependencies or strip away existing capabilities. This is why he has consistently opposed treating automation as a feature to be deprecated in favor of more consumer-friendly experiences. Consumer-friendly, in his reading, too often means "does less for the user while feeling easier to use."
