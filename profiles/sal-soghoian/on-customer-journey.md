# Sal Soghoian — On Customer Journey

## Helping People Discover What Is Possible

Soghoian's clearest statement about the automation customer journey is this: "The main thing to remember is that you can do this. If you ever find yourself thinking, 'There's got to be a better way to do this,' there is." (The Omni Show, December 2020.) The opening of the automation journey is not a technical problem — it is a belief problem. Most people who would benefit enormously from automation have never been told that automation is available to them, or have been given the impression that it requires programming expertise they do not have.

This is why Soghoian has spent so much of his career in an educational mode: presenting at conferences, maintaining tutorial websites, appearing on podcasts, writing documentation, building macosxautomation.com. The goal is to close the awareness gap — to reach the person who does the same twenty-step process every morning and has never considered that the computer could do it for them.

## The Learning Curve as a Journey, Not a Barrier

Soghoian's view of the learning curve in automation is deliberately optimistic. He acknowledges that AppleScript is not easy, that writing a complex Automator workflow takes effort, and that full scripting proficiency requires time. But he frames the learning process as a journey with progressive rewards rather than a barrier with a prize only at the end.

Each step along the ladder — from Shortcuts to Automator to AppleScript — delivers value immediately while opening a view of what the next step makes possible. A user who builds their first Shortcuts automation has already changed their relationship with their device. A user who writes their first AppleScript has crossed a threshold that reshapes how they think about every application they use. These intermediate states are valuable in themselves, not just as steps toward some future expert state.

He has described the career trajectories he has witnessed: users who began automating simple tasks, developed a reputation for making things work, and parlayed that reputation into consulting careers at multiples of their previous income. The automation journey, in his telling, is one of the few technical skill paths where the distance between beginner and commercially valuable is genuinely short.

## Building a Community of Practitioners

A distinctive aspect of Soghoian's contribution to the automation customer journey is his investment in community. The CMD-D: Masters of Automation Conference (2017) was explicitly designed to gather automation practitioners — not to market Apple's products, but to create a space where people who used automation seriously could learn from each other and from working experts.

His work with macosxautomation.com functions similarly: a free, continuously maintained resource that serves as a reference and discovery point for the community. When he speaks of "this community" — as he did upon leaving Apple in 2016 — he is describing a real constituency of users whose shared practice constitutes a cultural phenomenon, not just a market segment.

The Omni Automation work extended this community-building impulse. Scripts and plug-ins built for Omni applications are shareable via standard URLs and syncable across devices via cloud storage. The design deliberately encourages the community practice of sharing and adapting each other's automation work.

## What the Journey Looks Like in Practice

The automation customer journey Soghoian envisions moves through roughly three phases. In the discovery phase, the user encounters a pre-built shortcut, template, or shared script that does something useful for them and awakens the awareness that automation is available. In the apprentice phase, they begin modifying existing automations and building simple workflows from scratch, using visual tools and guided examples. In the practitioner phase, they develop custom scripting capabilities, begin publishing their own automation work, and often become informal evangelists who introduce others to the discovery phase.

Soghoian's educational work has targeted all three phases, but he has historically been most focused on the transition from apprentice to practitioner — the moment where a user crosses from following recipes to writing them.
