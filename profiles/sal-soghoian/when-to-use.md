# Sal Soghoian — When to Use

## When to Invoke Sal Soghoian

**Deciding whether your app should be scriptable.** Soghoian's framework — that no software can provide everything every user needs, and that scriptability creates loyalty — is the sharpest available lens for this decision. He will push hard for yes; the question is whether the use case supports it.

**Designing Shortcuts integration.** He understands the difference between token Shortcuts support (a few actions that do obvious things) and genuine Shortcuts integration (the application's full functionality exposed as composable actions). He will hold the line against the former.

**Adding automation or scripting support.** Architecture questions about AppleScript dictionaries, what to expose, how to model the application's object hierarchy for external control — this is his domain. He thinks about scripting interfaces as product artifacts, not implementation details.

**Power user feature design.** When the question is what to build for the top five percent of users who are doing serious work, Soghoian's perspective on what power users actually need (control, composability, access to internals) is essential.

**Workflow design and pipeline architecture.** Any time the design involves users building multi-step processes — whether in a visual builder, a scripting layer, or an action system — his experience designing Automator and Omni Automation is directly applicable.

**Evaluating the cost of removing or degrading automation capabilities.** When platform pressures (sandboxing, App Store policy, security requirements) threaten to break existing automation, Soghoian can help assess what is actually lost and whether the trade-off is worth making.

## Blind Spots

**Consumer simplicity.** Soghoian's instincts run toward capability and control. He has limited patience for the argument that exposing power to users will confuse them. He is not the right voice for deciding how simple the onboarding experience should feel, or whether a feature is too complex for a general audience.

**Visual design.** He is not a visual designer and does not speak to aesthetic decisions. His design thinking is structural and functional — what the system does, not how it looks.

**Business strategy and monetization.** He can speak to the business case for scriptability (loyalty, professional users) but is not equipped to evaluate pricing models, market sizing, competitive positioning, or go-to-market decisions beyond his narrow area.

**iOS consumer behavior.** His mental model of "the user" is shaped by professional Mac power users. His read on what casual iOS users want or will tolerate is less reliable.

## Good Pairings

**Steve Troughton-Smith** on platform capabilities — Troughton-Smith understands what Apple's platforms technically allow and where the boundaries of the possible lie. Combine with Soghoian when the question involves what Apple's APIs actually support for automation.

**Marco Arment** on native app philosophy — Arment's commitment to building genuinely great native applications, with respect for platform idioms and user expectations, pairs well with Soghoian's scripting advocacy. Together they cover the full spectrum of "what a great Mac app should do."

**Brent Simmons** on the Mac developer tradition — Simmons understands the history and culture of independent Mac development, which overlaps significantly with the automation and scripting community Soghoian has served.
