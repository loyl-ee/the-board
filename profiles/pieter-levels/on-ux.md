# Pieter Levels — On UX

## Get to Value Immediately

Levels' UX philosophy centers on a single principle: the user must understand what the product does and experience its core value as quickly as possible. He has not articulated a precise "60-second rule" in published writing, but his products are consistently built around the fastest possible path from landing page to meaningful use. Nomad List's homepage is a filterable city list — the value is immediate and requires no signup. Remote OK's homepage is a job list — visible and scannable within seconds of arrival.

The pattern across his products is: show the thing, then ask for money or signup only when the user has already received some value. Requiring an account before showing the product is, in his view, a conversion killer that signals a product not confident in its own value proposition.

## Simplest Possible Onboarding

Levels consistently reduces onboarding to its minimum. For Nomad List's paid membership, onboarding meant: arrive at the site, see the data, click to join, pay. For PhotoAI, onboarding is: upload a photo, see the AI result, then pay for more. The "try before you buy" structure is present in multiple products because it lets the product demonstrate value before asking for commitment.

He validated PhotoAI's demand manually — processing the first 100+ orders by hand before building automation — specifically to learn what the actual onboarding friction was before engineering a solution to it. This is characteristic: understand the real user experience by living it before automating it away.

## Pricing Page Clarity

Levels' pricing pages tend to be direct and simple: one or two tiers, clear feature differentiation, and no obscuring of the price behind "contact sales." He has argued that transparent pricing is both ethical and commercially superior — it pre-qualifies customers, reduces support burden, and signals confidence in the product's value.

In the MAKE book, he notes that "passive income is a myth" and that revenue requires active pricing decisions. He has experimented with pricing levels across his products — Nomad List went from a $5 one-time fee to a $99 lifetime fee to annual recurring subscriptions — each change driven by observed churn and conversion data rather than theoretical optimization.

For Remote OK, revenue comes from job listing fees paid by companies — a B2B pricing decision made by the employer, not the job seeker. This asymmetric pricing (free for the audience, paid for the advertiser) mirrors the logic of free city data (audience) and paid membership (monetization) in Nomad List.

## A/B Testing and Data Use

Levels has discussed using basic analytics and conversion tracking but has not documented sophisticated A/B testing frameworks. His approach is more empirical and direct: make a change, watch what happens to signups and revenue, keep or revert. At the scale he operates (tens of thousands of monthly visitors per product), he has sufficient data to make directional decisions without formal experimentation infrastructure.

His stated preference is for the "closest possible feedback loop" between himself, the product, and the user. Automation handles scale; direct observation handles insight. He has noted that building in public creates a secondary feedback mechanism — when he tweets about a product change, the Twitter replies are themselves a form of user research.

## What He Has Learned From Real Usage

Several documented UX lessons from Levels' product history:

- **Community creates stickiness that features cannot.** Nomad List's Slack community (later moving to its own platform) kept members paying because they were connected to each other, not just to the data. He deliberately underbranded the community as "#nomads" rather than "Nomad List" so it felt like a peer space, not a product marketing channel. (levels.io/build-a-community-around-your-startup/.)

- **Press spikes don't retain users; community does.** He observed that media coverage of Nomad List brought large traffic spikes that quickly decayed. Community members stayed because they had social connections on the platform.

- **Organic discovery signals product strength.** If a product needs paid advertising to attract users, he treats this as a signal that the product is not yet good enough. "If you don't get traffic, your product's just not good enough." (levels.io/startups/.)

- **Mobile users are global users.** Operating as a digital nomad himself, Levels has always prioritized mobile-responsive design, and his analytics consistently showed that a significant share of Nomad List visitors were on mobile devices — people looking up city data while already traveling.

**Gap note**: Levels has not written systematically about UX research methods, usability testing, or formal design systems. His UX decisions are driven primarily by his own direct use of the products, user feedback in community channels, and conversion/retention metrics. This is effective at his scale but underspecified as transferable methodology.
