# Pieter Levels — On Customer Journey

## Building in Public as Customer Acquisition

The most distinctive feature of Levels' customer acquisition model is that it happens in the open, before the product exists in finished form. His practice of sharing build progress, revenue milestones, and product iterations on Twitter (X) does not just attract followers — it creates pre-qualified customers who have watched the product develop and understand its value before they arrive on the landing page.

When PhotoAI launched in February 2023, Levels had 600,000 Twitter followers who had been watching him build in public for nearly a decade. The launch announcement reached an audience that already trusted him, understood his approach, and was predisposed to try the product. This is categorically different from a cold product launch into an anonymous market.

The practical implication is that building in public compresses the customer education phase. A normal product must explain its value proposition on arrival. Levels' products benefit from years of accumulated audience trust that does the explaining in advance.

## Twitter as Customer Development Tool

Levels uses Twitter both to broadcast and to listen. He has noted that replies to his product tweets function as immediate user research — when he announces a feature, the response tells him whether it is aligned with what his audience wants. When he tweets a revenue milestone, the commentary reveals which market segments are watching and what they care about.

He also uses Twitter to test product concepts before building them. An observation about a market gap — posted as a question or observation — generates signal from his audience about whether there is real demand. This is not formal customer development in the traditional product management sense; it is faster and noisier, but at 600K followers, statistically meaningful.

## Handling Customer Support Solo

Levels runs support for multiple products simultaneously as a solo operator. His approach: heavy automation, clear self-service documentation, and a policy of responding personally to complex issues while letting automated systems handle routine requests (refunds, password resets, billing questions).

He has used tools like Stripe's automated refund flow, FAQ pages, and community channels to offload common support questions. The Nomad List Slack community, in particular, functioned as a peer support network where experienced members answered new member questions, reducing direct support demand on Levels himself.

His admitted limitation: as products scale, solo support becomes a bottleneck. He has employed occasional contractors to handle support load during peak periods (major launches, press spikes), but the goal is always to automate the category of question rather than hire permanently for it.

## Community-Led Growth: Nomad List

Nomad List's community is Levels' most deliberate experiment in community-led growth. Observing that his users were tweeting and emailing him daily but had no connection to each other, he created a Slack workspace in 2015 and invited his most engaged users.

Critical decisions that shaped the community's health:
- Named it "#nomads" rather than "Nomad List," giving it a peer identity rather than a product identity.
- Seeded it with "authorities and influencers in the niche" (prominent digital nomad bloggers and tweeters) who set the culture before the community opened to all members.
- Kept growth gradual — inviting users slowly to maintain quality before scaling.
- Made membership require payment — free users could access city data; paying members accessed the community. This price filter meaningfully raised the quality of community participants.

The result: the Nomad List community became a core reason people maintained annual subscriptions. Members were not just paying for data; they were paying for access to thousands of vetted fellow nomads in the same cities. This community moat is genuinely difficult to replicate.

## The Customer Journey Arc

A typical Nomad List customer journey (documented through Levels' public writing):

1. **Discovery**: Arrives via Google search ("best city for remote work"), viral tweet, or press coverage.
2. **Immediate value**: The city comparison data is freely visible on arrival — no signup required to get value.
3. **Soft hook**: Free data is useful but filtered and limited; the full dataset and community require membership.
4. **Conversion trigger**: User planning a trip, preparing to go nomad, or wanting to connect with other nomads converts to paid membership.
5. **Retention**: Community access, nomad meetup features, and ongoing city data updates create reasons to maintain the subscription.
6. **Advocacy**: Active community members introduce the product to new nomads in their network.

This arc — free discovery, immediate value, graduated reveal, community retention — is a deliberate architecture that Levels built through iteration, not planning. The free tier's existence was originally a positioning decision; its role as the top of the acquisition funnel became clear from watching user behavior.

## What Levels Does Not Do

Levels does not run email marketing sequences, outbound sales, or paid advertising campaigns as primary customer acquisition tools. He has used email newsletters for product updates, but primarily as retention tools rather than acquisition. He does not optimize customer journey through funnel analytics in the formal sense — his method is direct observation and revenue signals rather than instrumented funnel tracking.

This means his documented customer journey knowledge is observational and experiential rather than systematic. It is real and hard-won, but it is not transferable as a framework in the way that a formally documented growth process would be.
