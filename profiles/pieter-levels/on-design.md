# Pieter Levels — On Design

## Functional Over Beautiful

Levels freely acknowledges that design is not his primary strength, and he has built this self-awareness into his product philosophy rather than treating it as a deficiency to conceal. His consistent position: an ugly product that works is more valuable than a beautiful product that doesn't — and early validation requires the ugly version first.

He explicitly prefers "a very ugly webpage in the beginning" for new products, citing Google and Facebook's early interfaces as evidence that visual polish is not a prerequisite for product-market fit. His inference is pointed: if a product looks too polished too early, it either signals that a VC spent money on designers before validating the idea, or that the founder is procrastinating on real product work by polishing surfaces.

## The "Ugly But Working" School

Levels' early products — Nomad List 1.0, Remote OK's first version — were visually minimal by any standard. Tables, basic typography, functional but unrefined layouts. He describes his sites as "very minimal and basic" in the MVP essay, not apologetically but as a feature. Simplicity reduces development time, reduces maintenance surface, and keeps the user's attention on the core value rather than decorative elements.

This is a coherent position rather than an excuse. The functional product teaches you what design problems actually need solving. A clean data table that users can filter tells you whether the data model is right; a polished landing page tells you nothing useful.

## When to Invest in Design

Levels does not argue against design investment permanently — only against premature design investment. The "normalization of non-deviance" essay (levels.io/deviance/) describes a maturation point where products that have found product-market fit benefit from professional design work. Once Nomad List had tens of thousands of paying members, visual polish became a legitimate priority because it reduced churn and improved the premium feel of a paid membership product.

His implicit rule: design spend is justified when users are paying and staying, and unjustified before then. Beautiful landing pages with gradients on unvalidated products are a signal, in his view, of over-engineering — and he has said as much about competitor products.

## Bootstrap Design Decisions

In the absence of a designer, Levels applies a set of practical heuristics:

- Use system fonts and avoid web font loading overhead on early MVPs.
- Mobile-first responsive layouts because his products are used globally on mobile.
- Tables and filters over complex UI components — simple to build, immediately functional.
- Minimize images and media that add visual weight without proportional value.
- Copy and typography carry more weight than decorative elements.

His copywriting post ("Copywriting for entrepreneurs: explain your product how you'd explain it to a friend," levels.io, 2020) extends this aesthetic: clarity over cleverness, plain language over marketing speak, direct explanation of what a product does over abstracted positioning.

**Gap note**: Levels has not written extensively about hiring or evaluating designers specifically. His podcast appearances discuss design primarily in the context of what to avoid (over-engineering) rather than what to seek in a design partner. This is a genuine gap in his documented views.
