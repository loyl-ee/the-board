# Pieter Levels — On Business

## The Economics of Solo Products

Levels has demonstrated that solo digital products can achieve economics that most VC-backed startups would envy. Remote OK runs at approximately 94% gross margin (his own figure, shared on the My First Million podcast). Nomad List, at $300K–$400K/year, involves near-zero cost of goods sold. PhotoAI, at $132K MRR in late 2024, runs on GPU compute costs that, while real, leave substantial margins. The portfolio in September 2024 was generating $420K/month at roughly 80% profit — as a single-person operation.

These numbers are possible because Levels has engineered out the costs that kill most businesses: offices, headcount, investor pressure for growth ahead of margins, and marketing spend. His customer acquisition cost is effectively $0 — every product grows through SEO, word of mouth, and the large audience he has built through a decade of building in public.

## Bootstrapping: No VC Required

Levels' anti-VC stance is not romantic contrarianism — it is a practical thesis that he has proven repeatedly. The argument: most internet products that are genuinely useful to a defined niche can reach profitable operation on the revenue they generate from early customers, without needing capital to fund growth to some future profitable state.

His evidence: Nomad List was built for free (or near-free), launched publicly, and was profitable within months. Remote OK was built while Nomad List was generating revenue. Each subsequent product was self-funded by the previous ones. No dilution. No board. No investor updates. Full ownership. "I wanted to prove you don't need to raise venture capital investment." (levels.io/deviance/.)

The practical corollary is that VC funding introduces pressure — growth mandates, hiring mandates, headcount targets — that is actively harmful for products built on the indie model. A product doing $30K/month with 94% margins is an outstanding lifestyle business. To a VC, it is a disappointment. Levels built for the former.

## Charge From Day One

Levels is categorical: the first version of a product must have payment integrated. Free tiers are acceptable when they serve as an acquisition funnel to a paid product (as with Nomad List's free city data leading to paid membership), but the default should be money in exchange for value from the earliest possible point.

His reasoning is that payment is the only true signal. A user who enters their credit card details has demonstrated real demand. A user who signs up for free has demonstrated curiosity, which is vastly weaker evidence. "Monetization is validation" is a phrase from his MAKE book, and it governs his launch approach.

In practice, this means Levels ships a payment button before shipping many features. He processed 100+ orders for Avatar AI (the precursor to PhotoAI) manually, by hand, before automating the pipeline. This validated that people would pay before he invested serious engineering time.

## Building in Public as Business Strategy

The "open startup" approach Levels pioneered is not just philosophical — it is a business strategy with measurable return. Sharing live revenue dashboards, MRR milestones on Twitter, and build logs serves multiple functions simultaneously:

- **Press and media attention**: Journalists covering the indie hacker story consistently cite Nomad List and Remote OK because the numbers are publicly verifiable. Levels estimates he has received millions in press coverage at $0 cost.
- **Customer trust**: Transparency signals confidence in the product. Potential customers can see real revenue numbers, which implies real users, which reduces signup friction.
- **Audience building**: Each revenue update is a content event. "$87K MRR in 17 days" is a shareable tweet that reaches beyond his existing audience. His X follower count grew primarily from these transparency posts.
- **Community effect**: Early adopters who follow the build process feel ownership in the product's success and become advocates.

## The Niche-First Approach

Levels explicitly recommends starting with micro-niches of 1,000–10,000 customers rather than large markets. His arithmetic from the MAKE book: "Just 1,000 people paying $83.33/month = $1M annual revenue." This reframing — that you need far fewer customers than you think — is central to his product strategy.

Large markets attract sophisticated competition, require substantial marketing budgets, and demand enterprise sales cycles. Small niches can be captured by a solo founder who understands the problem personally, can do adequate SEO, and is willing to ship a product good enough for that specific audience. Nomad List targeted digital nomads in 2014 when there were perhaps 100,000 of them globally — a tiny niche that was deeply underserved.

## Avoiding Enterprise

Levels has consistently avoided enterprise sales. His products use simple self-serve pricing (monthly or annual subscriptions, one-time purchases, in-app purchases), require no sales calls, and operate without account management. This is not just a lifestyle preference — it is architecturally correct for a solo operator. Enterprise sales require legal review, security questionnaires, procurement cycles, and relationship management that would destroy the leverage of a one-person operation.

The strategic implication is that Levels targets consumers and prosumers (individuals, freelancers, small teams) who can self-onboard. This limits total addressable market but maximizes revenue per unit of founder time invested.

## Revenue Diversification and Portfolio Thinking

After the 12 startups experiment established the pattern, Levels continued running a portfolio of products rather than concentrating on a single company. In his peak 2024 dashboard, the portfolio included PhotoAI ($161K/month), Remote OK ($93K/month), Nomad List ($61K/month), InteriorAI ($43K/month), and several smaller products — all running simultaneously with minimal marginal cost per product once built.

This portfolio approach hedges against platform risk, market saturation, and product aging. Remote OK has declined slightly as the remote work market normalized post-pandemic; PhotoAI's rapid rise compensated. No single product failure is existential.

## Getting to $1M ARR: The Actual Path

The path Levels walked and documents:

1. Identify a personal problem in a niche you inhabit.
2. Build the minimum viable version (days to weeks, not months).
3. Launch publicly on Product Hunt, Hacker News, Reddit, and Twitter simultaneously.
4. Charge immediately; iterate based on who pays and who complains.
5. Automate everything that can be automated to extend solo capacity.
6. Use the revenue and audience from Product A to launch Product B.
7. Repeat. The portfolio compounds.

The total timeline from his 2014 challenge to $1M ARR was approximately 5–6 years, but the curve accelerated dramatically once the audience was built and the automation infrastructure was in place.
