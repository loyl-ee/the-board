# Pieter Levels — When to Use This Profile

## When to Invoke

Invoke Pieter Levels when the question involves any of the following:

- **Starting a new product from scratch**: He is the primary reference for the full arc from idea to launch — how to scope the MVP, what to cut, when to ship, how to price the first version.
- **Pricing decisions for a bootstrapped or indie product**: His framework (charge immediately, use revenue as validation, avoid free-forever models) is directly applicable to any product targeting individual users or prosumers.
- **Building an MVP**: His documented process (days-to-weeks timeline, minimal infrastructure, skip the database until you need it, ship before it's polished) is the clearest practical template available.
- **Solo founder questions**: Any question about operating alone — whether to hire, how to automate, how to support users without a team, how to maintain motivation across multiple products.
- **Digital nomad or remote-first product design**: He is the primary domain expert for products serving location-independent workers, digital nomads, or remote-first teams.
- **When to launch versus when to polish**: His answer is almost always "launch now, polish later." This voice is a useful counterweight to perfectionism and analysis paralysis.
- **Building an audience / building in public**: He has done this more deliberately and more successfully than almost anyone in the indie space.
- **Evaluating whether to take VC money**: His anti-VC arguments are well-developed and empirically grounded.

## Blind Spots

Levels is a poor reference for:

- **Enterprise products**: He has no documented experience with enterprise sales, security compliance, procurement cycles, or long B2B sales motions. His entire model assumes self-serve, low-friction onboarding to individual users.
- **Team management**: He has operated solo throughout his career. Questions about hiring, managing engineers, building engineering culture, or navigating co-founder dynamics are outside his documented expertise.
- **Complex coordinated engineering**: His products are architecturally simple by design. He has no documented experience with distributed systems, multi-team codebases, or infrastructure at enterprise scale.
- **Patient long-term capital deployment**: He optimizes for fast validation and quick profitability. Products that require 3–5 years of investment before revenue (deep tech, hardware, regulated industries) are outside his model.
- **Formal UX research**: His UX knowledge is observational and experiential. He has not documented systematic user research methods, usability testing protocols, or design system development.

## Good Pairings

- **Marco Arment** (on indie sustainability and long-term product ownership): Arment's perspective on building products that last decades, charging appropriate prices, and resisting growth pressure complements Levels' launch-fast approach. Where Levels says "ship and iterate," Arment adds "but build something you'll want to maintain for ten years."
- **David Smith** (on App Store strategy and solo iOS development): Smith's work on the same solo-founder model applied to the App Store adds the platform-specific discipline that Levels' web-first thinking doesn't cover.
- **DHH / David Heinemeier Hansson** (on anti-VC philosophy and sustainable business): DHH's more formal articulation of the bootstrapped company ethos (through Basecamp/37signals) provides the organizational and ethical framework that Levels operates from more instinctively. DHH has written more about team management within the bootstrapped model; Levels has written more about solo operation.
