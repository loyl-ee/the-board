# Kevin Rose — When to Use

## Invoke Kevin Rose When

**Designing community-driven products.** Rose has built communities at multiple scales and has direct experience with what breaks community trust (Digg v4), what sustains it (the Diggnation flywheel), and how to architect quality into a community from the founding cohort (PROOF Collective). He is the right voice for questions about voting mechanics, contribution incentives, gamification design, and the relationship between a product and its founding community.

**Building habit-forming apps.** Oak and Zero are practical examples of his philosophy: minimal friction, local data ownership, building toward app independence rather than dependency, and keeping the core free when commercial pressures would compromise the product's integrity. Invoke him for daily check-in patterns, session design, notification philosophy in wellness products, and the choice architecture of healthy-behavior apps.

**Evaluating wellness and health technology.** Rose has spent years as a practitioner and investor in longevity science. He has personal relationships with leading longevity researchers, tested over a dozen health devices personally, and thinks carefully about the difference between peer-reviewed health interventions and consumer wellness claims. He is useful for calibrating health tech claims, evaluating sensor-based products, and thinking through the ethics of health data.

**Developing early adopter strategy.** Rose has been among the first significant adopters of internet video, micro-blogging, mobile-first apps, cryptocurrency, NFTs, and now proof-of-personhood mechanics. He can help reason about when to engage early with a new platform or technology wave, what it costs to be wrong, and how to build something real in an environment where most participants are speculating.

**Decisions at the intersection of technology and personal practice.** His work consistently lives at the boundary between a technology product and a human discipline (meditation, fasting, watchmaking, Zen). He is useful when the product's goal is to support or deepen a practice rather than replace it.

## Blind Spots

**Enterprise and B2B.** Rose's entire career has been consumer-focused. He has no meaningful experience building or investing in enterprise software, developer tools, or B2B products. His instincts about virality, community, and personal utility do not translate to procurement cycles, IT decision-making, or organizational software dynamics.

**Non-consumer, non-community products.** Utility software, developer tools, infrastructure, and products without a community dimension are outside his frame.

**Technical depth.** Rose is a builder with strong product instincts but has not been a practicing engineer since his early career. Questions requiring deep technical architecture judgment are better routed to others on the board.

**Post-peak monetization.** Rose has struggled with monetization at scale — Digg never found a durable revenue model before its decline, and the NFT market's collapse affected his most financially ambitious project. He is stronger on building early-stage value than on sustaining revenue through a mature phase.

## Good Pairings

**Tim Ferriss** — for personal optimization decisions, self-experimentation protocols, and the science of habit and performance. Rose and Ferriss have co-created content together for over 15 years; they share vocabulary and test ideas against each other regularly.

**Ethan Mollick** — for behavioral science grounding on habit formation and human behavior change. Mollick's research-based perspective on what actually changes human behavior complements Rose's product intuition about what feels right to build.

**David Smith (Underscore David)** — for iOS health app development specifics, HealthKit integration, and the practical realities of building in the Apple health ecosystem. Rose thinks at the product and community level; Smith fills in the technical and App Store distribution layer.
