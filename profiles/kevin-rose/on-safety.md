# Kevin Rose — On Safety

## Health Data Responsibility in Wellness Apps

Rose's approach to health data in his own products reflects a consistent philosophy of user ownership. When he launched Zero in 2017, he explicitly built local device storage as the default — user fasting data was stored on-device and exportable, not held in a cloud database controlled by the company. He wrote: "All of this data is stored on your device and is available for you to export at any time for further analysis." This was a genuine design choice, made before health data privacy was a regulatory or public relations concern for most consumer apps.

He also made a point of recommending users consult their doctors before beginning any fasting protocol, and he grounded the app's educational content in peer-reviewed research rather than trend content. In the Zero announcement, he distinguished personal experimentation from validated health interventions: "I knew this was a fun body hack to lose weight, but that doesn't mean it's healthy for you. To assess the health benefits (both positive and negative), we need to turn to the only data we can truly trust: research published by scientists in peer-reviewed journals."

These are modest but meaningful commitments. They reflect awareness that health apps occupy a different ethical register than social or productivity apps — the data they collect is intimate and potentially medically significant.

## The Technology-as-Double-Edge-Sword Acknowledgment

In his True Ventures interview on wellness apps, Rose acknowledged that technology in wellness is "a vicious double-edged sword" — powerful for building healthy habits but equally powerful for enabling compulsive or harmful behaviors. He pointed to social media addiction as the clearest example of technology optimizing for engagement at the cost of user wellbeing. This acknowledgment is unusually direct for a technology investor, most of whom prefer to attribute technology's harms to misuse rather than design choices.

His practice of keeping Oak free — funded personally rather than through subscriptions or advertising — is the operational consequence of this belief. He described not wanting to "go down that same path" as apps that charge for content, recognizing that monetization models create design incentives that can work against the user's actual interests.

## The NFT Period: Ethical Gaps

Rose's involvement with PROOF Collective and Moonbirds raises ethical questions that deserve honest acknowledgment rather than elision.

**Market dynamics and retail exposure.** Moonbirds generated $280 million in primary and secondary sales in two days, much of it from retail buyers purchasing at peak prices. The subsequent NFT market collapse saw Moonbirds lose approximately 95% of its value from peak. Rose has acknowledged the difficulty of this period — "Holy shit have the last few months been tough" — but has not addressed in detail the ethics of launching into a speculative frenzy or the adequacy of risk disclosure to retail buyers.

**The CC0 licensing controversy.** PROOF's decision to open-source the commercial rights to Moonbirds and Oddities — moving them to Creative Commons Zero licensing — was made without a community vote and was received by many Moonbirds holders as a unilateral change to the terms of what they had purchased. Rose defended the decision as philosophically aligned with open-source values, but it represented exactly the kind of implicit contract renegotiation that he identified as Digg's fatal error. The community response was sharp enough to be noted in multiple press reports.

**NFT wallet hack.** In January 2023, Rose's NFT wallet was hacked in a phishing attack, resulting in the theft of approximately $1.5 million in high-value NFTs. The incident exposed the security vulnerabilities of self-custody for high-value digital assets — an honest illustration of the risks Rose and others had encouraged retail participants to accept.

## Honest Gap: Absence of Systematic Ethics Writing

Rose has not published systematic thinking about the ethics of wellness technology, community platforms, or digital asset markets. His ethical positions are implicit in product decisions and occasional interview statements rather than explicit in written frameworks. This limits the confidence with which his views on safety and responsibility can be characterized beyond what his product choices directly demonstrate.
