# Kevin Rose — On Business

## The Digg Years: Rise and the Fall

Digg launched in December 2004 with minimal funding. Rose favored bootstrapping — he has said "bootstrapping is the best way; I hate to go out and raise money unless you can prove out an idea first" — and Digg's early growth was deliberately slow. He later recalled, "Digg has never been a viral site in any way. It's always been month over month slow growth." That organic growth built a community with genuine investment in the platform's success.

By 2007, Digg had reached a $164 million valuation and was in acquisition discussions with Google and News Corp. Rose famously turned down offers to retain control. The platform reached 38 million monthly users at its peak.

The fall came from the 2010 v4 redesign. Rose and the Digg team, watching traffic decline, tried to roll out a sweeping redesign and new features simultaneously. He later acknowledged that the mistake was not listening to the community: "We pushed too many things at once." The redesign stripped features users depended on, prioritized publisher relationships over user-submitted content, and launched with significant technical failures. Users migrated to Reddit en masse — Reddit's traffic grew 230% in 2010 while Digg's collapsed. Rose resigned from operations in March 2011, and Betaworks acquired Digg's brand and technology for $500,000 in 2012.

The lesson Rose carried forward was about the irreplaceable nature of community trust. He has said that the Digg experience was why he eventually built Oak — anxiety from the Digg collapse led him to meditation, which led to building a product around it.

## Google Ventures and the Investor Perspective

After Google acquired his mobile app studio Milk in 2012, Rose spent roughly three years as a General Partner at Google Ventures (now GV). His investments there included Nextdoor, Medium, Uber, Ripple, Blue Bottle Coffee, and Slack. The experience gave him the other side of the founder-investor relationship: he evaluated companies the way he wished investors had evaluated Digg — with attention to community quality, product clarity, and team cohesion rather than just metrics.

Rose's approach at GV reflected his product background. He invested emotionally first, analytically second — a method he has written about explicitly, describing a process of inhabiting a product as a user before running financial models.

## The Wellness App Era: Oak and Zero

In 2017, Rose launched both Oak and Zero in the same year — a deliberate moment of returning to building after years as an investor. Both apps emerged directly from personal practice: his meditation routine (intensified after the Digg period) produced Oak; his exploration of intermittent fasting, guided by circadian rhythm research from Dr. Satchin Panda, produced Zero.

Oak remained free, with Rose personally funding its development to avoid the commercial pressures that he felt corrupted competitor wellness apps. Zero grew to 25,000 new users per day with zero paid acquisition by 2020, a number Rose cited as evidence that genuine personal utility creates organic distribution. He sold Zero to Big Sky Health in 2018, retaining an advisory role, and the company subsequently raised $8 million in a Greycroft-led Series A.

## Hodinkee and the Niche Brand Playbook

Between his GV tenure and True Ventures partnership, Rose led Hodinkee as CEO for roughly two years (2015–2017). He merged his watch news app Watchville with Hodinkee after recognizing the brand's superior audience quality. During his tenure, Hodinkee tripled its team to 22 people, grew traffic by 35%, more than doubled revenue, and exited 2016 profitably. The Hodinkee period demonstrated his ability to apply Silicon Valley product and growth thinking to passion-economy niche media.

## True Ventures and Portfolio Philosophy

Rose joined True Ventures as a full Partner in 2017 and became a formal partner on the investment team in 2025. He focuses on consumer internet, life sciences, and AI. His portfolio reflects the same patterns visible in his founder career: companies at the intersection of technology and human practice, early bets on community products, and health/longevity technology. His investments include Oura, Wealthfront, and positions in Solana, OpenAI, and Anthropic.

He describes his investment style as contrarian-by-inclination. He prefers ideas that are "not me-too ideas" — not improvements on existing categories but bets on emerging behaviors that most investors dismiss as too early or too weird.

## PROOF, Moonbirds, and the NFT Period

PROOF launched in 2021 as a Web3 media company with Rose as co-founder alongside Justin Mezzell. The PROOF Collective sold 1,000 NFT memberships at 1 ETH each, creating a curated community of collectors. From that base, Moonbirds launched in April 2022, generating $280 million in primary and secondary sales in two days. Rose raised $50 million for PROOF from a16z and others.

The subsequent NFT market collapse was severe. Rose acknowledged the difficulty openly: "Holy shit have the last few months been tough." He reflected on the experience as a lesson about building during euphoria: "When everything is just 'up and to the right' and crazy and chaotic...it's easy to lose your true north." PROOF was acquired by Yuga Labs in 2023. Rose has continued to hold NFTs and engage with the space, though at a more measured pace.

## Digg Redux: The 2025 Relaunch

In 2025, Rose reacquired the Digg brand alongside Alexis Ohanian and relaunched it as Digg Labs, focused on proof-of-personhood mechanics, human community in the AI era, and technical minimalism. The relaunch represents a direct application of the lessons from the original Digg's failure: start smaller, protect community trust, and build verification into the platform's foundation rather than bolting it on later.
