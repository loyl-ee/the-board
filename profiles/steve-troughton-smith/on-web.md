# Steve Troughton-Smith — On the Web

## High Caffeine Content: The Blog

Troughton-Smith's primary long-form writing home is highcaffeinecontent.com, which functions as a technical journal covering platform capabilities, developer tooling, and the state of Apple software. Posts are infrequent but substantive: detailed analyses of Mac Catalyst's shortfalls, the iPad Pro Manifesto series, virtualization guides for Apple Silicon, and explorations of AI-assisted development workflows. The blog does not run ads, does not chase traffic, and is not optimized for discoverability — it is a record of things he found worth writing down in full.

Notable posts include:
- "Where Mac Catalyst Falls Short" (February 2022) — a comprehensive technical audit of UIKit on macOS
- "The iPad Pro Manifesto (2024 Edition)" (May 2024) — the fifth-year update of his platform capability critique
- "A Month With OpenAI's Codex" (March 2026) — an empirical account of AI-assisted multiplatform development

## Mastodon as the Right Home

He moved from Twitter to Mastodon in late 2022 and has remained there exclusively, at @stroughtonsmith@mastodon.social. His view of Mastodon is consistent with his broader open-web orientation: the fediverse's interoperability and lack of algorithmic amplification suit his communication style better than ad-supported platforms. He posted in July 2023 about Threads: "As long as it remains open/interoperable, that's a good thing. Meta is free to do whatever it likes over there that would never fly here, but may be necessary to build a well-rounded service of the like that can fully replace what came before." — a pragmatic rather than ideological position on federated social media.

## Web Standards and Platform Lock-In

His advocacy for web standards sits somewhat in tension with his deep Apple platform focus. He is critical of Apple's restrictions on third-party browser engines on iOS (effectively requiring all iOS browsers to use WebKit), seeing this as a competitive moat that harms web standards progress on mobile. His DMA commentary extended to Apple's web distribution policies, which he argued undermined the EU's intent to enable genuine alternative app distribution.

He does not have a strong "web-first" position — his work is firmly native-app-centered — but he consistently opposes ecosystem lock-in mechanisms that restrict user and developer choice, whether those mechanisms operate at the app store level, the browser engine level, or the social platform level.

## GitHub as Developer-Facing Web Presence

His GitHub (github.com/steventroughtonsmith) functions as a public technical portfolio — sample code, utilities, and experimental projects that show his platform work in executable form. It is, in many respects, the most useful part of his web presence for developers who want to learn from his techniques rather than just read his analysis.
