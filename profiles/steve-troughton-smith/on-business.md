# Steve Troughton-Smith — On Business

## The Indie Developer Model

Troughton-Smith has operated as a solo independent developer since 2007, with a brief period as a developer at Tapadoo before returning to independent work under his own studio, High Caffeine Content. His business model is a blend of App Store revenue, Patreon support, and Gumroad product sales — a structure that reflects both the economics of the App Store era and a deliberate choice to maintain commentary independence.

He has been building apps for Apple platforms for nearly two decades, which means he has lived through every major App Store policy shift, pricing model change, and competitive encroachment from Apple's own first-party apps. His commentary on Apple's business practices comes from that lived experience, not from abstract analysis.

## Patreon as Sustainability Tool

Troughton-Smith has used Patreon since at least 2017, when John Gruber linked to his campaign on Daring Fireball, describing him as "extraordinarily clever and extraordinarily generous about sharing what he learns with the world." The Patreon funds the commentary, sample code, and research work that does not have a direct App Store revenue line — the firmware analysis, the blog posts, the developer advocacy. This direct support model allows him to spend time on work that benefits the community without needing to tie every hour to a billable product.

## Views on the App Store for Developer Tools

Troughton-Smith has a complicated relationship with the App Store as a distribution channel for his own work. Several of his apps — Scarlet, Broadcasts, Pastel — are App Store products with paid unlocks or in-app purchases. He has also sold products directly via Gumroad, preserving the ability to reach developers outside the App Store's constraints.

His broader critique of the App Store is that it has not been a fair marketplace, particularly for developer tools. Apple's own apps benefit from private APIs, internal entitlements, and the ability to install and run code that would be rejected from the App Store. A third-party developer IDE cannot replicate what Apple's own Playgrounds ships — not because of technical inability, but because Apple reserves those entitlements for itself.

He described the relationship between Apple and developers plainly in a 2022 post: "iOS and the App Store has not been a truce. It has been a destructive, abusive, lopsided, mistrustful relationship" (take.surf, April 2022). This is not a position he arrived at quickly — it reflects years of watching the platform evolve in ways that consistently disadvantaged independent developers while benefiting Apple's own applications.

## Scarlet as a Product Philosophy Statement

His app Scarlet — a local-first issue tracker that stores data in a plain file within your project directory, supports version control alongside your code, and requires no accounts or cloud services — is a direct expression of his product values applied to business: respect for user data, no rent-seeking, no ecosystem lock-in. It is a small but coherent statement about how developer tools should behave.
