# Demis Hassabis — On Business

## DeepMind's Unusual Model

DeepMind was designed from the outset to be something that didn't easily fit existing categories: a for-profit research laboratory pursuing fundamental science with the explicit goal of building artificial general intelligence. Hassabis and his co-founders — Shane Legg and Mustafa Suleyman — were deliberate about this structure. They wanted the resources of a commercial company and the intellectual culture of an academic institution. They believed these were not irreconcilable, but they knew the combination required careful institutional design to sustain.

The early funding model — venture capital from investors including Founders Fund and Horizons Ventures — gave DeepMind runway to pursue basic research without immediate revenue pressure. Hassabis has described this period as essential: the money allowed DeepMind scientists the freedom to do blue-sky research without worrying as much about revenue, resulting in the breakthroughs (DQN, AlphaGo) that built the lab's reputation and justified the Google acquisition.

## The Google Acquisition and the Independence Question

In 2014, Google acquired DeepMind for approximately £400 million. Hassabis has been candid that the decision involved weighing multiple options — Facebook was also in discussions, and Elon Musk had approached the co-founders. Google was chosen because it offered the compute infrastructure DeepMind needed, a stated commitment to allowing the lab to operate with independence, and an ethics board as a condition of the deal.

That independence was more contested than the public narrative suggested. According to Sebastian Mallaby's biography *The Infinity Machine* (2026), Hassabis and Suleyman actually spent three years working with lawyers and bankers to try to spin DeepMind back out of Google as an independent entity, recruiting Reid Hoffman to pledge $1 billion to support a buyout. The effort ultimately failed, and DeepMind remained within Alphabet.

The practical implication is that Hassabis has had to manage a fundamental tension throughout his tenure: DeepMind's research culture and mission require long time horizons and willingness to pursue problems with no immediate commercial application, while its corporate parent has commercial imperatives and shareholder obligations. His management of this tension — publishing AlphaFold results in *Nature*, open-sourcing the code, releasing the protein database freely — has generally prioritized the scientific mission over short-term commercial capture.

## The 2023 Merger with Google Brain

When Google merged DeepMind and Google Brain into Google DeepMind in 2023, with Hassabis as CEO of the combined entity, it represented both an expansion of his authority and an intensification of commercial pressure. He described the post-merger period as one of deliberate acceleration — aligning talent "from around the company, sort of pushing in one direction" — while gaining access to Google's compute infrastructure at a scale DeepMind had previously lacked. He has been explicit that he still attempts to operate Google DeepMind "like a start-up" in terms of pace and focus.

## Scientific Publishing vs. Commercialization

One of Hassabis's most important business decisions was the choice to publish AlphaFold's results openly, release the code under open-source licenses, and make the protein structure database freely available to any researcher in the world. He called it "a gift to humanity." This decision was not commercially obvious — the structures could plausibly have been licensed, or the tool built into a subscription service. The rationale was philosophical: if the mission is to accelerate science, locking the output behind a paywall defeats the purpose. The goodwill and scientific credibility generated by the free release also proved strategically valuable, reinforcing DeepMind's identity as a scientific institution rather than a product company.

The AlphaFold 3 situation created more friction. DeepMind initially withheld the model weights when releasing AlphaFold 3 in 2024, facing significant criticism from the scientific community for impeding reproducibility. Following sustained pressure, DeepMind subsequently released the weights, consistent with its broader open-science commitments.

## Building a Culture That Attracts World-Class Researchers

Hassabis has spoken about his explicit aspiration to recreate the culture of Bell Labs in its golden era — an environment where world-class researchers from diverse disciplines (mathematics, physics, neuroscience, biology, philosophy) work in proximity, supported by institutional resources but free to pursue ambitious questions. He has described the goal as creating "an incredible melting pot" where the collision of disciplines produces unexpected insight.

Attracting and retaining this caliber of researcher in a commercial company requires explicit commitments: the right to publish, protected time for fundamental research, freedom to pursue questions without guaranteed commercial payoff, and a shared belief in the mission. These commitments have sometimes put DeepMind in tension with Google's product priorities, but Hassabis has consistently treated them as non-negotiable for maintaining the lab's scientific credibility.

He has also noted that London's position outside Silicon Valley is a deliberate asset — it provides access to European academic talent and creates a working environment less dominated by the culture of rapid product iteration and short-term commercial thinking.
