# Demis Hassabis — On Product

## Research-to-Product as a Long Game

Hassabis approaches product development with the patience of a scientist. For him, the path from research breakthrough to deployable tool is not a sprint — it is a sequence of careful steps, each requiring its own disciplines. DeepMind's history demonstrates this clearly: the Deep Q-Network paper was published in *Nature* in 2015; the underlying techniques eventually found their way into data center optimization tools and, much later, into consumer-facing AI. AlphaFold's initial results appeared at CASP13 in 2018; the full system launched publicly in 2021; clinical applications in drug discovery are only now entering human trials in 2026. This is an eight-year product arc for what most observers would consider a single breakthrough.

His implicit product philosophy is that the research must be fundamentally correct before the product can be meaningful. There is no point shipping a tool that is impressive but wrong. In biology, a wrong protein structure prediction used in drug design could waste years of laboratory effort or, worse, harm patients. This conviction that correctness precedes deployment distinguishes Hassabis from product leaders who accept "good enough" in the interest of speed.

## AlphaFold as Product: The Free Database Decision

AlphaFold's deployment is one of the most significant product decisions in the history of science communication. DeepMind chose to:

1. Publish the underlying research in *Nature* (2021), with full methods.
2. Open-source the code under permissive licenses.
3. Partner with EMBL-EBI to host the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database — containing predictions for all 200 million known proteins — freely accessible to any researcher, anywhere, at no cost.

By 2025, over 3 million researchers from 190 countries had used the database. Hassabis described the release as "a gift to humanity." The deliberate choice to make it free — rather than licensing it or building a subscription product — reflects his view that the primary beneficiary of AI-accelerated science should be the scientific community as a whole, not a particular company's revenue line. The secondary effect was significant: the free release created enormous goodwill and established DeepMind as a serious scientific institution with a mission that transcended commercial competition.

The AlphaFold 3 release in 2024 complicated this narrative somewhat. DeepMind initially withheld the model weights, citing concerns about misuse in synthetic biology. This generated substantial criticism from the scientific community and was eventually walked back. The episode illustrates a genuine tension in Hassabis's product philosophy: open access serves the mission, but some capabilities may require guardrails before release.

## Isomorphic Labs: From Tool to Treatment

The founding of Isomorphic Labs in 2021 — as a separate Alphabet company with Hassabis as CEO — represents the downstream product layer of AlphaFold. Where AlphaFold itself is a tool for researchers, Isomorphic is attempting to build drug candidates directly using AI-driven molecular design. Its partnerships with Eli Lilly and Novartis (worth up to $3 billion in milestone payments) and its first AI-designed cancer drug entering Phase 1 clinical trials in early 2026 represent the commercial realization of the research Hassabis has been building toward since DeepMind's founding.

This structure — open scientific tool (AlphaFold DB) plus commercial application layer (Isomorphic) — reflects a deliberate product architecture: democratize the underlying capability while building proprietary value at the application layer where domain expertise and regulatory knowledge matter.

## Gemini and the Consumer AI Layer

As CEO of Google DeepMind, Hassabis also oversees Gemini, Google's flagship AI model. His stated goal for Gemini is ambitious: a "universal assistant" that is genuinely helpful in daily life, consulted multiple times per day, deployed across devices including smart glasses. He frames this as a continuation of the same research agenda — building general-purpose AI that is useful across domains — but at consumer scale. He acknowledges that the challenges are different at consumer scale: reliability, safety, factual accuracy, and trust become dominant concerns in ways that pure research contexts do not demand.

Hassabis is not a consumer product operator by background, and he is candid about this. His strength is the research agenda that produces the capabilities; translating those capabilities into products that people actually use and trust is a second discipline that requires its own expertise.
