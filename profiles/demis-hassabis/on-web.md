# Demis Hassabis — On Web

## Note on Evidence

Hassabis has not given detailed talks or written extensively about web strategy, web design, or digital marketing. This profile is honest about that gap. What can be assembled is his approach to DeepMind's digital presence as a scientific communication platform, his views on open science vs. competitive secrecy, and his own use of the web as a publication and communication channel.

## DeepMind's Web Presence as Scientific Communication

DeepMind's website and blog function primarily as a scientific publication and communication channel rather than a conventional product or marketing platform. The organization has published over 2,000 research papers, including more than two dozen in *Nature* and *Science*, and its digital presence reflects this identity: research announcements, technical blog posts, and links to peer-reviewed publications are the primary content.

This is a deliberate positioning choice consistent with Hassabis's broader philosophy. DeepMind does not primarily compete on marketing; it competes on scientific credibility. Its web presence is designed to communicate to other researchers, potential recruits, and sophisticated observers who care about the rigor of the underlying work, not consumers who need to understand a product.

The AlphaFold database (alphafold.ebi.ac.uk), hosted by EMBL-EBI rather than on Google's infrastructure, reflects a preference for embedding in neutral scientific infrastructure over proprietary platform-building. The choice to make scientific data accessible through an established academic partner, rather than a branded DeepMind portal, communicates the organization's self-understanding: as a scientific institution first, a technology company second.

## Open Science vs. Competitive Secrecy

Hassabis has consistently advocated for open publication of research findings — the AlphaFold results were published in *Nature* with full methods, and the code was open-sourced under permissive licenses. He argues that locking scientific breakthroughs behind proprietary barriers defeats the mission of accelerating science. The network effects of open science — more researchers using the tool, more citations, more feedback, more downstream discoveries — ultimately outweigh the commercial value of secrecy.

The AlphaFold 3 episode created some tension with this position when DeepMind initially withheld model weights. It illustrates that even Hassabis navigates genuine tradeoffs between open access and concerns about misuse, particularly in dual-use biological research contexts.

## Personal Web Presence

Hassabis maintains an active presence on X (@demishassabis) and LinkedIn, primarily using them to announce major research milestones, share views on AI development and safety, and respond to public discourse about DeepMind's work. He is not a prolific content creator — his posts tend to mark significant events rather than sustain ongoing commentary. He also maintains a Google Scholar profile listing academic citations and has authored posts on Google's official blog around major announcements.

## Honest Gap

Hassabis has no documented views on web performance, SEO, content strategy, digital marketing, conversion funnels, or the commercial dimensions of web presence. Invoking him on these topics would require significant extrapolation from adjacent principles.
