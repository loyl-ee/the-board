# Demis Hassabis — On Customer Journey

## The Scientist as Primary Beneficiary

Hassabis's model of who AI systems are ultimately for is rooted in science. His primary mental model of the "customer" is the working scientist — a domain expert with deep knowledge of a problem who needs AI tools to amplify their capacity to explore the solution space, not to replace their judgment about what the problem is. This is not a commercial consumer model; it is a scientific collaboration model.

He has articulated this consistently: "The best scientists paired with these tools will be able to do incredible things." (Nobel Prize interview, 2024.) And with AlphaFold: "These systems, they're tools. They're very good at analyzing data... they can't figure out what the right question is to ask." The scientist brings the question; the AI brings the computational capacity to explore it.

This framing has a specific implication for how the user journey is designed: the on-ramp for the AI tool must be accessible to domain experts who are not AI specialists. The journey is not from "curious person" to "capable user" — it is from "accomplished scientist with a specific problem" to "scientist with a powerful new instrument." The customer already knows what they want; the tool needs to get out of the way and deliver it reliably.

## The Research Partner Model

DeepMind's relationship with EMBL-EBI for the AlphaFold database is a model for how Hassabis thinks about customer journey at an institutional level. Rather than building a proprietary platform that researchers have to adopt from scratch, DeepMind embedded AlphaFold into existing scientific infrastructure. EMBL-EBI already hosts the UniProt protein database that scientists use daily. The AlphaFold database sits alongside it, using the same data standards, the same interfaces, the same workflows. The user journey is: "you're already here, and now this tool is here too."

This is a deliberate reduction of adoption friction by meeting researchers where they already are, rather than asking them to come to a new platform. It also builds institutional trust — EMBL-EBI's credibility as a neutral scientific infrastructure provider is extended to AlphaFold. A biologist who trusts UniProt can extend that trust to AlphaFold data more readily than if DeepMind had hosted it alone.

## Designing for Expert Amplification

Hassabis's view of the customer journey for AI tools is fundamentally different from the consumer AI journey. The consumer AI journey often focuses on onboarding non-experts — explaining what the tool does, teaching them its affordances, building habits. Hassabis's customer is someone who already knows exactly what they need. The journey is about trust, reliability, and depth of capability rather than breadth of appeal.

This has specific implications: errors are more costly (a scientist who trusts a wrong prediction may waste months); honest uncertainty communication is essential (hence the confidence scoring in AlphaFold); and the tool's value is measured not in engagement metrics but in scientific output — papers published, compounds discovered, hypotheses generated.

## Scaling the Beneficiary Class

Hassabis's longer-term vision extends the beneficiary class from individual scientists to humanity at large. The 3 million researchers using AlphaFold today are the first wave. The downstream users — patients who benefit from drugs designed using AI-predicted structures, farmers who benefit from crop-resilience research, communities who benefit from materials science advances — are the ultimate customers, even if they never interact with the tool directly. He describes this as the "gift to humanity" framing: the customer journey ends not at the scientist's desk but at its furthest social consequences.

This long-tail customer model is philosophically important. It means that the value of the journey cannot be fully measured at the point of use. The proper metric of success for AlphaFold is not sessions or satisfaction scores — it is the acceleration of discovery across the global scientific community over decades. Hassabis is one of the few technology leaders who reasons systematically about this kind of diffuse, long-horizon value.
