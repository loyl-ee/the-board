# Demis Hassabis — On Safety

## Safety as a First-Order Commitment

Hassabis is among the small number of leading AI researchers who treat safety not as a constraint on capability development but as a co-equal research agenda that must be solved in parallel with, and in some cases before, deploying more powerful systems. He has stated this since DeepMind's founding: "Even back in 2010 when we started DeepMind from the beginning, we planned for success... ethics and safety as key components." (TED interview.) In 2015, he argued that concerns about advanced AI should be "discussed and debated now, decades before there's anything that's actually of any potential consequence or power that we need to worry about." (BBC, 2015.) This is not a position he arrived at under public pressure — it predates the current mainstream anxiety about AI risk.

In 2023, he signed the Center for AI Safety's statement that "mitigating the risk of extinction from AI should be a global priority alongside other societal-scale risks such as pandemics and nuclear war." He confirmed this publicly on X: "I've worked my whole life on AI because I believe in its incredible potential to advance science & medicine, and improve billions of people's lives. But as with any transformative technology we should apply the precautionary principle, and build & deploy it with exceptional care." (X, May 2023.)

## His Specific Concerns

Hassabis identifies two distinct categories of AI risk:

**Near-term misuse risks.** He is explicit that the most immediate threats are not from rogue AI systems but from human actors using AI capabilities for malicious ends. He has flagged bioweapons specifically — AI tools for protein design and molecular modeling could enable bad actors to engineer dangerous pathogens — and cyberattacks, where AI-assisted exploitation of vulnerabilities creates an asymmetric threat. As he told reporters: "This technology has such potential for enormous, enormous good, but it's a dual-use technology. So if bad actors get hold of it, it could be used for bad things." (New York Times, 2023.) He has stated that he worries "more about the humans — the bad actors — than machines" in the near term.

**Long-term alignment and controllability risks.** Hassabis acknowledges deep uncertainty about whether AGI or superintelligence systems would be controllable, and what their values and goals would be. He refuses to assign a numerical probability to catastrophic outcomes: "I don't have a p(doom) number because I think it would imply a level of precision that is not there." But he is explicit that the risk is "definitely non-zero and probably non-negligible. So that in itself is pretty sobering." He has expressed concern about unknown controllability — the possibility that when systems approach AGI, researchers encounter "really hard problems that are harder than we guess today." He does not assume these problems will be solvable on the timescales that capability development is moving.

## What He Calls For

On the research front, Hassabis has called for ten times more investment in AI safety research as systems approach AGI. He argues that safety research must be driven by the same scientific rigor as capability research — testable hypotheses, careful measurement, peer review — rather than by intuition or theoretical speculation. He is critical of approaches to safety that are untethered from empirical understanding of how models actually behave.

On governance, he has called for an international regulatory body for AI comparable to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) — a body capable of assessing risk at a global level, coordinating between governments, and providing authoritative scientific guidance. He has warned that AI's risks are "comparable to climate change" in their potential societal impact and require comparable institutional responses. He has also advocated for AI safety evaluation frameworks — structured tests of AI capability and controllability — as a prerequisite for deploying more capable systems.

## Interpretability Research

DeepMind has invested in interpretability research — the attempt to understand what is happening inside neural networks, not just what outputs they produce. Hassabis believes that progress on interpretability is essential for trust in AI systems: you cannot safely deploy a system whose decision-making is a black box, especially in high-stakes domains like medicine, scientific research, or policy. This is one area where he is more aligned with Anthropic's mechanistic interpretability agenda than with pure scaling advocates.

## Disagreements with Pure Accelerationists

Hassabis is not a pure accelerationist. He has explicitly stated that the "move fast and break things" approach that may be acceptable in consumer software is fundamentally inappropriate for AI development: "We should not do that with these kinds of technologies... breaking things in the real world could be very damaging." (TED interview.) He is particularly critical of the framing that safety and capability research are in tension — he believes they are complementary, and that systems developed without robust safety research pose greater long-term risks even if they deliver short-term capability gains.

His concern is not abstract: as CEO of Google DeepMind, he is one of the organizations actually building frontier systems. His safety commitment is therefore not only a public position but a set of internal constraints on how systems are tested and deployed. He has stated: "At the back of my mind, I've got this gnawing feeling that there's something much more important, much bigger than the commercial race, which is getting AGI safely over the line for humanity and to make sure that the benefits fully outweigh the risks." (Fortune, 2026.)

## Loss of Human Agency

Beyond extinction scenarios, Hassabis has expressed specific concern about softer but still serious risks: the loss of human agency in a world shaped by AI systems, and the concentration of AI benefits in a small number of individuals, companies, or nations. He has argued for equitable distribution of AI's benefits — supporting universal basic income and services as mechanisms for sharing prosperity — and warned that AGI developed by a narrow slice of humanity, for the benefit of a narrow slice, would be both ethically unacceptable and politically unstable. As he put it at Fortune in 2026: "The people that are making artificial intelligence shouldn't just be from 20 square miles of the U.S."
