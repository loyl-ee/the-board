# Chris Eidhof — On Customer Journey

## The Reader and Subscriber as Peer

Eidhof's relationship with his audience is built on a particular assumption: the reader is a working professional developer who wants to go deep, not a beginner looking for a tutorial. This assumption shapes everything about how objc.io's content is structured, marketed, and delivered.

The monthly issues format (2013–2015) made this explicit in its editorial voice — articles were written by and for experienced iOS and macOS developers, often covering topics that Apple's own documentation glossed over. The audience came because the content treated them as capable of handling complexity. The 67 authors who contributed to those 24 issues were themselves part of the community, which created a self-reinforcing loop: the audience trusted the publication because the publication reflected the community's own expertise back to it.

## The Issues Format as Community Object

The monthly issues were designed to function as a community artifact, not just a content product. Each issue was themed — Testing, Architecture, Strings, Concurrency — and drew on multiple expert voices covering the theme from different angles. This structure meant no single author had to be authoritative on the whole topic; the issue itself was the authoritative object, assembled from complementary perspectives.

Eidhof has described the editorial process as intensive: weeks of outlining, sample project construction, peer review, and professional copy-editing. The shared investment created shared ownership — between editors, contributors, and the community of readers who gave feedback. Within five months of launch, the mailing list approached 7,000 subscribers and readership exceeded 500,000 page views. The growth was driven almost entirely by reputation and word of mouth within the Apple developer community.

## Swift Talk Subscribers and the Weekly Rhythm

Swift Talk's subscriber relationship is built on consistency. The series has published a new episode every week since June 2016 — over nine years of uninterrupted weekly delivery. For subscribers, this creates a reliable rhythm: there is always a new Swift Talk. The implicit promise is kept.

The format reinforces the relationship. Episodes are deliberately not polished tutorials — they are live coding discussions where Eidhof and Kugler think out loud, make decisions, sometimes backtrack, and explain their reasoning. Ole Begemann, reviewing the series early, captured this: "The videos are not meant as tutorials for you to follow, but rather discussions of the pros and cons of their solutions, and the live-coding makes it easy to follow their thought process." Subscribers are watching expert practitioners work, not watching finished product.

Half of all Swift Talk episodes are free, which functions as a continuous acquisition channel. Potential subscribers can watch substantial content — including complete multi-episode series — before deciding to subscribe.

## Teaching Advanced Topics to Working Developers

Eidhof's workshop model extends the customer journey into in-person and remote learning. Two-day intensives for teams of up to 16, with pair exercises and group discussion, custom-tailored to the team's own codebase and product challenges. The curriculum covers SwiftUI's view tree, layout system, state management, and animation — topics from *Thinking in SwiftUI* and Swift Talk, delivered in a workshop context where feedback is immediate.

The workshop-to-book relationship is bidirectional: workshops surface the questions real teams have, which inform how books are written. The second edition of *Thinking in SwiftUI* was substantially revised based on workshop experience — Eidhof noted that conducting workshops with many companies had "evolved significantly" his approach to explaining SwiftUI.

## The Community as Colleague

Eidhof's public engagement — on Mastodon, at conferences, through his blog — treats community members as colleagues rather than customers. He shares works in progress (the SwiftUI Field Guide updates logged publicly, Pancake shared with friends before any public launch), posts unpolished observations alongside finished articles, and engages with feedback directly.

The "customer journey" for objc.io and Swift Talk is less a funnel and more a long-term professional relationship. Subscribers are developers who return for years because the content keeps pace with the platform, because the format respects their intelligence, and because the people producing it are visibly still practitioners themselves.
