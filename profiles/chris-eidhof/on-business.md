# Chris Eidhof — On Business

## The objc.io Model

objc.io operates as a small independent technical publishing business — in Eidhof's case, a deliberate alternative to VC-funded startups or large corporate employment. The business is structured around multiple complementary revenue streams, each reinforcing the others: self-published books, a subscription video series, paid workshops, and free open content that builds audience and reputation.

The model is lean by design. After Daniel Eggert stepped back from day-to-day involvement, the operation has been run primarily by Eidhof and Florian Kugler. Two people producing consistent, high-quality technical content across books, video, and live instruction is only possible because each format reuses and reinforces knowledge from the others: book chapters inform workshop curriculum, workshop feedback informs book revisions, Swift Talk episodes explore ideas that surface in both.

## From Issues to Books to Video

The business has evolved through distinct phases, each driven by what the market could support and what the team found sustainable.

**Phase 1 (2013–2015): Monthly Issues.** Twenty-four issues published over two years, featuring 125 articles by 67 different authors. The model was curatorial — Eidhof, Kugler, and Eggert commissioned and edited expert guest writing, building a publication that was authoritative without requiring the three founders to write everything themselves. Within five months of launch, they had 7,000 mailing list subscribers and more than 500,000 page views. They supported this with an optional Newsstand subscription while keeping all content free on the web.

**Phase 2 (2015–2016): Long-form Books.** When Swift was announced in 2014, Eidhof saw an opportunity. He and co-authors were positioned to explain functional programming concepts to an Objective-C community suddenly confronted with a language that supported them. *Functional Swift* was published in late 2014. Two more books followed in 2015. The shift from monthly issues to books reflected a preference for depth and a recognition that books have longer commercial shelf lives than articles.

**Phase 3 (2016–present): Swift Talk.** In June 2016, objc.io launched Swift Talk as a weekly live-coding video series. The business model is straightforward: half of episodes are free, the other half requires a $9/month subscription. The series has run without interruption for nearly a decade and has produced over 488 episodes. This consistency — the discipline to ship something every single week — is itself a business statement: reliability builds subscription trust.

## Quality Over Quantity

Eidhof has consistently prioritized depth over output volume. The monthly issues format — dense, expert-written, narrowly scoped — was the opposite of a content farm. When the team decided to stop publishing issues and focus on books, the announcement acknowledged: "Everyone involved put a lot of effort into every single issue."

Writing an article for objc.io required "at least a few days per article," including research, sample project development, peer review, and professional copy-editing. Eidhof has described the process as: build the sample project first, take notes throughout, write the article from the notes. The rigor of this process is part of what earned the publication its reputation.

## Workshops as Revenue and Feedback

In 2024, Eidhof launched a second company alongside objc.io to house workshops and new initiatives. The workshop business delivers two-day SwiftUI intensives for engineering teams of up to 16 people (in person or via Zoom), at companies including Dexcom, Etsy, Atlassian, and American Express. Workshops serve the business in two ways simultaneously: they are a direct revenue stream, and they continuously improve the product — "The learning has been tremendous from all sides," Eidhof noted in his 2024 review, describing how teaching deepened his own mental model of SwiftUI.

## Indie Technical Publishing as a Viable Path

The objc.io model demonstrates that a small team producing high-quality niche technical content can sustain itself without advertising, sponsorship, or investor capital. The key ingredients: a genuine niche (advanced Apple platform development), consistent high quality that earns word-of-mouth, multiple complementary revenue formats, and the discipline to ship on a schedule.

Eidhof has not written extensively about the business model in public, but his choices speak clearly. He has avoided the temptation to grow the team aggressively, to dilute quality for volume, or to pivot toward more commercially obvious but less technically rigorous content. The business remains recognizably the same project it was in 2013, updated for the technologies that matter now.
